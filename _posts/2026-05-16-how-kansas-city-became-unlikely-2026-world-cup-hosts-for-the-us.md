Kansas City, Missouri, a Midwestern city often overlooked by coast-bound travelers, will become the center of the soccer world this summer. [Arrowhead Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrowhead_Stadium), home of the NFL’s [Kansas City Chiefs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kansas_City_Chiefs), will host six matches at the 2026 FIFA World Cup, including a possible quarterfinal between [Lionel Messi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lionel_Messi)’s Argentina and [Cristiano Ronaldo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cristiano_Ronaldo)’s Portugal. The tournament, the largest in World Cup history, will span 39 days across the United States, Mexico, and Canada.

The selection of Kansas City marks a surprising upset in the bidding process. While cities like [Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles), [New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City), and [Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago) were expected hosts, Kansas City’s bid stood out despite ranking as only the 37th most populous city in the US. According to the 2020 census, Kansas City has just over 500,000 residents, a fraction of the populations in other host cities. Alan Dietrich, chief operating officer of [Sporting Kansas City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sporting_Kansas_City), attributed the city’s success to strong infrastructure, fan culture, and stadium readiness.

## How Kansas City won its World Cup bid

Kansas City’s bid relied on more than just numbers. The city’s compact downtown, robust public transit, and proximity to major highways made it an attractive option for fans navigating the tournament. Arrowhead Stadium, already one of the NFL’s most iconic venues, required minimal modifications for World Cup matches. The stadium’s 76,000 capacity will be reduced to 67,000 for soccer, but the atmosphere is expected to be electric. Fans attending matches will also benefit from Kansas City’s reputation for hospitality, including a thriving barbecue scene and strong local support for its MLS team, Sporting Kansas City.

The World Cup’s arrival in Kansas City comes amid a broader push to grow soccer in the US. The 2026 tournament will feature 48 teams, up from 32, making it the largest in history. Kansas City’s inclusion reflects FIFA’s strategy to showcase the sport in non-traditional markets. Dietrich noted that the city’s bid emphasized accessibility, affordability, and fan experience over sheer population size. “We showed FIFA that we could deliver a world-class event without the typical big-city infrastructure,” he said.

## Who will play in Kansas City?

While the full match schedule won’t be finalized until 2025, Kansas City is expected to host group-stage matches, a Round of 16 game, and the high-profile quarterfinal. The potential Argentina-Portugal clash would be a marquee matchup, given the global appeal of Messi and Ronaldo. The tournament’s format also allows for surprise teams to advance, meaning lesser-known nations could also vie for Kansas City’s attention. Organizers are already preparing for an influx of international fans, with hotels reporting near-full bookings for summer 2026.

Kansas City’s World Cup bid faced stiff competition from larger cities, including [Dallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dallas), [Houston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston), and [Atlanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlanta). Yet the city’s bid team argued that its smaller size would make it easier to navigate for fans. Local businesses are bracing for an economic boost, with projections of $100 million in direct spending from visitors. The city’s airport, [Kansas City International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kansas_City_International_Airport), is also undergoing upgrades to accommodate the influx.

## What’s next for Kansas City?

With less than two years until kickoff, Kansas City is racing to finalize preparations. The city’s bid included commitments to fan zones, transportation expansions, and cultural events to celebrate the World Cup. Local officials are also working to address potential challenges, such as housing shortages and security concerns. The experience could serve as a blueprint for other mid-sized cities seeking to host major sporting events in the future.

The World Cup’s arrival in Kansas City is more than a sporting event; it’s a chance for the city to shine on the global stage. For a region often dismissed as “flyover country,” the attention could reshape perceptions of the Midwest. Fans attending matches will experience a city that blends tradition with modern amenities, all while cheering on teams from around the world. As Dietrich put it, “This isn’t just about soccer. It’s about putting Kansas City on the map.”
