Wirestock just closed a $23 million Series A round, led by Blossom Capital, to expand its supply of multimodal creative datasets to AI labs. The Silicon Valley-based startup pivoted in 2023 from selling stock media to becoming a dedicated data provider for AI training. Its datasets now include high-quality images, videos, design assets, 3D models, and gaming content—everything AI models need to learn how humans create and interact with digital media. The funding will help Wirestock scale its operations, improve its licensing model, and expand the range of content it can offer to research labs and commercial AI developers. The round also included participation from existing investors like SignalFire and Kima Ventures. Wirestock didn’t disclose its valuation, but the size of the round signals strong investor confidence in the AI data supply space. The company claims its datasets are used by major AI labs, though it hasn’t named them publicly. What makes Wirestock different is its focus on real-world creative content rather than synthetic data or scraped internet images. That matters because high-quality, ethically sourced datasets are becoming a bottleneck for AI development. Most AI models today rely on publicly available data scraped from the web, which often leads to copyright issues and poor data quality. Wirestock’s approach avoids that by licensing content directly from creators and companies, giving AI developers cleaner, more reliable datasets. The company operates a marketplace where it aggregates content from thousands of contributors—photographers, designers, 3D artists, and game developers—then cleans, tags, and formats it for AI training. It’s a middleman role that’s increasingly critical as AI labs look to avoid legal and ethical pitfalls in their data pipelines. Wirestock isn’t the only player in this space, but it’s one of the few that’s focused specifically on creative multimodal content. Competitors like [Scale AI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_AI) and [Appen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appen) work more broadly in data annotation and labeling, while Wirestock specializes in high-end creative assets. The company’s timing lines up with a growing demand for better training data. As AI models get more sophisticated, they need richer, more diverse datasets to perform well. Wirestock’s datasets are used for tasks like image generation, video synthesis, and 3D scene reconstruction—areas where quality and variety of data directly impact performance. For example, an AI model trained on Wirestock’s 3D assets can generate more realistic virtual environments than one trained on low-quality or synthetic data. ## Will Wirestock change how AI gets trained? Wirestock’s bet is that its approach will become the standard for AI data supply. The company argues that its licensed datasets reduce legal risks for AI developers while improving model performance. It’s also betting that creators will prefer working with a platform like Wirestock over having their content scraped without compensation. The company plans to use the new funding to expand its content library, improve its AI-powered tagging and curation tools, and explore partnerships with more AI labs. It’s also looking at ways to make its datasets more accessible to smaller AI teams, not just the big labs with deep pockets. The bigger picture here is that the AI industry is realizing that data quality is just as important as model architecture. Wirestock’s growth reflects that shift. While it’s still early, the company could become a key supplier in the AI data supply chain—if it can maintain the trust of both creators and AI developers. For now, it’s one of the few companies making a clear business out of solving the data bottleneck. The next few years will show whether AI labs are willing to pay for curated, licensed data instead of relying on free, but riskier, alternatives.
