In a Copenhagen courtyard not far from the old city walls, Peter Aaby and Christine Stabell Benn have spent four decades arguing a simple but explosive idea: vaccines don’t just protect against the disease they’re designed for. They can also change how a child’s immune system handles unrelated infections, sometimes saving lives in unexpected ways. For years, the global vaccine establishment said it was nonsense. Then Robert F. Kennedy Jr. took over the US Department of Health and Human Services in January. Suddenly, the couple’s work isn’t fringe research anymore—it’s influencing policy decisions that could rewrite vaccination guidelines across America.

Aaby, an epidemiologist trained in Denmark and Guinea-Bissau, noticed the pattern in the 1980s while running a health clinic in West Africa. He saw that children who got the standard measles vaccine died at lower rates not just from measles but from other infections like diarrhea and pneumonia. The data held up even after accounting for better nutrition or healthcare access. Later, in the 1990s, Stabell Benn joined him and expanded the research. Together they showed that some vaccines, like the measles shot, could reduce overall child mortality by more than the disease they prevented. Their findings clashed with the dominant view that vaccines only work against their specific targets.

## The science that almost vanished

For decades, Aaby and Stabell Benn’s work faced stiff resistance. Peer-reviewed journals rejected their papers. Top vaccine scientists dismissed the findings as flawed or cherry-picked. The World Health Organization [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) convened panels to review the data and concluded in 2014 that the non-specific effects were real but small. Still, most researchers ignored them. The couple’s research institute in Guinea-Bissau, the Bandim Health Project, became known for publishing findings that others wouldn’t touch.

The turning point came last year when RFK Jr. was named acting secretary of the US Department of Health and Human Services [Department of Health and Human Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services). Kennedy, a longtime critic of mainstream vaccine science, has long pointed to Aaby and Stabell Benn’s work as evidence that vaccines can do harm. But his appointment has also forced federal health agencies to take the couple’s research seriously. The CDC and NIH have quietly begun reviewing the data, according to people familiar with the discussions. A federal advisory panel is now considering whether to adjust vaccination schedules based on non-specific effects, especially in low-income countries where child mortality remains high.

## What’s changing in vaccine policy now

The shift isn’t just about theory. In Guinea-Bissau, where the Bandim Health Project runs one of Africa’s longest-running demographic surveillance systems, Aaby and Stabell Benn have shown that a child’s chance of surviving past age five jumps by 30% if they receive the measles vaccine on time. The effect holds even in places where measles itself is rare. The data suggests that the vaccine trains the immune system to fight other threats, not just the measles virus. Their findings align with newer immunology research showing that early-life vaccines can reshape immune responses for years.

The US isn’t about to abandon its vaccine schedule, but Kennedy’s team is pushing for a closer look at the non-specific effects in policy decisions. A draft report from the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, released in March, acknowledges that the couple’s work has merit and calls for more funding to study these effects. The report stops short of recommending changes to current vaccine policies but sets the stage for future debates. Meanwhile, Aaby and Stabell Benn are preparing to publish a meta-analysis of 50 studies covering over a million children, a paper they hope will finally silence the skeptics.

## Why this matters beyond vaccines

The implications stretch past vaccination. If vaccines can have broad, lasting effects on immune health, the findings could influence how we design public health programs. Aaby points to the BCG vaccine for tuberculosis, which some studies suggest reduces child mortality from other infections by up to 50%. If true, that could mean rethinking how we deploy vaccines in places where tuberculosis is common but child mortality is driven by diarrhea and malaria. The couple’s work also raises questions about how we evaluate vaccines. Today, regulators judge a vaccine’s success by how well it prevents its target disease. Aaby and Stabell Benn argue that we should also measure overall child survival.

The controversy isn’t over. Critics still argue that the couple’s data relies too heavily on observational studies, which can’t prove cause and effect. They point out that randomized trials of the measles vaccine haven’t consistently shown the same mortality reductions. Aaby counters that these trials were never designed to capture non-specific effects. They only measured how well the vaccine prevented measles. For him, the debate is about more than science—it’s about saving lives. In Guinea-Bissau, where the average woman has five children and one in ten dies before age five, even a small improvement in survival rates is worth fighting for.

What happens next depends on whether the scientific establishment can accept that vaccines do more than they thought. Aaby and Stabell Benn’s research isn’t just being revisited; it’s being weaponized by a federal health chief who’s spent years attacking mainstream vaccine science. That’s a dangerous mix. But if their findings hold up, it could mark the first time in decades that a vaccine policy changed because of evidence, not politics.
