Labour leader Keir Starmer [hasn’t ruled out joining a leadership contest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer), UK Culture Secretary Lisa Nandy said Friday. Nandy told the BBC the prime minister had “shown before that he’s up for a fight” and she wouldn’t count him out. Her remarks suggest Starmer could still be a player if Labour’s internal debates turn into a formal contest. Party insiders say the comments are a signal to activists that Starmer isn’t stepping aside quietly.

The intervention comes as Labour grapples with criticism over its polling lead and internal divisions over policy. Some MPs fear the party’s lead over the Conservatives is slipping, while others argue Starmer’s cautious approach risks losing progressive voters. Nandy’s comments add to the noise, but they also show Labour’s leadership isn’t ready to close the door on a potential fight. The party’s next move could come within weeks, depending on how its internal reviews and polling perform.

Sources close to Starmer say he’s focused on delivering Labour’s agenda, not on internal battles. But the prime minister has faced pressure to be more assertive, especially after Labour lost ground in recent by-elections. Nandy’s remarks suggest Starmer’s team is keeping options open, even if they’re not actively campaigning for a leadership vote.

Labour’s rules allow for a leadership contest if 20% of MPs trigger one. That threshold is high, but not impossible if discontent grows. Starmer’s allies insist he’s not worried, pointing to his 2020 leadership win when he beat Rebecca Long-Bailey and Lisa Nandy herself. The party’s internal polls reportedly still show him as the most popular figure among members and voters.

The culture secretary’s comments also reflect broader tensions in Labour. Some MPs want a sharper left turn, while others push for a more centrist approach. Nandy, a former leadership candidate herself, knows the risks of internal splits. Her refusal to write Starmer off may be a tactical move to keep the party united—or at least to avoid a damaging public split.

What happens next? Labour’s NEC meets next week to review its strategy. If Starmer’s team senses weakness, they might preempt a challenge. But if the party’s polling holds, the pressure could ease. Either way, Nandy’s words add fuel to the fire.
