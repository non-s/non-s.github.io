Israeli forces secretly set up two military outposts in western Iraq months before the US-Israel war on Iran began, according to reports from The New York Times and The Wall Street Journal. Iraqi officials and lawmakers confirmed the existence of the bases, which were identified using satellite imagery near the country’s border with Syria. The makeshift sites were prepared as early as late 2024, with one base becoming operational just before the war started in March 2025. The Wall Street Journal reported the installation operated with Washington’s knowledge and included Israeli special forces along with logistical and search-and-rescue capabilities for downed pilots. Open-source analysts tracked the site’s construction using publicly available satellite images, pinpointing its location in Iraq’s Anbar province, a region known for militant activity and cross-border movements. Israeli forces launched air strikes from this base in early March against Iraqi units that had gotten too close to the site, according to the reports. The covert operation signals a major escalation in Israel’s regional military strategy, expanding its reach beyond traditional hotspots like Lebanon or Gaza. It also raises questions about Iraq’s ability to monitor its own territory, especially in areas where Iran-backed militias operate freely. While the US publicly distanced itself from the operation, unnamed officials told the Journal that Washington was briefed on the plan. The move comes as tensions in the Middle East hit record highs after Iran launched a direct attack on Israel in April 2025, prompting retaliatory strikes. Israel’s use of Iraqi territory for military purposes marks a sharp turn from decades of avoiding such entanglements, reflecting the urgency of its security concerns about Iran’s nuclear program and regional influence. The bases also suggest Israel is preparing for a prolonged conflict, not just a single strike. Analysts warn this could drag Iraq further into regional conflicts it’s tried to avoid since the US withdrawal in 2011. The operation may also strain Iraq’s already fragile relationship with the US, which has been working to rebuild trust with Baghdad despite ongoing tensions over militia groups and Iranian interference. For now, Iraq’s government has not commented publicly on the reports, but the discovery of these bases could fuel further instability in a country already struggling with political paralysis and economic crises. The revelation also puts pressure on the Biden administration to clarify its role in the operation, especially as it seeks to balance support for Israel with efforts to prevent a wider regional war. Military experts say the use of Iraqi territory for covert ops is risky, given the country’s weak central control and the presence of armed factions tied to Tehran.
