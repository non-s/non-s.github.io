London broil presents a budget-friendly steak option but often turns out tough if not prepared properly. A simple baking soda marinade transforms this cut into a consistently tender, juicy dish with basic ingredients. The science behind the method lies in baking soda’s alkaline pH, which breaks down muscle fibers for a more tender texture without altering flavor when used correctly.

## How the Baking Soda Marinade Works

The key to the method is timing. Baking soda reacts quickly with meat proteins, so a 15-30 minute marinade is sufficient—longer exposure risks a chemical aftertaste. Mix 1 teaspoon baking soda with 1 tablespoon water per pound of meat, then coat the steak evenly before refrigerating uncovered for the set time. Rinse thoroughly under cold water afterward to remove the baking soda residue, which prevents any soapy flavor from developing during cooking.

London broil typically refers to flank steak or top round, both of which benefit from this technique. The baking soda marinade works particularly well for grilling, broiling, or pan-searing, as the meat retains moisture during high-heat cooking. Avoid marinating cuts with visible fat or connective tissue, as the method is best suited for lean muscle meat where tenderness is the primary concern.

## Step-by-Step Preparation Guide

Start by selecting a 1.5–2 inch thick London broil cut to ensure even cooking. Trim excess fat but leave a thin layer for flavor during searing. Combine 1 teaspoon baking soda with 1 tablespoon water per pound of meat in a bowl, then whisk into a thin paste. Coat the steak thoroughly on all sides, place it on a plate, and refrigerate uncovered for 15-30 minutes. Rinse the steak under cold running water to remove the baking soda, then pat dry with paper towels before seasoning with salt, pepper, and optional herbs.

Preheat the grill or skillet to high heat. For grilling, sear the steak for 4-5 minutes per side for medium-rare, then move to indirect heat to finish cooking. For stovetop cooking, sear for 3-4 minutes per side in a hot pan with oil. Use a meat thermometer to check for 130°F internal temperature for medium-rare. Let the steak rest for 5-10 minutes before slicing against the grain to maximize tenderness.

## Variations and Flavor Pairings

The baking soda marinade acts as a blank canvas for seasonings. Classic pairings include garlic, rosemary, and black pepper for a simple herb crust. For an Asian-inspired twist, use soy sauce, ginger, and sesame oil in the marinade or as a post-cook glaze. Spicy variations with chili flakes or smoked paprika work well for those preferring a bold kick.

Avoid acidic marinades like lemon juice or vinegar in combination with baking soda, as the chemical reaction can turn the meat mushy. Instead, rely on dry rubs or oil-based marinades after the baking soda step for added flavor without compromising texture. Experiment with different cooking methods, such as reverse searing for thicker cuts, to achieve desired doneness.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Over-marinating with baking soda can result in a metallic or soapy taste, so stick to the 15-30 minute window. Skipping the rinse step leaves residual baking soda that alters the meat’s natural flavor during cooking. Using baking soda on cuts with high fat content, like ribeye, wastes the method’s benefits, as fat already contributes to tenderness.

Another frequent error is not preheating the cooking surface adequately, leading to uneven searing and potential toughness. Always pat the steak dry after rinsing to ensure a proper sear. For those new to London broil, start with thinner cuts to practice timing and temperature control before attempting thicker pieces.

The baking soda marinade technique gained popularity among home cooks after viral cooking demonstrations in 2020 showed its effectiveness for tough cuts. Chefs and food scientists have since endorsed the method for its simplicity and reliability, though it remains underused in traditional steakhouse preparations. The technique aligns with modern trends favoring affordable, high-quality cuts over premium but often expensive options.
