German police shot and killed an escaped tiger on Thursday after it attacked and injured a man near a private facility in Lower Saxony. The incident unfolded around midday local time at a facility owned by Carmen Zander, a trainer often nicknamed Germany’s 'Tiger Queen' in local media reports. Authorities confirmed the man, who suffered serious but non-life-threatening injuries, was taken to a nearby hospital and is now in stable condition.

## Where and how the escape happened
The tiger escaped from its enclosure at Zander’s facility in the town of [Ehmen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehmen), a small community near Wolfsburg. Police were called to the scene after reports of a large cat roaming free in the area. Witnesses described the scene as chaotic as the animal, a 10-year-old male Bengal tiger, charged at the trainer before fleeing toward nearby woods.

Officers tracked the tiger for about 90 minutes before it turned on its pursuers near a residential street. Police attempted to tranquilize the animal but were forced to fire when it charged, according to a statement from the Lower Saxony police. The tiger was shot dead at the scene. No other injuries were reported.

## Carmen Zander’s role and tiger trade in Germany
Zander, 54, has built a controversial reputation in Germany’s exotic animal scene. Local reports describe her as a trainer who has worked with big cats for decades, often performing in private events and online videos. Her facility, which is not licensed as a zoo, has faced criticism from animal welfare groups over the years for its handling practices.

Germany has strict laws governing the ownership and exhibition of wild animals. Private individuals can keep tigers, lions, and other big cats, but they must meet strict enclosure and safety standards. Zander’s facility has not been inspected in recent years, according to local officials, raising questions about compliance with regulations.

## The man’s injuries and ongoing investigation
The man, whose identity has not been released, suffered deep lacerations to his arms and legs during the attack. Doctors at Wolfsburg Hospital said he required emergency surgery and multiple blood transfusions but is now recovering. Police have launched an investigation into how the tiger escaped and why it turned on its trainer.

Zander was present during the incident but was not injured. She told local media she was 'shocked and devastated' by what happened and that the tiger had never shown aggressive behavior before. Authorities are reviewing security footage from the facility to determine how the animal got out.

## Broader concerns about big cat ownership in Germany
The incident has reignited debates about animal welfare and public safety in Germany, where private ownership of big cats remains legal despite multiple high-profile escapes. Animal rights activists argue that such facilities should be banned outright, calling them inherently dangerous for both animals and the public.

Germany’s federal government has considered tightening regulations in recent years, but progress has stalled due to lobbying from private owners and trainers. The country currently requires owners to register big cats and meet enclosure standards, but inspections are rare and penalties for violations are often minimal.

What happens next? Police have seized the facility’s records and are reviewing Zander’s compliance with animal welfare laws. The case could lead to new scrutiny of private big cat ownership in Germany, though similar incidents in the past have rarely resulted in lasting changes.
