Bowel cancer, also called colorectal cancer, affects the colon or rectum and is the third most diagnosed cancer worldwide, according to the [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization). Each year, nearly 16,800 people die from the disease in the U.S. alone. Survival rates exceed 90% when detected early, making awareness of symptoms critical. The disease often begins as non-cancerous polyps that can turn malignant over time. Regular screening and quick action on warning signs can prevent progression and save lives.

## What are the main symptoms of bowel cancer?

The most common signs include persistent changes in bowel habits, such as diarrhea or constipation lasting more than four weeks. Blood in the stool or bleeding from the rectum is another red flag, often appearing as bright red or very dark blood. Unexplained weight loss and persistent abdominal pain or bloating also warrant medical attention. Fatigue and anemia caused by chronic blood loss can develop in later stages. Experts emphasize that these symptoms do not always mean cancer but should be checked by a doctor promptly.

## Who is most at risk of bowel cancer?

Age is the biggest risk factor, with most cases diagnosed in people over 50. A family history of bowel cancer or polyps increases risk significantly, as does a personal history of inflammatory bowel disease, such as [ulcerative colitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulcerative_colitis) or [Crohn’s disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease). Lifestyle factors like smoking, heavy alcohol use, and a diet high in red or processed meats also raise risk. Obesity and lack of physical activity are additional contributors. Genetic conditions such as Lynch syndrome account for a small but notable percentage of cases.

## How can you reduce your bowel cancer risk?

Diet and exercise are among the most effective ways to lower risk. A diet rich in fiber from fruits, vegetables, and whole grains helps maintain healthy digestion and reduces polyp formation. Limiting red meat and avoiding processed meats, such as bacon and sausages, can cut risk by up to 40%, according to the [American Cancer Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Cancer_Society). Regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, and avoiding smoking and excessive alcohol also play key roles in prevention. Screening tests, such as colonoscopies, can detect polyps before they turn cancerous, offering another layer of protection.

## What screening tests are available for bowel cancer?

Screening programs vary by country but commonly include stool tests and colonoscopies. A [fecal immunochemical test (FIT)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fecal_immunochemical_test) checks for hidden blood in stool and is often the first step in many national screening programs. A colonoscopy allows doctors to view the entire colon and remove polyps during the procedure. In the U.S., the [American Cancer Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Cancer_Society) recommends starting regular screening at age 45 for average-risk adults. Higher-risk individuals may need to begin earlier or undergo more frequent testing.

## How accurate are bowel cancer symptom checks at home?

At-home symptom checks are not diagnostic tools but serve as early alert systems. If you notice persistent changes in bowel habits, blood in stool, or unexplained weight loss, see a doctor immediately. Self-checks can prompt timely medical evaluation, which is crucial for early detection. While not foolproof, awareness of symptoms helps bridge gaps in screening access and encourages those reluctant to seek care. Doctors stress that self-awareness combined with regular screening offers the best chance for early intervention.

Experts warn that delays in diagnosis lead to more advanced cancers and poorer outcomes. Public health campaigns in the U.S. and UK aim to increase screening participation, particularly among underserved communities. Advocacy groups like the [Colorectal Cancer Alliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorectal_Cancer_Alliance) push for policy changes to improve access to screening and reduce disparities in care.
