The Trump Mobile T1, the flagship smartphone from [Donald Trump’s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump) company, hits mailboxes this week after months of delays. But there’s a catch: it’s not the brand-new 2025 model that was first announced. Instead, it’s a slightly updated version of a 2024 handset, according to internal documents reviewed by CNET. The company originally promised the T1 would launch last year, but production snags and supply chain issues pushed the date back repeatedly. Now, the first batch of phones is shipping to preorder customers who put down deposits as early as last summer. Orders open to the general public on Tuesday, though inventory will be limited at first. The T1 retails for $799, matching its 2024 predecessor, with a few minor software tweaks and a new Trump-branded startup screen. It runs on Android, not the iPhone-style operating system some early teasers suggested, and packs a Snapdragon 8 Gen 3 chip — a top-tier processor when it launched over a year ago. The phone’s design keeps the same curved-edge glass and stainless steel frame, but adds a Trump logo on the back instead of a generic branding. Inside, the battery life and camera specs remain nearly identical to the 2024 model. Trump Mobile partnered with [Wiko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiko), a French-Chinese phone maker, to build the devices. Wiko handles manufacturing in China and provides most of the components, while Trump Mobile’s team in Florida handles branding, marketing, and software customizations. The delay has cost the brand momentum. Early tech reviewers who got early access in 2024 panned the phone for feeling outdated by launch time. Social media chatter has been mixed, with some supporters calling it a win for “Made in America” tech, while skeptics mock the similarity to last year’s model. Trump Mobile hasn’t commented on whether future models will launch on time or if the T1’s issues will push back the next device. Preorders are open now, but the company warns shipments could take four to six weeks due to limited stock. Analysts say the brand needs more than just a name to compete in the crowded $150 billion smartphone market. The T1’s launch is a test: can Trump Mobile turn political loyalty into a real tech player, or will it remain a novelty gadget with a famous name but little innovation? ## The Trump Mobile story: branding meets tech delays The T1 isn’t Trump Mobile’s first attempt at a phone. The company teased a Trump-branded device as far back as 2023, promising a “patriot-friendly” smartphone with conservative-friendly apps and a “Trump OS” that never materialized. The T1’s launch is the first concrete product, but it arrives with a quiet pivot: no custom operating system, no exclusive conservative apps, just a rebranded Android handset. The brand’s pivot suggests it’s learning from early mistakes. Instead of trying to build a new tech ecosystem, Trump Mobile is leaning on its name recognition and selling a familiar product with a new logo. But that strategy has risks. Tech reviewers note the T1 lacks standout features compared to $800 phones from Apple, Samsung, or Google. The camera system uses a 64MP main sensor, which was competitive in 2024 but now faces stiff competition from newer midrange phones with better zoom and night modes. Battery life is solid at 24 hours, but not exceptional. The Trump Mobile team has hinted that future models might include more unique features, like tighter integration with Truth Social or exclusive political content, but no details have been confirmed. For now, the T1 is a safe bet for loyalists willing to pay for the branding, but not a must-have for tech enthusiasts. ## Who’s buying the T1 — and why it matters The first wave of buyers is likely to be Trump supporters eager to own a piece of his brand. Preorders opened last summer with a $100 deposit, and CNET’s early checks show long waitlists in conservative strongholds like Florida, Texas, and parts of the Midwest. Some buyers told local reporters they see the phone as a political statement, not just a device. One preorder customer from Ohio said, “I don’t care if it’s 2024 tech. I want a phone that says I support Trump, and this is it.” That kind of enthusiasm could help Trump Mobile sell out its initial stock, but it won’t translate into long-term success unless the brand evolves. The bigger question is whether the T1 can attract non-loyalists. Tech reviewers who got early units called the phone “competent but forgettable,” noting it doesn’t outperform $500 Android phones from budget brands. The Trump Mobile website doesn’t list carrier deals, so buyers will likely pay full price upfront, another hurdle for mainstream adoption. The brand’s next move is critical. If the T1 sells well, Trump Mobile may double down on hardware with updated models. If it flops, the company could pivot to licensing the Trump name to other tech products, like tablets or smartwatches, or focus on software like a conservative-friendly app store. One thing is clear: the delay hurt the brand’s credibility. When the T1 was first announced, it was supposed to launch in 2025 with bold new features. Instead, it arrives as a 2024 handset with minor changes. That misstep has left some early supporters frustrated, and the brand will need to prove it can deliver on future promises. For now, the T1 is here — flawed, delayed, but finally real. The real test for Trump Mobile starts now.
