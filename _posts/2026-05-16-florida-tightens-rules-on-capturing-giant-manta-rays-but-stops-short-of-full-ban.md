Wildlife officials in Florida [Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida_Fish_and_Wildlife_Conservation_Commission) voted unanimously on Wednesday to adopt amended rules governing the capture of giant manta rays in state waters. The decision comes after a bipartisan push from lawmakers and public outrage sparked by a viral video showing a ray being roughly handled and placed into an unmarked boat off Florida’s coast last year. While the new regulations impose stricter controls, they do not impose an outright ban on capturing the threatened species for “responsible exhibition” in marine facilities.

The amended rule reserves authority for the [Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida_Fish_and_Wildlife_Conservation_Commission) to approve or deny permits for capturing manta rays based on location, timing, and intended use. Commissioners emphasized that the changes aim to prevent unnecessary harm and reduce public distress over the treatment of these large, gentle marine animals. Under the new policy, permits will only be issued for scientific research or educational display, with strict oversight on handling procedures and animal welfare standards. Violations could result in fines, permit revocation, or criminal charges.

## New limits on manta ray capture raise concerns among conservationists

Critics, including marine biologists and animal rights groups, argue the revised rules do not go far enough. They point out that giant manta rays, listed as threatened under Florida law, remain vulnerable to stress and injury during capture and transport. Conservation advocates had urged a complete ban, citing the species’ slow reproductive rate and sensitivity to human interaction. The viral video, viewed millions of times online, showed a ray being lifted by its wings and tossed into a boat, prompting widespread condemnation and calls for stronger protections.

## Political pressure builds for stricter marine animal protections

A bipartisan group of Florida lawmakers introduced legislation earlier this year seeking to end all commercial capture of manta rays in state waters. While that bill stalled in committee, it signaled growing legislative concern over the practice. Commissioners acknowledged public sentiment and political pressure in their decision, noting that 90% of public comments submitted during the rulemaking process opposed manta ray capture altogether. Still, the final rule reflects a compromise between animal welfare advocates and marine entertainment industry stakeholders.

## What happens next for Florida’s manta rays?

The updated rules take effect immediately, with the [Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida_Fish_and_Wildlife_Conservation_Commission) planning to review permit applications on a case-by-case basis. Conservation groups have vowed to monitor enforcement closely and push for further restrictions if violations occur. Meanwhile, marine theme parks and aquariums that rely on live ray exhibits will need to adapt to the new permitting process, which may limit availability of manta rays for public display. The broader debate over marine animal welfare in Florida continues, with activists already targeting other species in captivity. The outcome could influence similar regulations nationwide as public awareness of marine life protection grows.
