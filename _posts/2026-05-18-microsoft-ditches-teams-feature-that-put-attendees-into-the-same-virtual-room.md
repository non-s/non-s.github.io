Microsoft is wiping out its Teams ‘Together’ mode at the end of June, turning every meeting into a standard Gallery view instead. The feature, launched in April 2020 as a way to make remote workers feel like they were sitting in the same room, never really caught on beyond a few early adopters. Microsoft now says it adds unnecessary strain on servers and delivers blurry, stuttering video on phones and basic laptops. Gallery mode, where faces appear in neat rectangles, will become the default starting June 30, 2026. In a quiet blog post, Microsoft admitted Together mode ‘increases cognitive load for users’ and turns every call into a mini computer lab trying to keep up with effects like fake backgrounds and forced perspective. The company quietly rolled back its own promises that Together mode would feel more natural than staring at a grid of talking heads.


## The real reason Microsoft wants to kill Together mode
Microsoft’s engineers say Gallery mode is easier to run because it stops asking every device to render the same fake conference table or classroom every time someone joins. The software giant also promised faster fixes for video quality when it’s not distracted by spinning 3D rooms or cartoonish seating charts. Engineers working on Teams told Engadget that Gallery mode now uses ‘adaptive video tile counts,’ which means each person’s video feed automatically shrinks or grows based on how much power the device has left. The result should be fewer frozen faces and more stable calls on budget Android phones or eight-year-old Windows laptops.


Teams has never been a fan favorite. A 2025 survey by the UK software firm Okta found that 42% of workers still actively avoid using Teams whenever possible, often because calls cut out or the app feels like it needs a manual to operate. Microsoft itself ranked last in a JD Power reliability study released last month, with users complaining about delayed messages and random crashes. Killing Together mode won’t fix every problem, but it removes one of the flashiest features that nobody actually used while freeing up server space for things people do care about, like quicker message delivery and fewer dropped calls.


## What happens next to Teams users
On June 30, Together mode will vanish from the desktop app, the mobile app, and the web version. Anyone still trying to use it will get an automatic prompt to switch to Gallery mode the first time they open Teams after that date. Microsoft says it will keep a ‘legacy mode’ toggle in settings for six more months, but after December 31, 2026, the code will be deleted entirely. The company’s engineering team is already working on ‘super-resolution’ and ‘denoising’ upgrades that should make every face look sharper without draining battery life. Early testers in the Insider program say the improvements are noticeable, but only if users let Teams run in the background instead of closing it after every call.


Microsoft’s move comes as Slack [Slack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slack_(software)) and Zoom [Zoom Video Communications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoom_Video_Communications) keep grabbing market share by promising simpler, faster meetings. Teams still has 300 million users worldwide, but growth has stalled as remote work habits settle into a permanent hybrid routine. Microsoft’s next big push appears to be AI-generated meeting notes and real-time translation, features that don’t rely on fancy visual effects but do depend on rock-solid video feeds. If the Gallery-only future delivers on its promises, Teams might finally start shedding its reputation as the clunkiest major video calling app.


The death of Together mode also marks the quiet end of an era when Microsoft tried to make virtual meetings feel like real ones. Remember the fake lecture halls and coffee-shop tables? They’re gone now, replaced by the same grid of faces we’ve used since the early days of Skype. Microsoft’s gamble is that users would rather have a meeting that works than one that looks pretty.
