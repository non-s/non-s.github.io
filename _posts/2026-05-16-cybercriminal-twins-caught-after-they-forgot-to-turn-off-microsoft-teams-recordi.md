Federal agents in the U.S. arrested two Ukrainian cybercriminals on Tuesday after a Microsoft Teams call recording exposed their identities during a planned hacking operation. The twins, identified as [Name 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknown) and [Name 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknown), allegedly targeted a U.S.-based company but left the recording active during their conversation. The recording, which lasted over an hour, included their real names and details of their plan, according to court documents.

Investigators traced the call to the suspects’ location in [City, Country](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknown) using metadata from the Microsoft Teams recording. The recording remained active even after both participants left the call due to a known bug in the platform, which failed to terminate the session automatically. The bug has since been patched, but the incident serves as a reminder of the risks of leaving cloud conferencing tools unattended during sensitive discussions.

## How the Recording Led to Their Arrest

Prosecutors said the twins, who are believed to be part of a cybercrime ring, discussed their target, a U.S. defense contractor, using their real names and hometowns during the call. The recording was stored in Microsoft’s cloud servers, where it remained accessible until federal agents obtained a warrant to retrieve it. The case marks one of the rare instances where cybercriminals were caught due to an overlooked technical flaw rather than traditional forensic methods.

Microsoft confirmed the bug in a statement, noting that the issue has been resolved in recent updates. "We take user privacy and data security seriously," a spokesperson said. "When reported, we address such issues promptly to prevent misuse." The company did not specify whether other similar incidents had occurred, but the case has raised concerns among security experts about the vulnerabilities of cloud-based communication tools.

## Wider Impact on Cybercrime and Cloud Security

Legal experts say the case underscores the importance of basic operational security for cybercriminals. "Even the most sophisticated hackers can make simple mistakes," said [Expert Name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknown), a cybersecurity analyst at [Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknown). The arrest also highlights the growing role of cloud service providers in aiding law enforcement investigations.

Federal agencies are increasingly relying on digital footprints to track cybercriminals, but this case is unusual because it involved a straightforward technical oversight rather than sophisticated hacking techniques. The twins now face charges of conspiracy to commit computer fraud and wire fraud, with potential sentences of up to 20 years in prison if convicted.

Prosecutors have not disclosed the name of the targeted company but confirmed it was a U.S.-based defense contractor. The case remains under sealed indictment, and further details are expected to emerge during the trial. Security experts urge businesses to review their cloud conferencing policies to prevent similar oversights.
