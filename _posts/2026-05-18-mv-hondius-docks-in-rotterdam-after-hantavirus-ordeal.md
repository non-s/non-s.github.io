The MV Hondius, a luxury cruise ship registered in the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands), pulled into the [Port of Rotterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Rotterdam) on Monday morning, ending a voyage that turned deadly after a hantavirus outbreak. The ship had been carrying roughly 150 people from 23 countries when authorities reported the first deaths earlier this month. All three victims were from the Philippines, according to Dutch health officials. The remaining crew—25 sailors and two medics—will stay on board as part of a quarantine ordered by the [Dutch National Institute for Public Health and the Environment, RIVM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rijksinstituut_voor_Volkgezondheid_en_Milieu).

## How hantavirus spread on the ship
Hantavirus isn’t spread person-to-person. Instead, it jumps from rodents to humans through urine, droppings, or saliva. Infected particles can become airborne when disturbed, making ships, cabins, and storage areas risky spots. The outbreak on the Hondius likely started when rodents got into food supplies or crew quarters. The virus causes Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome, a severe lung infection that can turn fatal if untreated. Symptoms include fever, muscle aches, and breathing problems. The [U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention) says it’s rare but dangerous, with a fatality rate around 38% in severe cases.

## Rotterdam’s response and next steps
Dutch authorities moved fast once the outbreak was confirmed. The ship was rerouted to Rotterdam, where port health teams boarded it immediately. The RIVM set up a quarantine zone for the skeleton crew still on board. Port officials declined to say when the crew would be allowed off the ship, citing the need for further testing. Passengers who disembarked earlier have been monitored for symptoms, though none showed signs of infection.

## Why cruise ships are high-risk for outbreaks
Cruise ships pack thousands of people into tight spaces with shared food, water, and air systems. Rodents thrive in these conditions, and once they’re in, they’re hard to remove. Previous outbreaks on ships have included norovirus, measles, and now hantavirus. The Hondius case shows how quickly a rodent-borne illness can spread when hygiene and pest control fall short. Cruise lines often rely on fumigation and traps, but prevention isn’t foolproof.

## What happens to the ship now
The MV Hondius won’t sail again until health officials give the all-clear. The crew still on board will undergo medical checks and decontamination procedures. The ship’s food supplies are being destroyed to prevent further contamination. Rotterdam port authorities are working with the RIVM to decide whether the vessel needs a deep clean or structural repairs. The cruise line hasn’t commented on compensation for passengers or crew.

This outbreak highlights the hidden risks of cruise travel. Rodents can board ships almost anywhere, and once they do, they’re nearly impossible to track. The Hondius incident could push cruise companies to tighten pest control or even change how they handle food storage. For now, the focus is on containing the outbreak and preventing more deaths.
