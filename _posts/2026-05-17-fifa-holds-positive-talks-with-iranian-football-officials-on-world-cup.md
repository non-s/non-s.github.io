FIFA Secretary-General [Mattias Grafstrom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FIFA) called the meeting with Iran’s football chief [Mehdi Taj](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mehdi_Taj) “excellent” and “constructive,” expressing confidence Iran will compete in the June 11–July 19 tournament. Their group matches are set for the United States, but Iran’s participation was uncertain after the US and Israel launched attacks on Iran on February 28, igniting a regional conflict.

Iran’s football federation president, Taj, was denied entry to Canada for the FIFA Congress in Vancouver earlier this month. His delegation turned back at Toronto’s main airport, citing poor treatment by Canadian immigration officials. The incident added to worries about Iran’s ability to travel freely for the World Cup.

Taj confirmed to Reuters that FIFA addressed Iran’s concerns during the talks. While he didn’t detail the solutions, he said the discussions were productive. Iran’s team must still finalize travel plans and secure visas, but Grafstrom said FIFA is working closely with Iranian officials to ensure smooth participation.

The uncertainty over Iran’s World Cup spot comes as tensions spike between Iran and its regional rivals. The US and Israel have continued military actions against Iranian targets, including strikes on April 1, which killed seven Iranian military officers. Iran retaliated with missile and drone attacks on Israeli military sites, raising fears of a broader conflict that could disrupt global sports events.

Iran’s football federation has faced other hurdles too. In March, FIFA stripped the Iranian national team of its right to host the 2026 qualifier against Lebanon due to government interference in football, forcing the match to be played in a neutral country. The federation has since worked to regain FIFA’s trust, including reforms to reduce political influence in team selection and management.

Grafstrom’s optimism contrasts with earlier warnings from FIFA about the risks of holding matches in conflict zones. The governing body has emphasized that security and logistical concerns are its top priorities, but it also wants to keep the World Cup as global and inclusive as possible. Iran’s inclusion would mark a rare moment of sports diplomacy amid the current geopolitical storm.

For now, Iran’s players and fans are focused on the tournament’s kickoff. The team is set to open its campaign against Nigeria in [AT&T Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AT%26T_Stadium) in Texas, followed by matches against Argentina in Miami and Portugal in Houston. Each venue is in a major US city, raising questions about security and fan travel from Iran.

What happens next depends on whether Iran finalizes travel plans and secures entry for its delegation and supporters. FIFA’s next step is to confirm Iran’s participation in writing, likely by early May. If Iran plays, it would be their third consecutive World Cup appearance, after reaching the knockout stage in 2018 and 2022. If they skip the tournament, FIFA would need to find a replacement team quickly, a messy and costly process.

The situation also tests FIFA’s ability to balance sports and politics. The governing body has faced criticism for allowing Russia to compete in the 2022 World Cup despite its invasion of Ukraine. Now, FIFA is under pressure to avoid repeating those controversies while keeping the tournament on track.
