> 📰 **Continuing coverage:** [Starmer’s leadership under pressure as Labour MPs question authority](/politics/2026/05/16/why-are-we-even-doing-this-the-week-that-left-britains-pm-looking-like-an-interi/)

Labour’s internal polling shows the party’s approval rating has dropped sharply in the past month, leaving some MPs worried the party will lose the next election. Keir Starmer [met his Cabinet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer) on Tuesday and made it clear he wasn’t stepping down, according to three people briefed on the discussion. ‘He said there’s no contest and he’s not going anywhere,’ one told the BBC. Another described the mood as ‘tense but not chaotic’—a sign of deeper concern than the public statement suggests.

## Why the sudden pressure on Starmer

The last YouGov poll gave Labour a 12-point lead over the Conservatives, but internal party surveys show that gap closing fast. MPs who once backed Starmer out of loyalty now say they’re getting calls from donors and activists demanding answers. ‘People are asking what the plan is,’ said one backbencher. ‘They want to know why we’re still 10 points behind in the red wall seats.’ The ‘red wall’ refers to the former Labour strongholds in the Midlands and North of England that the party won in 2019 but now looks vulnerable.

Starmer’s team insists the party’s policies are solid, but critics point to recent missteps. The government’s decision to pause the rollout of new oil and gas licences drew criticism from business leaders, while the party’s stance on immigration remains a sticking point with voters. ‘We’re not losing because of policies,’ said a Shadow Cabinet minister. ‘We’re losing because people don’t feel we’re serious about winning.’

## The next move: who’s watching who

Labour MPs aren’t calling for a leadership challenge yet, but they’re watching closely. The party’s National Executive Committee meets next week to review polling and strategy. If the numbers don’t improve by then, pressure will mount. One MP told the BBC: ‘If we’re still behind in three weeks, you’ll see letters going in.’ That would trigger a leadership contest under Labour’s rules, though it’s far from certain Starmer would lose such a vote.

Starmer’s allies argue he’s the only leader who can unite the party after years of infighting. ‘He’s the one who beat Corbyn and won the 2024 election,’ said a senior figure. ‘If anyone can turn this around, it’s him.’ But even his supporters admit the clock is ticking. ‘We’ve got until the summer conference to show progress,’ said one. ‘After that, it’s anyone’s game.’

The Conservatives, meanwhile, are doing their best to fan the flames. Prime Minister Rishi Sunak [has repeatedly challenged Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rishi_Sunak) to a head-to-head debate, knowing Starmer has avoided one for months. ‘He won’t debate me because he knows he’ll lose,’ Sunak said in a speech yesterday. Starmer’s team dismisses the challenge as a distraction tactic, but the pressure isn’t just coming from the opposition. Labour’s own MPs are starting to ask tougher questions, and if the polls don’t budge, the calls for change will only get louder.

## What happens next

For now, Starmer remains in charge, but his grip on the party is weaker than it was a month ago. His team is planning a major policy push in the coming weeks, hoping to reset the narrative before the summer. But if the polls don’t improve, Labour’s internal crisis will deepen. The next few weeks could decide whether Starmer stays on as leader—or whether Labour enters another period of chaos.
