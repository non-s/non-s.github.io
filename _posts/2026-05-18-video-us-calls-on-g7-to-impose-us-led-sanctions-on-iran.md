US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent [led](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury) a fresh push Wednesday for Washington’s allies to impose new sanctions on Iran. Bessent made the plea as finance ministers from the G7 [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_of_Seven) arrived in Paris for a two-day meeting focused on global economic stress and rising geopolitical risks. His comments underscore how Iran’s actions in the Middle East have pushed its relations with Western powers to a new low, just months after Tehran’s direct military clashes with Israel and regional proxies escalated regional tensions even further.

## Why the US wants new sanctions now
The US isn’t starting from scratch. Washington already imposed wide-ranging sanctions on Iran after its 1979 revolution, targeting its oil exports, banking, and military programs. But Bessent argued current measures aren’t enough because Iran has found ways around them through allies and new trade routes. ‘The world needs to step up,’ Bessent told reporters outside the French finance ministry. ‘Iran’s oil revenues fund militias and ballistic missile programs that threaten regional stability.’ Iran’s foreign ministry called the move ‘economic terrorism’ and vowed to retaliate, but didn’t specify how.

The timing matters. Oil prices spiked last month after Iran launched a direct missile and drone attack on Israel, its first ever, in response to an Israeli strike on an Iranian consulate in Syria. Brent crude briefly topped $95 a barrel before easing back, but the episode showed how quickly regional conflicts can rattle global markets. European officials say they’re open to discussing new measures, but worry fresh sanctions could backfire by driving up fuel costs or pushing Iran closer to Russia and China.

## What the sanctions would target
Bessent didn’t spell out exact measures, but US officials have floated several options. One focus would be Iran’s oil exports, which still bring in around $50 billion a year despite existing restrictions. Another target would be the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps [IRGC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Revolutionary_Guard_Corps), which the US already labels a terrorist group but which still controls large parts of Iran’s economy. Washington also wants to choke off funding for groups like Lebanon’s Hezbollah, Yemen’s Houthis, and Iraq’s militias that have launched attacks on shipping and Israeli positions.

European diplomats say they’re divided. France and Britain lean toward stricter measures, but Germany and Italy worry tighter restrictions could hurt their energy imports or trade ties. Italy’s finance minister told reporters Tuesday that ‘we need to balance pressure with stability.’ Japan, another G7 member, has already reduced oil imports from Iran but hasn’t joined US sanctions so far.

## Iran’s response and regional fallout
Tehran isn’t sitting still. In April, Iran’s oil minister announced plans to triple production at the South Pars gas field, the world’s largest, to offset lost exports. The country also turned to barter deals with countries like Syria and Venezuela to keep goods flowing. Analysts say Iran has lost about 1.5 million barrels a day of oil exports since 2018, but still manages to sell roughly 1.2 million barrels daily through discounts and middlemen.

The broader impact is already visible. Shipping insurers have raised rates for vessels passing through the Strait of Hormuz [Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz), where one-fifth of the world’s oil passes. Tanker tracking shows some ships are taking longer routes to avoid Iranian waters. Meanwhile, Houthi rebels in Yemen continue to disrupt Red Sea shipping, and Israeli strikes on Iranian-backed groups in Syria and Lebanon have kept the border on edge.

## What happens next
G7 finance ministers finish their meetings Thursday, but sanctions talks could drag on for weeks. The US wants a united front before the next G7 leaders’ summit in Canada this summer. European diplomats say any new measures would likely target Iran’s central bank and specific shipping firms, not a full oil embargo. Bessent hinted that Washington might act alone if the G7 can’t agree, pointing to unilateral US penalties already in place against dozens of Iranian entities.

For Iran, the squeeze is tightening. The country’s currency, the rial, has lost more than half its value since 2020. Inflation topped 50% last year, and young Iranians are leaving the country in record numbers. But the regime shows no sign of backing down, doubling down on its ‘resistance economy’ strategy to survive under sanctions. If the G7 agrees, Iran’s next move could be even more aggressive—or desperate.
