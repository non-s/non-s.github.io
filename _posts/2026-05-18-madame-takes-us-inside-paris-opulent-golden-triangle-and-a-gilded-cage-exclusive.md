Hélène Rosselet-Ruiz’s first feature film, ‘Madame,’ storms into Cannes this week with a story set in Paris’ most exclusive neighborhood—the Golden Triangle. The triangle, bordered by Avenue Montaigne, Avenue George V, and Rue François 1er, is home to the world’s priciest real estate. Wealthy residents here never worry about rent, but Rosselet-Ruiz’s film asks what happens when money can’t buy freedom. The plot follows two women whose lives twist around a Saudi prince with deep pockets and unclear motives. Their gilded cage isn’t just about gold; it’s about the silent rules of a world where power moves faster than trust. The film lands in the Cannes Special Screenings lineup, a category for bold, unconventional projects that don’t fit the festival’s main competition. It’s the same section that gave us last year’s ‘Anatomy of a Fall,’ which Marie-Ange Luciani also produced. Luciani’s involvement lends weight to ‘Madame,’ signaling it’s not just another pretty Parisian drama but a story with teeth. Rosselet-Ruiz, who spent years working in fashion and photography before turning to film, brings a sharp eye for luxury’s underbelly. She knows how wealth reshapes relationships, and that’s the core of this film. The Golden Triangle’s glitter hides old secrets, and the prince’s arrival stirs them up. The film’s Cannes premiere isn’t just a debut—it’s a statement. France’s film industry is watching closely after last year’s Oscar win for ‘Anatomy of a Fall.’ The country’s cinema is in a moment of reinvention, and ‘Madame’ could be part of that shift. But the Golden Triangle isn’t just a backdrop. It’s a character itself—a place where every glance at a shop window or a café table carries weight. The women in the story aren’t just players; they’re prisoners of their own privilege. ## Paris’ Golden Triangle isn’t just rich real estate The Golden Triangle’s reputation isn’t hyperbole. It’s one of the few places on Earth where a single square meter of property can cost €200,000 or more. The streets are lined with Dior, Chanel, and Hermès boutiques, their windows lit like altars. Inside these stores, salespeople don’t just sell clothes—they curate identities. The triangle’s residents aren’t just rich; they’re insular. Their social circles rarely cross class lines, and their idea of adventure is a new vintage of Bordeaux. Rosselet-Ruiz’s film captures this world without romanticizing it. The women in ‘Madame’ move through these streets like ghosts, their wealth a shield and a cage. The Saudi prince isn’t just a guest in this world—he’s a disruptor. His arrival forces the women to confront the emptiness of their gilded lives. The film’s tension comes from watching how far they’ll go to keep up appearances. It’s not about love or even power. It’s about survival in a world where one wrong step means social exile. ## The Cannes premiere and what it means for Rosselet-Ruiz The Cannes Special Screenings lineup is a proving ground. Films here often fly under the radar of major awards but attract industry insiders who spot future trends. ‘Madame’ fits that bill. It’s a personal project for Rosselet-Ruiz, who drew from her own experiences navigating Paris’ high society after years in fashion. The film’s tone is icy but not cold—more like a winter afternoon in the Golden Triangle, crisp and clear. The cast hasn’t been fully revealed, but the chemistry between the two women and the prince will make or break the story. Early buzz suggests the prince’s character is layered, neither villain nor hero but something in between. His wealth gives him access, but his outsider status makes him dangerous. For Rosselet-Ruiz, this is more than a debut. It’s a test of whether France’s film industry is ready for stories that dissect its elite without flinching. Last year’s success of ‘Anatomy of a Fall’ proved there’s an appetite for sharp, morally complex tales. ‘Madame’ could be the next step in that evolution. If it resonates, it might push other filmmakers to tackle similar themes—wealth, power, and the cost of belonging. ## What happens next at Cannes and beyond ‘Madame’ premieres tonight at Cannes, with a red carpet that’s likely to be as crowded as any on the Croisette. The film’s inclusion in Special Screenings means it won’t compete for the Palme d’Or, but that doesn’t diminish its impact. Industry buyers will be watching closely for distribution deals, especially in markets where luxury and intrigue sell well. The Golden Triangle’s mystique isn’t just Parisian—it’s global. The film’s themes of power, privilege, and the traps of wealth will resonate far beyond France’s borders. For Rosselet-Ruiz, the next few months will be critical. If ‘Madame’ lands distribution deals in the U.S. or Asia, it could introduce her to a wider audience. But the real test is whether the film sparks conversations beyond film festivals. Does it make people question their own relationship with wealth? Does it expose the cracks in the Golden Triangle’s polished surface? The answers won’t come overnight, but Cannes is the perfect place to start.
