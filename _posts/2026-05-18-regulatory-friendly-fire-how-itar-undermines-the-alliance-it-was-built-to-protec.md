The U.S. has long been the world’s top arms dealer, selling over 40% of all weapons globally. That dominance wasn’t just about money—it gave Washington outsized influence as the security guarantor of choice. But today, the system built to protect allies is working against them. The [International Traffic in Arms Regulations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Traffic_in_Arms_Regulations) (ITAR), designed to control sensitive defense tech, now blocks partners from buying American gear even when the U.S. can’t supply it fast enough—or at all.

Take the [Ukraine war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russo-Ukrainian_War). Kyiv needs missiles, drones, and artillery rounds. The U.S. can’t keep up with its own demand, let alone Kyiv’s. Yet ITAR’s strict controls mean even NATO allies with approved export licenses often wait months for parts or upgrades. Poland, for example, had to send its [Patriot missile systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIM-104_Patriot) back to the U.S. for repairs because local firms couldn’t touch the classified components under ITAR.

Arab partners have faced the same problem. During recent conflicts, they burned through high-end interceptors like the [THAAD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_High_Altitude_Area_Defense) and [Patriot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIM-104_Patriot) systems. When missiles ran low, ITAR made it nearly impossible to restock quickly. Some had to rely on older, less effective systems or turn to other suppliers—like Israel or South Korea—who don’t tie their sales to the same red tape.

The issue isn’t just about speed. It’s about trust. Partners who once saw the U.S. as the only reliable supplier now hedge their bets. Poland recently bought [South Korean K2 tanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K2_BLACK_PANTHER) and [FA-50 jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KA-50_and_KA-52_families) because Seoul moved faster and with fewer strings attached. Saudi Arabia, a long-time U.S. client, has also turned to China for ballistic missiles after years of waiting for U.S. approvals that never came.

Even European allies are looking elsewhere. The [EU’s push for strategic autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union_defence_industrial_cooperation) isn’t just about breaking free from Russian dependence—it’s about avoiding U.S. export bottlenecks. Germany’s [IRIS-T air defense system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IRIS-T) and France’s [SCAF fighter program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Système_de_Combat_Aérien_Futur) are now top priorities for nations that once relied solely on American tech.

ITAR’s roots go back to the Cold War, when controlling tech leaks meant survival. But modern wars move at internet speed. The [F-35](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II) program alone has over 300,000 ITAR-controlled parts—each one a potential bottleneck. When a U.S. firm wants to ship a spare part to a partner, it often needs State Department sign-off. That can take weeks, even for non-sensitive gear. Meanwhile, adversaries like Russia and China streamline their sales with fewer restrictions.

The Pentagon knows the problem. In 2023, the [Defense Department’s ITAR review](https://www.defense.gov/) called for faster licensing and more delegation to trusted allies. But real change requires Congress to update the law—something that’s been stuck in partisan gridlock for years. Until then, America’s allies will keep shopping elsewhere, and U.S. influence will keep shrinking.
