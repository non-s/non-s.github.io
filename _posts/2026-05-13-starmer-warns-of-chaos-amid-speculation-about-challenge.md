UK Labour leader Keir Starmer [Keir Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer) has warned of party chaos amid growing speculation that Health Secretary Wes Streeting [Wes Streeting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wes_Streeting) is preparing to launch a leadership challenge as early as Thursday. Multiple Westminster sources told BBC News that Streeting is privately gathering support from Labour MPs, with at least 20 reportedly willing to back a challenge if he decides to proceed.

The reported move comes just days after Labour suffered heavy losses in local elections, raising questions about Starmer’s leadership. Streeting, a prominent centrist figure within the party, has privately expressed frustration over Starmer’s direction, particularly on economic policy and public service reforms. Insiders say he believes Labour risks losing the next general election unless significant changes are made to the party’s strategy.

## Labour Party divisions deepen over leadership speculation

Starmer responded to the growing unrest by convening an emergency meeting with senior Labour MPs on Tuesday night. During the meeting, he reportedly warned that a challenge would destabilise the party and damage its electoral prospects. "We cannot afford to repeat the mistakes of the past," Starmer told MPs, referencing Labour’s turbulent years under Jeremy Corbyn [Jeremy Corbyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn), when internal divisions contributed to heavy electoral defeats.

Streeting’s potential challenge is being closely watched by political analysts, who note that his rise has been meteoric. Elected to Parliament in 2015, he quickly became a vocal critic of Corbyn’s leadership and has since established himself as a key ally of Starmer. However, his recent public statements have become increasingly critical of the government’s handling of the NHS and economic inequality, fueling speculation about his ambitions.

## What happens next in Labour’s leadership crisis

If Streeting proceeds with a challenge, he would need to secure the support of at least 10% of Labour MPs—around 40 signatures—to trigger a leadership contest. While he is unlikely to secure enough backing to unseat Starmer outright, analysts suggest a challenge could weaken the leader’s authority and force significant policy concessions. Streeting has not publicly commented on the reports, but allies say he remains undecided about his next steps.

The situation reflects broader tensions within Labour over its path to power. Starmer has shifted the party to the centre ground since taking over in 2020, but some left-wing MPs and activists argue his policies are too cautious and fail to address growing inequality. Meanwhile, centrist MPs like Streeting worry that Labour’s current stance risks alienating key voter groups ahead of the next election, due by 2029.

Labour’s deputy leader Angela Rayner [Angela Rayner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angela_Rayner) has publicly backed Starmer, dismissing the leadership speculation as "Westminster bubble chatter." However, the growing unease among MPs suggests the party’s internal divisions run deeper than publicly acknowledged. Shadow Cabinet ministers are expected to hold private discussions over the coming days to assess the situation and determine whether Starmer’s leadership should be openly challenged.

For Streeting, the risks are high. A failed challenge could derail his leadership ambitions and marginalise him within the party. But analysts say inaction could also weaken his influence, particularly if Starmer’s leadership continues to face criticism over key policy failures. The coming days will determine whether Labour’s leadership crisis escalates into a full-blown internal battle.

The broader implications for UK politics are significant. Labour’s polling lead over the Conservatives has narrowed in recent months, and internal divisions could further erode voter confidence. If the party fails to present a united front, it risks handing the Conservatives another term in office. Meanwhile, Streeting’s potential challenge highlights the growing frustration among Labour MPs who believe the party must take bolder steps to win back working-class voters.
