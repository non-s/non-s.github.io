Tech giants have a space problem. Literally. The explosion of AI models like [Gemini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemini_(language_model)) and [Llama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Llama_(language_model)) needs more computing power than earth’s grids can reliably supply. Power-hungry data centres now account for about 1% of global electricity use—roughly as much as the whole country of Sweden. That’s why companies like [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google) and [SpaceX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX) are eyeing a radical fix: moving servers into orbit.

Inside Google’s [X lab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_X) in Mountain View, California, engineers have quietly tested concepts for satellite-cooled data centres. According to three people familiar with the project, the team is exploring how to use [SpaceX’s Starlink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlink) satellite network to beam data up and down. The goal? Cut the energy drain from cooling massive server farms, which can swallow up to 40% of a data centre’s power budget.

SpaceX isn’t just listening—it’s pitching in. [Elon Musk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Musk) confirmed last month that Starlink’s lasers are being tested for high-speed data links between satellites, a key step for orbiting data centres. These links could slash latency below 20 milliseconds, fast enough for real-time AI workloads like robotics or autonomous driving.

## How orbital data centres would work
The basic idea isn’t new. NASA and the military have used satellites for decades to process data in space. But today’s AI models need thousands of chips packed tightly together—impossible to launch in one rocket. So Google’s team is designing modular, stackable server racks that can be sent up in batches and assembled by robots in low Earth orbit.

Cooling is the trickiest part. On Earth, companies use giant water-cooled towers or immersion tanks filled with mineral oil. In space, there’s no air, so heat radiates away naturally—but slowly. Google’s solution? Heat pipes that transfer warmth to satellite radiators, then beam it down as infrared energy. Early tests show this could cut cooling costs by up to 90% compared to ground data centres.

## The catch: radiation and maintenance
Space isn’t kind to electronics. [Cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray) and solar flares can fry unshielded chips. Google’s engineers are testing radiation-hardened chips from companies like [NVIDIA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nvidia) and [AMD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices), but costs are sky-high. A single hardened AI accelerator can cost 10 times more than its Earth-bound cousin.

Then there’s maintenance. No astronauts will swap out fried hard drives in orbit. The plan hinges on robot swarms that dock with server satellites for repairs. [Amazon’s Project Kuiper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Kuiper) has already tested robotic servicing arms on its prototype satellites, giving Google a head start.

## Who else is looking up?
Google isn’t alone. [Microsoft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft) has partnered with [Loon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loon_LLC) (yes, the defunct balloon internet company) to study high-altitude data centres. Meanwhile, [IBM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM) is testing liquid-metal cooling for satellites that could handle AI workloads. Even [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA) is rumored to be exploring orbital supercomputing for Mars missions.

The race isn’t just about power. Orbital data centres could slash latency for global AI services. Today, a query sent to a data centre in Iowa takes 50 milliseconds to reach a user in Tokyo. A satellite link could cut that to 20 milliseconds—fast enough for real-time robotics or augmented reality.

## What happens next
Don’t expect a SpaceX rocket carrying Google servers next week. The project is still in the whiteboard phase, with no firm timeline. But the energy crisis is accelerating the timeline. Last year, data centres in Virginia’s [Loudoun County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudoun_County,_Virginia), the so-called “Data Centre Alley,” drew more power than the entire country of Kenya.

Regulators are starting to notice. The [European Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Commission) just proposed rules forcing tech giants to disclose energy use from AI workloads. Meanwhile, [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California) is considering a moratorium on new data centres until they prove they can run on 100% renewable energy.

If orbital data centres work, they could rewrite the rules of the internet. But first, Google and SpaceX have to solve radiation, maintenance, and the little problem of launching thousands of tons of hardware into space.

For now, it’s all still a thought experiment—one that might just save the planet from melting down under the weight of its own AI dreams.
