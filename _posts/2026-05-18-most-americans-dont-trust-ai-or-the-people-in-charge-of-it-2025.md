Americans have turned skeptical about the tech that promised to change everything. In the first major public surveys of 2025, Pew Research and Gallup both found that distrust in AI isn’t just common—it’s the default position for most adults. Pew’s nationally representative poll of 5,230 U.S. adults, conducted January 13-19, shows 61% say they don’t trust AI systems, while Gallup’s 1,016-person survey from February 3-10 puts the figure at 59%. The numbers sit near historic lows for emerging technology, and they’re flat across most demographics. No age, income, or education group crosses the 40% mark for trust in AI itself. Even tech workers—long assumed to be early adopters—split 48% distrust to 39% trust, a gap that surprised pollsters. 'People aren’t just worried; they’re resigned,' said [Lee Rainie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Rainie), who directs Pew’s internet research team. 'They see AI everywhere now—in hiring tools, loan approvals, even school assignments—and they don’t think anyone’s watching the store.' Rainie points to one stat that sticks out: only 12% of Americans say regulators are doing a good job overseeing AI. That distrust in oversight spills straight into the tech itself. When Pew asked whether AI will make life better or worse in a decade, 57% said worse, 22% said better, and the rest were unsure. The gap between pessimists and optimists is widest among Republicans, where 68% expect AI to make things worse, compared with 51% of Democrats. The partisan split mirrors broader skepticism toward tech elites in conservative media circles, where figures like [Elon Musk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Musk) and [Marc Andreessen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Andreessen) have been criticized for hyping AI while downplaying risks. The polling comes at a moment when AI systems are already woven into daily life. Hiring platforms like [HireVue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HireVue) use AI to screen job applicants, and [FICO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FICO) credit scores now factor AI risk models into lending decisions. Schools rely on AI tutors to grade essays and personalize lessons, and hospitals deploy AI to triage patients. Yet the public feels these systems are being rolled out too fast, with too little accountability. 'You don’t need to be a Luddite to ask who’s responsible when an AI makes a mistake,' said [Safiya Noble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safiya_Umoja_Noble), a UCLA information studies professor who studies algorithmic bias. She notes that even when AI performs well on average, it can fail disastrously for small groups—like Black patients in some medical AI models or women in hiring tools. 'Trust isn’t just about accuracy. It’s about fairness, transparency, and the sense that someone will help when things go wrong.' That’s the part that’s missing, according to the polls. When Gallup asked whether AI companies have the public’s best interests at heart, 73% said no. Even among tech workers, only 28% believe their industry is acting responsibly. The skepticism is strongest among older Americans. Only 22% of adults over 65 trust AI, compared with 44% of 18-29-year-olds. But even the youngest cohort isn’t sold: just 36% of Gen Z say they trust AI, a number that’s barely budged since 2023. The polls also show a growing belief that AI will deepen inequality. Nearly half of all respondents—47%—say AI will mostly help wealthy people and companies, while 19% think it will help regular folks. That gap is largest in lower-income households, where 56% worry AI will make life harder. The data suggest the tech industry’s long-running bet on 'move fast and break things' is backfiring. Companies like [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google) and [Microsoft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft) have spent billions on AI, but public goodwill hasn’t kept pace. Even [Sam Altman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Altman), CEO of [OpenAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenAI), admitted in a January earnings call that 'trust is the biggest bottleneck to scaling AI responsibly.' The question now is whether that bottleneck bursts before the next wave of AI products hits the market. Next month, the [Federal Trade Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission) plans to hold its first public forum on AI accountability, where lawmakers will hear from whistleblowers, ethicists, and tech executives. The FTC’s chair, [Lina Khan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lina_Khan), has already signaled she’ll push for stricter rules on transparency and bias audits. But even Khan’s supporters doubt the agency can move fast enough. 'Regulators are playing catch-up in a race where the runners keep changing shoes,' said [Matt Stoller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_Stoller), a policy director at the American Economic Liberties Project. Meanwhile, the tech industry is pushing ahead. [Nvidia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nvidia) just unveiled its next-generation AI chips, and [Meta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta_Platforms) announced a new 'AI Studio' for small businesses. The message is clear: the genie’s out of the bottle, and the public is supposed to trust the bottle-makers. But with two out of three Americans telling pollsters they don’t, the genie might not last long.
