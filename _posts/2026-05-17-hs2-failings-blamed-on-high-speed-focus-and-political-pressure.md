A damning new review from the Infrastructure and Projects Authority has laid bare why [HS2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Speed_2), Britain’s once-ambitious high-speed rail project, became a financial and engineering mess. The report, shared with ministers and MPs last month, calls out three core problems that plagued the scheme from day one: shoddy early engineering, politicians flip-flopping on what the line should actually do, and costs that spiraled out of control. It doesn’t pull punches. ‘The project’s original sins were baked in before digging even started,’ one reviewer told the BBC. ‘By the time anyone noticed, it was too late to fix without wrecking the whole thing.’

The review drops the most explosive detail first: the initial technical design was so flawed that it made later delays inevitable. Engineers working on the early phases say they were handed specs that assumed perfect conditions—no unexpected ground, no legal fights over land, no budget overruns. ‘They planned it like a toy model,’ said a senior civil engineer who worked on the first phase. ‘Real ground isn’t like that. Neither’s politics.’ The report confirms that by 2015, even before major construction began, insiders were privately warning that the design couldn’t handle the reality of building a 350mph railway through England’s crowded countryside.

Politics didn’t help. The review shows how ministers kept changing their minds about what HS2 was supposed to achieve. First it was a straight Manchester-London link. Then it had to serve Birmingham, Leeds, and the East Midlands too. Then the government demanded extra stations in towns that wouldn’t actually benefit from faster trains. Each tweak added months of redesigns, legal fights, and cost. ‘Every time a new minister got the job, they’d add another ‘must-have’ stop,’ said a former HS2 Ltd board member. ‘None of them paid for it.’ The review blames this ‘design by committee’ approach for turning a simple rail line into a Rube Goldberg machine of conflicting requirements.

Costs became the runaway train. The project was sold in 2009 with a £32bn price tag. By 2020, that had nearly tripled to £98bn. The review says the real number is likely even higher once you factor in inflation and the extra work now needed just to stop the whole thing collapsing. ‘They underestimated everything—land, labor, materials—like a first-time homebuyer who thinks a £200k house will cost £200k,’ said a Treasury source familiar with the numbers. Even the review’s authors admit they’re struggling to keep up with the latest estimates. In February, [Prime Minister Rishi Sunak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rishi_Sunak) announced HS2 would be scrapped north of Birmingham, cutting £36bn from the budget but also abandoning towns that were promised faster links.

The review’s timing isn’t accidental. With Parliament about to debate HS2’s future again, the government needed hard evidence to justify its cuts. Transport Secretary [Mark Harper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Harper) has already said he’ll use the report to argue that ‘radical action’ is needed to stop the project bleeding billions more. But critics say the review is too late—like closing the gate after the horse has bolted. ‘We knew HS2 was in trouble years ago,’ said [Darren Grimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darren_Grimes), a transport policy analyst. ‘The question now is whether anyone’s left who can actually fix what’s left of it.’

What happens now? The review recommends a ‘reset’—but doesn’t say how much that will cost or how long it’ll take. HS2 Ltd, the company running the project, is already shedding staff and contractors. Even the bits that are built, like the London-Birmingham line, face fresh scrutiny over whether they’re worth the money. Local councils in the North of England are furious, arguing that scrapping the rest of the line will strand their towns for decades. Meanwhile, campaigners say the whole saga proves Britain can’t build big infrastructure projects anymore. ‘We used to do this properly,’ said [Jenny Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenny_Jones,_Baroness_Jones_of_Moulsecoomb), a Green Party peer. ‘Now we just cancel things and call it ‘saving money.’’ The review leaves one question hanging: if HS2 was doomed from the start, what other major projects are next in line for the same fate?
