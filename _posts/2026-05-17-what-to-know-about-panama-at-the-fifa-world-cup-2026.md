Panama punched their ticket to the 2026 FIFA World Cup the same way they did in 2018—by grinding out results in CONCACAF qualifying. Their run started in the second round, where they faced Nicaragua, Guyana, Montserrat and Belize. They won all four games, scoring 10 goals and conceding just one. That perfect record set them up for the tougher third round, where they faced Suriname, Guatemala and El Salvador in a single table. After six matches, Panama had 11 points—good for fourth place—and secured their spot in the tournament. [Thomas Christiansen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Christiansen_(footballer)), the Spanish-born coach who took over in 2020, made it clear Panama aren’t there just to make up the numbers. “We want to show the world what we can do,” he said after the final qualifying match. “We’re not here to lose. We’re here to compete.”

Panama’s qualifying campaign wasn’t just about results—it was about proving they belong. In the second round, striker [Ismael Díaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ismael_D%C3%ADaz) led the charge with four goals, while midfielder [Adalberto Carrasquilla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adalberto_Carrasquilla) controlled the tempo. The team’s defense, anchored by [Michael Murillo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Murillo) at right-back, only let in one goal in four games. That defensive solidity carried over into the third round, where Panama’s backline, including center-back [César Blackman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Blackman), held firm against tougher opponents. “We knew the third round would be harder,” Christiansen said. “But we kept our focus. We didn’t panic when we dropped points. We just kept winning.”

## A team built on unity and grit

Panama’s squad isn’t packed with global superstars, but that’s part of their strength. Most players come from clubs in Mexico, Costa Rica or the U.S., where they’ve learned to fight for every inch. Goalkeeper [Jaime Penedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaime_Penedo), now 38, is the only player from the 2018 squad still in the team, but his experience is invaluable. “This team plays with heart,” Penedo said before the final qualifier. “We don’t have the biggest names, but we have the biggest desire.”

The squad’s backbone includes players like [José Fajardo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Fajardo_(footballer)), a defensive midfielder who broke into the national team in 2023 and has since become a leader. Fajardo’s work rate and tactical discipline have helped Panama stay compact in midfield, frustrating opponents who expected an easy ride. Up front, Díaz and [Eric Davis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Davis_(footballer)) provide creativity and pace, while [Jesús Medina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jes%C3%BAs_Medina_(footballer)) adds flair from the wings. “We’re not the prettiest team to watch,” Christiansen admitted. “But we’re effective. We make life hard for the other side.”

## What to expect in 2026

Panama will be in Pot 4 when the 2026 World Cup draw happens in December, meaning they won’t face the likes of Brazil, France or Spain in the group stage. Their first match is June 11 against either [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team) or the playoff winner between [Czech Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_Republic_national_football_team) and [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland_national_football_team). “We’ll know our group soon,” Christiansen said. “But no matter who we get, we’ll respect them. We’ll play our game.”

The tournament format has expanded to 48 teams, giving Panama a real chance to advance past the group stage. In 2018, they lost all three games, but they’ll be hungrier this time. “We’re not here to make up the numbers,” Fajardo said. “We’re here to show Central America what we’re made of.” Their path won’t be easy—Senegal, Iran or even a strong CONCACAF side like [Costa Rica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costa_Rica_national_football_team) could stand in their way. But after eight years of work, Panama’s players aren’t intimidated.

## The bigger picture

Panama’s qualification is more than just a sports story. It’s a reminder that football isn’t just about Europe and South America. Central America has produced talent like [Romell Quioto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romell_Quioto) and [Aníbal Godoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An%C3%ADbal_Godoy), who’ve played in Major League Soccer and Europe. For a country of just 4.5 million people, Panama’s consistency at the World Cup level is impressive.

The 2026 tournament will be played across three countries—the U.S., Canada and Mexico—giving Panama a home-field advantage in some ways. Their fans, known for their passion, will travel in big numbers to support the team. “This isn’t just about football,” Christiansen said. “It’s about putting Panama on the map.” With a squad that plays with pride and a coach who demands intensity, they might just do that.
