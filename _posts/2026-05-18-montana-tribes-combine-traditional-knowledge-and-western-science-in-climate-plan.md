The Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes in western Montana just moved ahead with their own climate plan even though both the state and federal governments backed off. The tribes wrote the plan after Montana pulled its funding last year and the federal government canceled grants tied to climate work. Tribal leaders say they had no choice—their land and water are changing fast, and they need a plan that works for their people and place.

## Two ways of knowing, one climate plan
The tribal climate plan isn’t just numbers and targets. It weaves together knowledge passed down for generations—like how fire shaped the land and when fish run upstream—with data from weather stations, satellite maps and soil tests. Tribal elders, hydrologists and foresters spent two years mapping risks: shrinking glaciers on [Mount Sentinel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Sentinel_(Montana)), earlier runoff that leaves rivers low by summer, and hotter fires that burn hotter and longer.

They’re not waiting for outsiders to fix it. The plan calls for restoring wetlands near [Flathead Lake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flathead_Lake) to hold water longer, thinning forests to slow wildfires, and reviving native plants that need less water. It also sets up a tribal climate office to keep tracking changes and adjusting tactics.

## Money and politics got in the way
The state used to chip in for tribal climate work, but Montana’s governor cut those funds in 2023 as part of broader budget trims. At the same time, the federal government paused several climate grants while it reviewed rules. That left the tribes with a gap of more than $1.2 million in planned projects. Instead of shelving the plan, they rewrote it to use tribal staff time, existing tribal programs and small grants from foundations like the [Kendeda Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kendeda_Fund).

Tribal council member Shelly Fyant said it wasn’t easy. “We had to get creative,” Fyant said. “But our people drink this water, fish these rivers, and hunt these hills. We can’t afford to wait.” The plan passed unanimously in March.

## What happens next
The tribes will start with $250,000 from the [Bureau of Indian Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Indian_Affairs) tribal climate resilience fund—a smaller slice of what they originally sought, but enough to hire two new staffers and buy equipment. They’ll also lean on partnerships with nearby universities and conservation groups to share data and tools.

Longer term, the plan aims to protect cultural sites like [St. Mary’s Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Mission) near Stevensville, where elders say the land has already shifted. They point to cottonwood trees dying along the [Flathead River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flathead_River) and bull trout struggling in warming streams. The tribes want to make sure their children see the same landscapes they grew up with.

For now, the tribes are treating this as a living plan. They’ll review it every year and adjust as fires, floods and droughts reshape the land. It’s a model other tribes watch closely, especially in places where state or federal climate work has stalled.
