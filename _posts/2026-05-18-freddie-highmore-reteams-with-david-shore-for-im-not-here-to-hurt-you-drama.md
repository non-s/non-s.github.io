Freddie Highmore is stepping back into the spotlight with a role that flips his usual on-screen persona. In 'I’m Not Here to Hurt You,' the actor plays a mild-mannered family man who moonlights as a criminal—but with a twist. Unlike most crime dramas, his character doesn’t rely on intimidation or violence. Instead, he operates with an almost unnervingly polite demeanor, even as he builds a lucrative criminal empire. The series is set to air on Bell Media in Canada and will likely be distributed internationally by Sony Pictures Television's global arm.


The project reunites Highmore with David Shore, the creator behind the iconic medical drama [House M.D.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_(TV_series)). Shore is writing the series and serving as an executive producer alongside Highmore, who is credited as an executive producer through his production company, Alfresco Pictures. The collaboration marks a shift for Highmore, who’s best known for his roles in 'Bates Motel' and 'The Good Doctor,' but hasn’t headlined a crime drama before.


## Sony Pictures Television and Blink49 Studios team up for the new drama

'I’m Not Here to Hurt You' is a co-production between Sony Pictures Television and Blink49 Studios, the latter being the production arm of Bell Media. The series was unveiled at the Los Angeles Screenings, an annual event where international buyers preview upcoming TV shows. Bell Media, which owns Canadian networks like CTV, will broadcast the series in Canada, while Sony handles global distribution. The project’s international appeal is clear—crime dramas with unconventional protagonists often attract audiences beyond their home markets.


Highmore’s character is a former accountant who stumbles into crime after discovering a loophole in a client’s financial records. Instead of reporting it, he starts using the same methods to build his own illegal business, all while maintaining his everyday family life. The show’s tone blends dark humor with suspense, a style that could resonate with fans of shows like 'Barry' or 'Better Call Saul,' where criminals operate in morally gray areas.


## Why this role stands out for Highmore

Highmore, who’s 32, has spent years playing characters with troubled pasts, like Norman Bates in 'Bates Motel' and Dr. Shaun Murphy in 'The Good Doctor.' But this role is different—it’s his first time playing a criminal who’s not a villain in the traditional sense. The show’s premise plays on the idea of an ordinary person slowly becoming something else entirely, a theme that’s been popular in recent TV. Think of it as 'Mr. Robot' meets 'Breaking Bad,' but with a much lighter touch in execution.


The reveal at the Los Angeles Screenings suggests Sony is betting on the series as a potential global hit. Crime dramas with high-concept premises and charismatic leads tend to travel well, especially when they subvert expectations. Highmore’s performance will be key—will he make the criminal likable enough to root for, or will the audience always see him as the fraud he is?


## What’s next for the project

With the series now unveiled to buyers, Sony and Bell Media will likely finalize distribution deals in the coming months. Highmore and Shore have a track record of success—Shore’s 'House' ran for eight seasons, and Highmore’s 'Bates Motel' was a critical darling. If 'I’m Not Here to Hurt You' lands on a major network or streaming service, it could quickly become a talking point in TV circles. For now, fans will have to wait for more details on the cast and release date, but the premise alone makes it one to watch.
