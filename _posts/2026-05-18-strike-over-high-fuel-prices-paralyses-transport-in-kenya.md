Kenya’s biggest transport strike in years paralyzed the country Friday as drivers walked out over fuel prices that have jumped by more than 50% in the past year. Trucks, buses, and matatus—shared minibuses—vanished from roads across Nairobi, Mombasa, and Kisumu, leaving the usually clogged highways eerily quiet. The strike hit at the start of the school term, and several private schools in Nairobi and Mombasa sent students home early or canceled classes outright. Public schools remained open but many parents kept children at home out of safety concerns. Schools in Eldoret and Nakuru followed suit, with some closing entirely for the day. The government called the strikes illegal and deployed police to keep key routes open, but protests turned violent in Kisumu and Eldoret, where officers fired tear gas to disperse crowds blocking roads. By midday, the interior ministry reported at least 12 injured protesters and two police officers, though the real toll may be higher as local media captured videos of chaotic confrontations. The strike organizers, led by the Matatu Owners Association, say fuel prices have made it impossible to run routes without massive losses. Diesel, which powers most trucks and buses, now costs about $1.30 per liter—up from $0.85 a year ago. Petrol prices have climbed even faster, jumping from $1.10 to $1.65 in the same period. ## Roads and businesses feel the pinch
The shutdown hit Kenya’s economy where it hurts. The Nairobi-Mombasa highway, the backbone of East Africa’s trade, was gridlocked in spots as drivers rerouted or abandoned their trucks. Mombasa port, the region’s largest, saw delays as trucks idled outside gates, waiting for strike-breakers or official convoys. Supermarkets in Nairobi reported empty shelves on staple goods like milk and bread as deliveries stalled. Small traders in Kibera, Nairobi’s largest informal settlement, said customers stayed away, fearing clashes with police. Even boda boda (motorcycle taxi) drivers, who usually ignore strikes, joined the protest, leaving Nairobi’s streets noticeably quieter than usual. The Kenya Association of Manufacturers warned the strike could cost businesses $50 million a day if it drags on. ## Government and unions dig in
President [William Ruto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Ruto) has refused to budge on fuel subsidies, arguing that cutting taxes on fuel would hurt Kenya’s credit rating and scare off investors. His government blames global oil prices and a weak Kenyan shilling for the high costs, but the opposition and protesters say the administration has mismanaged the economy. Ruto, who took office last year, faces growing anger over the cost of living, with inflation hitting 8.5% in July—the highest in 5 years. The fuel strike is the latest in a wave of protests over economic hardship, including demonstrations last month over new taxes on bread and cooking oil. Union leaders say they’re ready for a prolonged fight. The Kenya Transport and Allied Workers Union called the strike “total” and warned that more sectors could join if the government doesn’t intervene. ## What happens next
The strike was supposed to last 48 hours, but organizers say they’ll extend it if the government doesn’t negotiate. Ruto’s cabinet held an emergency meeting Friday night but gave no indication of concessions. Meanwhile, police arrested at least 40 protesters in Nairobi, Kisumu, and Eldoret, charging them with illegal assembly and disturbing peace. The opposition [Raila Odinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raila_Odinga) has called for dialogue, but his party is divided over whether to support the strike. Truck drivers say they won’t back down until fuel prices drop by at least 30%. For now, Kenyans are bracing for more disruptions—whether it’s empty roads, closed schools, or higher prices at the pump.
