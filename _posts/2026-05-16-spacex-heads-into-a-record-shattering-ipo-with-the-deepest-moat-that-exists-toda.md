SpaceX is barreling toward the largest initial public offering in history, accelerating its timeline to as early as June 11 for pricing shares. Reports indicate the company, led by [Elon Musk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Musk), could file publicly as soon as Wednesday with a roadshow launching June 4. The public debut on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX is scheduled for June 12, according to sources cited by Reuters.

The company, which recently merged with Musk’s AI startup, is seeking to raise up to $75 billion at a valuation of $1.75 trillion. This figure would shatter the current record held by [Saudi Aramco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Aramco), which raised $29.4 billion in 2019 at a $1.7 trillion valuation. SpaceX’s confidential filing had already set the stage for this move, but the accelerated schedule reflects surging investor confidence in the company’s commercial spaceflight and satellite businesses.

## Musk’s empire expands with AI and space merger

The IPO comes just months after SpaceX merged with xAI, Musk’s artificial intelligence venture, signaling a broader push into high-tech industries beyond aerospace. The combined entity now spans rocket launches, satellite internet via [Starlink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlink), and advanced AI research. Analysts say the merger has bolstered SpaceX’s long-term growth prospects, attracting institutional investors eager to capitalize on the convergence of space and AI technologies.

SpaceX’s commercial spaceflight division, which includes the [Starship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Starship) rocket, has seen rapid progress despite setbacks. The company completed a successful test flight in June 2024, demonstrating progress toward reusable heavy-lift rockets. Meanwhile, Starlink continues to expand its global broadband service, now serving over 2 million subscribers worldwide, according to company data.

## Investors bet big on SpaceX’s ‘deep moat’

Institutional investors are signaling unparalleled confidence in SpaceX’s dominance, with some publicly vowing never to bet against Musk’s company. The term “deep moat” has been used to describe SpaceX’s competitive advantages, including its vertical integration, proprietary technology, and first-mover status in commercial spaceflight. The $1.75 trillion valuation, if achieved, would place SpaceX among the most valuable publicly traded companies globally, trailing only tech giants and energy titans.

The IPO’s success hinges on investor appetite for Musk’s vision, which includes Mars colonization and global satellite internet dominance. However, skeptics point to the company’s history of delays and technical challenges as potential risks. Regulatory hurdles, particularly for Starlink’s broadband service, also pose challenges as SpaceX navigates international markets.

What happens next depends on market conditions and investor sentiment during the roadshow. If the June 11 pricing and June 12 debut proceed as scheduled, SpaceX will join an elite group of companies that have redefined industries through innovation and scale. The IPO could also set a new benchmark for future space industry valuations, influencing how investors perceive high-risk, high-reward ventures.
