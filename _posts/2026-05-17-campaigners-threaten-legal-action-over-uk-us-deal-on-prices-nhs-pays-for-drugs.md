Two campaign groups have fired a warning shot at the UK government, threatening legal action unless ministers scrap a key part of a proposed UK-US trade deal that could raise the prices the NHS pays for medicines. The groups, [Health Action International Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_Action_International) and [Global Justice Now](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Justice_Now), argue that a change to how drug treatments get approved for NHS use hands ministers too much power and could breach legal limits on public spending. They say the move risks pushing NHS drug costs even higher at a time when budgets are already stretched thin.

The dispute centers on a clause in the draft trade agreement that would let the government fast-track drug approvals by skipping some of the usual independent checks. Campaigners claim this bypasses Parliament and the NHS’s own expert bodies, effectively handing ministers the final say on which drugs get used—and at what price. The groups have written to the Department of Health and Social Care demanding the clause be removed, warning legal action will follow if they don’t get a clear response within weeks.

## How the UK-US drug price deal works
The proposed changes would let ministers set drug prices based on terms agreed with US negotiators, rather than relying on the standard process that involves independent health experts and public consultation. Critics say this weakens safeguards designed to keep costs down. For example, the current system uses a panel of clinicians and economists to assess whether a new drug offers value for money before it’s added to the NHS formulary. Under the new plan, that panel’s role could be reduced or even sidelined entirely.

The government insists the changes are necessary to speed up access to cutting-edge treatments, especially for rare diseases where patients often wait years for approval. A spokesperson for the Department of Health said the proposals would maintain high safety standards while making it easier for patients to get the drugs they need. But campaigners argue that faster approvals shouldn’t come at the cost of weaker scrutiny—or higher bills for taxpayers. They point to past US trade deals, like the one with [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia), where similar clauses led to disputes over drug pricing.

## What happens next and why it matters
The campaign groups have given the government until late October to respond formally to their demands. If they don’t get a satisfactory answer, they’ll take the case to court, arguing the changes are unlawful under UK public spending rules and international trade law. A win for the campaigners could force ministers to redraft the clause, potentially delaying the trade deal or forcing a renegotiation with the US.

For patients, the stakes are high. The NHS already spends over £20 billion a year on medicines, and price hikes could force cuts to other services. For the government, the deal is a chance to show post-Brexit Britain can strike ambitious trade agreements—but at what cost? The outcome of this legal battle could set a precedent for how future trade deals handle everything from drug prices to healthcare standards.
