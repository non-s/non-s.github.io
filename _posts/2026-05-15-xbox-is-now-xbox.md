Microsoft just flipped the switch on a decade-old naming convention. Xbox is now XBOX, all caps, all the time. The move came after [Asha Sharma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asha_Sharma_(executive)), Xbox’s CEO, asked fans on X whether Microsoft should keep the lowercase “Xbox” or switch to the uppercase “XBOX.” The poll closed with a clear preference for the all-caps version, and Microsoft acted fast: the company’s official X account now reads “XBOX” instead of “Xbox.”

The rebrand isn’t universal yet. Threads and Bluesky accounts for Xbox still show the old “Xbox” branding, but that’s likely temporary. Microsoft rarely makes half-measures when rolling out rebrands, so expect those accounts to update soon. When reached for comment, Microsoft simply pointed to Sharma’s post on X, offering no further explanation about the timing or scope of the change.

## A blast from the past

This isn’t the first time Microsoft’s gaming division has flirted with all-caps branding. The original Xbox console, released in 2001, used “XBOX” on its hardware and marketing. The lowercase “Xbox” came later, as Microsoft softened the brand to feel more approachable and less aggressive. The shift back to all caps suggests a deliberate attempt to reclaim some of that early identity, perhaps to signal a new era under Sharma’s leadership.

The rebrand arrives as Xbox faces pressure to stand out in a crowded gaming market. Sony’s PlayStation dominates console sales, Nintendo’s Switch remains a juggernaut, and cloud gaming is reshaping how people play. A bold visual change like this can help cut through the noise, even if it’s mostly symbolic. Microsoft’s gaming division has been aggressive lately, pushing Game Pass subscriptions, acquiring Activision Blizzard, and investing heavily in AI-powered gaming features. This rebrand might just be the next step in that strategy.

## What’s next for the brand

For now, the rebrand is limited to X. But Sharma’s poll and the rapid execution hint that Microsoft could expand the change across all platforms. The company hasn’t confirmed whether this is a full rebrand or just a social media tweak, but the consistency of the rollout so far suggests it’s more than a temporary stunt. Gamers on other platforms will likely see “XBOX” soon, especially if Microsoft wants to reinforce the shift in identity.

The move also raises questions about Xbox’s broader strategy. Is this a signal that Microsoft is doubling down on its gaming ambitions? Or is it simply a way to refresh the brand without making deeper changes? Sharma’s leadership has already overseen major acquisitions and service expansions, so a brand refresh could be part of a larger plan to make Xbox feel more dominant, more unified, and more distinct from competitors like PlayStation and Nintendo.

## Fan reaction is mixed

Early responses on X show a split reaction. Some fans love the nostalgia of the all-caps name, calling it a “return to form.” Others argue that the lowercase “Xbox” has defined the brand for years and that the change feels unnecessary or even confusing. The poll itself was a smart move—letting fans feel involved in the decision—but it also sets a precedent. If Microsoft continues to use public feedback for major branding choices, other companies might follow suit, turning social media polls into de facto market research tools.

Regardless of the fanfare, the rebrand is here to stay—at least on X. Whether it sticks across all platforms will depend on how Microsoft rolls it out next. For now, gamers can update their bookmarks and adjust to the new look. One thing’s clear: Xbox is making a statement, and it’s not subverting expectations by playing it safe.

The next few weeks will show whether this change resonates beyond social media. If Microsoft sticks with “XBOX,” it’ll be a rare case of a company reviving an old brand identity for a modern audience.
