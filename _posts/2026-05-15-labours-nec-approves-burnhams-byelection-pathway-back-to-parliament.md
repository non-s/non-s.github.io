Labour’s national executive committee (NEC) has granted Andy Burnham [Greater Manchester mayor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Burnham) permission to seek selection as the party’s candidate for the Makerfield byelection. The decision clears the first major hurdle for Burnham, who served as an MP from 2001 to 2017 before becoming mayor in 2017. The byelection is triggered by the resignation of Labour MP [Yvonne Fovargue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvonne_Fovargue), who stepped down last month.

Under Labour’s rules, sitting mayors must obtain NEC approval before standing for Westminster seats. Burnham still needs to be formally selected by local party members to secure the nomination. If he wins the Makerfield seat, his mayoral term would end early, triggering a separate mayoral byelection in Greater Manchester. Burnham has two years remaining in his current mayoral term, which ends in 2028.

The NEC’s decision reflects Burnham’s influence within the Labour Party and his high-profile role as mayor. His potential return to Parliament would add another former MP to Labour’s ranks amid ongoing party strategy discussions. Burnham has not publicly confirmed his candidacy but has indicated his interest in standing for the seat, which Labour is expected to target in the next general election.

Makerfield, a traditionally safe Labour seat in Wigan, has been represented by the party since 1997. The byelection date is yet to be announced, though it is expected to take place in the coming months. Local Labour branches will now begin the selection process, with Burnham among the candidates expected to apply.

Labour’s NEC approval of Burnham’s bid comes as the party navigates internal debates over candidate selections and future strategy. The decision also highlights the party’s approach to balancing local and national political roles. If Burnham succeeds, he would join Labour’s parliamentary group while maintaining his mayoral responsibilities until a potential resignation.

The approval sets the stage for a competitive selection process in Makerfield. Burnham’s candidacy could draw significant attention, given his profile and the seat’s political importance. Labour activists and local members will now assess his suitability for the role, with the byelection serving as a key test for the party’s direction ahead of the next general election.
