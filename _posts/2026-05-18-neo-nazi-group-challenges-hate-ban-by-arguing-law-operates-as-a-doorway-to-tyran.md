A neo-Nazi group that was banned as a hate organisation last Friday is taking the federal government to court, arguing that the legislation used to ban it is unconstitutional. The National Socialist Network (NSN)—also known as White Australia—filed documents with the High Court this week, claiming the law that led to their ban ‘operates as a doorway to tyranny.’ Their legal challenge will test whether Australia’s hate speech laws, introduced after the deadly Bondi Beach terror attack in December, can withstand constitutional scrutiny. The group’s ban came after years of mounting evidence linking them to violent extremism and racial hatred, including propaganda campaigns and clashes with police at rallies in Melbourne and Sydney. Authorities say the NSN’s ideology promotes white supremacy and incites racial violence, justifying its classification as a prohibited hate group under the [Anti-Discrimination Act 1975](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Discrimination_Act_1975_(New_South_Wales)).

## What the group is arguing
The NSN’s legal challenge hinges on a constitutional argument. Their lawyers claim the hate speech ban burdens their right to ‘freedom of governmental and political communication,’ a right implied by the Australian Constitution. This isn’t the first time extremist groups have used free speech arguments to challenge bans—the far-right [LNP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_National_Party_of_Queensland) made similar claims in 2019 when some of its factions were investigated for racist remarks. But this case is different. The NSN isn’t just a loose network of online trolls; it’s actively trying to form a political party, which makes their legal fight more urgent. Court documents show they’re pushing to overturn the ban before they can register as a party, arguing that banning them now would prevent their members from participating in democratic processes.

## How the ban works—and why it matters
Australia’s hate speech laws, tightened in March after the Bondi Beach attack that killed six people, give the federal government the power to ban groups deemed to incite violence or hatred based on race, religion, or ethnicity. The NSN was added to the list of prohibited organisations last Friday, joining groups like the [Australian National Socialist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_National_Socialist_Party), which was banned in 2016. Since the new laws passed, human rights groups have warned that far-right extremism is rising, with the NSN being one of the most visible groups recruiting young men through online forums and public stunts. The ban means NSN members can no longer meet in public, raise funds, or use social media platforms to spread their messages. Supporters of the law say it’s a necessary tool to prevent violence, while critics argue it could be used against peaceful political groups.

## The political stakes of this case
This isn’t just a legal battle—it’s a political one. The NSN’s attempt to register as a party before their ban was finalised shows how extremist groups are trying to exploit democratic systems to gain legitimacy. Their lawyers are arguing that the ban violates the implied freedom of political communication, a right the High Court has recognised in past cases, like the [Lange v Australian Broadcasting Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lange_v_Australian_Broadcasting_Corporation) ruling in 1997. If the court sides with the NSN, it could set a precedent that weakens Australia’s ability to ban hate groups. On the other hand, if the ban is upheld, it could embolden the government to take stronger action against other extremist organisations.

The federal government has defended the ban, with Attorney-General [Mark Dreyfus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Dreyfus) saying the NSN’s ideology and actions pose a ‘serious threat to social cohesion.’ Human rights advocates agree, pointing to the NSN’s history of violent rhetoric and their documented ties to international neo-Nazi networks. The case will be heard in the coming months, and the outcome could reshape how Australia deals with far-right extremism for years to come.

## What happens next
The High Court will now decide whether to fast-track the case or schedule it for a full hearing later this year. If the NSN wins, they could rebrand and try to register as a political party, potentially using the courts as a platform to spread their ideas. If they lose, the ban will stand, but the group may appeal or find other ways to operate underground. Either way, this case is a test of how far Australia is willing to go to balance free speech with the need to protect vulnerable communities from hate. For now, the federal government is standing by its decision, arguing that the NSN’s ideology and actions leave no room for doubt about their threat to public safety.
