The U.S. Air Force’s MQ-9 Reaper fleet has fallen to roughly 135 aircraft after combat attrition from Operation Epic Fury, the deputy chief of staff for plans and programs told senators Tuesday. Lt. Gen. David Tabor [U.S. Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Air_Force) confirmed the reduction during testimony before the Senate Armed Services Subcommittee on Airland, but stressed the service’s ability to maintain critical operations worldwide.

Tabor told lawmakers the fleet remains capable of fulfilling its contract of 56 combat lines—continuous intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance orbits maintained for combatant commanders globally. The Air Force is now pursuing options to backfill lost aircraft and field a cheaper, more expendable replacement for the MQ-9 Reaper, which has borne the brunt of recent drone operations.

## Senate questions focus on drone fleet resilience
Sen. Kevin Cramer, R-N.D., opened questioning by citing long-standing concerns over the MQ-9 fleet’s sustainability. Cramer pressed Tabor on whether the attrition rate posed risks to the service’s global ISR posture, particularly in high-threat regions. Tabor responded that the Air Force is adjusting operations to mitigate gaps while accelerating acquisition of next-generation systems.

The MQ-9 Reaper, a medium-altitude long-endurance drone developed by General Atomics [Aeronautical Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Atomics_Aeronautical_Systems), has been the backbone of U.S. unmanned aerial operations for over two decades. Its primary roles include strike missions, surveillance and reconnaissance, often operating in contested environments where manned aircraft face higher risks.

## Attrition and replacement strategies emerge
The reduction in MQ-9 numbers follows recent combat losses attributed to adversarial actions, including engagements in the Middle East. The Air Force has not disclosed the exact number of drones lost, but the fleet’s decline reflects the growing strain on unmanned systems amid sustained operations.

Tabor did not specify a timeline for fielding a replacement but indicated the service is evaluating options to improve fleet resilience. These may include modifying existing MQ-9s, accelerating development of the MQ-25 Stingray [unmanned aerial refueling system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MQ-25_Stingray), or investing in lower-cost, attritable systems designed for high-risk missions.

## Global ISR posture remains a priority
Despite the fleet reduction, the Air Force insists the 56 combat lines remain fully operational. These orbits provide continuous coverage for combatant commanders, supporting counterterrorism, monitoring adversarial movements and gathering intelligence in key regions.

The service’s ability to sustain this posture hinges on rapid replenishment and innovation. Tabor acknowledged that while the MQ-9 remains effective, its vulnerabilities in contested airspace have prompted a broader reevaluation of unmanned systems’ roles in future conflicts.

The Air Force’s next steps include finalizing replacement strategies and ensuring minimal disruption to global ISR operations. Lawmakers are expected to continue pressing for details on fleet resilience and modernization plans in coming hearings.
