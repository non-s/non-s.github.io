An Ethiopian woman, 35, delivered five babies on Wednesday at a hospital in Addis Ababa after a 12-year wait for a child, her doctors announced. The rare multiple birth occurred via planned cesarean section at Tikur Anbessa Specialized Hospital, one of Ethiopia’s largest medical centers. Medical staff confirmed the healthy delivery of three boys and two girls, weighing between 1.2 and 1.8 kilograms each. The mother, identified only as Mrs. Alemu, said she was “overjoyed” after years of infertility treatments and repeated disappointment. “I prayed every day for this moment,” she told reporters outside the hospital. “I never lost hope, even when doctors said it might not happen.”

## Medical team hails rare birth as a success

Doctors at Tikur Anbessa Specialized Hospital described the quintuplet delivery as a medical success despite the high risks associated with multiple births. Dr. Kebede Worku, head of obstetrics, said Mrs. Alemu had undergone fertility treatments for over a decade, including hormone therapy and ovulation induction. “This case required close monitoring from the start,” Worku said. “We adjusted her treatment plan multiple times to reduce complications.” Ethiopia has one of the highest maternal mortality rates in the world, according to [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) data, making such high-risk pregnancies particularly challenging. The hospital reported no immediate complications for the mother or babies, who are in stable condition under neonatal care.

## Fertility treatments and maternal health in Ethiopia

Experts say infertility affects about 15% of couples in sub-Saharan Africa, with limited access to advanced reproductive technologies. Mrs. Alemu’s journey reflects the struggles of many women in the region who face stigma and financial barriers while seeking fertility treatments. “I sold livestock and borrowed money to pay for my treatments,” she said. “But I never gave up.” Her case highlights broader challenges in Ethiopia’s healthcare system, where rural women often lack access to specialists. The government has expanded maternal health programs in recent years, but infertility remains underfunded and stigmatized.

## Global rarity of quintuplet births

Quintuplet births are exceptionally rare, occurring in roughly 1 in 55 million pregnancies worldwide, according to medical literature. The most famous recent case occurred in 2021 in [Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morocco), where a woman delivered five babies after fertility treatments. In Ethiopia, only a handful of such births have been documented in the past two decades. Dr. Worku noted that multiple births carry higher risks of preterm delivery, low birth weight, and developmental delays, but early intervention helped ensure positive outcomes for Mrs. Alemu’s babies.

Mrs. Alemu’s story has drawn local and international attention, with many praising her perseverance. Community leaders in Addis Ababa have organized support for her family, including donations for medical expenses. The hospital plans to monitor the babies’ progress closely over the coming months. For now, Mrs. Alemu said she is focused on recovery and bonding with her children. “They are my miracle,” she said. “I will cherish every moment.”

Ethiopia’s health ministry is reviewing the case to assess any lessons for future high-risk pregnancies in the country.
