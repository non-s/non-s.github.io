Ukraine and Russia executed the first stage of a landmark prisoner exchange Saturday, returning 205 detainees to each side as part of a planned 1,000-for-1,000 swap. The agreement was mediated by the United Arab Emirates, marking one of the most significant humanitarian gestures since Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

The exchange, confirmed by Ukrainian President [Volodymyr Zelenskyy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volodymyr_Zelenskyy) and Russian state media, signals a rare moment of cooperation between the warring nations. Ukrainian presidential adviser [Mykhailo Podolyak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mykhailo_Podolyak) called the swap “a step toward restoring humanity in wartime.” Russian officials framed it as an act of goodwill amid stalled peace talks.

## UAE plays key mediator role in prisoner swap
The United Arab Emirates confirmed its involvement in brokering the deal through its foreign ministry. UAE Foreign Minister [Abdullah bin Zayed Al Nahyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdullah_bin_Zayed_Al_Nahyan) stated the country facilitated talks to secure the release of civilians and military personnel on both sides. The UAE has increasingly positioned itself as a neutral mediator in regional conflicts, including facilitating previous smaller exchanges.

Ukrainian officials reported that among those released were soldiers captured during the 2023 battle for [Bakhmut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Bakhmut) and civilians detained in occupied territories. Russian state news agency [TASS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TASS) said some prisoners were fighters from the 2022 siege of [Mariupol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Mariupol).

## Next phase depends on continued negotiations
While Saturday’s exchange represents progress, both sides acknowledged that further talks are needed to finalize the full 1,000-for-1,000 swap. Ukrainian officials emphasized that the process remains fragile and dependent on mutual trust. Russian negotiators cautioned that additional conditions may be imposed before continuing.

International observers welcomed the deal as a rare positive development in a conflict marked by stalled diplomacy. The International Committee of the Red Cross [ICRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_of_the_Red_Cross), which often assists in prisoner swaps, confirmed its support role but did not disclose details about its involvement in this exchange.

The swap comes as Ukraine faces mounting pressure to secure the return of thousands of its citizens detained in Russia and occupied regions. Since the invasion began, thousands of prisoners have been exchanged in smaller, negotiated deals, but this marks the largest coordinated swap since late 2022.

Analysts suggest that such exchanges, while humanitarian, do not signal an end to hostilities. The war continues with active frontline fighting in eastern Ukraine, including around [Avdiivka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Avdiivka) and [Chasiv Yar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chasiv_Yar). The prisoner swap, however, offers temporary relief to hundreds of families separated by the conflict.
