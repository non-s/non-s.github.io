The Trump administration is raising alarms over Cuba’s recent purchase of over 300 military drones from Russia and Iran, according to a classified US intelligence report cited by Axios on May 18, 2026. US officials told the outlet that Havana has discussed using these drones to target American military assets—including the naval base at [Guantanamo Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base)—as well as ships and possibly Key West, Florida. The report also mentions the presence of Iranian military advisers in Cuba, which a senior US official described as deeply troubling. ‘When we think about those types of technologies being that close, and a range of bad actors from terror groups to drug cartels to Iranians to the Russians, it’s concerning,’ the official said. ‘It’s a growing threat.’

## Cuba denies claims as ‘fraudulent’

Cuba’s foreign minister, [Bruno Rodríguez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruno_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Parrilla), slammed the report as a ‘fraudulent case’ designed to justify economic sanctions and potential military action against the island nation. In a statement, Rodríguez accused the US of fabricating evidence with ‘no legitimate excuse whatsoever.’ The Cuban government has long opposed the US military presence at Guantanamo Bay, which it considers an illegal occupation of its territory. Cuba has also accused Washington of hypocrisy, pointing to the US’s own extensive drone program and military bases in the region.

Since 2023, Cuba has been quietly expanding its drone arsenal, purchasing models from both Russia and Iran that are capable of surveillance and, in some cases, armed strikes. The US report suggests Havana has explored integrating these drones into its military strategy, including plans for offensive operations near its shores. The timing of the report—just days after the US presidential election—has raised eyebrows, with analysts questioning whether it’s part of a broader effort to pressure Cuba’s government ahead of potential policy shifts in Washington.

## US officials push back on Cuban denials

A senior Trump administration official told Axios that the drone purchases and alleged attack plans are part of a broader pattern of Cuban and Iranian cooperation, which the US views as a direct threat to regional security. The official pointed to recent joint military exercises between Cuba and Iran as evidence of deepening ties. ‘This isn’t just about drones,’ the official said. ‘It’s about the kind of military cooperation that could destabilize the entire Caribbean.’ The US has been monitoring these developments closely, with intelligence reports indicating that Cuban officials have held multiple meetings with Iranian military advisers in Havana over the past year.

The report comes as tensions between the US and Cuba remain high, particularly over issues like immigration, human rights, and the ongoing US embargo on the island. Cuba has repeatedly called for the closure of Guantanamo Bay, which it argues is a symbol of US imperialism. The Trump administration, however, has shown no signs of backing down, with officials emphasizing the need to counter what they see as growing threats from state and non-state actors in the region.

## Broader implications for the Caribbean and beyond

The drone purchases signal a significant shift in Cuba’s military capabilities, one that could have ripple effects across the Caribbean and Latin America. If Cuba proceeds with its alleged plans to use drones offensively, it would mark the first time in decades that a country in the Western Hemisphere has deployed armed drones against another nation. The move could also embolden other US adversaries in the region, such as Venezuela, to pursue similar military buildups. For the US, the situation presents a delicate balancing act: how to respond to perceived threats without escalating tensions or giving Havana more ammunition to claim US aggression. The Trump administration’s options are limited, with economic sanctions already in place and military action unlikely given the risks of a broader conflict. Analysts say the most likely response will be diplomatic pressure, including calls for regional allies to condemn Cuba’s actions, but even that could backfire if Cuba digs in its heels.
