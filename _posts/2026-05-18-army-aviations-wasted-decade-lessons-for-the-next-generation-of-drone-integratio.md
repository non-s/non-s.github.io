In 2006, the U.S. Army’s 25th Combat Aviation Brigade arrived in Iraq with a new playbook. They brought Task Force ODIN—Observe, Detect, Identify, and Neutralize—a group of drones designed to spot roadside bombs and track insurgents. They paired it with an Apache battalion from the 82nd Airborne Division, a match that turned out to be a game-changer in practice, if not in policy. The drones’ sensors fed real-time data to the helicopter crews, who could then swoop in to destroy targets. Captain Jamie LaValley, then serving with the 82nd, watched it all unfold and called it the future of war. He wasn’t wrong. The system worked. It worked well. And then it sat on a shelf for years.[Task Force ODIN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Task_Force_ODIN) was born out of desperation. Iraq was a killing field of IEDs, and the Army needed every edge it could get. The drones—mostly unarmed MQ-1 Predators and Shadow UAVs—scanned roads and neighborhoods while Apaches lurked nearby, ready to strike. The synergy was immediate. One drone could watch a suspected bomb site for hours, relaying images to a helicopter crew that could confirm the threat and call in precision fire. Within months, Task Force ODIN’s teams were dismantling dozens of IEDs daily and taking out high-value targets like bomb makers and weapons caches. The kill chain shrank from days to minutes. It was the first real example of how drones and helicopters could work together in combat, not just side by side.[MQ-1 Predator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Atomics_MQ-1_Predator) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Atomics_MQ-1_Predator) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Atomics_MQ-1_Predator) // [AH-64 Apache](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AH-64_Apache) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AH-64_Apache) // [82nd Airborne Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/82nd_Airborne_Division) But here’s the thing: once the brigade rotated home, the idea died. Task Force ODIN was disbanded. The drones got repurposed. The Apaches went back to flying solo. The Army’s aviation branch moved on. And for nearly a decade, the service treated drones as something separate from its helicopter force, not as a critical partner. It wasn’t until Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine that the U.S. military started to seriously revisit the concept of pairing drones with helicopters again. Even then, the progress has been slow. Today, the Army’s top aviation units still struggle to integrate drones into their core operations. Exercises like Project Convergence focus on drones and sensors, but the day-to-day training doesn’t reflect the lessons learned in Iraq 16 years ago. The Army’s current drone strategy leans heavily on the Future Attack Reconnaissance Aircraft (FARA) and the Future Long-Range Assault Aircraft (FLRAA), both still years from full deployment. Meanwhile, the Ukrainians have shown what real-world integration looks like, using drones to spot targets and guide helicopter strikes in real time. The Army’s delay isn’t just about technology. It’s about culture. Aviation leaders still see drones as a support tool, not as an equal partner in combat. The Iraq experiment proved drones could do more than just scout ahead—they could actually direct lethal force. But the Army’s leadership didn’t push hard enough to make that change permanent. The result? A decade of wasted potential. The good news is that the lessons from 2006 are still relevant. In fact, they’re more important than ever. The Army’s new Multi-Domain Operations doctrine calls for tight integration of sensors, shooters, and communications. Drones are central to that vision. The question is whether the service will finally act on what it already proved works. The Ukrainians are doing it every day. The Israelis are doing it in Gaza. The U.S. Army needs to catch up before the next war starts.
