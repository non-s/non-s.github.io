Greenwich Entertainment has locked down North American distribution for Light Pillar, the debut feature from Shanghai-based director and screenwriter Xu Zao. The deal, announced Wednesday, covers a theatrical release across the U.S. set for early 2025. Light Pillar premiered in the Perspectives section at the 2024 Berlin International Film Festival, a program reserved for fresh, unconventional cinema from around the world. The festival’s selection put the film on the radar of critics and buyers alike, and Greenwich’s move confirms its commercial potential beyond its art-house roots.

The film’s animation style mixes traditional 2D with live-action sequences featuring real performers. Xu Zao, who’s been called one of China’s most promising new voices in animation, wrote and directed the project for Fengduan Pictures, a Shanghai-based production company. Light Pillar isn’t just a technical showcase—it tells the story of a young woman navigating grief after losing a parent, a theme that resonated strongly in Berlin. Festival reviewers noted how the hybrid style amplifies the emotional beats without relying on flashy effects.

Greenwich Entertainment, known for mid-budget indie gems like The Lighthouse and Nomadland, is positioning Light Pillar as a bridge between niche appeal and wider audiences. The company’s co-president, Richard Lorber, said in a statement that the film’s emotional core and striking visual approach made it a perfect fit for their slate. “Xu’s ability to blend genres while keeping the story human is rare,” Lorber said. “We see strong crossover potential in both theaters and festivals.”

## How Light Pillar stands out in animation
The film’s animation style is getting attention for how it uses 2D animation as the foundation while weaving in live-action elements. The live-action sequences aren’t just background dressing—they interact with the animated world, creating a layered visual experience. Xu Zao has described the process as “trying to make the audience feel the textures of memory,” a nod to the film’s themes of loss and connection. Early screenings have drawn comparisons to works like The Red Turtle and Wolfwalkers, though Light Pillar carves its own path with a distinctly Asian perspective.

Fengduan Pictures, which produced the film, is based in Shanghai’s Zhangjiang Hi-Tech Park, a hub for China’s growing animation industry. The company has been quietly building a reputation for pushing boundaries in the medium, and Light Pillar marks their first feature-length release. Fengduan’s CEO, Wang Lin, told trade press that the film’s success at Berlin was a turning point for the studio. “We knew we had something special,” Wang said, “but seeing critics and audiences respond the way they did was validating.”

For U.S. audiences, the film’s arrival next year could tap into a growing appetite for international animation that isn’t just for kids. Light Pillar’s Berlinale buzz alone has already sparked interest on festival circuits, and Greenwich’s theatrical push suggests they’re betting on word-of-mouth momentum. If the rollout lands well, it could open doors for more Chinese animation in mainstream American theaters—a rare opportunity for the genre to cross over without a global franchise attached.

## What to expect from the U.S. release
Greenwich hasn’t announced exact dates or a full lineup of theaters, but they’ve confirmed a wide enough rollout to capitalize on early festival buzz. The company plans to pair the theatrical run with outreach to film societies and specialized venues, a strategy that’s worked for other mid-tier indies in recent years. Critics who saw Light Pillar in Berlin are already calling it a “quietly powerful” experience, which bodes well for its U.S. chances.

The film’s distributor is also eyeing awards season, though its animated status may limit it to niche categories. Still, Light Pillar’s emotional depth and visual inventiveness could make it a contender for year-end critics’ lists. Greenwich’s marketing will likely lean into the film’s hybrid style and Xu Zao’s rising profile, positioning it as a must-see for animation fans tired of the usual superhero fare. If the gamble pays off, it could prove there’s room in U.S. theaters for thoughtful, original animation from outside Hollywood.
