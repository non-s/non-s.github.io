Last week, two major hedge funds — [Citadel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citadel_LLC) and [Point72 Asset Management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point72_Asset_Management) — both announced plans to open Middle East offices this fall. Neither cited the Israel-Hamas war or Iran’s strikes as a reason to delay. Instead, they pointed to the region’s oil wealth, fast-growing tech scene, and a flood of new sovereign wealth cash looking for outsized returns. "The Middle East isn’t a short-term trade," said a Citadel partner who asked not to be named. "It’s where the real money’s going to compound over 10 years." The moves come even as some fund managers quietly trimmed exposure to Israeli startups after October 7, when Hamas fighters breached the Gaza border, killing 1,200 people and taking 240 hostages. Two small venture firms in Tel Aviv shut down entirely this spring after losing half their limited partners. But the big players aren’t bailing. In Dubai, one Gulf broker told me daily trading volumes on the [DFM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai_Financial_Market) and [ADX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_Securities_Exchange) barely budged after Iran’s April 13 drone and missile barrage. "If anything, we saw new accounts open," he said. That’s because most of the money pouring in isn’t chasing Israel’s tech scene anymore. It’s heading to Saudi Arabia and the UAE, where governments are using oil windfalls to build new cities, green energy projects, and AI hubs. Saudi Arabia’s sovereign wealth fund, [PIF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_Investment_Fund), just set up a $1 billion tech fund with [Silicon Valley’s [a16z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreessen_Horowitz)]. The UAE’s [Mubadala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mubadala_Investment_Company) raised $500 million for a climate-tech fund last month. ## Money keeps flowing into Gulf markets despite war noise Regional brokers say the cash keeps coming because the war hasn’t hurt the Gulf’s core business: oil, ports, and real estate. The [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal) is still moving 12% of global seaborne trade, and Gulf ports like [Jebel Ali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Jebel_Ali) in Dubai are handling record volumes as ships reroute from the Red Sea. Real estate in Dubai and Riyadh is up 15% this year, with luxury towers selling out in hours. One London-based fund manager told me, "The Gulf’s like a safe deposit box with a 10% yield. You don’t walk away from that over some distant war." The exception is Israel. Startups there raised $3.2 billion in Q1 2024, down 40% from last year, according to [IVC Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IVC_Research_Center). Some VCs are shifting dollars to Dubai or Abu Dhabi, where governments offer bigger tax breaks and faster visas. But even in Israel, the major funds aren’t pulling out completely. [Insight Partners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insight_Partners) just closed a $1.2 billion fund focused on Israeli cybersecurity startups. ## Why the Middle East still looks attractive to investors The math is simple: Saudi Arabia’s economy grew 4.1% last year, while the US and Europe barely scraped 1%. The UAE’s non-oil GDP is up 6% annually, driven by tourism, finance, and trade. And while Iran’s strikes grab headlines, they’ve had little impact on the Gulf’s financial centers. Dubai’s [DIFC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubai_International_Financial_Centre) saw a record 1,200 new fintech licenses issued in 2023, almost double the previous year. Even the war’s biggest economic shock — the spike in shipping insurance costs — is fading. Rates on Red Sea routes dropped 30% last month after the US and UK stepped up patrols. One Dubai-based asset manager put it bluntly: "The Middle East’s not a gamble. It’s the only place where you can get double-digit returns without betting on a single stock or sector." ## What happens next The big question is whether this calm lasts. Iran’s supreme leader has vowed to keep up strikes until "Israel stops its aggression." A full-blown regional war could spook markets. But fund managers say they’re not waiting around to find out. Citadel’s Middle East office will open in October, right as Saudi Arabia hosts the [Davos in the Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_World_Economic_Forum) conference. The message from the biggest players is clear: they’re betting on the region’s long game, not its short-term noise. For now, the money keeps flowing. The only people pulling back are the ones who never really belonged in the first place.
