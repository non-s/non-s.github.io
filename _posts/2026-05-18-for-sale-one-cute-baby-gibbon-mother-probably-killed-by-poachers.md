A two-month-old gibbon named Chokdee sits on a wildlife officer’s lap at Thailand’s Omkoi sanctuary, kicking his legs in a baby’s tracksuit. His dark skin peeks through white fur except on his face and hands. “We call him Chokdee,” the officer says. “It means ‘good luck.’” But luck ran out for his mother. Poachers likely killed her to capture the infant for Thailand’s brutal exotic pet trade, officials say. Chokdee is now one of thousands of gibbons orphaned or killed each year as demand for rare pets skyrockets across Southeast Asia.

## How the illegal pet trade fuels extinction
Gibbons are highly social animals that mate for life and raise offspring together for years. That makes them easy targets for poachers, who often shoot one parent to capture the baby. “They’re not just pets—they’re family,” said [Suranaree University of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suranaree_University_of_Technology) primatologist Dr. Nongluk Pongpeng. “When you take a baby, you destroy an entire social unit.” Thailand, once a hub for gibbon trafficking, still sees dozens of confiscations annually, though numbers are hard to track.

The [Hylobatidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hylobatidae) family includes 20 species, all threatened by habitat loss and the pet trade. In 2023, Thailand’s Department of National Parks recorded 38 gibbon rescues, up from 22 in 2020. Most were infants under six months old. “It’s not just one species at risk,” said [Wildlife Friends Foundation Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_Friends_Foundation_Thailand) director Lek Chailert. “Every week, we see new cases of gibbons smuggled from Myanmar or Laos.”

## Where Chokdee fits in the grim trade
Chokdee was likely snatched from a forest in Thailand’s northern hills, near the Myanmar border. Traffickers often move gibbons through informal networks, using social media to advertise “cute pets” for sale. Prices can reach $1,500–$3,000 per animal in Thailand’s underground markets. “People see them as status symbols,” said Chailert. “But most die within a year. They’re not domesticated—they’re wild animals.”

Sanctuaries like Omkoi struggle to care for orphans. Chokdee’s diet includes fruit, insects, and formula, but gibbons need constant social interaction to survive. Without his mother, he’s at risk of chronic stress or illness. “He’s lucky to be here,” the officer said. “Most don’t make it.”

## What happens next for Chokdee and other gibbons
Officials plan to rehabilitate Chokdee for eventual release into a protected forest. But rehabilitation takes years, and success isn’t guaranteed. Meanwhile, Thailand has strengthened penalties for wildlife trafficking, including up to four years in prison and fines of $3,000. “The law is there,” said [Department of National Parks director-general](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_National_Parks,_Wildlife_and_Plant_Conservation) Adul Sangsingkeo. “But enforcement is inconsistent.”

Conservationists say the real solution is reducing demand. Campaigns targeting social media platforms have banned gibbon sales, but traffickers adapt quickly. “We need to change how people see these animals,” said Pongpeng. “They’re not accessories—they’re wild, endangered, and struggling to survive.” Chokdee’s fate hangs on whether Thailand can curb the trade before another orphan ends up on a stranger’s lap.
