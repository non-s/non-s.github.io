> 📰 **Continuing coverage:** [Global executions hit 44-year high in 2025, Iran leads surge](/world/2026/05/18/executions-nearly-doubled-in-the-us-last-year-and-soared-abroad/)

Amnesty International’s annual report on global executions shows the grim reality of 2025: at least 2,707 people were put to death, the highest number since 1981. That’s a 78% jump from the previous year, driven almost entirely by Iran, which executed over 2,100 people—more than 75% of the global total. The surge marks the first time executions have topped 2,500 since the early 1980s, reversing a decades-long decline in state-sanctioned killings. Amnesty’s researchers warn that governments, particularly in Iran, are using capital punishment as a tool to project authority and silence dissent. They point to a pattern where executions are framed as part of ‘tough-on-crime’ policies, even when evidence suggests they’re used to punish political opponents or control populations. The report underscores how capital punishment is being weaponized—not for justice, but for power. Iran’s execution spree included mass killings after protests and trials that Amnesty calls grossly unfair. The country now accounts for the vast majority of the world’s known executions, far surpassing Saudi Arabia, which executed 172 people in 2025, and Iraq, with 91. Other countries, like Egypt and Somalia, also saw increases, though their numbers remain in the dozens rather than the hundreds. China, however, remains the biggest unknown. The government refuses to release any data on executions, but Amnesty estimates it carries out thousands each year, making it the world’s top executioner by a wide margin. Researchers believe the real global total is likely much higher than 2,707, given the lack of transparency in countries like North Korea, Vietnam, and Afghanistan. Amnesty’s data relies on reports from media, activists, and official sources, but many executions happen in secret, especially in authoritarian states. ## The numbers don’t lie, but they don’t tell the whole story either. The rise in executions in 2025 isn’t just about crime or punishment—it’s about control. Governments are using capital punishment to send a message: resist, and you could be next. In Iran, executions surged after protests in 2023 and 2024, with authorities targeting protesters, journalists, and minority groups. The government has framed these killings as necessary for ‘stability,’ but critics say they’re a form of terror. The same tactic is playing out in other countries. Saudi Arabia executed 172 people in 2025, one of its highest yearly totals in decades, often for non-violent offenses like drug possession. Iraq, too, ramped up executions, with 91 confirmed in 2025, many after sham trials. The trend isn’t limited to the Middle East. In Africa, Somalia executed at least 42 people in 2025, while Egypt put 15 to death. Even in countries with moratoriums, like the U.S. and Japan, executions crept up slightly. The U.S. carried out 24 executions in 2025, up from 20 in 2024, while Japan executed three people. The global spike raises serious questions about the future of human rights. Amnesty’s report suggests that as authoritarian governments face internal pressure, they’re turning to executions as a way to maintain power. The group warns that unless the international community pushes back, the numbers could keep climbing. The report also highlights the role of secrecy. Without transparency, it’s impossible to know the true scale of executions. China’s refusal to disclose data means its numbers aren’t included in Amnesty’s totals, but the group estimates they far exceed the rest of the world combined. The same is likely true in North Korea, where executions are rumored to be frequent but never confirmed. ## What happens next? Amnesty is calling for urgent action to reverse the trend. The group urges countries to abolish the death penalty entirely, arguing that it’s never been an effective deterrent and is always subject to abuse. They also demand that the UN and other international bodies pressure countries like Iran and China to end their execution sprees and release data. For now, the outlook is grim. With no major shifts in policy expected in the near term, the risk is that 2025’s record numbers become the new normal. The death penalty isn’t just a punishment—it’s a political tool, and until that changes, the killing will keep rising.
