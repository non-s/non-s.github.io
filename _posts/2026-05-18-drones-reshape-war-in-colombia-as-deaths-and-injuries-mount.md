A Tuesday night football match in southern Colombia turned deadly when a drone dropped a grenade from above, killing a 10-year-old boy and injuring 12 others. The attack in Cauca province in 2024 marked the first time a civilian in Colombia died in a drone strike, according to reports. Local authorities confirmed the child’s death and said the blast also left children and adults with shrapnel wounds. The incident shows how armed groups are adding drones to their toolkit in Colombia’s decades-long conflict with guerrillas, paramilitaries, and drug cartels.

The attack happened in the town of [El Tambo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Tambo,_Cauca), a rural area where armed groups often clash over control of coca fields and smuggling routes. Witnesses said the drone circled overhead before releasing its payload, then flew off before security forces arrived. Cauca has seen a surge in violence since the 2016 peace deal with the [Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutionary_Armed_Forces_of_Colombia) collapsed in parts of the region. Now, smaller armed groups are filling the power vacuum, and drones are becoming a cheap, low-risk way to strike targets without putting fighters in danger.

## Armed groups turn to drones as war shifts

Drones cost little compared to artillery or helicopter attacks, and they’re hard to track. Groups like the [National Liberation Army (ELN)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_(Colombia)) and dissident FARC factions have been spotted using them for surveillance and strikes in recent years. In 2023, Colombia’s military said it had destroyed at least 15 armed drones, up from just two in 2022. The military now trains soldiers to shoot down drones using rifles and nets, but the tactic is reactive, not a real solution.

Communities in Cauca and neighboring [Nariño](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nari%C3%B1o_Department) say they’re living in fear. Parents keep kids indoors after dark. Farmers avoid fields where drones have been seen. Local leaders blame the government for not stopping the spread of armed drones before they became a regular threat. ‘The state isn’t here,’ said one community leader in Cauca who asked not to be named. ‘We’re caught between groups that act like they own the sky.’

## Government scrambles to react

Colombia’s military admits it’s struggling to keep up. It’s set up drone detection systems in hotspots but says the devices are too slow to stop fast-moving attacks. In 2024, it began deploying counter-drone radar in Cauca, but locals say it’s already too late. The government has also blamed drug cartels and armed groups for importing drones from the U.S. and China, then modifying them to carry explosives. Some drones are even built in Colombia, assembled in hidden workshops and sold to the highest bidder.

The drone threat is spreading beyond rural areas. In 2023, a drone strike in [Bogotá](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1) hit a police station, though no one was killed. That attack was claimed by a little-known group, but it showed how drones can turn into urban weapons. Police now patrol with anti-drone guns, but experts warn the technology is evolving faster than defenses.

## What’s next for Colombia’s drones and the people caught in the middle

For now, civilians in Cauca and other hotspots are paying the price. Schools have canceled night classes. Markets close early. Families with kids avoid open spaces after sundown. The boy who died in the 2024 attack was a bright student, said his teacher. ‘He loved football. Now he’s gone, and no one even knows who did it.’

The military vows to step up patrols and destroy more drones before they’re used. But analysts say the real fix needs political will—something that’s been missing for years. Until then, armed groups will keep using drones, and civilians will keep dying.
