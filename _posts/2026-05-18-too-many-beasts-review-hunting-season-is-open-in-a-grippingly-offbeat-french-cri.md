Too Many Beasts isn’t your usual crime thriller. It’s a raw, almost documentary-style look at simmering tensions in rural France, where hunting isn’t just a sport—it’s a way of life. Sarah Arnold’s debut feature pulls no punches, showing how quickly respect turns to rage when farmers blame hunters for crop damage and cops try to keep the peace. The film’s title hints at the chaos: too many beasts means too many bullets, too many grudges, and too little room for compromise. By the end, the forest becomes a powder keg, and no one walks away clean.


Alexis Manenti [Alexis Manenti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Manenti) and Ella Rumpf [Ella Rumpf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ella_Rumpf) anchor the chaos. Manenti, fresh off his breakout role in Les Misérables, plays a hunter tangled in the mess, while Rumpf brings a quiet intensity as a gendarme caught between duty and personal stakes. Their chemistry grounds the film’s brutality in something relatable—even when the violence feels extreme, you understand why these people can’t let go. The supporting cast, mostly non-actors from the region, add a layer of authenticity that most crime films fake.


## A powder keg of rural France
Too Many Beasts doesn’t waste time on backstory. The conflict starts early, with farmers accusing hunters of reckless shooting that ruins their livelihoods. The gendarmes, stretched thin, try to mediate but end up squeezed between two angry sides. The film’s setting—thick forests and misty fields near the Belgian border—becomes a character itself, claustrophobic and inescapable. Arnold shoots in tight close-ups and handheld camerawork, making every argument feel intimate and every gunshot jarring. It’s not pretty, but it’s effective.


The violence isn’t glorified, but it’s not shy either. There’s a scene where a hunter’s dog gets shot mid-hunt, and another where a farmer’s tractor is torched in retaliation. These aren’t just plot points—they’re real stakes. The film doesn’t flinch from showing the ugliness of rural life when people feel backed into a corner. Even the cops start questioning their own rules when their families get caught in the crossfire.


## Why this film stands out
What makes Too Many Beasts different is its refusal to take sides. Arnold doesn’t portray the hunters as villains or the cops as saints. Everyone’s got a reason to be angry, and everyone’s got blood on their hands by the final act. The dialogue feels natural, like overheard conversations in a bar, not scripted lines. It’s the kind of film where you leave the theater thinking about the real people behind the roles—because the cast mostly *are* real people from the region.


The film’s pacing is deliberate, almost slow at times, but that’s intentional. Arnold wants you to sit in the tension, to feel the weight of every decision. When the violence finally explodes, it hits harder because you’ve watched these characters drown in their own frustrations for 90 minutes. It’s not a feel-good movie, but it’s a satisfying one if you crave realism over escapism.


Too Many Beasts is already drawing comparisons to French crime classics like La Haine, but it carves its own path. This isn’t a film about heroes or antiheroes—it’s about people who make terrible choices because they feel they have no other option. The ending leaves you wondering who, if anyone, actually wins. In rural France, sometimes the only way out is through the barrel of a gun.
