British Gas, one of the UK’s largest energy suppliers, has been ordered to pay £20 million after the energy regulator confirmed it breached licence conditions designed to protect vulnerable customers. The penalty follows an investigation into allegations that the company forced prepayment meters on households unable to pay bills through standard methods. The scandal has drawn sharp criticism from consumer groups and lawmakers, with calls for stricter oversight of the energy sector.

The [Ofgem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ofgem) investigation found that British Gas failed to follow proper procedures when installing prepayment meters, a move that can leave customers without access to consistent heating and electricity. The regulator concluded that the company’s actions disproportionately affected low-income households, elderly people, and those with disabilities. Ofgem’s enforcement team determined that British Gas breached rules requiring energy suppliers to provide reasonable payment options for struggling customers.

## Energy regulator cites repeated failures in customer protection

Ofgem’s report highlights multiple instances where British Gas ignored its own policies and regulatory guidelines. Investigators found that company representatives used aggressive tactics to install prepayment meters, including visiting homes without adequate notice and failing to assess customers’ vulnerability properly. Some affected households reported being left without power for extended periods, exacerbating financial and health pressures during colder months.

The £20 million fine is one of the largest ever imposed by Ofgem for breaches of consumer protection rules. The penalty includes £15 million in compensation to affected customers and a £5 million payment to Ofgem’s redress fund, which supports vulnerable energy consumers. British Gas has accepted the findings and pledged to improve its practices, including additional staff training and stricter oversight of meter installations.

## British Gas responds to penalty with commitments to reform

In a statement, British Gas acknowledged the failures and expressed regret for the distress caused to customers. The company’s chief executive stated that the incidents were unacceptable and pledged to review all recent prepayment meter installations. British Gas also committed to refunding customers who were wrongly charged for installations and to providing clearer information about payment options.

Consumer advocates welcomed the penalty but called for broader reforms in the energy sector. They argue that fines alone are insufficient to prevent future abuses, citing ongoing issues with high energy prices and inadequate support for vulnerable households. Campaigners are pushing for stronger regulatory powers and mandatory compensation for customers affected by improper installations.

The scandal comes amid broader concerns about the UK’s energy market, where millions of households face rising costs and limited payment flexibility. Critics say the sector remains under-regulated, allowing companies to prioritize profits over customer welfare. The government has faced pressure to introduce stricter rules, including bans on forced prepayment meter installations for vulnerable groups.

British Gas has until October to pay the fine and begin compensating affected customers. Ofgem has warned that future breaches could result in even harsher penalties, including licence revocation. The case may also prompt further investigations into other energy suppliers’ practices regarding prepayment meters.
