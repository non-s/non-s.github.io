[Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump)’s recent negotiations over [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon) have drawn sharp criticism from analysts and regional observers, who argue his administration is worsening the country’s deepening political and economic crisis. The talks, framed as efforts to mediate sectarian tensions and economic instability, have instead been accused of publicly undermining Lebanon’s fragile government, deepening divisions and stoking public anger.

Critics point to Trump’s public statements and diplomatic posturing as key drivers of the crisis. In a series of interviews and social media posts, Trump has repeatedly criticized Lebanese leaders, calling for sweeping reforms while publicly humiliating key officials. Analysts say such rhetoric has emboldened opposition factions and weakened the legitimacy of [President Michel Aoun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Aoun) and [Prime Minister Najib Mikati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najib_Mikati), whose government is already struggling to maintain control amid widespread protests and economic collapse.

## Lebanon’s Collapse Deepens Amid Political Paralysis

Lebanon’s crisis has reached historic levels, with the country defaulting on its debt, the lira losing over 90% of its value, and more than half the population living in poverty. The financial meltdown, triggered by decades of corruption and mismanagement, has been exacerbated by political paralysis and sectarian divisions. International donors, including the [International Monetary Fund (IMF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund), have stalled aid packages, demanding sweeping reforms that Lebanon’s divided leadership has failed to deliver.

Trump’s involvement, analysts argue, has further complicated efforts to stabilize the country. His administration has pushed for quick fixes, including sanctions on Lebanese officials and public pressure on Hezbollah, which the U.S. considers a terrorist organization. However, critics say these moves have backfired, uniting political factions against external interference and hardening positions within Lebanon’s deeply fractured political class.

## Regional Powers React to Trump’s Approach

The escalating tensions have drawn reactions from regional powers, including [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran), [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia), and [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France), all of which have historical and strategic interests in Lebanon. Iran, a key backer of [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), has accused the U.S. of destabilizing Lebanon to serve its own geopolitical goals. Saudi Arabia, meanwhile, has remained largely silent, focusing on its own regional priorities. France, Lebanon’s former colonial power, has called for dialogue but has been sidelined as Trump’s administration takes center stage.

Domestically, Lebanese protesters have grown more vocal, accusing both their government and foreign powers of failing to address their suffering. The October 2019 uprising, which brought down the previous government, has morphed into a broader movement demanding systemic change. However, the entry of U.S. mediation has shifted focus away from domestic reform, further polarizing an already divided society.

## What Happens Next?

The coming months will determine whether Lebanon can avoid further collapse. The IMF is expected to resume stalled negotiations in the near future, but success hinges on Lebanon’s ability to form a unified government capable of implementing reforms. Trump’s administration, meanwhile, faces a dilemma: continue its aggressive approach and risk further destabilization, or adopt a more conciliatory tone to encourage cooperation. Analysts warn that without a shift in strategy, Lebanon’s crisis will deepen, with regional and global implications that could fuel further migration, extremism, and conflict.
