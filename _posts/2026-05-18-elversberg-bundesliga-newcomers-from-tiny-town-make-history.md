Elversberg’s story isn’t just about football—it’s about a small-town club proving that size doesn’t always matter. Located in Spiesen-Elversberg, a municipality in Germany’s smallest state of Saarland, the club’s population of just 7,000 residents makes it one of the smallest communities ever to produce a Bundesliga team. To put that in perspective, Elversberg’s potential home ground, the Waldstadion an der Kaiserlinde, has a capacity of 10,000. That means the stadium itself is larger than the town’s entire population.

The club has spent most of its history in Germany’s lower divisions, but its recent rise began in 2017 when Nils-Ole Book joined as a scout before becoming sporting director. Book, who had no prior experience in professional football before Elversberg, quickly became a driving force behind the club’s transformation. In 2018, Horst Steffen—[a former Bundesliga player](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horst_Steffen) with stints at clubs like [Karlsruher SC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlsruher_SC) and [Eintracht Frankfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eintracht_Frankfurt)—took over as head coach. Together, they built a team that steadily climbed the ranks.

## From near-collapse to top flight

Elversberg’s journey hasn’t been smooth. The club was founded in 1907 but folded during World War I before re-forming in 1918. It spent decades in regional leagues before briefly reaching the third division in 2013 alongside [RB Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RB_Leipzig), only to be relegated immediately. Two playoff heartbreaks in 2016 and 2017 kept the club in the fourth tier a while longer. But after Book and Steffen took charge, things changed. They secured promotion to the third division in 2022, then stunned the league by winning back-to-back promotions in 2023 and 2024, reaching the Bundesliga for the first time in the club’s 119-year history.

The 2022-23 season was pivotal. A young striker named Nick Woltemade—who later joined [Newcastle United](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newcastle_United_F.C.)—scored 11 goals in 27 appearances as Elversberg won the third-division title. The following season, the club finished second in the second division, securing automatic promotion. Their final match of the 2023-24 campaign was a 3-0 win over [1. FC Magdeburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1._FC_Magdeburg), a club with nearly 30 times Elversberg’s population.

## The man behind the miracle

Nils-Ole Book’s role can’t be overstated. Before joining Elversberg, he worked in insurance and had no professional football experience. His hiring as a scout in 2017 was a gamble that paid off. Book focused on scouting overlooked talent and building a squad with chemistry over star power. Under his leadership, Elversberg became known for its relentless pressing and tactical discipline, traits instilled by Steffen.

The club’s success has also drawn attention to Saarland, a region often overshadowed by bigger German footballing hubs like Bavaria or North Rhine-Westphalia. Saarland’s football culture has historically been modest, but Elversberg’s rise could inspire other small clubs. The town itself has few amenities—a lack of a train station and just three bakeries—but it now has a Bundesliga team to call its own.

## What’s next for Elversberg

As the 2026-27 Bundesliga season approaches, Elversberg faces an uphill battle. The league is one of the toughest in Europe, and survival is rarely guaranteed for promoted sides. Historically, only four out of the last ten promoted teams have stayed up in their first Bundesliga season. But Elversberg’s squad, built on grit and teamwork, might just defy expectations. Their first home game will be a spectacle in a town that’s never hosted top-flight football before.

For the players, staff, and fans, this is more than a promotion—it’s a once-in-a-lifetime moment. The team’s story has already become a favorite in German football circles, a reminder that dreams can come true even in the unlikeliest places. Whether they stay up or not, Elversberg has already made history.
