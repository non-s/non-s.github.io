The World Health Organization [WHO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on Sunday, the highest alert the agency can issue, after an Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo [DRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo) killed at least 84 people since August 2018. The declaration came after the virus spilled into neighboring Uganda, where two people died last month. This marks the second-largest Ebola outbreak in history, behind only the 2014-2016 West Africa epidemic that killed over 11,000 people. Health officials say the risk of further international spread remains low but can't be ruled out, especially as the virus moves closer to urban areas like Goma, a city of nearly 2 million people near Rwanda's border. The WHO's emergency committee met six times before making the call, debating whether the outbreak had reached a tipping point requiring maximum global attention. They decided yes, largely because of the risk of cross-border spread and the lack of a proven vaccine for this strain. The announcement follows criticism that the WHO and global partners moved too slowly during the West Africa outbreak, allowing the virus to spread unchecked for over a year before declaring an emergency. This time, they're trying to act faster, but the challenges on the ground are massive. Congo's health system is fragile after years of conflict, and many communities distrust outsiders, making it hard to track cases or convince people to get vaccinated with existing shots that don't match this strain. The current outbreak involves the Ebola Zaire strain, the deadliest form of the virus, which kills up to 90% of those infected in some outbreaks. Symptoms start with fever, muscle pain, and fatigue, then progress to vomiting, diarrhea, and internal bleeding. Patients usually die within days without treatment. Doctors Without Borders [MSF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res) and the Congolese government have set up treatment centers, but access is limited in remote areas where the virus often hides. The WHO's emergency declaration doesn't legally force countries to do anything, but it's meant to shake loose more money, resources, and political attention. Countries like the U.S. and European nations have already poured millions into the response, but experts say more is needed to stop this outbreak before it becomes a full-blown catastrophe. The biggest worry? That this emergency declaration comes too late. The virus has already spread through Congo's North Kivu and Ituri provinces, two regions plagued by armed groups and weak governance. Aid workers report that many people refuse to believe Ebola is real, hiding sick relatives or avoiding treatment centers. Others believe the disease is a conspiracy to harvest organs or steal blood. These misconceptions make controlling the outbreak nearly impossible without community buy-in, which takes time and trust neither side has right now. There's also the question of what happens if the virus reaches a major city like Kinshasa, Congo's capital of 12 million people. The country's health minister has warned that the infrastructure isn't ready to handle a large-scale outbreak in an urban setting. Meanwhile, the WHO is racing to test experimental vaccines that could work against this strain, but results won't come for weeks. In the meantime, they're relying on tried-and-true methods: isolating patients, tracing contacts, and educating communities. But with violence against health workers on the rise—over 300 attacks have been recorded since January—those efforts are getting harder by the day. The WHO's emergency committee will meet again in about three months to reassess the situation. By then, they'll know if this declaration worked or if the outbreak has spiraled beyond control. Either way, one thing is clear: the world is watching, and the stakes couldn't be higher.
