Wales’ Six Nations campaign ended in familiar disappointment as Italy claimed their first win over the Dragons since 2013 with a 27-13 victory in Rome. The defeat sealed Wales’ second winless tournament in seven years and their fifth Wooden Spoon in the last decade. Fans inside the Stadio Olimpico booed the team off the field, a clear sign of the frustration that’s grown with each passing defeat.

This wasn’t just another loss—it was the latest in a string of poor results that have left Welsh rugby fans questioning whether their team still belongs among Europe’s elite. Italy, meanwhile, celebrated their first Six Nations win over Wales since 2013, a result that will boost their confidence heading into future campaigns. The Italians started strong, scoring two early tries to take a 14-0 lead within the first 15 minutes. Wales never recovered, managing just two penalty goals from Dan Biggar in a game they barely threatened Italy’s defense.

The defeat in Rome means Wales finish the tournament with zero wins for the second time since 2017. It’s a far cry from the glory days of 2012, when they completed a Grand Slam under Warren Gatland. This time, there’s no silver lining—just another disappointing campaign that raises serious questions about the team’s direction. Gatland, who’s been in charge since 2020, has come under fire before, but this performance could be the final straw for many supporters.

Wales’ problems weren’t just about the scoreboard. Their attack looked toothless, their defense shaky, and their set pieces fell apart at critical moments. Italy, meanwhile, played with real belief, and their scrum-half [Stephen Varney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Varney) was one of the standout performers, orchestrating his side’s play with confidence. The Italians now have a psychological edge over Wales, something they’ll carry into future matches.

For Wales, the focus will now shift to the summer Tests and the 2025 World Cup qualifiers. Gatland has already suggested he’ll stay on, but the pressure on him and the players has never been higher. Fans are tired of watching a team that seems stuck in neutral, and unless there’s a clear plan for improvement, the calls for change will only get louder.

The Six Nations isn’t just about winning—it’s about proving you belong. Wales failed that test again. Italy, on the other hand, can hold their heads high after a performance that suggests they’re finally turning a corner.

What comes next for Wales? That’s the question everyone’s asking. With Gatland’s future in doubt and the players’ confidence shattered, the Dragons face a summer of reckoning. Will they regroup and come back stronger, or will this Wooden Spoon mark the beginning of a deeper decline?
