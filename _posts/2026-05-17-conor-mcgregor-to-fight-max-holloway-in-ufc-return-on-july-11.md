Conor McGregor’s UFC comeback is official. The Irish MMA icon, who hasn’t stepped into the cage since July 2021, will face former featherweight champion Max Holloway in Las Vegas on July 11. The fight headlines UFC 329 as part of International Fight Week, the UFC’s annual promotional event in Sin City. It’s a rematch of their 2013 featherweight bout at UFC on Fox 11, which McGregor won by unanimous decision despite tearing his ACL mid-fight. Holloway, 32, is coming off a split-decision loss to Brian Ortega in April 2024. The two men have split their two meetings—the first in 2013, the second in December 2021 when Holloway outworked McGregor over three rounds at UFC 268. This time, the weight class is welterweight for both fighters, though the bout isn’t for a title. That keeps the focus strictly on McGregor’s return and Holloway’s bid to stay in title contention in a stacked division. McGregor, 37, has fought just once since September 2019—a leg-breaking loss to Dustin Poirier in July 2021. The fight ended with Poirier knocking McGregor out in the first round, though McGregor showed heart by immediately getting back up and continuing to throw strikes. The loss left McGregor with a 1-3 record in his last four bouts, a slide that’s fueled speculation about his future. His last win came in January 2021 against Poirier at UFC 257, where he finished the American with a flying knee in the first round. Before that, McGregor hadn’t fought since October 2018 when he lost to Khabib Nurmagomedov at UFC 229, a fight that ended with McGregor getting choked unconscious in the fourth round. McGregor’s resume still sparkles: he’s the first UFC fighter to hold two belts simultaneously, winning the featherweight title in December 2015 and the lightweight title in November 2016. He’s also the former Cage Warriors featherweight and lightweight champion, giving him a rare cross-promotion pedigree. But the past three years haven’t gone his way. A planned return in June 2024 against Michael Chandler was scrapped after McGregor suffered a toe injury in training. Chandler went on to knock out Justin Gaethje in a main event at UFC 304 in October 2024. McGregor’s camp has been tight-lipped about his future, but his presence on the July 11 card suggests he’s still chasing a third UFC title. Holloway, meanwhile, is fighting for relevance. The Hawaiian, known for his relentless pace and volume striking, has been a mainstay in the top five of the featherweight and lightweight divisions for a decade. He’s 27-9 overall with 12 finishes, but his last two losses came against Ortega and Arnold Allen, both in close decisions. A win over McGregor would instantly elevate him back into title contention, especially with the welterweight division wide open after Islam Makhachev’s dominant run at 170 pounds. ## The stakes for McGregor’s legacy The timing of McGregor’s return is almost as calculated as his striking. He’s fighting on UFC 329, a card designed to generate buzz during International Fight Week when thousands of fans, media, and broadcasters descend on Las Vegas. The UFC has long relied on McGregor as a draw, and his absence since 2021 has left a gap in its promotional calendar. A win over Holloway would silence critics who question whether he’s still elite. A loss, especially by stoppage, could force McGregor into retirement. His leg injury against Poirier in 2021 was brutal, and the fear of another serious injury looms over every fighter. McGregor’s team has controlled his schedule meticulously since that fight, avoiding the kind of rapid-fire matchups that once defined his career. His last camp lasted nearly two years, a break that included training in Dublin and a brief foray into boxing in 2023. This time, the camp has been shorter, suggesting McGregor isn’t looking to grind through multiple fights in 2025. Instead, the July 11 bout feels like a statement: he’s back, and he’s ready to remind the world why he was once the most marketable athlete on the planet. ## What’s next for Holloway and McGregor’s rivals Holloway enters the fight with a chip on his shoulder. He’s 32, and while he’s still a top contender, the window to challenge for a title is closing. A win over McGregor would put him in line for a shot at the interim lightweight belt, currently held by Dustin Poirier. Poirier, McGregor’s last opponent, has been vocal about staying active, and a loss for Holloway could push him toward a trilogy with the Irishman. For McGregor, the fight is about legacy. He’s 47-6 overall in MMA, but his last five fights have been a mixed bag. His 2016 double-champ run is ancient history to some fans, and his post-2020 career has been defined by injuries and stoppages. A victory over Holloway won’t instantly restore him to the top of the pound-for-pound rankings, but it would reopen doors. The welterweight division is in flux, with Islam Makhachev’s recent dominance at 170 pounds leaving a power vacuum. If McGregor can prove he’s still dangerous at 170, he could force the UFC’s hand into giving him a title shot. That’s a long shot, but McGregor has always played the long game. The UFC’s next move will depend on how this fight goes. If McGregor looks sharp, expect a quick return. If he stumbles, his retirement could come sooner than anyone expects. Either way, July 11 is just the beginning of what promises to be one of the most talked-about comebacks in sports.
