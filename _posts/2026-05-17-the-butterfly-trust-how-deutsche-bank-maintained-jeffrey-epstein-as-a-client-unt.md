On May 10, 2019, Stewart Oldfield, a director in Deutsche Bank’s U.S. wealth unit, sent an email with the subject line “Epstein account closure.” In it, he wrote that some of Epstein’s accounts were still open, contradicting the bank’s public claim that it had severed ties with the financier months earlier. Oldfield’s message came just seven weeks before Epstein’s July 6, 2019 arrest on sex trafficking charges. The bank had previously told regulators and the public it had cut Epstein loose after a 2018 Miami Herald investigation exposed his abuse of underage girls and his failure to register as a sex offender in New York. Deutsche Bank later admitted in court filings that it had profited from Epstein’s business for years, handling transactions worth hundreds of millions through his accounts. The bank paid $75 million in settlements with Epstein’s victims and regulators over its handling of his accounts. Epstein killed himself in jail in August 2019 while awaiting trial. The revelations from Oldfield’s email raise fresh questions about Deutsche Bank’s compliance failures and whether it ignored red flags about Epstein’s activities for years. Court documents and internal emails show Deutsche Bank employees flagged Epstein’s accounts as high-risk as early as 2013, but the bank continued to do business with him. In 2015, the bank’s compliance team even recommended closing his accounts, but the decision was overturned by senior managers who cited Epstein’s connections to wealthy clients and his influence in New York’s elite circles. ## The bank’s slow-motion breakup with Epstein Deutsche Bank first told Epstein to close his accounts in November 2018, after a Miami Herald expose detailed his decades of abuse. But Oldfield’s email shows the bank was still processing transactions for Epstein in May 2019, including accounts it claimed were closed. The bank’s internal systems showed multiple accounts under Epstein’s name remained active, with balances that didn’t match what Oldfield told colleagues had been shut down. Oldfield’s email suggests the bank was scrambling to reconcile its public statements with the reality of its ongoing relationship with Epstein. He wrote, “Since the client intends to have the rest of their accounts closed this week or next, let’s agree on what is still open.” The phrasing implies Epstein’s team was finally pushing for closure, but Deutsche Bank’s systems still listed accounts as active. ## Why Deutsche Bank kept Epstein for so long Epstein’s ties to wealthy clients and his reputation as a powerful financier in New York made him a valuable client for Deutsche Bank, despite his growing notoriety. Documents show the bank viewed Epstein as a “high-net-worth individual” who brought in significant revenue through fees and transactions. In 2017 alone, Epstein moved $14.2 million through his Deutsche Bank accounts, according to court filings. Senior managers at the bank reportedly resisted closing his accounts because they feared losing other wealthy clients who did business with Epstein. The bank’s compliance team, however, repeatedly warned that Epstein’s activities posed serious risks, including potential money laundering and reputational damage. In 2016, Deutsche Bank’s New York branch filed a suspicious activity report (SAR) on Epstein’s accounts, citing his history of underage sex trafficking allegations and his failure to register as a sex offender. The SAR was one of at least five filed on Epstein’s accounts between 2013 and 2018, but the bank continued to do business with him. ## The fallout and what’s next for Deutsche Bank After Epstein’s arrest, Deutsche Bank faced a wave of lawsuits from his victims, regulators, and shareholders. The bank admitted fault in court filings and agreed to pay $75 million in settlements, including $58 million to Epstein’s victims and $17 million to regulators. The settlements resolved claims that the bank had failed to properly monitor Epstein’s accounts and ignored red flags about his activities. The bank also agreed to overhaul its compliance systems and hired an independent monitor to oversee its anti-money laundering (AML) practices. Deutsche Bank’s reputation suffered severe damage, and some clients withdrew their money in protest. The bank’s U.S. wealth management division, which handled Epstein’s accounts, was later sold off as part of a broader restructuring. The case also led to broader scrutiny of how banks handle high-risk clients, particularly those with histories of misconduct. Regulators have since tightened rules for private banks, requiring more rigorous due diligence on clients with questionable backgrounds. The revelations about Deutsche Bank’s handling of Epstein’s accounts underscore the risks banks take when prioritizing profits over compliance. Epstein’s arrest and death didn’t just end his story—they exposed systemic failures at one of the world’s largest banks.
