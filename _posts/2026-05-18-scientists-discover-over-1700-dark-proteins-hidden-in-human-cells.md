A team of researchers has just uncovered a massive blind spot in human biology. By digging into RNA that scientists used to ignore, they found 1,700 proteins never seen before. These 'dark' proteins were produced by RNA segments the genome once labeled as junk. The discovery rewrites part of how cells work and could explain why some diseases stay hidden for decades.\n\nThe breakthrough came from [ENCODE Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ENCODE) researchers who analyzed RNA from 20 different human tissues. They focused on small RNA molecules that don’t code for traditional proteins, but instead create tiny, overlooked proteins. Most of these proteins are tiny—just 50 to 100 amino acids long—but they play roles scientists are only starting to understand.\n\n## Why these proteins stayed hidden for so long
Until recently, scientists thought 98% of human DNA didn’t do anything useful. They called most of it 'junk DNA' because it didn’t produce proteins like the rest of our genes. But new tools let researchers peer deeper into this genetic no man’s land. They found that some of that 'junk' RNA actually makes proteins after all. These proteins don’t show up in standard protein databases, which is why they’ve flown under the radar for years.\n\nThe ENCODE team used a technique called ribosome profiling to catch these proteins in the act. Ribosomes are the cell’s protein factories, and this method lets scientists see which RNA molecules are actually being read to make proteins. When they ran the test on human tissues, the numbers shot up unexpectedly. Instead of a few dozen new proteins, they found 1,700.\n\n## What these proteins might do
No one knows yet what most of these proteins do. But early clues suggest they’re involved in cell signaling, immune responses, and even brain function. Some may act like tiny switches that turn other genes on or off. Others could be involved in diseases we don’t yet understand. For example, a few dark proteins were only found in cancer cells, raising questions about whether they help tumors grow.\n\nThe researchers also spotted dark proteins in brain tissue linked to memory and learning. If these proteins turn out to be key regulators, they could become targets for new drugs. Right now, scientists are scrambling to study them in labs, but it’ll take years to figure out their real jobs.\n\n## Next steps for science
The team published their findings in *Nature*, and now other labs are racing to confirm the results. They’re using CRISPR gene editing to delete some of these proteins in cells and see what breaks. Others are looking at how these proteins change in diseases like Alzheimer’s or diabetes.\n\nThis isn’t the first time 'junk DNA' has surprised scientists. Earlier discoveries like microRNAs and lncRNAs rewrote textbooks. These dark proteins might do the same. The big difference now is that technology finally lets us see them clearly. Soon, they could lead to new treatments, diagnostic tests, or even explanations for why some people get sick while others don’t.\n\nFor now, the discovery is a reminder that the human genome still has secrets left to uncover. Every time scientists think they’ve mapped it all, something new pops up.
