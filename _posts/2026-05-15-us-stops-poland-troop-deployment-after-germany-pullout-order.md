WASHINGTON — The Pentagon has halted the planned deployment of 4,000 U.S. troops to Poland, citing orders to reduce forces in Europe following President Donald Trump’s directive to withdraw 5,000 soldiers from Germany. The abrupt policy shift has drawn criticism from both Democratic and Republican lawmakers, who warn it sends a damaging message to allies amid rising global tensions.

General Christopher LaNeve, acting chief of staff of the U.S. Army, told the House Armed Services Committee on Saturday that the head of U.S. European Command had received instructions to reduce forces in the region. LaNeve said it "made the most sense" for the 2nd Armored Brigade Combat Team to delay its deployment to Europe but provided no further rationale. Secretary of the Army Dan Driscoll confirmed the halt occurred "a couple of days ago."

## Bipartisan backlash intensifies in Congress

Lawmakers from both parties expressed alarm over the decision. Representative Joe Courtney, a Democrat from Connecticut, called the move a "horrible message" to NATO allies, particularly Poland, which has sought stronger U.S. military presence to counter Russian threats. Republican lawmakers, including Senate Armed Services Committee member Thom Tillis, also criticized the abrupt withdrawal, calling it disruptive to military planning and alliance cohesion.

The Trump administration has increasingly questioned the value of maintaining large U.S. troop deployments in Europe, arguing that allies should shoulder more defense responsibilities. However, critics argue the withdrawal weakens deterrence against Russian aggression and undermines NATO commitments. Poland has been a key ally in Eastern Europe, hosting a permanent U.S. military base since 2020.

The Pentagon’s decision to pause the Poland deployment comes as Defense Secretary Mark Esper faces mounting pressure to justify the troop reductions. Esper has previously stated that the move is part of a broader strategy to realign U.S. forces globally, though specifics remain unclear. The administration has not detailed how the withdrawn troops from Germany will be reassigned, raising concerns about operational gaps in Europe.

NATO Secretary General Jens Stoltenberg has emphasized the alliance’s unity amid the U.S. policy shifts, but internal divisions are evident. Poland’s government has not publicly commented on the halted deployment but has repeatedly called for expanded U.S. military support to counter Russian military buildup near its borders.

The policy reversal follows Trump’s May 2025 announcement that he would reduce U.S. troop levels in Germany by nearly 25%, a move that drew sharp rebuke from European leaders. The administration has framed the cuts as a cost-saving measure, though critics argue they weaken U.S. influence in the region. The halt to the Poland deployment suggests further disruptions to military planning are likely as the Pentagon scrambles to implement the changes.
