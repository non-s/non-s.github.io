Taipei, Taiwan — Taiwan reaffirmed its sovereignty and independence on Saturday, saying the self-governing island will maintain the "cross-strait status quo" while deepening ties with the United States following remarks by former President Donald Trump. The statement came after Trump, during an interview with Fox News, said he did not support Taiwanese independence during his meetings with Chinese leader Xi Jinping in Beijing.

Taiwan’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs issued a statement on Saturday clarifying that the island is "sovereign and independent," addressing concerns raised by Trump’s comments. The government emphasized it will not officially declare independence from China, adhering to a long-standing policy that avoids provoking Beijing while asserting its autonomy. The announcement followed Trump’s remarks in an interview aired Saturday, where he stated, "I’m not looking to have somebody go independent," referring to Taiwan.

## Trump’s stance on Taiwan draws sharp response

Trump’s comments have reignited tensions over Taiwan’s political status, a decades-old dispute rooted in the Chinese civil war that ended in 1949. While the U.S. does not formally recognize Taiwan as an independent nation, Washington has maintained a policy of strategic ambiguity, supplying arms and signaling potential military support in the event of a Chinese attack. Successive U.S. administrations have upheld this approach, balancing relations with both Taipei and Beijing.

Taiwan’s government stressed it will continue to strengthen its relationship with the U.S. amid the uncertainty sparked by Trump’s remarks. The island has increasingly sought diplomatic and military support from Washington to counterbalance China’s growing pressure, including military drills and diplomatic isolation campaigns. Taiwan’s President Tsai Ing-wen [Tsai Ing-wen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsai_Ing-wen) has repeatedly vowed to defend the island’s democracy and sovereignty, despite Beijing’s insistence that Taiwan is part of its territory.

## China reacts to U.S. and Taiwan statements

China, which claims Taiwan as part of its territory, has not yet responded publicly to Taiwan’s declaration or Trump’s comments. Beijing has long opposed any moves that suggest Taiwanese independence, including U.S. arms sales to Taipei. The Chinese government has warned that it reserves the right to use force to reunify Taiwan with the mainland if necessary.

Trump’s visit to China, his first since leaving office, included meetings with Xi Jinping [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping) focused on trade and global security. While Trump’s comments on Taiwan were brief, they have drawn attention from policymakers in Taipei and Washington, raising questions about the future of U.S.-Taiwan relations. The U.S. State Department has not issued an immediate response to Trump’s remarks, maintaining its long-standing policy of supporting Taiwan without recognizing its independence.

Analysts suggest Taiwan’s statement is a calculated move to assert its autonomy while avoiding direct confrontation with Beijing. The island’s leadership faces pressure from both domestic voters and international allies to stand firm against China’s claims. Meanwhile, Trump’s comments could signal a shift in U.S. policy toward Taiwan, though his exact intentions remain unclear.

The coming months may bring further diplomatic maneuvers as Taiwan prepares for its next presidential election in 2024. Observers will watch closely to see whether the U.S. and China adjust their approaches to the Taiwan issue, potentially altering the delicate balance of power in the region.
