UK companies are slamming the brakes on hiring and fresh investment as the fallout from the war between the US, Israel and Iran tightens its grip on British boardrooms. The latest data shows job vacancies in April dropped 7.7% month-on-month, the sharpest single-month fall since the pandemic shock in 2020. Employers say they can’t keep up with soaring energy, transport and raw material costs triggered by the conflict, forcing them to freeze expansion plans and even cut existing roles. The British Chambers of Commerce [British Chambers of Commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Chambers_of_Commerce) surveyed 5,200 firms and found 63% had delayed or cancelled investment projects in the last three months alone. That’s up from 51% in January, when the war first flared up after Iran launched a major strike on Israel. Businesses now admit they’re playing defence, not offence, when it comes to growth. ‘Costs have jumped 18% for manufacturers and 14% for service firms since January,’ says Shevaun Haviland, director-general of the British Chambers of Commerce. ‘We’re seeing companies hold back on everything from new factory lines to office expansions because the numbers no longer add up.’ Even profitable firms that weathered the pandemic and energy crisis are now battening down the hatches. ‘We’ve got a 12-month freeze on all new hires and capital spending,’ says Mark Smith, owner of a 200-employee engineering firm in the Midlands. ‘Our biggest customer in Germany just told us they’re cutting orders by 30% because their own costs are spiralling. If they sneeze, we catch a cold.’ The Bank of England [Bank of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_England) warned last week that the war’s economic drag could last well into 2025, keeping inflation stubbornly high and choking demand. Its latest forecast shows UK growth stalling at just 0.25% this year, half what it predicted in February. Politicians in Westminster are scrambling to respond. Chancellor Rachel Reeves [Rachel Reeves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rachel_Reeves) has promised ‘targeted support’ for energy-intensive industries, but details are thin. Labour MPs are pushing for a temporary freeze on business rates and VAT cuts on fuel to ease the squeeze. ‘We’re caught between a rock and a hard place,’ says Darren Jones, Labour’s shadow business secretary. ‘Every pound we take from taxpayers to help businesses comes with a political cost, but doing nothing risks deeper job losses.’ The retail sector is feeling it first. High street chains like Next and Marks & Spencer have already warned of weaker profits this year, blaming higher shipping costs and falling footfall. Online retailers are cutting back too—Amazon UK recently paused plans to open three new fulfilment centres, cancelling 1,200 planned jobs. The picture isn’t uniform, though. Some export-focused firms in renewables and defence are thriving as global demand for non-Russian energy tech and military hardware surges. ‘We’re actually hiring 50 engineers to meet US orders for offshore wind components,’ says a spokesperson for a Scottish renewables firm. ‘The war’s made our sector hot.’ But those bright spots are rare. Most small businesses, especially in manufacturing and hospitality, are cutting shifts, delaying supplier payments, or shutting down entirely. The Federation of Small Businesses [Federation of Small Businesses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation_of_Small_Businesses) reports that one in eight UK small firms now owes money to HMRC as they struggle to meet tax bills on time. What happens next depends on how long the war lasts and whether the US-Israel campaign widens. If the conflict drags on, economists expect business investment to drop by 5% this year, the steepest fall since the financial crisis. Even if the fighting stops tomorrow, rebuilding supply chains and restoring confidence will take months. For now, UK plc is in a holding pattern. ‘We’re not hiring, we’re not investing, and we’re not taking on new risks,’ says Smith. ‘That’s not how you build a business. But when the world’s on fire, you don’t build anything.’
