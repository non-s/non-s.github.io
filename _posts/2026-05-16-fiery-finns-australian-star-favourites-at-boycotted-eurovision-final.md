Finland’s [Käärijä](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4%C3%A4rij%C3%A4) and Australia’s [Electric Fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_Fields) are the betting favorites to win Saturday’s Eurovision 2024 grand final in Malmö, Sweden. Both acts have drawn strong praise for their performances, but the contest is overshadowed by an unprecedented boycott over Israel’s inclusion after the country’s ongoing conflict in Gaza.

The Finnish duo, known for their high-energy violin and vocals, and Australia’s electric pop duo, praised for their harmonies and stage presence, headline a field of 26 finalists vying for the trophy. Eurovision organizers have faced mounting pressure from artists and fans demanding Israel’s exclusion due to its military actions in Gaza, where more than 35,000 Palestinians have been killed since October, according to Gaza health officials.

## Global spotlight on boycott ahead of final

The boycott movement has gained traction across Europe, with multiple countries calling for Israel’s removal from the competition. Iceland’s broadcaster RÚV announced it would not air the final in solidarity with the boycott, while several artists from other nations have withdrawn support or voiced criticism. The European Broadcasting Union (EBU), which runs Eurovision, has defended Israel’s participation, stating the contest is a non-political event.

Electric Fields, representing Australia—a first-time full participant in the final—has emerged as a fan favorite with their anthemic pop sound. Their song “One Milk” has amassed millions of streams online, boosting their odds with bookmakers. Meanwhile, Käärijä’s rap-metal infused track “Cha Cha Cha” has dominated Finnish airwaves and TikTok trends, making it a viral contender.

## Security concerns and protests loom over Malmö

Malmö, a coastal city in southern Sweden, is preparing for a high-security final amid concerns over potential protests. Swedish police have increased patrols and set up exclusion zones around the Malmö Arena, where the event will be held. Authorities are monitoring social media for calls to disrupt the show, following threats from pro-Palestinian groups.

Eurovision’s executive supervisor Martin Österdahl told reporters the EBU remains committed to hosting a safe and inclusive event. “Our priority is to ensure every artist and viewer can participate without fear,” he said. Still, some delegations have reportedly considered withdrawing ahead of the broadcast.

The final will air live in over 160 countries, with an expected global audience of 160 million. Viewers can watch via broadcasters like the BBC, France 2, and SVT, or streaming platforms including YouTube and TikTok. This year’s contest has seen a record number of countries submitting songs, reflecting Eurovision’s enduring global appeal despite the controversy.

As the boycott movement grows, the contest’s reputation as a celebration of music and unity faces its biggest test in years. Whether the final delivers a moment of harmony or further division remains to be seen.
