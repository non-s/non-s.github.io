The physics and astronomy preprint server [arXiv](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv) announced Thursday that submissions containing inappropriate AI-generated content will face a one-year ban and a permanent requirement for future publications to undergo peer review before being hosted. The move underscores growing concerns about the proliferation of AI slop—low-quality, unchecked AI output—contaminating academic literature before editors or reviewers even see it.

Thomas Dietterich, a prominent figure in the decision, outlined the policy on social media, emphasizing the server’s role in maintaining scientific integrity. The new rules apply to any submission where AI tools generate fake citations, unedited responses, or nonsensical diagrams without human oversight. ArXiv’s moderators will enforce the ban retroactively, meaning existing accounts linked to such submissions could face penalties.

## Why arXiv is cracking down on AI slop now

AI-generated content has increasingly infiltrated peer-reviewed journals, often slipping past editors and reviewers due to the sheer volume of submissions. Unlike peer-reviewed outlets, arXiv operates as a preprint server, meaning it hosts papers before they undergo formal review. This makes it particularly vulnerable to AI slop, which can flood the platform with misleading or low-quality material. The new policy is an attempt to stem the tide before peer review or journals even get involved.

The server’s administrators argue that unchecked AI output risks eroding trust in scientific communication. Fake citations and nonsensical diagrams not only waste reviewers’ time but also mislead other researchers building on flawed foundations. By imposing stricter rules, arXiv aims to protect the credibility of its repository and the fields it serves.

## Peer review requirement targets repeat offenders

Under the new policy, offenders will face a one-year ban from submitting to arXiv and will be required to have all future publications peer-reviewed before being hosted. This dual penalty reflects the severity of the issue and the server’s commitment to upholding standards. The peer review requirement is a significant shift, as arXiv traditionally hosts papers without prior review, relying instead on moderators to filter out obvious issues.

The policy also signals a broader trend in academia, where institutions and journals are grappling with the rise of AI-generated content. Some journals have already begun retracting papers found to contain AI slop, while others are tightening their submission guidelines. ArXiv’s move could set a precedent for other preprint servers to follow.

## Broader implications for scientific publishing

The crackdown on AI slop at arXiv reflects a growing recognition that AI tools, while powerful, require human oversight to ensure accuracy and reliability. The server’s decision highlights the challenges of moderating AI-generated content in a landscape where tools like large language models can produce convincing but incorrect outputs. It also raises questions about how other preprint servers and journals will adapt to this new reality.

For researchers, the policy serves as a reminder to scrutinize their work before submission, even when using AI assistance. The move could also accelerate the development of tools to detect AI-generated content, further complicating the process of publishing in scientific fields. Ultimately, arXiv’s policy is a step toward preserving the integrity of scientific communication in an era of AI proliferation.

What happens next remains unclear, but the server’s decision sends a strong message to the scientific community about the risks of unchecked AI use.
