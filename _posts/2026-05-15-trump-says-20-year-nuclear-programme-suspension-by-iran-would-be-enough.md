US President Donald Trump [on Friday](https://www.whitehouse.gov) set a fresh condition for reviving nuclear talks with Iran, demanding Tehran suspend uranium enrichment and remove nuclear fuel for two decades. Speaking to reporters outside the White House, Trump framed the 20-year suspension as the minimum acceptable commitment from Iran to prove its seriousness about rejoining a revived nuclear accord.

The demand marks a sharp departure from previous diplomatic efforts led by his administration. During his presidency, Trump withdrew the US from the 2015 [Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_Comprehensive_Plan_of Action), citing concerns over weak enforcement and sunset clauses. His latest stance could further complicate indirect negotiations between Washington and Tehran, which have stalled for months amid escalating regional tensions.

Trump’s comments came as his administration intensifies pressure on Iran through sanctions and diplomatic isolation. The US has repeatedly accused Tehran of expanding its nuclear program beyond JCPOA limits, while Iran insists its activities remain within legal boundaries. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) has reported increased uranium enrichment levels, raising concerns about Iran’s nuclear advancements.

## Iran rejects new demands

Iranian officials quickly dismissed Trump’s latest conditions as unrealistic and politically motivated. Foreign Ministry spokesman Nasser Kanaani said Tehran would not accept preconditions that undermine its sovereignty or violate the principles of any future agreement. He reiterated Iran’s position that all sanctions must be lifted before any new talks can proceed.

The stalemate threatens to derail months of indirect negotiations in Doha and Vienna, where European mediators have struggled to bridge gaps between Washington and Tehran. Analysts warn the impasse could push Iran closer to nuclear threshold capability, while the US risks losing leverage as global attention shifts to other crises.

## Regional allies react cautiously

Saudi Arabia and Israel, both opposed to a revived JCPOA, have cautiously welcomed Trump’s tougher stance. Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has long argued that Iran’s nuclear program poses an existential threat to the region. Meanwhile, European diplomats continue urging restraint, warning that further escalation could trigger a military or cyber confrontation.

Trump’s demand for a 20-year freeze contrasts with the original JCPOA, which imposed temporary restrictions on Iran’s nuclear activities. The 2015 deal allowed enrichment limits to gradually expire, a provision critics say enabled Tehran to accelerate its program after the US withdrawal. Iran currently enriches uranium up to 60% purity, far exceeding JCPOA limits and approaching weapons-grade levels.

## What happens next

With Trump’s demand now part of the public negotiating framework, the path to a revived deal appears narrower than ever. The US State Department has not indicated whether it will soften its position, while Iran shows no signs of backing down. Analysts predict the standoff could persist through the US presidential election cycle, with both sides unlikely to make concessions ahead of November’s vote.
