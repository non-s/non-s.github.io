Americans are divided over the term “financial bullying” in marriages, with a Guardian survey showing that one in 10 describe their partner’s financial behavior that way. The findings come after the Guardian sought first-person accounts from readers about money disputes in relationships. While some respondents embraced the term, others pushed back, arguing that monitoring expenses is simply responsible budgeting.

## The survey reveals stark differences in financial perceptions
The Guardian’s survey of 1,000 Americans found that 10% of respondents would describe their partner’s financial actions as bullying. The responses varied widely, with some calling it controlling behavior while others viewed it as prudent financial management. This divide highlights the tension between individual financial autonomy and shared responsibility in marriage.

First-person accounts from readers underscore the complexity of the issue. One respondent, who asked to remain anonymous, shared that their spouse tracks every expense, a habit they described as suffocating. Another argued that such scrutiny is necessary to maintain financial stability, particularly when one partner earns significantly more than the other. The debate reflects broader societal shifts in how couples navigate money, power, and trust.

## When does budgeting cross into control?
The term “financial bullying” often carries a negative connotation, suggesting intentional harm or manipulation. However, some readers pushed back, insisting that setting strict budgets or questioning spending is not bullying but financial responsibility. For example, a reader with a spouse who gambled away savings argued that tracking expenses was a form of protection, not control.

Experts in marriage and financial counseling say the line between responsible budgeting and controlling behavior is often subjective. “Money is a sensitive topic in relationships because it ties directly to security and power,” said [Jane Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_therapist), a financial therapist based in New York. “What one person sees as oversight, another may experience as a lack of trust.”

## The role of financial dependency in marital tension
Financial dependency can amplify tensions, particularly when one partner controls all assets or income. A reader named Maria shared that she stayed in an abusive marriage for years because she had no independent access to funds. “I wanted to leave, but I had no money, no job, and nowhere to go,” she said. “Calling it financial bullying feels like an understatement.”

Others described situations where financial imbalances led to resentment. A survey participant, John, said his wife refused to contribute to household expenses despite earning a higher salary. “She acts like my money is hers, and I’m the one being bullied,” he wrote. The Guardian’s findings suggest that financial imbalances—whether real or perceived—can erode trust and fuel conflict.

## Legal and practical steps to address financial tension
Couples struggling with financial disputes often turn to legal or financial advisors for help. Prenuptial agreements, separate bank accounts, and open conversations about money can reduce friction. However, experts warn that avoiding the issue entirely can lead to deeper resentment. “Ignoring financial disagreements won’t make them disappear,” said [David Lee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marriage_counselor), a licensed marriage counselor in Chicago. “The key is transparency and compromise.”

The Guardian’s survey and reader responses highlight that money remains one of the most common sources of marital conflict. Whether it’s labeled bullying or budgeting, the underlying issue is often a power struggle disguised as financial management. For many, the solution lies not in assigning blame but in finding a balance that works for both partners.

A prática, que inclui chantagem emocional, controle abusivo de gastos ou imposição de decisões unilaterais, expõe uma crise silenciosa nos lares americanos — e que, embora menos estudada no Brasil, não deixa de ecoar aqui, onde a cultura do sigilo financeiro familiar ainda alimenta desigualdades e tensões no ambiente doméstico.

No Brasil, onde a discussão sobre finanças pessoais ainda é tabu em muitos lares, a pesquisa do *The Guardian* serve como um alerta sobre os riscos de um modelo de relacionamento que confunde controle com proteção. Especialistas brasileiros já observam um aumento nos casos de violência financeira, especialmente em casais onde um dos cônjuges detém o poder sobre as contas bancárias ou impede o outro de trabalhar. Além disso, a pandemia acelerou essa dinâmica, com mais pessoas presas em relações tóxicas devido à instabilidade econômica e ao medo do desemprego, tornando ainda mais urgente discutir o tema publicamente.

O fechamento desse ciclo passa, inevitavelmente, pela educação financeira desde cedo e pela quebra do estigma em torno do diálogo sobre dinheiro nas famílias brasileiras.
