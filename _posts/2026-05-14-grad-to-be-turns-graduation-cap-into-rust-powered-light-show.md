Eric Park, a Purdue University computer science student set to graduate this Friday, built a graduation cap that lights up when he swings his tassel to the left. He published a blog post this week explaining the project, which started as a joke about setting his mortarboard on fire if the tassel moved a certain way. Turns out, universities like Purdue rent out gowns and caps for commencement ceremonies, so setting anything ablaze isn’t an option—even if the idea sounds fun in a dorm room brainstorm.

Park’s cap uses a tiny microcontroller, a few LEDs, and Rust—a programming language known for its speed and safety—to detect when the tassel swings left. The code triggers the lights to flash in a pattern, turning a simple graduation accessory into a small, interactive light show. He wired everything under the cap’s stiff brim, hiding the tech so the cap still looks like a traditional mortarboard from a distance.

## How it works: Rust, motion sensors, and hidden LEDs

Park chose Rust for its reliability and performance, two things you want when you’re wiring electronics to a cap that’s about to be tossed in the air. The code runs on a microcontroller tucked inside the cap, paired with an accelerometer to detect the tassel’s movement. When the tassel shifts left, the board sends power to the LEDs, lighting them up for a fraction of a second. The whole setup weighs just a few ounces, so it doesn’t sag or feel awkward while walking.

He tested the build over weeks, adjusting the code to make sure the lights flashed reliably without draining the battery too fast. The final version runs off a small rechargeable battery hidden in the cap’s lining, good for hours of use. Park even wrote a simple web interface so friends could watch the lights sync remotely during the ceremony.

## The inspiration: flaming mortarboard vs. flashing LEDs

Park admits the idea started as a meme in his group chat. Someone joked about rigging a cap to set itself on fire when the tassel moved, turning graduation into a viral TikTok moment. But after checking Purdue’s rules, Park realized rented caps and gowns come with strict no-alteration policies. That killed the fire idea fast. Instead, he leaned into the tech side—something he’s comfortable with as a CS major.

He posted the build online as an open-source project, sharing the code and wiring diagram so others could try it. So far, reactions have been positive, with classmates asking if they can borrow his build or tweak it for their own caps. Park’s cap might inspire more tech-themed graduation stunts, especially as students look for ways to stand out in photos and videos.

## Why this matters beyond the ceremony

Park’s project shows how students are blending tech with tradition in creative, harmless ways. It’s also a real-world example of Rust’s growing role outside big tech—from web servers to tiny embedded systems like this cap. For students, it’s a fun way to showcase skills without breaking rules or risking safety.

Purdue hasn’t commented on the build, but the university’s code of conduct doesn’t prohibit small electronics in caps as long as they’re safe and don’t damage the rented gear. That means Park’s cap is likely to sail through the ceremony without issues.

For now, Park’s just focused on finishing his degree and enjoying commencement. After that? He’s considering open-sourcing more projects or maybe even building a tool to help other grads add tech to their caps. Either way, his mortarboard won’t be set on fire—but it’ll definitely stand out under the stadium lights.
