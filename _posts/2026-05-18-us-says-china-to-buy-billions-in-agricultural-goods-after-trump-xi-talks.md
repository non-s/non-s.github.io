China will buy at least $17 billion worth of American agricultural products every year through 2028 under a new trade agreement announced by the White House after President Donald Trump’s meeting with Chinese leader Xi Jinping in Beijing. The deal covers soybeans, beef, and poultry, and follows earlier commitments made during their October summit in South Korea. China has not yet commented on the announcement, leaving the agreement’s future uncertain despite the White House’s fact sheet detailing the terms.

The White House said China will restart imports of US beef by renewing expired listings for more than 400 production facilities. It will also resume poultry imports from states declared free of avian flu by the US Department of Agriculture. The agreement builds on China’s earlier pledge to buy at least 87 million metric tons of US soybeans, a commitment made during the October summit. These moves aim to ease trade tensions that have simmered since 2018, when the Trump administration imposed tariffs on Chinese goods.

## New trade bodies to manage US-China deals

Trump and Xi also agreed to create two new institutions to oversee trade and investment: the US-China Board of Trade and the US-China Board of Investment. These bodies will handle disputes and set rules for future deals, though details about their structure or powers remain unclear. The announcement comes as both countries seek to stabilize a relationship strained by tariffs, tech bans, and competition over global influence.

The White House framed the deal as a step toward balancing trade, which has long favored China. In 2023, the US trade deficit with China hit $279 billion, the highest since 2019. American farmers have pushed for expanded access to Chinese markets, arguing their products are often shut out by non-tariff barriers like lengthy inspections or sudden policy shifts.

## China silent for now

China has not publicly responded to the White House’s announcement, a pattern seen in past trade talks where Beijing delays confirmation or adds conditions. During Trump’s first term, China often made similar pledges that were later scaled back or ignored. Analysts say the latest deal could face similar hurdles unless both sides agree on enforcement mechanisms.

The broader implications depend on whether this deal sticks. If China follows through, it could ease some pressure on US farmers hit by falling prices and foreign competition. But if the agreement collapses like past ones, it may reinforce skepticism about China’s willingness to open its markets. For now, the White House is treating the announcement as progress, even without Beijing’s official response.
