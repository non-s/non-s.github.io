Israeli naval commandos seized control of a Gaza-bound flotilla early Sunday, activists said, after live broadcasts showed troops rappelling from helicopters onto several ships trying to breach Israel’s long-standing maritime blockade. Footage from the Global Sumud flotilla showed masked commandos boarding boats in darkness, with activists shouting over loudspeakers and waving phones, capturing the confrontation as it unfolded.

The flotilla, which organizers say includes medical aid and construction supplies for Gaza, was intercepted roughly 60 nautical miles northwest of Cyprus—well outside Gaza’s territorial waters but still within the eastern Mediterranean. Israel’s military has not yet confirmed the operation, but it typically enforces a blockade around Gaza [since 2007](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockade_of_the_Gaza_Strip) to restrict the flow of weapons and dual-use goods into the Palestinian territory, which Hamas [controls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas).

## Activists claim ships were in international waters

Organizers of the Global Sumud flotilla say their vessels were in international waters when commandos boarded them. The group, which has run similar missions in the past, insists the aid is purely humanitarian and accuses Israel of violating maritime law. Cyprus, which has a maritime boundary agreement with Israel, has not commented on the incident. Israel maintains its blockade is legal under international law and necessary for security.

Earlier this year, a smaller Gaza aid flotilla was intercepted by Israeli forces near the same area. That incident drew international criticism but no major escalation. This latest operation comes as regional tensions remain high following the October 7 attacks by Hamas and Israel’s subsequent military campaign in Gaza, which has killed over 35,000 people according to Gaza health officials.

## What happens next?

The flotilla’s organizers say they expect the ships and activists to be taken to an Israeli port, likely Ashdod, where they will be questioned and the aid inspected. Israel has historically allowed some humanitarian aid into Gaza through land crossings but has repeatedly warned against attempts to bypass its maritime restrictions. The military has said it will handle the situation according to its standard procedures for such incidents. Activists have vowed to challenge the interception in international courts, calling it a violation of freedom of navigation. The outcome could hinge on whether the ships were truly in international waters and whether their cargo violates Israel’s blockade rules.

For now, the focus remains on the detained activists and the fate of the aid shipments. The flotilla’s organizers have not yet released a full list of participants or the exact contents of the cargo, though they’ve emphasized the mission’s humanitarian goals.
