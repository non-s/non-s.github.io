Russian President Vladimir Putin landed in Beijing on Monday for a two-day visit with his Chinese counterpart Xi Jinping, just four days after former U.S. President Donald Trump wrapped up his own high-profile summit with Xi. The timing of Putin’s trip—set for Tuesday and Wednesday—isn’t a coincidence. It’s a clear signal that Beijing and Moscow are keeping their relationship close even as the U.S. tries to pull China away from Russia over the war in Ukraine.

Xi and Putin exchanged “congratulatory letters” on Sunday, with the Chinese leader praising how their countries’ cooperation has “continuously deepened and solidified” over the past three decades. State media in both countries marked the occasion, noting that 2024 marks the 30th anniversary of their strategic partnership. The two leaders have met nearly 40 times since 2013, and their governments have grown closer on trade, military drills, and energy deals—especially since Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022.

## Why the West is watching closely
Western governments, especially in Washington and European capitals, have grown more uneasy about China’s ties to Russia as the war in Ukraine drags on. The U.S. has repeatedly warned Beijing against providing military or economic support to Moscow, but China has refused to condemn the invasion. Instead, Beijing has called for “peace talks” while deepening its trade with Russia, buying up discounted Russian oil and selling Beijing-made goods to Moscow to help offset Western sanctions.

China’s role here isn’t neutral. It’s the biggest buyer of Russian fossil fuels since the war started, and its exports to Russia—everything from cars to smartphones—have surged. Last year, trade between the two countries hit a record $240 billion, up 59% from 2022. That’s a problem for the West because it helps fund Putin’s war machine. The U.S. has sanctioned Chinese banks and firms for aiding Russia’s military, but Beijing keeps finding ways around them.

## What Putin and Xi will likely discuss
Putin’s visit isn’t just about photo ops. The two leaders are expected to sign new energy deals, expand military cooperation, and push back against U.S. influence in Asia and Europe. Russia wants China’s help to blunt Western pressure, while China gets access to Russian oil, gas, and advanced weapons tech. They’ll also coordinate on how to handle Trump if he wins back the White House in November. Trump’s recent China trip—where he pushed Xi to stop aiding Russia—didn’t go as planned. Xi called the talks “candid and constructive,” but there’s no sign China’s backing off.

This week’s meetings could also touch on North Korea. Both Beijing and Moscow have resisted tougher U.N. sanctions on Pyongyang, even as Kim Jong-un’s regime ramps up missile tests. Putin visited North Korea in June, where he and Kim signed a new military pact. China’s kept its distance publicly, but privately, it’s likely discussing how to avoid upsetting either ally.

## What happens next
Putin’s trip is just the latest in a long line of high-level exchanges. After this, expect more joint military drills, energy projects, and diplomatic statements blaming the West for global instability. For the U.S., the challenge is clear: China’s not backing down from Russia, and Trump’s return to power might not change that. Europe’s already bracing for more Chinese goods flooding markets, while Ukraine’s allies are trying to figure out how to cut off Russia’s lifelines without pushing Beijing further into Moscow’s arms.

The bigger question is whether this partnership can last. China’s economy is slowing, and Russia’s is shrinking under sanctions. But so far, both sides see each other as the only major powers willing to openly defy Washington. Until that changes, Xi and Putin will keep meeting—and the West will keep worrying.
