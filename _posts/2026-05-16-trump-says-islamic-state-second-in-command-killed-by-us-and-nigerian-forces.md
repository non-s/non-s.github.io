> 📰 **Continuing coverage:** [US, Nigeria kill Islamic State's second-in-command in Nigeria](/world/2026/05/16/us-and-nigeria-claim-killing-of-islamic-state-second-in-command/)

U.S. and Nigerian forces killed Abu-Bilal al-Minuki, a top leader of the [Islamic State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant), in a high-stakes counterterrorism operation late Friday, President Donald Trump announced on [Truth Social](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_Social). Al-Minuki, identified as the group’s second-in-command, was described by Trump as the ‘most active terrorist in the world.’ The operation was conducted under the president’s direct orders and involved careful coordination between American and Nigerian military units.

The mission unfolded in a remote region of Nigeria, where al-Minuki was reportedly hiding. Trump characterized the operation as ‘very complex’ and praised the ‘brave American forces and the Armed Forces of Nigeria’ for flawlessly executing the plan. The U.S. has not confirmed the exact location or the specific units involved, but the operation highlights growing counterterrorism cooperation between Washington and Abuja.

## Islamic State’s leadership under pressure

The killing of al-Minuki marks another significant blow to the Islamic State’s global network, which has been steadily degraded since the collapse of its self-declared caliphate in 2019. Intelligence agencies have tracked al-Minuki’s movements for years, noting his role in coordinating attacks, fundraising and recruitment across Africa, the Middle East and Europe. His elimination is expected to disrupt the group’s operations, though officials warn that Islamic State affiliates remain active in several regions.

Trump’s announcement came hours after the operation’s completion, signaling a rare public acknowledgment of a covert military mission. Such disclosures are uncommon, as the U.S. typically avoids confirming counterterrorism operations to protect operational details. Nigerian authorities have not yet issued a formal statement, but local media reported heavy military activity in the area where the strike occurred.

## Broader implications for counterterrorism efforts

The joint operation underscores the increasing collaboration between the U.S. and African nations in combating extremist groups. Nigeria, facing persistent threats from [Boko Haram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boko_Haram) and Islamic State’s West Africa Province, has relied on U.S. intelligence and logistical support in recent years. The success of this mission could strengthen military ties between the two countries and bolster efforts to dismantle regional terror networks.

Analysts caution, however, that the death of a single leader rarely dismantles an entire organization. Islamic State affiliates in the Sahel, Somalia and Mozambique continue to pose threats, and the group has repeatedly demonstrated resilience through decentralized leadership. The U.S. and its allies are expected to maintain pressure on remaining cells, particularly in areas with weak governance.

The operation also raises questions about the future of U.S. counterterrorism strategy in Africa. While the Trump administration has emphasized a ‘maximum pressure’ approach, President Joe Biden’s policies have shifted toward a more measured engagement. The killing of al-Minuki may reignite debates over the effectiveness of targeted strikes versus broader stabilization efforts in conflict zones.

For now, the focus remains on verifying al-Minuki’s death and assessing the impact on Islamic State’s operations. Intelligence agencies will review captured materials and interrogate any surviving operatives to determine the extent of the disruption. The operation serves as a reminder of the persistent threat posed by extremist groups and the ongoing need for vigilance.
