A Mexican factory that recycles hazardous steel dust imported from the U.S. has agreed to relocate its most polluting operations after an investigation uncovered dangerous levels of heavy metals in surrounding neighborhoods. Zinc Nacional [Zinc Nacional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_Nacional), based in the Monterrey metropolitan area, processes toxic waste from American steel mills to recover zinc. But reporting by the Guardian and Mexico’s Quinto Elemento Lab found that nearby communities face exposure to lead, arsenic, and cadmium—metals tied to serious health problems like cancer and neurological damage. The company now says it will move the worst-offending parts of its operation away from residential areas within months, though it hasn’t specified a timeline or location yet.

## Why this factory matters to the US and Mexico
The factory’s role highlights a quiet but growing trade in hazardous waste between the two countries. U.S. steel mills generate millions of tons of toxic dust each year, much of it laced with heavy metals. Instead of paying to dispose of it domestically, many companies ship it to Mexico, where regulations are looser and enforcement is weaker. Zinc Nacional has been a key player in this market for decades, turning that waste into reusable zinc while profiting from lower costs. But the environmental cost has landed on nearby families, many of whom say they’ve dealt with unexplained illnesses for years.

Residents in the Santa Catarina neighborhood, just a few hundred yards from the factory, describe a persistent smell of chemicals, discolored water, and a spike in respiratory problems. One local, María González, told reporters her granddaughter was diagnosed with lead poisoning at age three—an outcome doctors linked to exposure in the area. "We’ve complained for years," she said. "No one listened until now." Independent tests commissioned by the investigation found soil and water samples contaminated with levels of heavy metals far exceeding Mexican safety limits.

## What the investigation uncovered
The Guardian and Quinto Elemento Lab spent months gathering records, testing samples, and interviewing workers and families. They found that Zinc Nacional’s plant—one of the largest in Latin America—routinely exceeded Mexican environmental standards for emissions and waste disposal. Documents showed the company had been cited multiple times by Mexican environmental authorities for violations, including improper storage of hazardous materials and failure to report pollution incidents. Yet penalties were minimal, and enforcement was inconsistent.

The investigation also revealed how U.S. companies exploit a loophole: as long as waste is classified as "recyclable," it can be shipped to Mexico without the same scrutiny as regular hazardous waste. That’s how tons of steel dust—laden with lead, zinc, and cadmium—ended up in Monterrey. Zinc Nacional’s process recovers some zinc for reuse, but the leftover sludge, called "jarosite," is dumped in unlined pits, where rainwater can leach toxins into the ground. Local activists say the company has long treated the area as a sacrifice zone, prioritizing profits over public health.

## What happens next—and why it’s not over
Zinc Nacional has pledged to relocate its most polluting operations, but critics say that’s not enough. Environmental groups want the entire plant shut down, not just moved. They argue that even if the worst processes leave Monterrey, the remaining operations still pose risks. "Relocating pollution isn’t solving the problem," said Ana Martínez, a spokeswoman for the Mexican environmental group [Greenpeace México](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenpeace). "It’s just pushing it somewhere else."

Mexican authorities have also come under fire for their slow response. While Zinc Nacional’s plant has been operating for decades, regulators only recently started cracking down after public pressure. The company now faces new scrutiny, including potential fines and mandatory cleanups. But activists worry that without stronger laws or cross-border accountability, nothing will change. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency [EPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency) has said it’s reviewing the trade in hazardous waste exports, but no major policy shifts are expected yet.

For the families living near the plant, the promise of relocation brings cautious relief—but no guarantees. Many say they’ll keep pushing for justice, including medical monitoring for those exposed and a full cleanup of contaminated sites. "We’re not against progress," said González. "But progress shouldn’t come at the cost of our children’s health."

O México dará um passo decisivo contra a poluição industrial ao transferir as operações mais tóxicas da Zinc Nacional, uma das maiores produtoras de zinco do país, após uma investigação do *The Guardian* que revelou níveis alarmantes de metais pesados nas proximidades de sua fábrica. A decisão, anunciada nesta semana, chega após denúncias de moradores e ambientalistas sobre contaminação do solo e da água, colocando em xeque a saúde de comunidades inteiras e a falta de fiscalização adequada em uma indústria historicamente negligente com seus resíduos.

O caso ganha ainda mais relevância no Brasil, onde a exploração de minerais e a gestão de resíduos industriais também enfrentam desafios semelhantes. O episódio no México serve como alerta para o risco de se replicar aqui a lógica da impunidade: empresas que externalizam os custos ambientais enquanto lucram com processos poluentes. No Brasil, estados como Minas Gerais e Pará já registraram casos de contaminação por metais pesados em regiões mineradoras, exigindo maior rigor na fiscalização e transparência por parte das autoridades. A transferência da Zinc Nacional, embora seja um avanço, levanta dúvidas sobre o destino final dos resíduos e se haverá de fato uma redução do impacto ambiental ou apenas um deslocamento do problema.

Agora, a pressão recai sobre o governo mexicano — e deve inspirar cobranças similares no Brasil — para garantir que a realocação da Zinc Nacional não seja apenas uma manobra para burlar a lei, mas o início de uma mudança concreta na gestão de resíduos industriais tóxicos.
