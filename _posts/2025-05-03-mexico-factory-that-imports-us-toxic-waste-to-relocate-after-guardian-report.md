Zinc Nacional, a major Mexican recycler of industrial waste, has pledged to relocate the most polluting parts of its plant in the Monterrey metropolitan area after an investigation found high levels of heavy metals in nearby neighborhoods. The factory processes toxic steel dust imported from U.S. steel mills, extracting zinc and other metals for reuse. But the waste it creates—loaded with lead, cadmium and arsenic—has contaminated soil and water in the surrounding communities, according to testing by The Guardian and Mexico’s Quinto Elemento Lab.

The Monterrey plant sits near residential areas and schools, exposing thousands of families to potential health risks. Local residents told reporters they’ve long worried about rashes, respiratory problems and unexplained illnesses they blame on the factory’s operations. Government data obtained by the investigation showed spikes in heavy-metal levels in soil samples taken just a few hundred yards from the plant’s gates.

## How the factory works and why it matters

Zinc Nacional’s facility imports thousands of tons of steel dust each year from U.S. steel producers. This dust, a hazardous byproduct of steelmaking, contains zinc that can be recovered—but also toxic metals that can’t be removed. The company uses high heat and chemicals to separate the zinc, leaving behind a toxic residue that’s stored on-site or sometimes sold to other industries. The investigation found that some of this waste had leaked into the environment, contaminating local water supplies and farmland.

The plant is one of dozens across Mexico that handle hazardous waste imported from the U.S. under a 2004 trade agreement that allows the U.S. to export certain types of toxic waste to neighboring countries for processing. Mexico has weaker environmental enforcement than the U.S., and critics say that makes it a cheaper—and riskier—place to handle America’s hazardous waste. The country imported over 180,000 tons of such waste in 2022 alone, according to Mexican government records.

## What happens next for the Monterrey plant

Zinc Nacional says it will shut down its most polluting operations within 18 months and move them to a new, more isolated site. The company insists it’s already taking steps to reduce emissions, including installing new filters and tightening waste storage rules. But activists and local officials say the move doesn’t go far enough. They’re calling for a full shutdown of the plant, not just a relocation of its dirtiest processes.

Mexico’s environmental agency has launched an inspection of the plant, but critics argue the agency lacks the resources—and political will—to force meaningful change. The agency’s own records show that Zinc Nacional has been cited for violations at least 12 times in the past five years, including for exceeding permitted emission levels and failing to properly dispose of hazardous waste.

## Broader fallout from the investigation

The revelations have sparked outrage in Mexico and put pressure on U.S. officials to tighten rules on waste exports. Environmental groups are pushing for stricter limits on what kinds of hazardous waste can be sent to Mexico and for better tracking of where that waste ends up. Some U.S. lawmakers have also called for a review of the 2004 trade agreement, arguing it puts Mexican communities at risk while letting U.S. companies avoid the cost of proper disposal.

For the families living near the Monterrey plant, the investigation has brought long-overdue attention to their concerns. Maria Rodriguez, a mother of two who lives two blocks from the factory, said she’s been dealing with chronic skin infections for years. ‘My kids used to play outside all the time,’ she said. ‘Now we keep them inside as much as possible.’ Doctors in the area report seeing more cases of lead poisoning in recent years, though they can’t directly link them to the factory without further testing.

The case highlights a growing problem across the U.S.-Mexico border, where lax enforcement and economic pressures have turned Mexican towns into dumping grounds for America’s hazardous waste. With Zinc Nacional’s relocation plans still uncertain, the fight over who bears the cost of industrial pollution is far from over.

## 🇧🇷 Resumo em Português

O México toma uma decisão histórica ao obrigar uma gigante recicladora de zinco a fechar uma de suas fábricas nos Estados Unidos, responsáveis pelos resíduos mais tóxicos do país, após uma investigação do *The Guardian* que revelou níveis alarmantes de metais pesados contaminando comunidades vizinhas. A Zinc Nacional, um dos maiores recicladores de zinco do mundo, com sede em Monterrey, agora precisará transferir suas operações mais poluentes para instalações próprias no México, onde as regulações ambientais são menos rigorosas — um movimento que levanta sérias dúvidas sobre a ética da exportação de poluição.

A decisão chega em um momento crítico para o Brasil, país que enfrenta desafios semelhantes na gestão de resíduos industriais, especialmente em setores como mineração e metalurgia. Enquanto o México age sob pressão internacional, o Brasil ainda debate a implementação de leis mais duras contra a poluição transfronteiriça, embora já tenha sofrido com casos como o rompimento da barragem de Brumadinho, que deixou um rastro de contaminação por metais pesados. A movimentação da Zinc Nacional serve como um alerta para as autoridades brasileiras sobre os riscos de permitir que empresas multinacionais exportem seus passivos ambientais para países com fiscalização mais frágil.

A próxima etapa será observar se o México realmente fiscalizará a nova planta com o mesmo rigor aplicado à norte-americana ou se, como temem ambientalistas, a mudança será apenas um rearranjo da poluição para outro território.
