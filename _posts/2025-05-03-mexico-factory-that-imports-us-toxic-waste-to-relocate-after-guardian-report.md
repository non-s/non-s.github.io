Zinc Nacional, a major Mexican zinc producer, will move what Mexican environmental authorities describe as its most polluting operations after a joint investigation revealed heavy-metal pollution in surrounding neighborhoods. The Monterrey-based company processes toxic steel dust imported from US steel mills at its plant in the city’s northern industrial belt. The dust contains hazardous heavy metals like lead, cadmium, and arsenic, which the plant recovers for reuse in zinc production. 

A recent investigation by The Guardian and Mexico’s Quinto Elemento Lab documented visible plumes of black smoke and fine white dust drifting over nearby homes and schools. Residents said they’d noticed a rise in respiratory illnesses and skin rashes since the plant expanded operations in 2020. Local doctors told reporters they’d seen more children with asthma and adults with chronic bronchitis in the area. 

## How US waste ends up in Mexico

The Monterrey plant is part of a growing industry that imports hazardous waste from the US for recycling or disposal. Under the 1989 Basel Convention, countries can export hazardous waste if the receiving nation agrees, but critics say enforcement is weak. The US isn’t a party to the convention, so it can’t legally block exports. Instead, US steel mills ship their toxic dust to Mexico under permits that allow for "recycling" rather than disposal. 

Zinc Nacional’s facility in Monterrey is one of the largest of its kind in Latin America. The company, which also operates in Texas, processes about 300,000 tons of steel dust annually. Most of it comes from US steel plants in states like Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Indiana. The recovered zinc is sold back to US manufacturers, creating a closed loop that keeps hazardous waste moving across borders. 

Mexico’s environmental regulator, SEMARNAT, inspected the plant in May after receiving complaints. Officials found levels of lead in nearby soil that exceeded national safety limits by nearly 50%. Cadmium levels were three times higher than allowed. Zinc Nacional initially denied wrongdoing, but after the investigation’s findings went public, the company announced it would relocate its dirtiest processes to a new industrial park 20 miles away. 

The company’s statement didn’t specify a timeline but said it would invest $30 million in pollution controls at the new site. Local activists remain skeptical. "Moving the pollution doesn’t make it disappear," said Maria Lopez, a community leader whose family lives two blocks from the plant. "They’re just hiding it where no one’s watching." 

## What happens next

Zinc Nacional’s move won’t shut down the Monterrey plant, but it will shift the most toxic parts of the operation away from dense residential areas. The company plans to keep recycling zinc at the original site, albeit with stricter pollution controls. SEMARNAT has ordered quarterly air and soil tests for the next two years. 

The case has reignited debates about cross-border waste trade. Environmental groups in both countries are pushing for stricter rules. The EPA has said it’s reviewing its hazardous waste export policies but hasn’t announced changes. For now, the Monterrey plant remains a critical link in the supply chain for US steel, even as its toxic footprint shrinks on paper.

O México decidiu fechar e realocar uma fábrica de zinco após uma investigação revelar altos níveis de metais pesados tóxicos em bairros próximos, expondo os riscos da gestão negligente de resíduos industriais. A Zinc Nacional, que importa resíduos tóxicos dos Estados Unidos, será obrigada a interromper suas operações mais poluentes, em um movimento que reacende o debate sobre os impactos ambientais e sociais do comércio ilegal de lixo perigoso na América Latina.

A reportagem do *The Guardian* destacou que a fábrica, localizada no estado de Hidalgo, contaminou o solo e a água com chumbo, cádmio e outros metais pesados, colocando em risco a saúde de milhares de moradores. No Brasil, onde também há um histórico de importação irregular de resíduos e fiscalização frágil, a notícia serve como alerta para os riscos de se repetirem cenas semelhantes, como as registradas em municípios como Paulínia (SP) e Camaçari (BA). Especialistas brasileiros já haviam alertado para a necessidade de endurecer leis e monitorar empresas que lidam com substâncias perigosas, sob pena de novos casos de contaminação e doenças crônicas nas populações afetadas.

A decisão da Zinc Nacional pode ser o primeiro passo para responsabilizar outras indústrias e governos que fecham os olhos para o destino de resíduos tóxicos, mas exigirá fiscalização rigorosa para evitar que a poluição simplesmente se desloque para outras regiões.
