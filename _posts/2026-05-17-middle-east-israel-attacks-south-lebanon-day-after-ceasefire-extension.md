Israel carried out a major series of airstrikes on southern Lebanon early Saturday, striking what it described as Hezbollah infrastructure and military positions. The attacks came less than 24 hours after Israel and Lebanon’s Hezbollah agreed to extend a fragile truce for a fifth time, showing just how thin the line between ceasefire and conflict remains in the region. At least three people were killed in southern Lebanon, according to local health officials, while Israel reported rocket fire from Lebanon hours later, which it said was intercepted by its air defenses. The strikes targeted areas near the border towns of Bint Jbeil and Marjayoun, both hotspots in the months-long exchanges of fire between Israel and Hezbollah following the October 7 Hamas attacks on Israel and the subsequent war in Gaza. Israel’s military said it hit command centers, rocket launch sites, and weapons storage facilities used by Hezbollah, adding that the operation was in response to recent attacks on Israeli towns near the border. Hezbollah confirmed the strikes but did not immediately provide details on casualties or damage. The group has repeatedly warned that any major Israeli operation in Lebanon could lead to a wider conflict, though it has largely abided by the truce terms so far. The ceasefire, brokered by the United States and France in late October, has been repeatedly extended in one-week increments, each time narrowly avoiding a full-scale war. Analysts say both sides are walking a tightrope, trying to avoid another major escalation while maintaining pressure on the other. Israel’s strikes Saturday came just hours after the latest extension was announced, raising questions about how long the fragile calm can hold. The U.S. State Department reiterated its call for restraint on both sides, urging de-escalation after the latest violence. The situation remains volatile, with families on both sides of the border living under constant threat of sudden escalation. The Israeli strikes Saturday were some of the heaviest in weeks, with residents in southern Lebanon reporting explosions shaking the ground early in the morning. Social media videos showed thick plumes of smoke rising over the border areas, and emergency services described damaged homes and power outages in several villages. Israel’s military said it would continue to target Hezbollah positions if attacks on its territory persisted, while Hezbollah vowed to respond if Israel crossed what it called red lines. The back-and-forth highlights how fragile any progress toward peace remains in a region where a single miscalculation can spiral into full-blown war. With Gaza still in flames and tensions simmering across the Middle East, the stakes couldn’t be higher. The next few days will be critical in determining whether the truce can hold or if the cycle of violence will resume.
