The UN climate talks in [Belém, Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bel%C3%A9m) this week don’t have the biggest names on stage—President Trump isn’t there, and neither are the leaders of China, India, or the UK. That’s a first for a COP summit, where heads of state usually show up to give speeches and set the tone. But missing leaders don’t mean the meeting’s pointless. The real work at COP30 is happening behind closed doors, where diplomats and negotiators hash out the rules that will decide how countries cut emissions and report progress over the next decade.

## Why COP still matters without the presidents
The Paris Agreement in 2015 set a goal: keep global warming under 2°C, ideally 1.5°C. But the agreement didn’t spell out how countries had to prove they were doing their part. That’s where COP comes in. Every year, negotiators tweak the rules so that all countries play by the same book. Without Trump at the table, the U.S. is still bound by the rules because they were set in 2015 after he left office. The same goes for China and India—they can’t just ignore the climate talks even if their leaders skip the opening speeches.

## The one rule that could change everything
The biggest fight at COP30 is over a single word: "transparency." Countries want to know exactly how others are cutting emissions, not just vague promises. Right now, some big polluters like China and India report their progress in ways that make it hard to compare. The new rules would force them to use the same measuring system, so scientists and watchdogs can actually check if they’re hitting their targets. If they refuse, they’ll face public pressure—and that’s almost as powerful as a signed law.

Brazil’s role at COP30 is especially messy because its president, [Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva), promised to save the Amazon but is still approving new oil drilling. Environmental groups are furious, but the country’s diplomats are pushing hard for stricter global rules. That’s because Brazil’s economy depends on trade deals that now require climate commitments. If Brazil blocks the transparency rules, it risks losing access to markets that demand stronger environmental standards.

## The activists who won’t let leaders off the hook
Even without presidents on stage, thousands of activists, scientists, and business leaders are in Belém making noise. They’re pushing for two things: faster cuts to fossil fuels and money to help poor countries adapt to climate disasters. The activists know the rules set at COP won’t force countries to act overnight, but they’re betting on public shame to speed things up. Last year, protests at COP28 forced oil-producing nations to agree—after years of resistance—to mention "transitioning away from fossil fuels" in the final text. That small phrase became a rallying cry for investors and cities to ditch coal and oil.

## What happens if COP30 fails
If negotiators can’t agree on transparency rules, the Paris Agreement will stay vague and toothless. Countries could keep making empty promises, and the world would miss its 1.5°C target. But if COP30 succeeds, it’ll set a standard that forces every major polluter to show its work. The U.S. and EU already follow strict reporting rules. If China, India, and Brazil also have to disclose their emissions in real detail, it could finally force them to cut pollution faster.

The summit also decides how much money rich nations like the U.S. and Europe will send to poorer countries to deal with climate damage. At COP28, wealthy nations pledged $300 billion over three years, but only a fraction has actually been delivered. COP30 needs to make those promises real—or the trust between rich and poor countries will collapse entirely.

O Brasil, que sediará a COP30 em novembro, se prepara para um evento decisivo mesmo sem a participação dos Estados Unidos, tradicionalmente um dos principais atores nas negociações climáticas globais. Embora a ausência de Donald Trump possa gerar dúvidas sobre o impacto da cúpula, especialistas ressaltam que o encontro segue como uma peça-chave para definir regras climáticas internacionais e pressionar nações a cumprirem metas mais ambiciosas de redução de emissões.

A COP30 chega em um momento crítico para o Brasil, que enfrenta pressões crescentes por desmatamento na Amazônia e precisa consolidar uma agenda verde para atrair investimentos e credibilidade internacional. Além disso, o país tem a oportunidade de liderar discussões sobre transição energética e financiamento climático, temas que ganharam ainda mais relevância após os recentes recordes de temperatura global. Para os brasileiros, o evento pode significar não apenas um passo rumo à sustentabilidade, mas também a definição de políticas que impactarão diretamente o futuro da maior floresta tropical do mundo e das comunidades que dependem dela.

Caso a COP30 consiga avançar em compromissos concretos, o Brasil poderá não só reafirmar seu papel de protagonista na agenda climática, mas também influenciar outras nações sul-americanas a seguirem o mesmo caminho.
