The annual UN climate talks kick off Monday in Belém, Brazil, but the guest list reads like a who’s-who of no-shows. Only 15 of the world’s 193 heads of state plan to attend, including Brazil’s [Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva), French president [Emmanuel Macron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron), and South Africa’s [Cyril Ramaphosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyril_Ramaphosa). The United States, the planet’s second-biggest carbon emitter, won’t send a delegation after Donald Trump [returned to the White House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidency_of_Donald_Trump_(2025–present)). The UK, Germany, and China also declined invites, leaving negotiators scrambling to fill empty seats with mid-level officials instead. The absence of top leaders isn’t new—COP29 in Azerbaijan saw just 20 heads of state—but the stakes feel higher this year. The world is off track to meet the Paris Agreement’s 1.5°C target, and the summit is supposed to finalize a plan for richer nations to pay poorer ones for climate damages. With the US out, that plan just got harder to pull off.

Belém sits on the edge of the Amazon, where deforestation dropped 50% in the first year of Lula’s new term. But that progress is fragile. Fires in the Amazon surged 146% in September compared to last year, and Brazil’s overall emissions rose 9% in 2024. The city’s hosting role isn’t just symbolic—it’s a test for Lula’s claim that the Amazon can drive green growth. He’s pushing for a $250 billion fund to protect the rainforest, but so far, only Norway and Germany have pledged money. The summit’s agenda includes talks on ending fossil fuel use, protecting oceans, and tripling clean energy by 2030. Yet without the US, Europe, or China at the table, the deals on offer might not add up to much. 

## What COP30 can still accomplish without world leaders
Even with fewer presidents and prime ministers, COP30 isn’t a total wash. The summit is where companies, cities, and states cut side deals that often outpace national policies. Last year, the EU and seven African countries agreed to a $10 billion clean energy partnership during COP29. This year, Brazil wants to rally support for a global pact to end deforestation by 2030, similar to the 2014 New York Declaration. Tech firms like [Microsoft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft) and [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google) are expected to announce new AI tools for tracking carbon emissions in real time. Cities like São Paulo and Jakarta are pushing for stricter air quality rules. These aren’t headline-grabbing moments, but they move the needle faster than slow-moving governments. 

The elephant in the room is climate finance. At COP29, wealthy nations finally agreed to a $300 billion fund to help poor countries cope with disasters and transition to clean energy. But the US, the largest historical emitter, refused to contribute. Without American money, the fund is underfunded by at least $100 billion. The EU, now distracted by its own political crises, hasn’t stepped up either. Brazil is trying to broker a compromise where oil-rich nations like Saudi Arabia and the UAE chip in, but their delegates are arriving late and unprepared. The summit’s host, [Brazil’s environment minister Marina Silva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marina_Silva), called the finance gap a “ticking time bomb.” If no deal is struck, the UN’s climate goals could stall for another year.

The summit’s location adds urgency. The Amazon is nearing a tipping point where it could turn from rainforest to savanna, releasing billions of tons of stored carbon. Scientists warn that if deforestation hits 20-25% of the biome, the collapse becomes irreversible. Lula has slowed the damage, but loggers and ranchers are pushing back. Meanwhile, Brazil’s oil regulator just approved 60 new offshore drilling licenses, directly contradicting Lula’s climate promises. The contradiction isn’t lost on activists. Indigenous groups from the Amazon will stage protests outside the summit, demanding land rights and an end to fossil fuel projects. Their message: words won’t save the forest—action will.

## Where COP30 stands after Trump’s absence
Trump’s decision to skip COP30 isn’t just symbolic—it’s a rejection of the UN process. During his first term, he pulled the US out of the Paris Agreement and rolled back over 100 environmental rules. His return to office in January 2025 dashed hopes for American leadership on climate. The US delegation that does attend will be led by [John Kerry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Kerry), the former secretary of state, but his role is limited to observing, not negotiating. Kerry called the absence “a mistake,” but Trump’s team sees it as a win. 

The vacuum left by the US is being filled by smaller players. The African Union, representing 54 countries, is pushing for a seat at the table, arguing that Africa suffers the worst impacts of climate change despite contributing the least to it. The EU is still sending officials, but its climate ambitions are hamstrung by political infighting and a surge in far-right support. China, the world’s top emitter, is attending but has signaled it won’t make new pledges. That leaves Brazil as the unlikely face of climate progress—or at least as the last hope for a deal. 

What happens next depends on whether negotiators can squeeze out even modest wins. The summit ends on November 22, and the goal is to finalize a “Belém Action Plan” that sets new targets for 2035. But without buy-in from the biggest polluters, the plan risks being ignored. The real test will come in 2026, when the next round of national climate pledges are due. If COP30 fails to deliver, those pledges could be weaker than ever. 

For now, the world will watch Belém—not because the leaders are there, but because the planet can’t afford for them to stay away.

O Brasil recebe neste ano uma das reuniões mais importantes do planeta sobre o futuro da Terra: a COP30, que acontece em Belém, entre 10 e 21 de novembro, promete ser o palco de cobranças e cobranças sem precedentes na luta contra a crise climática.

Pela primeira vez em mais de uma década, a cúpula do clima da ONU não contará com a presença de líderes como Donald Trump ou Recep Tayyip Erdogan, o que levanta dúvidas sobre o peso político do evento. No entanto, especialistas destacam que a COP30 é crucial para o Brasil, não só por sediar o encontro, mas por pressionar o governo a apresentar planos concretos de redução de emissões, especialmente após o desmatamento na Amazônia ter batido recorde em 2024. O evento também deve aprofundar discussões sobre financiamento climático para países em desenvolvimento, um tema sensível para nações como a nossa, que precisam equilibrar crescimento econômico e preservação ambiental.

A expectativa é que a COP30 defina diretrizes para a implementação do Acordo de Paris, mas o verdadeiro desafio será transformar palavras em ações — e, para isso, a participação da sociedade civil e dos governos locais será tão importante quanto a de líderes internacionais.
