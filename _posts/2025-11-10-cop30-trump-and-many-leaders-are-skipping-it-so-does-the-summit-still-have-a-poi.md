The COP30 climate summit in Belém, Brazil started this week with a glaring absence: US President Donald Trump didn’t show up. His no-show is the biggest by a US leader since the US pulled out of the Paris Agreement in 2017 under his first term. That move drew global criticism then, and while the US rejoined after Biden took office, Trump’s return to the White House has reignited worries about America’s commitment to cutting emissions. The White House didn’t immediately explain why Trump skipped the talks, but his administration has already rolled back dozens of climate regulations, including rules on methane emissions and clean car standards.

Despite Trump’s absence, over 140 world leaders did attend, including Brazil’s President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva and European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen. Brazil is hosting COP30 in the Amazon, which makes the summit’s location symbolic—both for the world’s largest rainforest and for the Indigenous communities fighting to protect it. Lula used his opening speech to call for rich nations to pay poorer countries to stop deforestation, a demand that’s likely to dominate closed-door talks. Von der Leyen, meanwhile, announced €4 billion in new climate funding for Latin America, but critics say that’s a drop in the bucket compared to what’s needed.

## Why COP30 matters even without the US

COP30’s main goal is to get countries to agree on a plan to phase out fossil fuels by 2050. Right now, the world is on track to overshoot the 1.5°C warming limit set in the Paris Agreement, and the UN says current pledges aren’t nearly enough. The summit’s location in the Amazon isn’t accidental—Brazil’s Amazon policies under Bolsonaro led to record deforestation, and Lula’s government has vowed to reverse that trend. But environmental groups warn that without stronger financial commitments from wealthy nations, promises to protect the rainforest won’t hold. Indigenous leaders, who’ve been sidelined in past COPs, are demanding a seat at the table this time around.

The summit also faces pressure to address the growing gap between rich and poor countries. Developing nations argue they can’t afford to transition to clean energy without outside help, and COP30 is where those debates will play out. The EU and US have historically been the biggest funders, but with Trump possibly back in power next year, even that support isn’t guaranteed. Meanwhile, China—a top emitter—sent its climate envoy, but no head of state, signaling cautious engagement at best.

## What’s at stake in Belém

The Amazon isn’t just a carbon sink; it’s a ticking time bomb. If deforestation continues at current rates, scientists warn the rainforest could tip into a savanna, releasing billions of tons of stored carbon and accelerating climate change. Lula’s government has cut deforestation by 50% since taking office, but illegal logging and mining are still rampant. COP30 is where Brazil will try to sell its progress to the world—and where it’ll face scrutiny over whether it can enforce its own laws.

The summit’s outcome will also shape the next round of national climate pledges, due in 2025. Countries were supposed to submit stronger plans this year, but so far, only a handful have done so. The UN says the world needs emissions to drop 43% by 2030 to meet the Paris goals, but current policies put us on track for just a 2% cut. That gap is why activists are pushing for COP30 to deliver binding agreements—not just more empty promises.

## What happens next

The next two weeks will see marathon negotiations over funding, fossil fuel phase-outs, and transparency rules. The biggest fights will likely be over money: how much rich countries will pay to help poorer ones adapt to climate change, and whether fossil fuel companies will finally face stricter rules. The summit ends on November 22, but its impact won’t be clear for months. Even if leaders agree on strong language, enforcement will depend on domestic politics—something Trump’s absence makes even more uncertain.

For now, the world is watching Belém to see if COP30 can deliver more than just photo ops. The stakes couldn’t be higher: the Amazon, the 1.5°C target, and the credibility of global climate action all hang in the balance.

## 🇧🇷 Resumo em Português

O Brasil, que há décadas lidera discussões globais sobre clima, recebe mais de 140 chefes de Estado e de governo na COP30, em Belém, mas a ausência de figuras como Donald Trump levanta dúvidas sobre o peso da cúpula. Enquanto líderes como o presidente dos Estados Unidos, tradicionalmente reticente a compromissos ambientais, optam por não comparecer, a conferência segue como um dos últimos fóruns de negociação antes de um 2025 marcado por cobranças ainda maiores por ações climáticas. O encontro no Pará, cercado pela Amazônia, promete ser um termômetro das reais intenções da comunidade internacional em um ano de eleições decisivas nos EUA e na União Europeia.

A COP30 chega em um momento crítico para o Brasil, que, após anos de desmatamento crescente, agora vê na presidência da cúpula uma chance de reafirmar sua liderança ambiental — ainda que com contradições internas. A presença de países como China, Índia e nações africanas, que também enfrentam pressões por transição energética, deve pautar debates sobre financiamento climático e metas de redução de emissões, temas que dividem o Sul e o Norte global. Para o brasileiro comum, a conferência pode soar distante, mas seus resultados influenciam diretamente desde o preço da gasolina até a sobrevivência das comunidades ribeirinhas.

Se a cúpula não produzir acordos concretos, o risco é que a confiança nas negociações multilaterais se esvazie justamente quando o mundo mais precisa de respostas urgentes.
