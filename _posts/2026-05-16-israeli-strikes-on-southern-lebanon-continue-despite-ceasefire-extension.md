Israel carried out fresh airstrikes in southern Lebanon on Saturday, defying a 45-day ceasefire extension agreed just 24 hours earlier. The strikes targeted at least five villages, local media reported, as tensions remained high along the border. Lebanon’s state-run National News Agency (NNA) confirmed attacks on villages near Sidon and Nabatieh, including Qaaqaaiyet and al-Snoubar. The Israeli military also issued new forced displacement orders for nine villages, forcing residents to evacuate amid escalating violence.

## Ceasefire extension fails to halt strikes
The airstrikes came despite a deal brokered in Washington to extend the temporary truce by another six weeks. Israel’s military claimed it struck "Hezbollah infrastructure sites" in multiple southern areas, including near the towns of Kfar Tebnit, Arnoun, and the Arnoun-Kfar Tebnit road. Artillery fire also hit Yohmor al-Shaqif, according to NNA reports. The strikes follow days of intense clashes that have displaced thousands in Lebanon’s south.

## Displacement orders issued as violence persists
The Israeli army’s latest orders target villages close to major southern cities, including Sidon and Nabatieh. Displaced residents face growing humanitarian crises, with aid groups warning of shortages in food, water, and medical supplies. Local officials say at least 12,000 people have been forced from their homes in the past month alone. The United Nations has repeatedly called for an immediate halt to hostilities to prevent further civilian harm.

## Hezbollah’s role remains central to conflict
Analysts say the continued strikes reflect unresolved tensions between Israel and [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), the Lebanon-based militant group. Despite reports of Hezbollah’s weakening position, Israel appears determined to dismantle its military infrastructure in southern Lebanon. The group has vowed to retaliate if strikes continue, raising fears of a wider regional conflict. Regional diplomats are urging both sides to uphold the ceasefire terms to avoid further escalation.

The latest violence underscores the fragile nature of the Washington-brokered agreement. While both sides have pledged to reduce hostilities, sporadic attacks have persisted since the initial ceasefire took effect weeks ago. International observers warn that without sustained diplomatic pressure, the truce could collapse entirely, plunging southern Lebanon into deeper instability. Aid organizations are preparing for worst-case scenarios as the humanitarian toll mounts.

What happens next remains uncertain. Lebanese Prime Minister [Najib Mikati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Najib_Mikati) has called for urgent international intervention to enforce the ceasefire, while Israel’s government has reiterated its right to self-defense. With no clear path to lasting peace, civilians on both sides of the border continue to bear the brunt of the conflict.
