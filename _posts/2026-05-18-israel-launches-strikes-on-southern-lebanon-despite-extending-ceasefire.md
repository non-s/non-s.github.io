Israel launched multiple airstrikes across southern Lebanon on Sunday, killing at least five people and wounding 15 more, according to local health officials. The attacks targeted areas near Tyre, a coastal city in southern Lebanon, and other villages. The strikes came just hours after Israel and Lebanon agreed to extend a temporary ceasefire that had been in place for weeks. The ceasefire was meant to reduce tensions along the border, but the timing of the strikes raised immediate questions about its stability. The Israeli military has not commented on the specific targets or casualties from the operation, which involved both aerial and artillery strikes in densely populated areas. Residents described the strikes as heavy and prolonged, with some reporting direct hits on homes and roads. Hospitals in Tyre and nearby towns were overwhelmed with the wounded, many of whom suffered shrapnel injuries and burns. The Lebanese government summoned the Israeli ambassador to protest the violations, calling them a 'direct threat to stability.' Meanwhile, Hezbollah [](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), the Lebanese militant group, has remained largely silent since the strikes, though tensions with Israel have been escalating for months. Residents in southern Lebanon said they were bracing for further violence despite the ceasefire extension, which was supposed to last until at least the end of the week. Power cuts and disrupted communications made it hard to get accurate casualty counts, but local officials said the toll could rise as more people are treated for injuries. The United Nations called for restraint on both sides, warning that any escalation could spiral into a broader conflict. Families in the strike zones described frantic efforts to pull survivors from rubble, with children among the injured. Doctors in Tyre hospitals said they were running low on medical supplies, including blood for transfusions. The Israeli strikes followed a series of rocket attacks from southern Lebanon into northern Israel, which Israel blamed on Hezbollah. The militant group has denied responsibility, though it has vowed to respond to any Israeli aggression. The ceasefire extension was brokered by international mediators, including the U.S. and France, in hopes of preventing a full-blown war. But Sunday’s strikes show just how fragile that agreement has become. The Lebanese army has deployed additional troops to the border areas, but their ability to prevent further strikes is limited. Analysts say the recent surge in violence reflects deeper political fractures in Lebanon, where the government is struggling to maintain control. The strikes also come as Lebanon faces an economic crisis, with many families already displaced by years of conflict. International aid groups warned that further violence could displace thousands more, worsening the humanitarian crisis. For now, southern Lebanon remains on edge, with families locked in homes and schools closed as a precaution. The question now is whether this latest flare-up will force both sides back to negotiations or push them closer to war.
