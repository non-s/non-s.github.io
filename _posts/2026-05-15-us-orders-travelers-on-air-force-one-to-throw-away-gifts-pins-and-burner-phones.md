The US government quietly ordered travelers returning from President Biden’s recent trip to China to dispose of gifts, lapel pins, and burner phones they’d used during the visit. The move underscores Washington’s unease about China’s aggressive intelligence operations, which US officials view as a persistent and growing threat.

The directive came after the delegation landed back in the US, where staffers were instructed to toss items they’d brought back from China. Gifts like pens, notebooks, and small keepsakes were targeted because they could contain hidden tracking devices or embedded chips. Lapel pins, often given as diplomatic tokens, were also flagged due to fears they might include microphones or transmitters. Even burner phones—cheap, temporary devices used by staffers to avoid hacking risks—were confiscated to prevent China from accessing sensitive communications.

The timing of the order suggests it wasn’t just routine caution. President Biden’s trip to China in November 2023 was one of the first high-profile diplomatic visits since tensions between the two nations escalated over espionage, trade, and military activity. During the trip, US officials met with Chinese counterparts in Shanghai and Beijing, where the risk of surveillance is always high. Chinese intelligence agencies are known for using sophisticated methods to monitor foreign visitors, including exploiting seemingly harmless items.

This isn’t the first time the US has taken extreme steps to counter Chinese espionage. In 2022, the Pentagon banned TikTok on government devices over concerns the app could funnel user data to Beijing. The FBI has also warned repeatedly about China’s efforts to steal technology and intellectual property, from hacking corporate networks to pressuring Chinese-American scientists to share research. The latest order reflects how seriously US agencies take the threat.

Travelers affected by the disposal order included White House staff, Secret Service agents, and other officials who accompanied Biden. Some reported being told to leave items behind at the airport or destroy them on the plane. The exact number of people or items involved hasn’t been disclosed, but insiders say the move was widespread enough to disrupt the trip’s aftermath.

The broader implications are clear: China’s intelligence operations are so advanced that even routine diplomatic exchanges require extreme countermeasures. The US isn’t just worried about spies in embassies—it’s concerned about everyday objects being weaponized. For travelers, this means future trips to China could involve even stricter protocols, including mandatory device inspections or temporary phone lockers. For businesses and diplomats, it’s a reminder that trust in China isn’t just naive—it could be dangerous.
