A San Francisco jury began deliberations Monday in the closely watched trial between [Elon Musk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Musk) and [Sam Altman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Altman), two of Silicon Valley’s most prominent figures. Over five weeks, testimony exposed sharp disagreements, alleged betrayals, and the often-hidden mechanics of tech industry power struggles.

The dispute centers on a 2022 deal where Musk’s X Corp. (then Twitter) sought to acquire AI startup [OpenAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenAI), co-founded by Altman. Musk alleges Altman sabotaged the deal by leaking internal discussions to reporters, damaging negotiations and his public image. Altman denies any wrongdoing and says Musk’s version of events is self-serving.

## Emails reveal internal chaos at Twitter during bot crisis

Internal emails entered into evidence showed Musk’s team grappling with Twitter’s bot problem in March 2022. One email from Musk to then-CFO [Ned Segal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ned_Segal) said, “We need to fix the bot issue before the deal closes or the valuation is a sham.” The correspondence highlights the turmoil inside Twitter as Musk prepared to close his $44 billion acquisition that October.

Prosecutors also presented messages suggesting Musk considered using OpenAI’s technology to improve Twitter’s algorithms, but Altman’s team refused, calling it a misuse of proprietary code. The refusal reportedly frustrated Musk, contributing to the breakdown in talks.

## Allegations of corporate espionage and secret deals surface

Altman testified that Musk and his team tried to obtain OpenAI’s early research through improper channels, including targeting employees with job offers to extract sensitive data. OpenAI’s legal team called the attempts “corporate espionage” in court filings. Altman denied any involvement but acknowledged that some employees left amid the pressure.

## Financial irregularities and side agreements under scrutiny

Court documents revealed that Musk allegedly made secret side deals with other investors during the OpenAI acquisition talks, promising them preferential treatment in exchange for funding. The agreements, if proven, could violate securities laws. Jurors also heard testimony about irregular expense reimbursements and undeclared perks given to key executives.

## What happens next: Jury to decide on damages and liability

The jury must now decide not only whether Altman or Musk breached contracts but also the extent of financial harm caused. Legal experts say the case could set precedents for how tech deals are negotiated and enforced. A verdict is expected within days, but appeals could draw out the legal battle for years.

Regardless of the outcome, the trial has already exposed vulnerabilities in the tech industry’s culture of secrecy and rapid deal-making. Observers say the revelations could prompt stricter oversight of corporate governance in Silicon Valley.
