A drone strike near the [Barakah nuclear power plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barakah_nuclear_power_plant) in Abu Dhabi sparked a fire Tuesday, though the UAE’s nuclear regulator confirmed no radiation leaked and no one was hurt. The plant, located about 50 kilometers west of the capital, is the UAE’s first commercial nuclear facility and runs four South Korean-built reactors. Authorities haven’t said who launched the drone, but all signs point toward Iran, which has targeted regional energy sites before. The UAE’s state news agency called the strike a “terrorist attack,” stopping short of naming any group. Iran has denied involvement, as it typically does in such cases. Still, regional tensions are high after a string of similar incidents in recent months, including attacks on oil tankers and Saudi oil facilities. Experts say the drone used was likely a long-range model, possibly launched from Iraq or Yemen, where Iran-backed groups operate. The fire was controlled quickly, and the plant’s reactors were never at risk, according to the [Federal Authority for Nuclear Regulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Authority_for_Nuclear_Regulation). But the incident raises fresh questions about the vulnerability of the UAE’s critical infrastructure. The Barakah plant alone supplies about 25% of the country’s electricity, and any prolonged disruption could strain energy supplies during the summer peak demand months. The UAE has invested billions in the project to diversify its energy mix and reduce reliance on fossil fuels. Tuesday’s strike comes just weeks after Israel accused Iran of trying to smuggle ballistic missiles to Yemen, a move Tehran denied. The timing adds weight to the suspicion that Iran is behind the attack, though no group has claimed responsibility yet. Regional analysts say Iran often uses proxies to carry out such operations, making it harder to directly implicate Tehran without clear evidence. The UAE has not released details about the drone’s type or where it was launched from, but satellite imagery from the hours before the attack shows unusual activity near the plant’s perimeter. Security footage reviewed by local outlets shows a small drone entering the restricted zone before the fire broke out. The UAE’s military hasn’t commented on whether it intercepted the drone or if it evaded air defenses. The incident has already drawn international attention, with the US, UK, and EU calling for restraint and urging a thorough investigation. Diplomats say the attack could complicate ongoing talks to revive the 2015 Iran nuclear deal, which the UAE has supported. The US is also watching closely, with President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump) scheduling a May 18 meeting with top national security advisers to discuss possible responses. Options reportedly include cyberattacks, targeted strikes on Iranian facilities, or increased sanctions. Iran has warned that any US retaliation would trigger a “strong” response, raising fears of a broader escalation. The UAE has been tight-lipped about its next steps but is expected to bolster security around nuclear and other critical sites. The fire at Barakah forced a temporary shutdown of one reactor, though the others continued operating normally. The plant’s operator, [Emirates Nuclear Energy Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emirates_Nuclear_Energy_Corporation), said the disruption lasted less than three hours and didn’t affect power supply to the grid. Still, the attack has exposed gaps in how the UAE protects its nuclear assets. The country’s air defenses rely heavily on American-made systems, including Patriot missiles and early warning radar, but drones remain a blind spot. Analysts say the UAE may now fast-track upgrades to its counter-drone technology, including electronic jamming and kinetic interceptors. The incident also puts pressure on Gulf states to coordinate better on regional security, especially as Iran’s nuclear program inches closer to weapons-grade uranium enrichment. For now, the UAE is treating this as a one-off attack, but the worry is that it could be a test run for something bigger. If Iran is indeed behind the strike, it’s a clear message: no target in the region is off-limits.
