Novo Nordisk’s weight loss pill Wegovy just became a blockbuster in the US, but the Danish drugmaker isn’t stopping there. After blowing past its own sales forecasts in America, it’s now eyeing Europe and China as the next big battlegrounds for the obesity drug market. The company believes the same success could repeat overseas—if it can clear hurdles like pricing and supply delays. This isn’t just about selling more pills. It’s about proving obesity drugs aren’t just a US trend but a global shift in how the world treats weight loss.

Wegovy, a higher-dose version of Novo Nordisk’s diabetes drug Ozempic, has rewritten the rules for weight loss treatment in America. Patients and doctors have snapped it up since its US launch in June 2021, with sales hitting $2.9 billion in the first half of 2024—triple last year’s pace. But the real test comes overseas. In Europe, regulators are fast-tracking approvals for broader use, and Novo Nordisk is lobbying payers to cover the $1,000-a-month price tag. China, where obesity rates hit 12% and keep climbing, is an even tougher sell: the drug isn’t approved there yet, and cultural stigma around weight loss drugs runs deep.

The stakes are huge. Novo Nordisk spent $12 billion in January to buy Versanis Bio, a small US biotech working on a next-gen obesity pill that could outperform Wegovy. That deal signals the company isn’t just betting on one drug—it’s building an entire obesity portfolio. Analysts at [Goldman Sachs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldman_Sachs) now say Novo’s obesity sales could triple to $50 billion by 2030 if it wins over doctors, insurers, and regulators worldwide. But the roadblocks are real. Supply shortages keep Wegovy off shelves in several countries, and doctors in some markets still see obesity drugs as a last resort, not a first-line treatment.

## How Novo Nordisk is tackling Europe’s obesity pill resistance

Europe isn’t an easy market. While the EU approved Wegovy in 2022, many national health systems still refuse to pay for it, calling the cost too high. Novo Nordisk is fighting back with data showing Wegovy cuts heart disease risk by 20% in high-risk patients—a pitch to convince payers that the drug saves money long-term. The company’s also pushing for earlier use, targeting patients with lower BMIs than the US cutoff, which could expand the market by millions.

In China, Novo Nordisk is playing the long game. The country’s obesity rates have tripled in two decades, but only 1% of eligible patients have tried prescription weight loss drugs. Cultural barriers are part of the problem: many Chinese see obesity as a lack of willpower, not a chronic disease. Novo’s building relationships with local hospitals and running education campaigns to change minds. It’s also testing a lower-dose version of Wegovy that could be more affordable for Chinese patients.

## The competition isn’t sleeping

Novo Nordisk isn’t the only player chasing this gold rush. [Eli Lilly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eli_Lilly_and_Company) launched Zepbound, a rival obesity pill, in the US last year and is now pushing into Europe. Both drugs work similarly—mimicking gut hormones to reduce hunger—but Lilly’s version has shown slightly better results in some clinical trials. Meanwhile, [Pfizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pfizer) and [Amgen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amgen) are racing to bring their own pills to market, and even China’s [Sinopharm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinopharm_Group) is developing a homegrown version. The obesity drug market could hit $100 billion by 2030, and everyone wants a slice.

What happens next? If Novo Nordisk can crack Europe and China, it’ll cement its lead in the obesity drug race. But success isn’t guaranteed. Payers will demand deeper discounts, doctors will push back on early use, and regulators in some countries may drag their feet. For now, though, the company’s bet is clear: obesity isn’t just an American problem anymore. It’s a global opportunity—and Novo Nordisk plans to own it.
