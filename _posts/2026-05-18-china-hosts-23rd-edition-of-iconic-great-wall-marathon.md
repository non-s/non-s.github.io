The 23rd Great Wall of China Marathon kicked off at dawn on May 16, with runners from more than 50 countries lining up for a brutal 13.1-mile race across the historic wall. The route starts and ends near the Huangyaguan [fortress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huangyaguan_Great_Wall), a restored Ming Dynasty section of the wall 120 km northeast of Beijing. This year’s edition drew nearly 1,000 competitors, including elite athletes and first-timers chasing bragging rights (and Instagram photos) from one of the world’s most famous landmarks.

## A course designed to break bodies—and wills
The race isn’t just long; it’s punishing. Runners climb 1,500 feet over 5,164 stone steps, many of them uneven and slick from morning dew. The steepest section—a 37-degree ascent near the 10-mile mark—left even seasoned marathoners gasping. Last year’s winner, Ethiopia’s Sisay Lemma, called it ‘the toughest race I’ve ever run.’ The brutal mix of altitude, stairs, and wind means most finishers walk the final mile.

Organizers say about 40% of starters don’t cross the finish line. Blisters, knee pain, and exhaustion are common, but the real challenge is mental. ‘You look down and realize you’re on a 2,000-year-old wall with nothing but air beneath you,’ said 28-year-old competitor Priya Patel from Mumbai. ‘Your legs burn, but your brain tells you to stop long before your body does.’

## Why runners line up for this pain
Despite the suffering, the Great Wall Marathon is a bucket-list race for many. Forbes added it to its 2023 list of top marathons worth traveling for, alongside events like the Boston Marathon and the Big Five Marathon in South Africa. The scenery—rolling mountains, restored watchtowers, and sweeping views of the wall snaking into the distance—helps distract from the agony. Runners also get a medal shaped like the dragon-head battlements and a finisher’s certificate printed on scroll paper.

Elite athletes chase the course record: 3 hours, 45 minutes, set in 2019 by Kenya’s James Kipsang. But most are here for the experience. ‘I didn’t train for stairs,’ admitted 34-year-old Daniel Lee from Vancouver. ‘I trained for flat roads. But when else can I say I ran a marathon on the Great Wall?’

The event isn’t just about the race. Organizers include a post-race banquet with local dishes like zha jiang mian (fried noodles) and jiaozi (dumplings), plus a tour of the Huangyaguan section of the wall. Many runners extend their trips to explore Beijing or take the high-speed train to Shanghai.

## The numbers behind the pain
This year’s race featured a 25% increase in international runners compared to 2022, as travel restrictions lifted post-pandemic. The youngest finisher was 18; the oldest was 72. About 60% of participants came from outside China, with strong showings from the U.S., Germany, and Australia. Registration fees—$350 for foreigners—include race bibs, meals, and a finisher’s shirt, but not flights or hotels.

Weather played its part too. Race day dawned cool and overcast, with temps in the 50s°F (10-15°C). Runners wore layers they’d shed mid-race, only to regret it when the wind picked up on the exposed sections. Organizers added extra water stations after complaints in past years about runners running out of fluids on the climbs.

## What’s next for the Great Wall Marathon
The 24th edition is already in the works, with dates likely in mid-May 2025. Organizers are expanding the race to include a half-marathon for those intimidated by the full 13.1 miles. They’ve also added a ‘veteran’ category for runners over 60, recognizing that age is just a number on this brutal course.

For now, the focus is on the memories made this year. Runners will post their bruised shins and triumphant photos online for months. And a few will start training for next year’s race the moment they get home—because once you’ve run the Great Wall, flat marathons just don’t feel the same.
