Keir Starmer [has refused](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer) to set a timetable for his departure despite at least 15 Labour MPs publicly demanding one. The prime minister told reporters today he’s ‘fighting on’ as leader, brushing off the internal calls for clarity. His response came after Labour MP [Richard Burgon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Burgon) became the latest to urge Starmer to name a date, saying the party can’t afford more months of uncertainty. Other MPs have privately warned that the longer the uncertainty drags on, the harder it’ll be to rebuild trust with voters ahead of the next election.

David Lammy [took to the airwaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Lammy) this morning to urge Labour to close ranks, framing the moment as a test of the party’s discipline. ‘We can’t let infighting distract us from the job we were elected to do,’ Lammy told BBC Radio 4’s Today programme. His message echoed similar calls from shadow cabinet colleagues who fear the public is losing patience with the party’s internal battles. One senior Labour source said the infighting risks overshadowing the government’s legislative agenda, including planned reforms to planning rules and worker rights.

## Why the calls for Starmer to go now?

The pressure on Starmer ramped up after Labour’s weaker-than-expected results in last month’s local elections. While the party held onto key councils, the losses in areas like [Wigan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wigan) and [Warrington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warrington) sparked fresh doubts about Starmer’s leadership. MPs from the left and right of the party have since spoken out, with some accusing Starmer of drifting too far from Labour’s core values. Others argue the party needs a fresh face to revive its fortunes before the next general election, expected in 2029.

Starmer has defended his record, pointing to progress on economic growth and his handling of international crises. But the criticism isn’t just coming from backbenchers. Former Labour leader [Jeremy Corbyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn) weighed in yesterday, saying the party needs ‘new energy’ to win back trust. Starmer’s allies counter that the infighting is overblown and that Labour’s poll ratings remain competitive. One insider said the prime minister’s team is focused on delivering key policies, including a promised ban on no-fault evictions and a crackdown on private rent increases.

## What happens next?

Lammy’s intervention suggests Labour’s leadership is scrambling to prevent a full-blown revolt. Party whips are reportedly holding private meetings with disaffected MPs to gauge support for Starmer. But the clock is ticking. If the calls for Starmer to step aside grow louder, Labour risks repeating the chaos that followed [Corbyn’s leadership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn#Leadership_of_the_Labour_Party) before Starmer took over in 2020. That period saw the party haemorrhage members, donors, and voters, a lesson Lammy appears determined to avoid.

For now, Starmer shows no sign of backing down. His team has pointed to recent polling that shows Labour still leading the Conservatives on key issues like healthcare and cost of living. But the longer the uncertainty drags on, the harder it’ll be to maintain discipline. One Labour MP, who asked not to be named, said: ‘We can’t keep pretending this isn’t damaging the party. At some point, someone’s going to have to make a move.’

The next few weeks will be critical. If Starmer can’t silence the dissent, the party’s internal crisis could dominate headlines through the summer, overshadowing its policy agenda and giving the Conservatives a fresh opening to attack. For a government that prides itself on stability, this is the last thing it needs.
