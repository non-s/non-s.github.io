South Korea’s Kospi index [KOSPI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korea_Composite_Stock_Price_Index) and Kosdaq index [KOSDAQ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KOSDAQ) swung wildly on Monday after foreign investors pulled $13.2 billion from the country’s stock market in just seven days. That’s the biggest weekly outflow since the global financial crisis in 2008, according to data from the Korea Exchange [Korea Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korea_Exchange). The sudden rush to exit triggered a jump in the Kospi’s volatility index to levels last seen during the 2022 global market rout. Local traders say the panic selling hasn’t been this bad since the early days of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020.

The selloff hit South Korea’s tech and chipmaking giants the hardest. Samsung Electronics [Samsung Electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samsung_Electronics) saw its shares drop 4.2% on Monday alone, while SK Hynix [SK Hynix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SK_Hynix) lost 5.1%. The two companies are among the country’s most valuable exporters, and their struggles reflect broader concerns about global demand for semiconductors. Foreign investors, who own about 35% of the Kospi’s market cap, have been pulling money out for weeks, but last week’s exodus was the most aggressive yet.

## Why are foreign investors fleeing South Korea now?

Analysts point to a mix of global and local factors. On the global side, rising U.S. Treasury yields have made American bonds more attractive than emerging market stocks. The 10-year U.S. yield [U.S. Treasury yield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treasury_yield) jumped to 4.6% last week, its highest since 2007, pulling capital away from riskier assets like South Korean equities. Meanwhile, China’s economic slowdown is hurting demand for South Korea’s exports, including chips and displays. Chinese buyers, who once drove a third of South Korea’s semiconductor sales, are cutting back orders.

Locally, South Korea’s own economic troubles are adding to the pressure. Inflation is stuck above 3%, while household debt has ballooned to $1.7 trillion—nearly 100% of the country’s GDP. Consumer spending, a key driver of growth, has been sluggish for months. The Bank of Korea [Bank of Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_Korea) has kept interest rates at 3.5% since January, but even that hasn’t been enough to revive confidence. Small investors, who make up about 40% of the Kospi’s trading volume, are also pulling back, fearing the market could keep sliding.

## How bad is the damage so far?

By Monday’s close, the Kospi had fallen 2.8% for the day, bringing its weekly loss to nearly 6%. The Kosdaq, which is heavy on tech startups, is down 8% over the same period. Brokerages like Mirae Asset Securities [Mirae Asset Securities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirae_Asset_Securities) and KB Securities [KB Securities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KB_Securities) have slashed their year-end Kospi targets by 10-15%, citing weaker earnings outlooks for exporters. The won [South Korean won](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korean_won) also took a hit, dropping 1.5% against the dollar as foreign money left the country.

Retail investors, who’ve been propping up the market for years, are starting to crack. Online stock forums are filling with posts from small traders blaming the government for not doing enough to stabilize the won or boost growth. Some are even calling for the finance minister to resign, though President Yoon Suk-yeol [Yoon Suk-yeol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoon_Suk-yeol) has so far resisted major policy shifts. The government did announce a $10 billion package last month to support small businesses, but traders say it’s too little, too late.

## What happens next?

The big question is whether this is just a temporary pullback or the start of a deeper slide. Most analysts expect volatility to stay high through October as earnings season ramps up. Samsung and SK Hynix will report third-quarter results later this month, and if their outlooks disappoint, the selloff could worsen. The Bank of Korea meets next week to decide on interest rates, but few expect a cut—especially with inflation still stubbornly high.

For now, foreign investors are unlikely to return unless they see clear signs of a rebound in global demand or a policy shift from Seoul. Local fund managers, meanwhile, are bracing for more pain. One trader at Hyundai Securities [Hyundai Securities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyundai_Securities) put it bluntly: “This isn’t a correction. It’s a retreat. And retreats don’t end until someone stops the bleeding.”
