Two months ago, you served two plates at dinner. Now you serve one. The shift hits hardest at 7:30 p.m. when the cabinets echo. Bon Appétit’s Dear BA columnist got hundreds of letters from empty nesters and newly divorced cooks asking the same thing: what do I actually cook when it’s just me?

The answers had to fit three rules. First, no giant batches that spoil before you finish. Second, no sad leftovers hiding in the back of the fridge for a week. Third, no recipes that feel like punishment for being alone at the table. After testing dozens of options, the team landed on 14 meals that check every box. They’re fast, they’re flavorful, and they make enough for tonight without drowning you in tomorrow’s lunch.

## Lemony red lentil soup hits the spot when the kitchen feels too quiet

Red lentils cook in 15 minutes, which is half the time it takes to explain to your kids where the can opener is. The soup is brightened with lemon zest and a squeeze of juice, so it doesn’t taste like it’s punishing you for eating alone. Serve it with crusty bread or a handful of herbs if you’re feeling fancy. Leftovers? One bowl. That’s it.

If lentils aren’t your thing, the same column has a towering chicken and cabbage salad that looks like a party on one plate. Shredded chicken, thin cabbage ribbons, and a mustardy vinaigrette pile high enough to make you forget you’re eating solo. The dressing keeps for a few days, so you can make it ahead and skip the measuring mid-week.

## One-pan meals mean fewer dishes, more time to hate your toaster

The next batch of recipes are built to cook in one pan, so cleanup is just a sponge swipe. Sheet-pan salmon with smashed potatoes and green beans takes 25 minutes start to finish. The salmon gets crispy skin, the potatoes turn buttery, and the green beans steam in the same space. You’ll use one sheet, one spatula, and one plate.

For nights when you want pasta but don’t want to wrestle a colander, the columnist recommends gemelli with brown butter, sage, and Parmesan. The butter browns in the same pot you boil the pasta, so the pan goes straight from stovetop to table. The sage leaves crisp up like tiny chips, and the Parmesan melts into the sauce without clumping. One pot, one fork.

## Spice blends and shortcuts turn boring proteins into fast flavor

If chicken breasts taste like punishment now, try sheet-pan chicken thighs with harissa and honey. The thighs stay juicy, the harissa adds North African heat, and the honey balances it without turning your mouth into a fire drill. The whole thing cooks while you scroll through the news you used to watch with your family.

Shortcuts work too. Canned chickpeas tossed with smoked paprika, cumin, and olive oil roast in the oven for 20 minutes until they’re crispy enough to snack on like popcorn. No blender, no fuss. Just open, season, roast, eat.

## The trick? Buy the good stuff when it’s just you

The columnist noticed something else: the recipes taste better when the ingredients do. A single lemon costs more than a bag of five, but one squeeze brightens the lentil soup enough to make the price worth it. A block of Parmesan tastes richer than the green can, and it lasts longer than you think.

The real secret isn’t the recipe. It’s treating yourself like someone worth cooking for. That’s the part no one tells you when the house goes quiet. The food helps, but the mindset shift is what sticks.

Next time you open the fridge at 8 p.m. wondering what to make, remember: you’re not just feeding yourself. You’re feeding the person who still deserves a good meal even when the table’s empty.
