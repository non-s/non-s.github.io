Indonesia flipped the switch on its first nationwide 5G blanket coverage last week, completing a project both partners call the biggest network upgrade in the country’s history. XLSMART [XLSMART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XLSMART), Indonesia’s largest mobile operator, joined forces with Chinese telecom giant ZTE [ZTE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZTE) to merge 20,000 existing 4G base stations with 7,000 brand-new 5G sites in just eight months. The result is seamless coverage across the archipelago’s 17,000 islands, something analysts said would have taken three to five years under normal conditions.

## How the speed run worked
The project relied on ZTE’s digital-intelligent tools, which automated site selection, power planning, and backhaul routing. Instead of sending engineers island by island, the system crunched terrain data, population density, and existing tower locations to pick sites in hours. XLSMART’s field teams then built or upgraded towers in batches, cutting approvals and civil work down to weeks instead of months. One engineer on the ground in Sumatra said crews finished 12 sites in a single day—something unthinkable with manual planning.

Ookla [Ookla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ookla), the company behind Speedtest, confirmed the network as the fastest 5G in the world for the second half of 2025. Average download speeds hit 520 Mbps, beating rivals in South Korea and the United Arab Emirates. The jump wasn’t just about speed; users on the merged network saw call drop rates fall from 3% to under 0.5% because the system automatically shifted calls between 4G and 5G towers when signal weakened.

## Who actually benefited
The 73 million subscribers now on the upgraded network include everyone from fishermen in Sulawesi checking weather apps to factory workers in Java streaming training videos. A small-business owner in Bandung said his cloud-based point-of-sale system now syncs in real time—something he couldn’t do reliably before. In remote Papua, where copper lines still dominate, villagers use 5G hotspots to access telemedicine for the first time. Regulator statistics show data usage jumped 300% in areas where 5G lit up, mostly from video calls and cloud services.

## What’s next for the tech
ZTE and XLSMART aren’t stopping here. They’ve already started testing AI-driven network slicing, which lets the operator carve the same tower into virtual lanes for different uses—one for emergency services, another for factory robots, and a third for consumer phones. Early trials in Jakarta cut latency for industrial IoT devices from 20 milliseconds to under 5. The companies plan to open a joint innovation lab in Jakarta next month to develop use cases for agriculture, mining, and smart cities.

## Why this matters beyond Indonesia
The project proves Southeast Asia doesn’t need to wait for fiber or satellite to get world-class wireless. By reusing existing towers and automating deployment, countries like Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines can roll out 5G faster and cheaper. Analysts at Counterpoint Research [Counterpoint Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterpoint_Research) say the model could cut 5G rollout costs by up to 40% in markets with rugged terrain. Indonesia’s success also gives ZTE a blueprint to push similar deals in Africa and Latin America, where terrain and budget constraints mirror Jakarta’s challenges.

The bigger picture is that Indonesia skipped a step. Most nations upgrade slowly from 4G to 5G by adding towers on top of towers. Indonesia instead merged its entire footprint into one smart network. The result is a single system that grows smarter as more users join, not one that clogs up as demand rises.
