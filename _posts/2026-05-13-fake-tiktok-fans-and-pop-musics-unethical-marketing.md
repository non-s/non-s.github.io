Indie bands [Geese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geese_(band)) and [Sombr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sombr) face accusations of artificially inflating their chart positions through TikTok bot campaigns run by their marketing agency, [Chaotic Good Projects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaotic_Good_Projects). The revelation, disclosed in a *Billboard* interview with agency heads Andrew Spelman and Jesse Coren, has triggered widespread criticism from fans who believed these artists achieved success through organic growth.

## Marketing firm admits orchestrating TikTok trends
Chaotic Good Projects openly described its strategy as simulating trends by posting from multiple accounts to create the illusion of viral popularity. The firm represents several indie acts, including Geese, Sombr, [Jane Remover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Remover) and [Mk.gee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mk.gee), whose rapid ascents on streaming charts have been questioned by followers. The admission contradicts the indie music ethos of authenticity, where artists are presumed to succeed based on merit rather than fabricated engagement.

Music industry observers note that chart manipulation is not new, but TikTok’s influence has amplified its reach. Bots and fake accounts can generate millions of views overnight, tricking algorithms into promoting songs to real users. Critics argue this practice distorts the fairness of streaming platforms, where visibility often determines commercial success. Independent artists without label backing rely on organic discovery, making fabricated trends particularly damaging to fair competition.

## Fans react to perceived betrayal
Social media platforms erupted with accusations of deception after the *Billboard* interview surfaced. Many fans, who pride themselves on supporting "authentic" indie artists, felt misled. The backlash forced some musicians to address the controversy publicly, though most have avoided direct responses. Industry insiders suggest the practice may be widespread, with smaller agencies using similar tactics to secure placements on playlists and charts.

## Ethical concerns in music promotion
The debate extends beyond indie circles, raising questions about the broader music industry’s reliance on fabricated metrics. Streaming services like [Spotify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spotify) and [Apple Music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Music) prioritize songs with high engagement, creating incentives for manipulation. While platforms have implemented fraud detection, critics say loopholes remain, allowing agencies to exploit system vulnerabilities. Some analysts predict stricter regulations, but enforcement remains inconsistent.

Artists caught in the crossfire face reputational damage. The indie genre, historically associated with grassroots support, now struggles to distinguish genuine talent from algorithmically boosted acts. Fans increasingly demand transparency, while agencies defend their methods as necessary in an oversaturated market. The controversy underscores the tension between commercial success and artistic integrity in modern music.

## What happens next?
The fallout from the TikTok bot revelations could reshape how indie artists promote their work. Streaming platforms may tighten fraud detection, while fans could shift toward supporting artists who refuse artificial growth tactics. Agencies like Chaotic Good Projects may face legal scrutiny, though no lawsuits have been filed yet. For now, the indie music scene grapples with the erosion of trust in an era where virality often trumps authenticity.
