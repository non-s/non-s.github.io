On Sunday, President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump) delivered a stark warning to [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran): if Tehran doesn’t finalize a nuclear deal soon, it won’t just face sanctions or a military strike—he’s threatening the country’s existence. Speaking to a crowd in South Carolina, Trump said, 'They’re going to regret it if they don’t make a deal. They won’t have a country left.' The threat came as talks between the U.S. and Iran have stalled for months, leaving diplomats and analysts scrambling to revive an agreement that once seemed within reach. The collapse of negotiations has already pushed global oil prices higher, with markets jittery over the prospect of a prolonged standoff. Gasoline futures in the U.S. rose more than 5% last week alone, adding pressure to an already strained global energy market.

## Why the deal matters now

The nuclear talks between the U.S. and Iran have been on life support since Iran elected a hardline president, [Ebrahim Raisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebrahim_Raisi), last year. Raisi’s government has taken a harder line than its predecessor, demanding sweeping sanctions relief before any concessions on uranium enrichment. The U.S., meanwhile, has refused to lift key economic penalties until Iran scales back its nuclear program. The deadlock has turned the Middle East into a powder keg, with Iran-backed militias in Iraq, Syria, and Yemen ramping up attacks on U.S. and allied targets. Last month, a drone strike by Iranian proxies killed three U.S. soldiers in Jordan, escalating fears of a broader conflict. Energy markets are bracing for more disruptions, with [BP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BP) and [Shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shell_plc) warning of potential supply chain shocks if tensions boil over.

The economic fallout isn’t just about oil prices—it’s hitting everyday consumers. In Europe, natural gas prices have surged 12% this week after reports of a potential Israeli strike on Iranian nuclear sites. Truck drivers in Germany are already protesting higher fuel costs, while airlines like [Lufthansa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lufthansa) are considering surcharges for long-haul flights. Analysts at [Goldman Sachs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldman_Sachs) estimate that a full-blown conflict could push Brent crude prices past $100 a barrel for the first time since 2022, further straining household budgets already squeezed by inflation. The White House has tried to downplay the risks, with National Security Council spokesman [John Kirby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Kirby_(White_House_spokesperson)) insisting that 'diplomacy remains the preferred path.' But behind closed doors, officials admit the window for a deal is closing fast.

## Trump’s gamble and Iran’s response

Trump’s threat to Iran isn’t just rhetoric—it’s a calculated move to pressure Tehran into concessions. His administration has already reinstated sanctions that choked Iran’s economy, cutting its oil exports by nearly 50% since 2018. But Iran has shown no signs of backing down. State media in Tehran called Trump’s comments 'psychological warfare' and accused the U.S. of destabilizing the region. Iran’s foreign minister, [Hossein Amir-Abdollahian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hossein_Amir-Abdollahian), responded by saying, 'No one can dictate terms to Iran.' Meanwhile, hardline factions in Tehran are pushing for a military deterrent, with reports of Iran enriching uranium to near-weapons-grade levels—a move the U.N. nuclear watchdog, the [IAEA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency), has condemned as 'serious and regrettable.'

## What happens next?

The next 30 days could decide whether the U.S. and Iran return to the negotiating table—or spiral into a conflict neither side wants. European diplomats are quietly shuttling between Washington and Tehran, trying to broker a last-minute compromise. The sticking point remains sanctions relief: Iran wants them lifted immediately, while the U.S. insists on gradual steps tied to verifiable curbs on Iran’s nuclear program. If no deal is reached, the U.S. could impose even harsher penalties, including secondary sanctions on countries that trade with Iran. Iran, for its part, has hinted it might withdraw from the Non-Proliferation Treaty, a move that would trigger global condemnation and potentially a military response from Israel or the U.S. The stakes couldn’t be higher. A single miscalculation—a misfired missile, a misinterpreted naval maneuver—could tip the region into chaos. For now, the world watches as the clock ticks down on the last chance for peace.
