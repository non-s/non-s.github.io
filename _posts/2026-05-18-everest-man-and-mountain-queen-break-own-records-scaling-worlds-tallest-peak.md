Kami Rita Sherpa [made his 32nd summit of Mount Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kami_Rita_Sharma) on Wednesday, breaking his own record for the most climbs of the world's tallest peak. The 54-year-old Nepali guide reached the 8,849-meter summit at 7:30 AM local time, according to the Nepal Department of Tourism. Just days earlier, Lakpa Sherpa topped Kami Rita's previous record when she summited for the 11th time on May 11, 2024. She's now the most summited woman on Everest, a title she's held since 2018 when she broke her own record for the fifth time that year. Lakpa was 49 during this year's climb, showing age isn't slowing her down either. Both climbers completed their record-breaking feats during the 2024 spring climbing season, which runs from April to May when weather conditions are most favorable on the mountain.

## What makes these records so rare
Everest has been summited over 11,000 times since the first confirmed ascent in 1953, but most climbers only do it once or twice. Kami Rita first reached the top in 1994 and has been climbing nearly every year since. His father was also a famous Sherpa guide, and Kami Rita started helping other climbers make their first summits before taking on leadership roles himself. Lakpa Sherpa began climbing in 1999 and has been breaking records almost every decade since, including becoming the first woman to summit twice in one week in 2003. Their consistency shows how the Sherpa community keeps Everest's climbing industry alive through skill, endurance, and experience.

## More than just numbers
While the records are impressive, the real story is what these climbers represent. Kami Rita and Lakpa Sherpa didn't just climb Everest multiple times—they built careers around it. Both spent years guiding paying customers up the mountain, carrying gear, setting ropes, and making sure clients made it safely to the top. Their experience means they know every crevasse, every shortcut, and every time when conditions turn dangerous. Lakpa Sherpa has also become a vocal advocate for women in mountaineering, pushing for better recognition and support for female climbers in Nepal. She once told reporters that her goal isn't just to climb, but to show other Nepalese women they can do it too.

## The risks and costs behind the records
Everest isn't getting easier to climb. The 2023 season saw 17 deaths on the mountain, the highest toll in years, with many climbers struggling with altitude sickness and extreme weather. Kami Rita and Lakpa Sherpa both rely on decades of experience to avoid those dangers. The Sherpas who work on Everest face the highest risks—most of the 17 deaths last year were Sherpa guides or support climbers. Their families receive compensation when accidents happen, but it's often not enough to cover the loss of a breadwinner. Both record-setters have spoken about the need for better safety measures and fairer pay for Sherpas, though little has changed in practice.

## What happens next 
Kami Rita Sherpa says he's not done yet. In interviews after his latest summit, he mentioned aiming for 35 climbs before he retires from guiding. Lakpa Sherpa has hinted she might not stop at 11 either, though she's also focused on mentoring younger climbers. Nepal's tourism officials expect both to return next spring, weather and health permitting. Their records won't last forever—other Sherpas are already climbing more than 20 times—but for now, Kami Rita and Lakpa Sherpa remain the standard all others are measured against. More importantly, they're living proof that the human spirit can push limits when skill, determination, and experience come together.
