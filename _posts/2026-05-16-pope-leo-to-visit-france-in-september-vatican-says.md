Pope Leo XIV will make his first official state visit to France in nearly two decades when he travels to Paris in September, the Vatican announced Saturday. The announcement comes as the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church) continues global outreach efforts under the leadership of [Pope Leo XIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Leo_XIV). The Vatican confirmed the trip dates and itinerary, though specific events remain undisclosed.

The last papal state visit to France occurred in 2006, when [Pope Benedict XVI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI) met with French officials and addressed religious and political leaders. Since then, popes have made private or pastoral visits, but none have held the formal status of a state visit. The upcoming trip underscores the Vatican’s ongoing diplomatic efforts in Europe.

French President [Emmanuel Macron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron) welcomed the announcement, calling it a significant moment for Franco-Vatican relations. Macron’s office stated the visit would include meetings with religious leaders and discussions on global humanitarian issues. The French government is coordinating with the Vatican to finalize the agenda.

## State visit details and historical context

The Vatican confirmed the visit would take place September 12-15, with Paris as the primary destination. While the full itinerary remains under wraps, officials anticipate major public events, including a papal address at [Notre-Dame Cathedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notre-Dame_Cathedral), which remains a symbol of French heritage despite its 2019 fire damage.

Pope Leo XIV’s visit follows months of diplomatic engagement between the Holy See and France. The two nations have collaborated on issues including refugee support and climate change, reflecting shared values. The trip is expected to strengthen ties amid rising secularism in France and growing challenges to religious freedom in Europe.

## Security and public response

French authorities are preparing for the visit with heightened security measures, given the global profile of the pope and potential for large crowds. The government has not yet released specific security plans but assured the public of a safe and orderly event. Local parishes and Catholic organizations are mobilizing volunteers to assist with logistics and crowd management.

Public reaction in France has been mixed, with some Catholic groups expressing enthusiasm for the visit and others questioning its timing amid domestic political tensions. Polls suggest moderate interest among the general population, with younger demographics showing less engagement. Religious leaders, however, have framed the visit as an opportunity for unity.

## Broader implications for international diplomacy

The papal visit comes at a time of shifting global alliances, with the Vatican playing an active role in mediating conflicts and advocating for social justice. Pope Leo XIV has made international diplomacy a cornerstone of his papacy, traveling to conflict zones and engaging with world leaders. His visit to France is seen as part of a broader strategy to reinforce the Church’s presence in Western Europe.

Analysts suggest the trip could influence France’s domestic policies on issues like abortion and religious education, areas where the Catholic Church has historically held strong positions. The Vatican’s ability to shape public discourse in secular France remains limited, but symbolic gestures like this visit can have outsized impact.

The Vatican has not announced whether the pope will meet with French bishops or issue any formal declarations during the visit. However, the trip is expected to include symbolic acts of reconciliation, particularly in light of recent tensions between the Church and the French state over secularism laws. The visit concludes with a public mass at the [Parc des Princes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parc_des_Princes) in Paris, organizers confirmed.
