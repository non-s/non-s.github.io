Dan Levy didn’t just act out a drug scene in Netflix’s ‘Big Mistakes’—he lived it. For a chaotic sequence involving cocaine, the show’s co-creator and star actually did “lines and lines” of the drug on set. The move wasn’t planned as a stunt. It was a creative choice to capture the raw, unfiltered energy of the moment. “We wanted it to feel real,” Levy told The Hollywood Reporter. “It’s not something we took lightly, but the scene demanded it.” The episode, titled “Big Mistakes,” quickly became a talking point for its bold approach to portraying substance use on screen.

The scene isn’t just a stunt—it’s a reflection of the show’s tone. ‘Big Mistakes’ is a dark comedy with sharp wit and unflinching honesty about messy lives. Levy, best known for his role in [Schitt’s Creek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schitt%27s_Creek), co-created the series with [Andrew Kushnir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Kushnir) and [Sarah Gadon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarah_Gadon). The show follows a group of friends in Toronto making questionable decisions, and Levy’s character is right in the thick of it. The cocaine scene fits the show’s theme: sometimes the worst choices feel the most real.

Levy isn’t just talking about the drug scene. He’s already teasing what’s next for season two. “It’s bigger, bolder, sexier,” he said. “We’re not holding back.” The first season leaned into chaos and dark humor, but season two is expected to push further into edgier territory. Levy hinted at more provocative storylines, including deeper dives into the characters’ flaws and risky behaviors. Fans can expect the show to escalate in intensity, with Levy’s character facing even tougher choices.

The show’s chaotic vibe didn’t just come from Levy’s risky filming choices. The entire cast and crew embraced the unpredictability. Levy described the set as “a pressure cooker of creativity,” where spontaneity often led to the best moments. The cocaine scene, in particular, was shot in one take. “We didn’t have time to overthink it,” he said. “That’s when the best performances happen.” The episode’s director, [Shane Belcourt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shane_Belcourt), worked closely with Levy to balance authenticity with safety. Crew members were briefed on harm reduction, and the drug used was a substitute, though Levy’s commitment to the scene’s realism blurred the line between acting and actual use.

For Levy, the scene was about more than shock value. It was a commentary on how people often romanticize risky behavior without seeing the consequences. “We wanted to show the mess behind the myth,” he said. “Cocaine isn’t glamorous. It’s chaotic and ugly, and that’s what we aimed for.” The episode’s portrayal of drug use sparked conversations online, with some praising the show’s boldness and others questioning the ethics of filming such a scene. Levy defended the choice, arguing that art sometimes requires pushing boundaries to make a point.

Season two is already in the works, with filming expected to begin later this year. Levy and his team are aiming for an even rawer, more unfiltered experience. “We’re not toning it down,” he said. “If anything, we’re doubling down.” The show’s first season established ‘Big Mistakes’ as a must-watch for fans of dark comedies with bite. Season two is poised to take that energy to the next level, with Levy promising more sex, more chaos, and more unapologetic choices. Whether it’s bigger, bolder, or simply more controversial, one thing’s clear: ‘Big Mistakes’ isn’t playing it safe.

If the first season’s reception is any indication, season two will likely spark more debate. The show’s unflinching look at flawed characters resonated with audiences tired of sanitized portrayals of messy lives. Levy’s willingness to take risks—both on and off screen—has set ‘Big Mistakes’ apart. As season two approaches, fans are already speculating about what’s next. Will the show dive deeper into addiction? Explore more extreme behaviors? Or simply escalate the chaos? One thing’s for sure: Dan Levy isn’t slowing down.
