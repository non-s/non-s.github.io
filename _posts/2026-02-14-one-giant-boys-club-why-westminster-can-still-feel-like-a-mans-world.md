Peter Mandelson’s surprise return to government as a top advisor has done more than shuffle the Westminster deck—it’s reminded everyone that some things haven’t changed. The former Labour minister, known for his sharp political instincts, joins a government where the top jobs still skew heavily male. His appointment isn’t breaking any records for gender representation. It’s just the latest reminder that despite years of promises and reforms, Westminster’s power structure looks a lot like it did in the 1980s. Women now make up 35% of MPs in the House of Commons. That’s progress from the 9% they held in 1992, but it’s still not close to parity. And when you look at who really runs things—the cabinet, the whips’ offices, the key committees—the imbalance is starker. Women hold just over a quarter of cabinet positions. In the 2019 parliament, only 34% of select committee chairs were women. These aren’t just numbers. They shape what gets debated, how policies get made, and whose voices get heard in the corridors where deals happen. It’s the classic ‘old boys’ network’ effect—even when women get in the room, they’re often not in the rooms where the real decisions are being made. That’s the dynamic Mandelson’s return highlights, whether it’s intentional or not. His appointment comes as Labour faces pressure to prove it’s serious about modernising politics. The party has more women MPs than the Conservatives—45% versus 26%—but even that gap hasn’t translated into proportional power. The shadow cabinet is 40% women, but only four of the top 20 roles go to women. It’s not just about who’s sitting on the green benches. It’s about who’s shaping the agenda. When all-male panels dominate TV news shows discussing political strategy, or when male MPs still outnumber women by 2:1 in prime-time political interviews, the message is clear: Westminster still rewards the same old faces. The 2019 intake of MPs was the most diverse in history, but diversity hasn’t yet broken through to the top tables. The Commons Library found that in 2023, women made up just 30% of MPs who spoke in debates on the floor of the House. Even in Labour’s ranks, where women MPs outnumber men in some cohorts, the senior leadership remains overwhelmingly male. This isn’t just about Mandelson himself. He’s been a fixture in British politics for 40 years, serving under Tony Blair and Gordon Brown. His return isn’t shocking—it’s the system that keeps recycling the same profiles that raises eyebrows. The real question isn’t why Mandelson is back. It’s why, after decades of campaigns, quotas, and pledges, the same names keep popping up in the same roles. The UK isn’t alone in this. Across Europe, women make up an average of 32% of parliamentarians. Sweden and Finland score higher, but even there, power isn’t evenly distributed. The difference in the UK is the concentration of power in the hands of a few. The prime minister’s office, the Treasury, the Foreign Office—they’re still the domains where men dominate. When Rishi Sunak became PM in 2022, he inherited a cabinet that was 60% male. His reshuffles didn’t change that much. The message to women isn’t that they’re not welcome—it’s that they’ve got to work twice as hard to get half as far. The silver lining? Public opinion is shifting faster than the corridors of power. Polls show most Britons now think it’s important to have equal representation in government. But polls don’t run governments. Until the people who hold the real levers of power decide that change matters more than comfort, Westminster will keep feeling like a club that’s still mostly for the boys.
