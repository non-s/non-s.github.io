The appointment of [Peter Mandelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Mandelson) to a senior government role has reignited scrutiny of gender representation in UK politics. Critics argue the decision underscores the persistent dominance of men in Westminster, despite recent efforts to address imbalances. Women currently hold just 35% of ministerial positions in the government, a figure unchanged since 2019, according to official data. The decision to bring back Mandelson, a long-time Labour figure and former EU Trade Commissioner, has been met with skepticism by advocates for gender equality in politics. They point to the lack of women in top-tier roles as evidence of systemic barriers that remain unaddressed.

## Gender gap in UK government remains wide
The underrepresentation of women in government is not new. A 2023 report by the [Fawcett Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fawcett_Society) found that women make up just 34% of MPs in the House of Commons, a figure that has barely shifted in decades. The report also highlighted that women from ethnic minority backgrounds are even more underrepresented, holding fewer than 10% of seats. Critics argue that recent appointments, including Mandelson’s, reflect a broader trend of overlooking qualified women in favor of returning male politicians, particularly those with established reputations.

The government has defended its record, pointing to initiatives aimed at increasing diversity. A spokesperson for [No 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10_Downing_Street) stated that the prime minister is committed to improving representation but acknowledged that progress has been slow. The spokesperson cited recent appointments of women to junior ministerial roles as evidence of incremental change. However, advocates argue that these measures fall short of addressing the structural issues that keep women out of top positions.

## Calls for urgent action grow louder
Pressure is mounting on the government to take concrete steps to address the gender imbalance. The [Women and Equalities Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_and_Equalities_Select_Committee) has called for mandatory targets to ensure gender parity in ministerial appointments. Committee chair Caroline Nokes MP said, “Without clear targets, progress will remain painfully slow.” Other campaigners have gone further, demanding quotas to ensure women are represented in proportion to their numbers in the population.

The debate comes at a time when public confidence in politics is already low. A 2024 poll by [YouGov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YouGov) found that 62% of respondents believe the government does not represent the diversity of the UK population. Women, in particular, expressed dissatisfaction, with 70% saying they feel underrepresented. The government’s response has been to point to its record on other diversity metrics, such as the number of LGBTQ+ and disabled MPs, but critics argue these efforts are not enough to address the core issue of gender inequality.

## What happens next?
The government has not indicated whether it will adopt the committee’s recommendations or introduce new measures to address the gender gap. Meanwhile, Mandelson’s appointment has already sparked renewed calls for reform from advocacy groups. The [50:50 Parliament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/50:50_Parliament) campaign, which campaigns for equal representation, has urged the prime minister to take immediate action. “We cannot afford to wait another decade for progress,” said campaign founder Frances Scott. The government’s next reshuffle, expected later this year, will be closely watched for signs of change.
