The appointment of former Labour minister [Peter Mandelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Mandelson) to the House of Lords has brought renewed scrutiny to Westminster’s persistent gender imbalance. Despite pledges to improve representation, men still occupy roughly 70% of parliamentary seats across both the Commons and Lords, according to 2024 figures. The latest appointment follows a pattern where male politicians—often with decades of experience—continue to dominate top roles while women remain underrepresented. Critics argue this reflects deeper structural issues in politics and party selection processes. The Conservative Party, currently in power, has only 25% female MPs, while Labour, the largest opposition party, fares slightly better at 39% following its 2024 intake. ## Gender gap persists despite equality laws While laws like the 2010 Equality Act sought to address workplace discrimination, Westminster’s culture and selection processes have lagged behind. A 2023 report by the Fawcett Society found that progress in increasing female representation has stalled since 2019, with parties struggling to field enough women candidates in winnable seats. The report highlighted that childcare responsibilities, media scrutiny and internal party biases contribute to the disparity. ## High-profile appointments draw criticism Mandelson’s elevation to the Lords joins a long list of male heavyweights in senior roles, including [Rishi Sunak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rishi_Sunak), the UK’s first Asian prime minister, and [Keir Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer), who leads the opposition Labour Party. Their appointments contrast with the experiences of women like [Dame Margaret Hodge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Hodge), who faced sustained criticism during her tenure as an MP, or [Yvette Cooper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvette_Cooper), who has repeatedly called out the gender pay gap in politics. ## Party selection processes under fire The lack of progress has led to calls for mandatory quotas for female candidates. Parties currently set their own targets, but critics say these are inconsistently enforced. The Liberal Democrats, for example, have achieved near parity in their parliamentary party, while the Conservatives trail significantly. ## Broader implications for democracy Analysts warn that the imbalance undermines public trust in democracy, with women making up 51% of the UK population but just 30% of lawmakers. The issue has gained traction ahead of the next general election, where parties are under pressure to demonstrate progress. Campaign groups like 50:50 Parliament continue to push for structural changes, arguing that without intervention, meaningful equality may remain decades away.
