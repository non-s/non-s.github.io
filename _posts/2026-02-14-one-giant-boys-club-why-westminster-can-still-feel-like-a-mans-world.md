The appointment of [Peter Mandelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Mandelson) to a senior government role has reignited scrutiny over why Westminster politics still feels like a male preserve. With women holding just 35% of UK ministerial positions—a figure unchanged since 2014—the gap between political rhetoric and reality remains stark.

## Gender balance in UK politics remains stagnant
Officials point to the 35% figure as evidence that progress on gender representation has stalled. Despite repeated commitments from political parties to improve diversity, the proportion of women in ministerial roles has barely moved in a decade. The current figure places the UK behind several European peers on ministerial gender balance.

Critics argue that structural barriers persist in Westminster, where informal networks and traditional power structures favor men. The recent appointment of Mandelson, a long-serving Labour figure, has drawn particular attention because of his prominence in a party that has consistently pledged to advance gender equality.

## Parties struggle to meet diversity targets
Both major UK parties have set targets for gender balance in their parliamentary groups, yet neither has achieved consistent progress. The Labour Party, which has historically had higher female representation, still falls short of its own goals. The Conservative Party, which has faced repeated criticism over its gender imbalance, has seen only marginal improvements.

Analysis of ministerial appointments over the past decade shows that women are more likely to hold junior roles or roles with less direct influence. Senior cabinet positions, particularly those with economic or security portfolios, remain overwhelmingly male-dominated.

## Cultural barriers persist in Westminster
Former MPs and political analysts describe a culture where informal networking often takes place in male-dominated spaces such as gentlemen’s clubs and private clubs. These environments can exclude women, particularly those with caring responsibilities or from less privileged backgrounds. The recent resignation of a senior female minister over workplace culture issues has added to concerns.

The issue extends beyond ministerial roles. Women make up just 34% of UK MPs, a figure that has risen only slowly over the past 20 years. Campaign groups argue that without systemic changes, progress will continue to lag.

## What happens next?
The government has pledged to review diversity in public appointments, but campaigners say concrete action is needed. Some call for mandatory quotas, while others urge parties to reform their selection processes. The debate shows no sign of abating as the next general election approaches.

For now, Westminster’s male dominance endures, with little evidence of the rapid change many advocates have demanded.
