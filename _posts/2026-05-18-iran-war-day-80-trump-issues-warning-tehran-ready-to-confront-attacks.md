The standoff between the US, Israel, and Iran is getting sharper by the hour. On Sunday, Iranian Defense Ministry spokesperson Reza Talaei-Nik told reporters Iran’s military is “fully prepared” for any new aggression from Washington or Tel Aviv. That came just hours after Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that the “clock is ticking” for Iran to end the war. “For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there won’t be anything left of them,” he wrote. “TIME IS OF THE ESSENCE!” The warning signals a major escalation could be coming soon, likely targeting Iran’s energy infrastructure if talks stall. Trump’s posts follow reports that the US and Israel are planning air strikes on Iranian oil and gas facilities. Iran has repeatedly called such threats empty bluster, but the rhetoric is now louder and more specific. The country’s military leaders say they won’t back down, even if it means direct confrontation with the US or Israel. This isn’t just talk. Over the weekend, Saudi Arabia confirmed it shot down three drones near the United Arab Emirates’ [Barakah Nuclear Energy Plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barakah_Nuclear_Energy_Plant). The UAE reported a drone strike on the plant on Saturday, raising fears of a wider regional spillover. The incident came just days after Iran-backed Houthi rebels in Yemen claimed responsibility for a deadly attack on a cargo ship in the Red Sea. The war isn’t just burning in Iran. Israel has kept up its bombing campaign in southern Lebanon, despite what both sides call an “extended ceasefire.” The truce has done little to stop the strikes, with Israeli warplanes hitting targets across the border almost daily. Lebanon’s government says the attacks have killed over 200 people and displaced more than 100,000 since October. The fragile “ceasefire” looks like a paper tiger to many on the ground. US officials insist they’re still pushing for a diplomatic solution, but the military buildup tells another story. The US has sent two aircraft carrier strike groups to the Persian Gulf, along with B-52 bombers and additional fighter jets. Israel, meanwhile, has mobilized reservists and moved heavy armor closer to the Lebanese border. The tension isn’t just about Iran’s nuclear program anymore. It’s also about who controls the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow waterway through which a third of the world’s oil passes. Iran has threatened to block the strait if it faces severe attacks, a move that would send global oil prices soaring. The UAE and Saudi Arabia are watching closely. Both countries have intercepted drones in recent days, signaling they’re preparing for the worst. The Iran war’s 80th day shows no signs of cooling. If anything, the threats are getting bolder, the strikes are getting louder, and the risks of a wider conflict are growing by the minute. The question now isn’t just whether a deal can be struck—it’s whether either side will blink before the first shot is fired.
