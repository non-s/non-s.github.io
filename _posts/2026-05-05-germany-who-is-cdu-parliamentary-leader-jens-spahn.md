Jens Spahn still has the itch to climb. At 45, the [CDU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)) firebrand just won his second term as leader of his party’s parliamentary group, collecting 85% of the vote in a secret ballot this week. The job puts him in charge of the biggest opposition bloc in Germany’s parliament, the Bundestag, and puts him one step closer to a chancellery run he’s quietly dreamed about for years. That ambition hasn’t dimmed, even after scandals, investigations, and the kind of polling numbers that usually end careers. This isn’t some quiet backbencher’s promotion. Spahn’s climb has been loud, combative, and full of sharp elbows from the start. He was only 35 when he became Germany’s health minister in 2018, the youngest in modern history. The pandemic handed him the biggest crisis of his career, and he stepped into it with the kind of confidence that made allies nervous and critics furious. The job also left him under a legal cloud. State prosecutors investigated him over whether he misused public funds to promote a book deal during the pandemic. Those charges were eventually dropped, but the shadow lingered. For a politician who’s spent his career selling himself as the future of the right, the stain didn’t stick. Not yet, anyway. Not enough to stop his party from handing him the top job in parliament anyway. That tells you something about the CDU right now. The party’s been in the political wilderness since Angela Merkel’s 2021 exit, and the bench isn’t deep. Friedrich Merz, the party boss, is polling worse than Spahn, which isn’t a great look for either man. Merz survived a leadership challenge from Spahn in 2018, but the party’s base clearly hasn’t forgotten the bruises. Spahn’s rise has been a mix of raw talent and ruthless calculation. He cut his teeth in Merkel’s CDU, but he’s always been more of a Merkel critic than a Merkel loyalist. When she pushed Annegret Kramp-Karrenbauer as party leader in 2018, Spahn openly challenged her. He lost decisively, but the fight proved he wasn’t going anywhere. After years of playing second fiddle in the party, he’s now calling the shots in parliament. That’s power, even if it feels a bit like inheriting a sinking ship. His leadership style is blunt, direct, and often polarizing. He’s not the kind of politician who wins over doubters with charm. He wins by being loud, by taking clear positions, and by making sure everyone knows where he stands. That’s worked for him in the past, but the numbers suggest it might not be enough anymore. A recent poll put Spahn at the bottom of Germany’s popularity rankings, below Merz and even some far-left figures. That’s not a death knell, but it’s a warning sign. The CDU’s base is aging, its message is stale in some quarters, and the party’s struggling to connect with younger voters who don’t remember Merkel’s era. Spahn’s trying to fix that. He’s pushing a harder line on immigration, climate policy, and Germany’s role in Europe. He’s also been a vocal critic of the current government’s handling of the economy and energy crisis. But so far, the public isn’t buying it. The next federal election isn’t until 2029, but the CDU’s already gearing up for a fight. Spahn’s parliamentary leadership is a test case. If he can turn the party’s fortunes around, he’ll have a shot at the top job. If he can’t, his political career might stall right here. For now, Spahn’s playing the long game. He’s been written off before, only to bounce back stronger. He’s outlasted rivals, survived scandals, and kept his ambitions alive. But the clock is ticking. Germany’s voters are restless, and the CDU’s running out of time to prove it still has a future.
