Mexico’s government announced a crackdown on industrial pollution Thursday, slapping a $4.8 million fine on Zinc Nacional [Zinc Nacional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_Nacional), a factory in Monterrey that processes toxic waste from the US. The move follows investigations by the Guardian and Mexico’s Quinto Elemento Lab, which found dangerously high levels of arsenic, lead, and cadmium in soil and water near the plant. Those results forced Mexico’s environmental agency to act after years of complaints from locals about health issues like rashes and respiratory problems. The fine is the largest ever issued under Mexico’s hazardous waste laws, signaling a rare but serious response to corporate pollution that’s long plagued the country’s industrial heartland. Zinc Nacional, a major supplier of zinc and other metals to US manufacturers, had operated for decades with minimal oversight despite documented violations. Mexico’s environment minister confirmed the plant violated at least six environmental regulations, including improper storage of toxic sludge and unpermitted waste disposal. Neighbors say they’ve lived with the stench of burning chemicals for years and report a spike in childhood asthma cases near the factory. One mother, who asked not to be named, said her 8-year-old son was diagnosed with lead poisoning last year after playing outside daily. “We thought it was just the smell,” she said. “Now we know it’s poisoning us slow.” Government officials didn’t respond to questions about whether Zinc Nacional will face criminal charges or be forced to shut down pending cleanup efforts. The fine alone won’t fix the decades of contamination, but it’s a start. For context, Monterrey’s air quality has ranked among the worst in North America for years, with industrial zones like this one contributing heavily to pollution linked to heart disease and lung cancer. The region’s economy depends on manufacturing goods for US brands, creating a tension between jobs and health. ## New air monitors coming online next month Officials also revealed plans for a $2.3 million network of 50 air-quality monitors across Monterrey’s industrial belt. The system, expected to go live in October, will stream real-time data on pollutants like sulfur dioxide and fine particulate matter. The data will be publicly accessible through a government website, something activists have demanded for years. Environmental groups say the move is long overdue. “We’ve been begging for this for a decade,” said Ana Contreras, a spokeswoman for Monterrey’s Clean Air Coalition. “Now at least we’ll know what we’re breathing.” The monitors won’t immediately reduce pollution, but they’ll help identify the worst offenders and pressure companies to clean up. Some industry leaders argue the new rules will hurt business. “We’re already struggling with US tariffs and energy costs,” said Carlos Gómez, a spokesman for the Monterrey Business Council. “Adding more regulations could push factories to relocate.” Others counter that pollution is costing the region more in healthcare and lost productivity. Studies estimate air pollution in Monterrey causes 4,000 premature deaths annually. Meanwhile, Zinc Nacional’s owners haven’t commented publicly since the fine was announced. The company’s website still boasts about its “sustainable practices” and “commitment to the environment.” Critics say such claims ring hollow given the violations. The government’s next steps are unclear. Will Zinc Nacional be allowed to reopen after paying the fine, or will it face stricter controls? Will other polluting factories in the region also face consequences? For now, the fine and the monitors are the biggest signs yet that Mexico’s environmental enforcement might be turning a corner. But activists warn it’s only the first step. “This won’t fix the damage done,” said Contreras. “It’s like putting a bandage on a bullet wound.”

## 🇧🇷 Resumo em Português

O México aplica uma multa milionária de US$ 4,8 milhões em uma fábrica tóxica após investigações revelarem perigos ocultos em resíduos industriais na região de Monterrey. A decisão do governo mexicano surge em resposta a denúncias que expuseram como toneladas de dejetos químicos perigosos, muitas vezes originários dos Estados Unidos, eram descartadas de forma irregular, colocando em risco a saúde de milhares de pessoas e o meio ambiente.

A investigação do *The Guardian* mostrou que o descarte inadequado de resíduos tóxicos, inclusive com presença de chumbo, cádmio e outros metais pesados, afetava não só o solo e os recursos hídricos de Monterrey, como também colocava em xeque a aplicação de leis ambientais no país. Para o Brasil, o caso serve como alerta sobre os riscos do comércio irregular de resíduos entre fronteiras e a necessidade de fiscalização rigorosa, especialmente em regiões industriais densas. Além disso, reforça a importância de acordos internacionais para evitar que países exportem sua poluição, um problema que já foi alvo de críticas no Mercosul.

A multa aplicada é um sinal de que governos começam a endurecer contra crimes ambientais, mas especialistas cobram ações mais rápidas, como a recuperação das áreas contaminadas e o endurecimento de leis para evitar novos casos.
