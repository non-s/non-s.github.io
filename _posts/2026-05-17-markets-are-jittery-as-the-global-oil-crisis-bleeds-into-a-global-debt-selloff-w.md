Stock futures slipped Sunday as investors woke up to a harsh reality: the Strait of Hormuz is still closed, oil markets are tightening, and there’s no immediate fix in sight. Dow Jones futures fell 174 points, or 0.35%, while the S&P 500 and Nasdaq futures dropped 0.26% and 0.32% respectively. U.S. oil futures rose 1.75% to $107.26 a barrel, and Brent crude climbed 1.32% to $110.70. Gold, often a haven in crises, slipped 0.37% to $4,545 an ounce. The dollar gained ground too, up 0.09% against the euro and 0.06% against the yen. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed 1.6 basis points to 4.611%, a sign that investors are pricing in higher borrowing costs ahead. None of this happened overnight. Last Friday’s U.S.-China summit ended with no breakthrough on the Strait of Hormuz, the world’s most critical oil choke point. About 20% of global oil passes through this narrow strip of water between Oman and Iran. Without an agreement, the supply squeeze is tightening, and markets are reacting. Traders aren’t just worried about fuel. They’re bracing for a ripple effect that could hit everything from corporate profits to household budgets. The failure to reopen the strait means oil prices stay high, inflation risks rise, and central banks like the Federal Reserve face a tougher balancing act. It’s a domino effect that’s already starting to take shape. The U.S. dollar’s strength adds another layer of pressure. A stronger dollar makes oil, priced in dollars, more expensive for countries that use other currencies. That includes many in Europe and Asia, where economies are already struggling with high interest rates and weak growth. Central banks like the European Central Bank and the Bank of Japan might have to hold off on cutting rates, even if inflation cools, just to defend their currencies. Meanwhile, the Treasury yield jump tells us investors expect higher U.S. borrowing costs. That could mean higher mortgage rates, business loans, and credit card bills down the road. For a government that’s already running trillion-dollar deficits, every extra basis point on the 10-year note adds to the strain. The gold price drop is a bit counterintuitive, given the usual fear trade. But it makes sense if you look at the dollar’s surge. A stronger greenback reduces gold’s appeal since the metal doesn’t pay interest and isn’t as attractive when cash is king. That’s exactly what’s happening now. ## Trump eyes military moves as Iran tensions escalate Iran isn’t just a bystander in this crisis. The country has repeatedly threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz in response to sanctions and military pressure. Now, with oil shipments stuck and prices climbing, the White House is weighing new military options. President Trump has been vocal about his willingness to use force to protect U.S. interests in the region. His administration is considering a mix of naval patrols, airstrikes on Iranian oil infrastructure, and even covert operations to disrupt Tehran’s ability to threaten shipping lanes. The stakes are high. A military miscalculation could spiral into a full-blown conflict that drags in global powers. Even a limited strike risks triggering Iranian retaliation, which could include attacks on oil tankers or missile strikes on U.S. bases in the Gulf. But doing nothing also carries risks. Prolonged oil disruptions could tip the global economy into a deeper slowdown, especially in energy-importing countries like India and Japan. The U.S. is already grappling with inflation above 3%, and another oil shock would make the Fed’s job even harder. ## Foreign lenders eye U.S. debt as borrowing costs rise One of the quieter but more dangerous side effects of this crisis is the growing wariness among foreign holders of U.S. debt. Countries like Japan and China, the top foreign holders of Treasury bonds, have been trimming their holdings for months. Now, with oil prices surging and the dollar strengthening, some are considering dumping more Treasurys to bring cash back home. That could push borrowing costs even higher for the U.S. government and American consumers. Japan holds about $1.1 trillion in U.S. debt, while China owns roughly $770 billion. If even a fraction of that money heads for the exits, it would force the Treasury to offer higher yields to attract buyers. That means higher interest rates for mortgages, car loans, and student debt. It also puts pressure on the Fed, which has been trying to ease rates to support the economy. If foreign sales accelerate, the central bank might have to pause or even reverse its rate cuts, keeping financial conditions tighter for longer. ## What happens next? The next few days will be critical. If the Strait of Hormuz stays closed, oil prices could push past $120 a barrel, adding another inflationary shock to the global economy. That would force central banks to keep rates high for longer, hurting growth and jobs. Investors are already pricing in a higher chance of a recession in 2025. Meanwhile, the U.S. and China are under pressure to find a diplomatic solution. Both sides have economic incentives to avoid a full-blown conflict, but neither wants to appear weak. The risk is that neither side blinks, and the crisis drags on. For now, markets are in wait-and-see mode. But with oil supplies tight and military options on the table, the stakes couldn’t be higher. The next move could come from Tehran, Washington, or Beijing. Whoever acts first will shape the next phase of this global crisis.
