Donald Trump and China’s leader Xi Jinping [Xi Jinping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Jinping) wrapped up two days of talks in Washington with a batch of deals that will boost U.S. exports and lock in new investments. Boeing [Boeing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing) landed orders for at least 260 jets, worth roughly $37 billion, while American farmers secured deals for soybeans and pork that could top $20 billion over the next few years. The White House called it the biggest trade package China has ever signed with a foreign country. Both sides framed the pacts as proof that the U.S. and China can cooperate even as they compete on technology and military power.

In the same room, Trump and Xi also put Iran’s nuclear ambitions on the table. They issued a joint statement vowing to block Tehran from getting a bomb and to keep the Strait of Hormuz open for shipping. Iran’s oil still flows through those waters, and any disruption could spike global fuel prices. The agreement doesn’t include sanctions, but it signals Washington and Beijing will at least try to speak with one voice on the issue. Energy traders took notice: oil prices dipped slightly after the announcement.

What didn’t make the White House fact sheet was Taiwan. The island, which China claims as its own, has been a flashpoint for decades. Trump had hinted days earlier that he might reconsider U.S. arms sales to Taipei, a move Beijing would see as a major provocation. Yet when the official summary of the talks dropped, Taiwan wasn’t mentioned—not once. The omission is striking because it’s the first time in years that a U.S.-China summit left out the issue entirely. Analysts say it could mean Trump chose to keep the conversation quiet or that Beijing pressed hard to keep it off the record.

The silence on Taiwan reflects the tightrope Trump is walking. He’s pushed China hard on trade and tech, but he also needs Beijing’s help on North Korea and Iran. Selling advanced weapons to Taipei would anger Beijing, which has warned Washington for years not to interfere. Trump’s team insists the U.S. still supports Taiwan’s defense, but they’re not shouting it from the rooftops right now. Meanwhile, Taipei’s government said nothing publicly, though diplomats there are watching every word.

China’s state media framed the trip as a win for cooperation. They praised the trade deals and the Iran agreement, calling it proof that Beijing can work with Washington without giving up its core interests. But the missing Taiwan line shows how sensitive the topic remains. Every U.S. president since the 1970s has sold some weapons to Taiwan under the Taiwan Relations Act, but the timing and scale can shift with each administration. Trump’s silence now could be a signal—or just a pause.

The broader fallout matters more than the headlines. U.S. and Chinese companies will start inking contracts in the next few weeks, and farmers in Iowa and Illinois are already dusting off their order books. But the real test comes later. If Iran tests the Strait of Hormuz or if Beijing ramps up pressure on Taipei, Trump’s quiet approach may not hold. For now, the deals are done, the pictures are posted, and the missing Taiwan line is the only thing anyone’s really talking about.
