Operators of over 100 planned UK datacentres have asked to connect to the gas network to generate their own electricity instead of waiting for the overstretched National Grid. The requests add up to 15 terawatt hours per year—enough to power about 3 million homes—which means these facilities could pump extra carbon into the atmosphere if they start burning gas permanently. The situation has raised eyebrows in Whitehall, where officials admit the delays have forced some datacentre owners to take drastic measures just to keep their servers running.

## Why datacentres are choosing gas over the grid

Datacentres need huge amounts of reliable power 24/7, and waiting years for a grid connection isn’t an option. The National Grid has been overwhelmed by demand from new datacentres, renewables projects, and industrial expansions. In some cases, companies have been told they’ll have to wait until 2030 or later just to plug in. That’s why so many are now applying for gas connections, even though it’s a step backward for the UK’s climate ambitions. The government set a target to cut carbon emissions by 78% by 2035 compared to 1990 levels, but burning gas for datacentre power could make that goal much harder to hit.

Officials say they’re stuck between a rock and a hard place. The grid’s slow upgrades can’t keep up with demand, so some operators are effectively being pushed toward fossil fuels. One senior civil servant called the situation an "interesting question" about the UK’s climate plans, hinting that the government may have to rethink its approach to datacentre energy if it wants to meet its targets.

## The climate cost of waiting for the grid

The 15TWh of annual gas demand from datacentres isn’t just a drop in the bucket—it’s a significant chunk of the UK’s total energy use. For comparison, the entire country consumed about 230TWh of gas in 2023 for all purposes. If these datacentres start burning gas, their emissions could add up quickly, especially since many are designed to run around the clock. Even temporary gas use could lock in higher carbon output for years, making it tougher for the UK to phase out fossil fuels.

Some industry watchers argue the government should have acted sooner to speed up grid upgrades. The National Grid’s own projections show that without faster investment, the UK could face power shortages by the mid-2020s. But critics say the delays reflect deeper problems—bureaucracy, underfunding, and a lack of coordination between energy policy and digital infrastructure.

## What happens next for datacentres and the grid

The government now faces a tough choice: speed up grid connections to avoid more gas use, or accept that some datacentres will keep burning fossil fuels. The first option would require fast-tracking billions in infrastructure spending and cutting red tape, something that’s rarely happened in the past. The second option risks making the UK’s climate targets even harder to meet.

For datacentre operators, the situation is a brutal reality check. Many of these facilities are being built to support AI, cloud computing, and big data—industries that the UK government has actively encouraged. Yet the energy supply isn’t keeping pace. Some companies are already looking at alternatives, like on-site renewables or battery storage, but these solutions are expensive and still years away from being scalable.

The National Grid has promised to speed up connections, but progress has been slow. In the meantime, the UK’s energy landscape is getting more complicated. More gas, less certainty, and a big question mark over how the country plans to power its digital future without wrecking its climate goals.
