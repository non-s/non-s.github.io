France’s top justice official [Gérald Darmanin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rald_Darmanin) landed in Algiers Monday morning with one goal: get Algeria to stop the DZ Mafia from calling the shots on Europe’s drug trade from across the Mediterranean. Intelligence reports claim the group’s top leaders are living freely in Algeria, directing cocaine and cannabis shipments bound for France and Belgium while avoiding European arrest warrants. French prosecutors say DZ Mafia’s bosses have been running the operation from safe houses in Algiers for at least two years, moving tons of drugs through the port of Oran and small boats along the Algerian coast.## France wants Algeria to act France is treating Darmanin’s two-day trip as a make-or-break moment. Algerian courts have blocked past extradition requests from France, arguing their constitution bars handing over nationals. But Paris is betting Algeria’s president [Abdelmadjid Tebboune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelmadjid_Tebboune) is under enough pressure from Europe’s drug surge to loosen the rules. Darmanin’s team is pushing for a new judicial cooperation treaty that would let French investigators enter Algeria to gather evidence and arrest suspects without waiting for Algerian approval.## Evidence points to Algeria’s soil Algerian police have seized only a handful of mid-level DZ Mafia figures since 2022, while the gang’s profits have surged. French customs intercepted 21 tons of cocaine hidden in containers from Algeria last year—almost double the 2022 total. Prosecutors say the gang uses encrypted apps like Sky ECC to coordinate shipments and launder money through real estate in Marseille and Brussels.## Europe’s drug war is spilling over Algeria’s refusal to hand over suspects has frustrated French officials for years. Last spring, a Paris court sentenced six Algerian nationals in absentia to life for running a 70-million-euro cocaine network, but none are behind bars. French police say the gang’s current boss, a man known only as ‘The Engineer,’ is believed to be living in a villa outside Algiers, protected by local fixers tied to Algeria’s security apparatus.## What’s at stake for both sides Algeria relies on France for trade and investment, but public anger over drug deaths is rising. President [Tebboune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdelmadjid_Tebboune) faces protests after 34 people died from tainted ecstasy in Oran last month. Darmanin’s visit comes as France prepares to deploy 500 extra police to the Paris banlieues, where gang wars have killed 22 people this year.## Next steps could come fast If Algeria agrees to joint raids or extraditions, French commandos and judges could move within weeks. But if talks stall, France may impose travel bans on Algerian officials linked to the gang, a move that could freeze a $4 billion trade deal signed last month. Either way, the pressure is on Darmanin to show results before he flies back to Paris Wednesday night.
