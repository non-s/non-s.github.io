RAMALLAH, West Bank — The Fatah faction of the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) held a rare meeting Tuesday to elect its top decision-makers, a move seen as an attempt to address deepening public dissatisfaction revealed in recent polls. The gathering comes as surveys indicate declining trust in Fatah’s leadership among ordinary Palestinians, who criticize the party for perceived detachment from their daily struggles and political stagnation.

Analysts say the meeting reflects internal pressure within Fatah to revitalize its image and regain public support. Party officials framed the event as a step toward unity, but critics argue it may be too late to reverse growing skepticism about Fatah’s ability to address Palestinian demands for statehood and economic stability. The faction, led by [Mahmoud Abbas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmoud_Abbas), has faced mounting criticism over its failure to hold elections and its handling of the Gaza crisis.

## Fatah’s leadership faces scrutiny as polls show disillusionment

Public opinion surveys, including those conducted by Palestinian research centers, reveal a sharp decline in support for Fatah. A recent poll by the [Palestinian Center for Policy and Survey Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palestinian_Center_for_Policy_and_Survey_Research) found that only 34% of Palestinians view Fatah favorably, down from 42% six months ago. The decline is particularly pronounced among younger voters, who increasingly favor alternative factions, including Hamas and smaller leftist groups.

Critics accuse Fatah’s leadership of prioritizing internal power struggles over addressing pressing issues like unemployment, corruption, and stalled peace negotiations with Israel. The party’s refusal to hold legislative elections since 2006 has further fueled public frustration, with many Palestinians viewing Fatah as an entrenched, unresponsive establishment.

## Meeting aims to shore up legitimacy but faces skepticism

Fatah’s leadership has defended the meeting as a necessary step to strengthen the party’s structure and prepare for future challenges. [Mahmoud Abbas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmoud_Abbas), who has led Fatah and the Palestinian Authority since 2005, remains the dominant figure, but his advanced age and declining health have raised questions about the party’s future direction. The meeting is expected to solidify the influence of Abbas’s inner circle while sidelining potential rivals.

Yet the optics of the gathering are unlikely to mollify critics who argue that cosmetic changes will not address systemic issues. The West Bank-based Palestinian Authority, dominated by Fatah, continues to face accusations of authoritarianism and ineffectiveness, further eroding public confidence. Meanwhile, Hamas, which controls Gaza, has capitalized on Fatah’s perceived weaknesses by positioning itself as a more assertive alternative.

## Broader implications for Palestinian politics

The outcome of Fatah’s meeting could have significant consequences for Palestinian politics, particularly as pressure mounts for reforms and accountability. The party’s ability to regain public trust may hinge on its willingness to address corruption allegations, improve governance, and engage in meaningful dialogue with rival factions. Failure to do so risks further fragmentation within Palestinian ranks and growing support for more militant groups.

For now, Fatah’s leadership appears intent on consolidating power internally rather than addressing external demands for change. But with polls showing discontent at record levels, the party’s legitimacy is increasingly at risk, raising questions about its long-term viability as a unifying force in Palestinian politics.
