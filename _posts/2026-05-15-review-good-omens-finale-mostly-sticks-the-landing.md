Prime Video’s *Good Omens* finale finally arrived after three years of waiting, but the 90-minute single episode didn’t quite stick the landing. The first half of the finale feels chaotic and rushed, cutting corners that leave the story undercooked. Based on Neil Gaiman and Terry Pratchett’s beloved 1990 novel, the series follows angel [Aziraphale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aziraphale) (Michael Sheen) and demon [Crowley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crowley_(Good_Omens)) (David Tennant) as they team up to stop Armageddon. After two solid seasons, the final episode tries to wrap everything up in one go, which backfires in places.

The biggest problem isn’t the source material—it’s the pacing. The first half of the finale jumps between subplots without enough breathing room, leaving key moments feeling rushed. The show’s trademark humor and wit take a backseat to exposition, and the stakes don’t always land. For fans who’ve waited years for this conclusion, the uneven delivery is a letdown. But once the episode hits its stride, the magic of Tennant and Sheen’s chemistry returns, pulling the story back from the brink.

## The core duo carries the finale

David Tennant and Michael Sheen have always been the heart of *Good Omens*, and the finale proves why. Their banter, chemistry, and effortless timing feel as fresh as ever, even when the plotting around them falters. The two actors elevate scenes that would otherwise feel undercooked, turning a shaky narrative into something watchable. Their scenes together are where the show’s soul lives, and the finale leans on that strength to keep the story afloat.

The finale also doubles down on its themes of friendship and unlikely alliances. Aziraphale and Crowley’s bond has always been the show’s emotional core, and the final episode leans into that, even if the execution stumbles. The stakes are high—Armageddon looms—but the personal stakes between the two feel more compelling than the apocalyptic scale. It’s a reminder that the best stories aren’t about the end of the world, but about the people who matter most.

## What worked and what didn’t

The finale’s biggest win is its commitment to the source material. Neil Gaiman, who co-wrote the original novel with [Terry Pratchett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Pratchett), helped adapt the series, and the finale stays true to their vision. Moments like the expanded role of [Newton Pulsifer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_Pulsifer) and [Anathema Device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anathema_Device) add depth, even if some threads feel underdeveloped. The show’s visual flair—from the neon-lit Hell to the cozy bookstore Aziraphale calls home—also shines, giving the finale a distinct, stylish look.

But the pacing kills the momentum. The first half rushes through key plot points, leaving little room for character moments or humor. Scenes that should feel climactic instead feel crammed, and the emotional beats don’t land as hard as they should. The finale’s biggest misstep is trying to do too much in too little time, sacrificing what made *Good Omens* special in the first place.

## What’s next for Good Omens

With the finale out, the *Good Omens* story is over—for now. There’s no word on a spin-off or continuation, but the door is left open for more adventures. David Tennant and Michael Sheen have both moved on to other projects, and the show’s creative team has hinted at other collaborations in the future. For now, fans can rewatch the series or revisit the novel, which expands on the world in ways the show couldn’t. The finale might not be perfect, but it’s a satisfying enough end to a beloved series.

The show’s legacy will likely rest on its strengths: the chemistry between Tennant and Sheen, the sharp humor, and the heart beneath the chaos. Flaws and all, *Good Omens* remains a standout adaptation, proving that even imperfect finales can leave a lasting impression.
