Shares of Mizuho Financial Group [Mizuho Financial Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mizuho_Financial_Group) slid more than 7% in Tokyo trading Monday after the bank pushed back on reports that it was reconsidering a stake in Rakuten Bank. The denial came just one day after Nikkei Asia reported that Mizuho was examining alternatives to its planned investment in the digital lender, which is Japan’s largest internet-only bank. The news sent investors scrambling, wiping about $1.2 billion off the bank’s market value in a single session. Investors clearly didn’t like the uncertainty around a deal they’d been watching closely for months. Mizuho, one of Japan’s three megabanks, has been in talks with Rakuten Group [Rakuten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakuten) for months about taking a minority stake in Rakuten Bank. The goal was to deepen ties in digital banking and compete with rivals like [MUFG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitsubishi_UFJ_Financial_Group) and [SMBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumitomo_Mitsui_Financial_Group), which have been aggressively expanding their own digital platforms. But on Monday, Mizuho issued a rare statement saying it hasn’t made a final call on the investment. “No decision has been made regarding investment in Rakuten Bank,” the bank said in a filing to the Tokyo Stock Exchange. The statement didn’t explain why the denial came so suddenly or what factors might be delaying a decision. That left analysts and investors guessing. Many had assumed the deal was all but done, given the months of negotiations and the strategic fit. Rakuten Bank has grown fast since launching in 2019, now serving over 3 million customers. It’s built a reputation for slick mobile apps and quick loan approvals, which has put pressure on traditional banks to step up their digital game. ## Mizuho’s stakes are high in digital push While Mizuho insists no decision is final, the stakes are high for both sides. For Mizuho, investing in Rakuten Bank would give it a direct foothold in Japan’s fast-growing digital banking market. The country’s megabanks have struggled to keep up with the speed of change, and partnerships like this are seen as a way to catch up. Rakuten, meanwhile, gets a cash infusion and a powerful ally in its push to expand beyond e-commerce into financial services. The Japanese government has been pushing banks to modernize, and deals like this fit that agenda. But not everyone’s convinced this is the right move. Some analysts argue Mizuho is already spread too thin with its digital ambitions. The bank has poured billions into tech upgrades and new products, but profits in its retail banking unit have lagged. Others worry about the optics: Mizuho walked away from a similar deal with [Seven Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Bank) last year after months of talks, leaving some to question whether it can close any big digital banking deals. ## What happens next The timing of Mizuho’s denial is tricky. The Nikkei report surfaced late Friday, and by Monday morning, the stock was already under pressure. Investors hate uncertainty, especially in Japan’s notoriously cautious market. Mizuho has until late November to file its next earnings report, and the bank is likely to face tough questions about its strategy. The bank’s CEO, [Tadashi Kuroda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadashi_Kuroda), has made digital banking a priority, but he’s also under pressure to deliver steady profits. Rakuten, for its part, isn’t likely to wait forever. The company has been clear about its goal to become a top financial services player in Japan, and it’s already in talks with other potential partners. If Mizuho drags its feet too long, Rakuten could walk away and take a deal elsewhere. For now, the market’s reaction shows how sensitive investors are to any sign of hesitation in Japan’s banking sector. The country’s megabanks are under the microscope as they try to prove they can keep up with the pace of change in global finance. Mizuho’s next move will be closely watched—not just by shareholders, but by rivals, regulators, and customers alike.
