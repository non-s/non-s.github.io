Christopher Nolan just dropped a major hint about his next movie. Speaking to 60 Minutes, the director called his upcoming film based on Homer’s Odyssey "the most extreme version" of the story he could make. That’s a bold claim, even for someone who’s made some of the biggest blockbusters of the last 20 years. Nolan hasn’t worked from ancient Greek literature before, so this project feels like a fresh challenge for him. The movie is still in production, with no release date announced yet. All Nolan would say is that it’s pushing hard on what’s possible—maybe even finding some limits he didn’t know existed.


## Why this film stands out in Nolan’s career

This isn’t Nolan’s first time tackling a massive story. His last two films, Dunkirk and Oppenheimer, were epic in scale but grounded in real history. The Odyssey, though, is a myth with gods, monsters, and a hero who just won’t die. Nolan has never made a fantasy epic before, so this is uncharted territory for him. He’s known for his obsession with practical effects and real stunts, but mythology brings a whole new set of challenges. How do you film a cyclops or a sorceress turning men into pigs? Nolan’s answer seems to be: push as far as you can, then push a little further.


Nolan told 60 Minutes he and his team "pushed pretty hard" on this one. That’s a familiar phrase for anyone who’s followed his work. Whether it was the ear-splitting IMAX scenes in Dunkirk or the three-hour runtime of Oppenheimer, Nolan’s films always feel like they’re testing how much an audience can take. With The Odyssey, he’s doing the same thing with mythology. The story’s themes—homecoming, revenge, temptation—are timeless, but Nolan’s approach is anything but traditional. He’s not interested in a straightforward retelling. Instead, he’s treating it like one of his mind-bending thrillers, just with more swords and sandals.


## What we know about the production

Details are still scarce, but Nolan’s production team is already making waves. Warner Bros., his longtime studio, is handling the film, which means it’ll likely land in theaters rather than streaming. The budget is expected to be in the nine-figure range, judging by Nolan’s past projects. His crew is also reportedly using practical effects for as much as possible, even for the mythical elements. That’s a big deal in an era where so many films rely entirely on CGI. If Nolan can pull off a convincing cyclops or a storm conjured by Poseidon without digital trickery, it’ll be a technical marvel.


The cast is another mystery. Nolan doesn’t leak much about his actors until the film is closer to release, but you can bet he’s hunting for the right faces to bring these legendary characters to life. Think of the scale of Dunkirk’s ensemble cast, but with Greek gods and warriors instead of soldiers. The film’s tone is also up in the air. Is it a straight adventure? A psychological drama? A dark fantasy? Nolan’s films often blur those lines, so don’t be surprised if The Odyssey ends up being something entirely new.


## Why this matters beyond Nolan’s film

Mythology in modern cinema isn’t new. Films like Troy and 300 proved there’s an appetite for ancient stories, but they leaned into spectacle over substance. Nolan’s take could change that. If he succeeds, it might inspire more directors to go back to the classics—not as relics, but as living, breathing stories that still have something to say. His focus on practical effects and real-world filmmaking could also push other big-budget films to rely less on green screens and more on what they can do in the real world.


There’s also the question of whether this will be Nolan’s last epic. At 54, he’s still young, but his films often feel like they’re the work of someone racing against time. The Odyssey could be his grand finale in the world of blockbusters, or it could just be another step in his career. Either way, it’s clear he’s not interested in coasting. If anything, this film sounds like the most Nolan thing he’s ever done—big, bold, and impossible to ignore.
