Justice Secretary David Lammy is set to unveil a major overhaul of youth justice rules on Monday, including a pledge to reduce by a quarter the number of children held in prison while awaiting trial. The white paper, which Lammy will publish, also proposes ending lifelong criminal records for under-18s—a move aimed at breaking the cycle of reoffending and giving young people a fairer chance.

Lammy, who grew up in Tottenham in the 1980s, said the reforms were deeply personal. ‘This is something I feared most as a child in my community,’ he told reporters. ‘Seeing kids locked up for minor offences or stuck with criminal records for life is exactly why I went into politics.’ The justice secretary has long argued that England and Wales lock up too many young people, often for non-violent crimes, and that the system disproportionately harms Black and working-class children.

## The numbers behind the plan

More than 1,000 children a year in England and Wales are held in youth detention centers while waiting for their cases to go to court, according to the latest Ministry of Justice figures. Around 40% of those remanded are later acquitted or given non-custodial sentences, raising questions about whether jail was ever necessary. The new rules would prioritize community-based supervision for most young defendants, reserving custody only for the most serious or high-risk cases.

The white paper also targets the long-term consequences of a criminal record. Under current laws, even minor convictions can haunt young people for decades, making it harder to get jobs, secure housing, or access education. Lammy’s plan would automatically wipe these records for under-18s after a set period of good behavior, though violent or repeat offenders would still face consequences.

## What’s changing—and who it affects

The reforms go beyond remand and criminal records. The white paper will push for earlier intervention in schools, better mental health support for young offenders, and stricter limits on the use of strip-searching and solitary confinement in youth prisons. Lammy has also signaled he’ll tighten rules around charging children for public order offenses, which critics say are often used disproportionately against Black and minority ethnic kids.

Opposition MPs and youth justice campaigners welcomed the move but urged caution. ‘Ending remand for children is long overdue,’ said Labour MP [Diane Abbott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diane_Abbott), who represents Hackney North and Stoke Newington. ‘But we need to see real funding for youth services, not just policy changes.’ Charities like [The Howard League for Penal Reform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Howard_League_for_Penal_Reform) have long argued that England and Wales jail more children per capita than almost any other European country, despite falling crime rates.

## The road ahead

Lammy’s white paper is the first major step in what he calls a ‘generational reset’ of youth justice. But turning these proposals into law will require cross-party support in Parliament, where critics argue the reforms go too far or not far enough depending on their view. The next phase will involve consultations with police, judges, and young people themselves before any legislation is introduced, likely in early 2025.

For now, the plans mark a clear shift in how the UK treats young offenders. By reducing unnecessary jail time and wiping the slate clean for minor offenses, Lammy is betting that prevention and rehabilitation will ultimately save money—and lives—by keeping kids out of the criminal justice system for good.
