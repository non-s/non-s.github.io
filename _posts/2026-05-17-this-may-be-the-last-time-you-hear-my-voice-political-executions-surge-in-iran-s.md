The United Nations confirmed on Monday that at least 32 political prisoners have been executed in Iran since late February. The executions followed attacks by the US and Israel on Iranian military sites. The UN’s verification process includes interviews with witnesses, court documents, and cross-referenced reports from rights groups. The spike in killings suggests Iran’s government is tightening its grip on dissent, especially among minority groups and political activists. Iran has long used executions as a tool to suppress opposition, but the recent surge is unprecedented in scale and timing, coming just after the military strikes. Human rights organizations say the numbers are likely higher, as many executions go unreported in Iran’s closed prison system. Families of executed prisoners often receive death certificates listing causes like "heart failure" without explanations. One family told BBC Persian that they were told their son died of a heart attack five days after his execution. The UN’s human rights office called the executions a "serious escalation" and demanded Iran halt the practice immediately. Iran’s judiciary dismissed the criticism, calling the executions lawful and justified. The surge in executions coincides with a broader crackdown on protests and dissent. Since 2022, Iran has executed over 860 people, according to Amnesty International, including political prisoners and protesters. The recent wave includes members of the Baloch and Kurdish minorities, who have faced decades of state persecution. Rights groups say the executions are part of a strategy to intimidate the public into silence. ## Iran’s judiciary defends executions as ‘legal process’ Iran’s judiciary spokesman, Massoud Setayeshi, defended the executions as part of a legal process. He claimed the prisoners were convicted of serious crimes, including terrorism and murder. Setayeshi told state media that Iran follows strict judicial procedures and that international criticism is politically motivated. However, rights groups point out that many of those executed were sentenced in sham trials without fair legal representation. Some were children at the time of their alleged crimes, a violation of international law. Iran is one of the world’s top executioners, second only to China in numbers. The recent surge puts it on track to surpass last year’s total of 866 executions. The UN’s special rapporteur on Iran, Javaid Rehman, warned that the pace of executions is accelerating. He said Iran’s government is using the tactic to crush any challenge to its authority. The executions also come as Iran faces internal pressure over economic struggles and protests. The government appears to be using executions as a way to signal strength and deter further unrest. ## Families of executed prisoners face threats and silence Families of executed prisoners say they’re terrified to speak out. Many receive threats from security forces if they try to share details. One mother, whose son was executed in March, told BBC Persian that she was warned not to hold a funeral. "They told me if I make noise, the next one will be my other son," she said. Others say they’re pressured to sign confessions or statements blaming their loved ones for crimes they didn’t commit. Iran’s prison system is notoriously opaque, with limited access for independent monitors. The recent executions have drawn sharp condemnation from the EU and US, which have imposed sanctions on Iranian officials. But Iran shows no signs of slowing down. The UN says it’s documenting each case to build a case for future accountability. Meanwhile, activists inside Iran are scrambling to document the names and stories of those executed before they’re erased from public memory.
