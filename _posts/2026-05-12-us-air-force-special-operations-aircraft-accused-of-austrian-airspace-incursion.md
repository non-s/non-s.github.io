Austria’s military scrambled its Eurofighter Typhoons twice in 48 hours after two U.S. Air Force turboprop aircraft allegedly violated the country’s airspace, according to the Austrian Ministry of Defense. The incidents, confirmed on Sunday and Monday, involved aircraft identified as PC-12s, which defense officials said are most likely the U-28A Draco, a turbine-powered aircraft operated by the [U.S. Air Force Special Operations Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1st_Special_Operations_Wing). These aircraft are typically used for intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance missions.

## Austria’s response to unauthorized flights

The Austrian Air Force confirmed its [Eurofighter Typhoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurofighter_Typhoon) were scrambled at 12:31 p.m. local time on both days to intercept the U.S. aircraft. Austrian Defense Ministry spokesperson Michael Bauer shared details on X, stating the jets were launched with highest priority due to the overflights. The incidents occurred in Austrian airspace, which the country maintains as neutral under international law, though violations have occurred before without major diplomatic consequences.

## U.S. military aircraft identified

The U.S. Air Force uses the PC-12 turboprop, particularly the U-28A Draco variant, for specialized missions, including ISR operations. These aircraft are not typically armed but are equipped for intelligence gathering. The [U.S. Air Force Special Operations Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Force_Special_Operations_Command) operates these platforms, often in support of global counterterrorism and special operations missions. The exact purpose of the flights over Austria remains unclear, as neither the U.S. nor Austrian defense officials have provided further details.

## Austrian neutrality and airspace violations

Austria has maintained a policy of neutrality since 1955, but its airspace has been violated by foreign military aircraft before. The country’s military is required to respond to unauthorized incursions under NATO and EU air defense protocols, even in neutral states. The recent incidents follow a pattern of occasional airspace violations by military aircraft from neighboring and distant countries, though such events are rarely reported publicly.

## What happens next?

The Austrian Defense Ministry has not indicated whether diplomatic protests will be filed with the U.S. government. Typically, such incidents are resolved quietly between military and diplomatic channels. The U.S. Embassy in Vienna and the Austrian Foreign Ministry have not responded to requests for comment. Further details about the flights, including their intended routes or missions, remain undisclosed. The incidents may prompt increased aerial monitoring of Austrian airspace in the coming weeks.
