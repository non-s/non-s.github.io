A UK company director just placed an order for 2,000 solar panels to protect the business from future energy price shocks. The director, who asked not to be named, told the BBC the move will cut the company’s energy bills by at least half within two years. "We saw how energy prices spiked after the Ukraine war and decided we couldn’t rely on the grid anymore," the director said. "These panels will cover our daytime use and we’ll sell the excess back to the grid when prices peak." Solar panel prices have fallen 50% since 2010 thanks to cheaper imports and better manufacturing, making installations affordable for small firms and households alike. In 2023, UK solar sales jumped 40% compared with 2022, according to the [Solar Trade Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Trade_Association). Installers report that orders are up 60% in the first quarter of 2024 alone. The typical system now costs about £6,000 for a 4kW setup that fits a three-bedroom home, down from £12,000 in 2010. Most panels come with 25-year warranties and pay for themselves in under six years at current electricity prices. ## Energy crisis shifts attitudes toward solar power The surge in sales follows two years of extreme volatility in UK energy markets. Wholesale gas prices peaked at £120 per megawatt-hour in March 2022 after Russia invaded Ukraine, pushing household bills past £2,500 a year. Even after prices eased, they stayed double pre-crisis levels. That pushed many households to rethink their energy strategy. "People used to see solar as an environmental statement," said [Stuart Elmes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stuart_Elmes), founder of UK solar installer [Viridian Solar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viridian_Solar). "Now it’s a financial one. With bills still high and prices low, the maths works for almost every home." Elmes says his company installed 30% more systems in 2023 than in 2022, with half going to homeowners under 40. Many are couples with young kids who worry about long-term costs. ## Solar panels beat the grid on price and payback time The economics have flipped in solar’s favour. In 2010, a typical 4kW system cost £12,000 and took 12 years to pay back. Today the same setup costs £6,000 and pays for itself in 5.5 years at an average 24p per kWh import rate and 10p export tariff. Batteries that store daytime power for evening use have also dropped in price, falling from £4,000 to £2,500 over the same period. A 2023 study by [Imperial College London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_College_London) found that households adding batteries cut their annual bills by an extra £350 compared with solar alone. The government’s Smart Export Guarantee pays 5.5p per kWh for surplus electricity fed back to the grid, an improvement from the old feed-in tariff that paid just 4p. But installers say that rate is still too low to justify big battery systems. ## Supply chain catches up after pandemic delays Shortages of solar panels and inverters during the pandemic pushed wait times to nine months for some installers. Now supply chains have normalised and prices are falling again. "We can get panels shipped from Turkey or India within six weeks," said [Sarah Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarah_Jones_(British_politician)), operations manager at [Eco2Solar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eco2Solar). "Labour costs are the only thing rising, so we’re pushing installers to quote fixed prices for 2024 to avoid surprises." Jones says her company now offers 10-year installation warranties, up from five years two years ago. ## What’s next for UK solar? The sector is bracing for another boost if Labour wins the next election and restores the zero-rate VAT on solar installations that was removed in 2023. The opposition party has pledged to bring it back, which would shave another £300 off the cost of a typical system. Meanwhile, the government is consulting on raising the minimum energy efficiency standard for rental homes, which could force landlords to install panels or face fines. For homeowners, the message is clear: wait, and prices may fall further, but act now and you lock in today’s rates for decades. As one installer put it: "The best time to go solar was ten years ago. The second-best time is right now."
