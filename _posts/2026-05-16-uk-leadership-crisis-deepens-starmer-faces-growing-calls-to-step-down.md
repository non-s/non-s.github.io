UK Prime Minister [Keir Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer) faced escalating calls to resign Tuesday after weeks of mounting political pressure and declining public approval. The crisis deepened as senior lawmakers within his own party joined opposition figures in demanding his immediate departure. Polls released Monday showed Starmer’s approval ratings at their lowest point in over a year, with just 28% of voters expressing confidence in his leadership.

## Escalating criticism from within Labour ranks
Criticism of Starmer has intensified not only from opposition benches but also from within his [Labour Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)), with backbench MPs openly questioning his direction. Former cabinet ministers and high-profile supporters have publicly aired grievances over policy reversals and perceived weak handling of key issues, including economic stagnation and public sector strikes. Reports suggest at least 15 Labour MPs have privately urged party leadership to consider a change at the top before the next general election.

## Public discontent fuels demands for resignation
Public frustration has surged in recent weeks, fueled by rising living costs, delays in promised reforms, and a perception of indecision on major policy decisions. Protests have been reported in several cities, including London, Manchester, and Birmingham, where demonstrators carried signs reading “Starmer must go” and “Enough is enough.” Social media platforms have been flooded with hashtags such as #StarmerOut and #LabourFail, reflecting widespread dissatisfaction. A YouGov poll published Tuesday found 52% of Britons believe Starmer should step down to allow the party a fresh start.

## Political analysts weigh in on the fallout
Historians and political scientists, including [Martin Farr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Farr), Senior Lecturer in Contemporary British History at [Newcastle University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newcastle_University), suggest the crisis could reshape the UK political landscape. Farr told France 24 that while leadership challenges are not uncommon, the speed and scale of the current backlash are unusual for a government less than two years into its term. “The combination of internal dissent and external pressure points to a government struggling to maintain coherence,” Farr said. “This could signal the beginning of a longer-term realignment in British politics.”

## What happens next?
The coming days will be critical as Labour’s National Executive Committee convenes an emergency meeting Thursday to assess the situation. Speculation is rife over potential successors, with names such as [Chancellor Rachel Reeves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rachel_Reeves) and [Shadow Foreign Secretary David Lammy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Lammy) already circulating in Westminster corridors. Meanwhile, the opposition [Conservative Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)) has called for a snap election, arguing the government has lost its mandate. Political observers say the crisis could either force a rapid leadership contest or trigger a broader collapse of public trust in Labour’s ability to govern effectively.
