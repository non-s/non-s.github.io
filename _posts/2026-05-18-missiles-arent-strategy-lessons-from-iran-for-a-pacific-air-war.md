In 2026, Iran launched more than 1,200 ballistic missiles in the opening hours of a war with the U.S. and its allies. The targets weren’t cities or tanks—they were runways, fuel depots, and command centers at three critical airbases in the Persian Gulf. Within six hours, two of those bases were unusable, and the third was operating at a fraction of its normal capacity. Analysts had spent years warning that U.S. airpower in the Middle East was vulnerable to missile barrages, but no one predicted how fast the damage would stack up when the real shooting started. The result wasn’t just a setback—it was a rout of the kind that changes how wars are fought and who wins them. [U.S. Central Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Central_Command) later called it a “strategic shock,” one that forced a complete rethink of how America projects airpower overseas. The lesson for the Pacific isn’t that China’s missiles are unbeatable. It’s that relying on inventories of missiles, aircraft, or even runways to predict early war outcomes is a losing bet. Real combat doesn’t work that way. Numbers matter, but they’re not destiny. Russia learned the same hard truth in Ukraine. After the first week of its 2022 invasion, Moscow’s generals assumed their long-range missiles would cripple Ukraine’s air defenses and pave the way for air superiority. Instead, Ukrainian forces moved critical assets to hidden locations, used decoys, and kept their jets flying from makeshift strips. By the time Russia adjusted, it was too late. The Ukrainians had already made the war about mobility and deception, not raw firepower. China is betting its anti-access/area-denial strategy on the same flawed logic. Beijing’s planners assume that missiles and sensors can hold U.S. and allied aircraft at bay long enough to decide the war before it really begins. But the Iran 2026 campaign shows what happens when the other side refuses to play by those rules. The U.S. didn’t just lose runways. It lost the assumption that airbases are safe, predictable targets. Suddenly, every aircraft sortie required a new route, a new refueling plan, and a new way to hide when the missiles started falling. The Pentagon calls this “expeditionary basing,” a fancy term for scattering operations across makeshift strips, commercial airfields, and even highways. It’s not glamorous, and it’s not in any war manual. But it works when the alternative is getting wiped out on Day One. The bigger problem isn’t China’s missiles. It’s that America’s war planners are still thinking in terms of fixed targets and predictable timelines. The U.S. Air Force trains for large-scale conflicts by running massive exercises like Red Flag, where hundreds of jets and thousands of troops descend on Nevada for two weeks of controlled chaos. Those drills are useful, but they don’t prepare aircrews for the chaos of real war. In 2026, Iranian missiles hit three airbases simultaneously, each with different defenses and different weaknesses. The bases that survived weren’t the ones with the most missiles or the thickest concrete—they were the ones where crews improvised. Pilots used civilian GPS apps to navigate around missile strikes. Maintenance teams jury-rigged repair bays in hangars meant for cargo planes. Fuel trucks became mobile refueling stations. None of this was in the playbook, but it kept the war going. China’s planners are counting on the U.S. to repeat the same mistakes. They assume American forces will cling to big, obvious bases and try to fight the way they always have. But the Iran example proves that’s a losing strategy. The real question isn’t whether China can disrupt U.S. air operations. It’s whether America can adapt fast enough to keep fighting. So far, the signs aren’t great. The Air Force’s latest bomber fleet, the [B-21 Raider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-21_Raider), is designed to survive in contested environments, but it’s still years away from full deployment. The service’s top priority is building more long-range missiles, not making aircraft harder to find. Meanwhile, China is pouring billions into drones, hypersonic weapons, and AI-driven targeting systems. The arms race isn’t just about who has more firepower. It’s about who can change plans faster when the shooting starts. The next time you hear an analyst say the U.S. can’t win a war with China because it lacks enough missiles or runways, ask them about Iran 2026. Then ask them how many of today’s war plans account for losing half their airbases in the first 24 hours. The answer might surprise you.
