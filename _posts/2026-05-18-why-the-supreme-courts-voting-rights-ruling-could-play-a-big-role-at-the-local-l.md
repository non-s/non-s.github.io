In late 2024, the Supreme Court rewrote the rules for proving racial discrimination in voting. The case [Louisiana v. Callais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_v._Callais) made it harder to challenge election maps that dilute the voting power of racial minorities under the Voting Rights Act’s Section 2. Now, that change is rippling through courthouses across the country, with local governments suddenly scrambling to defend their maps—or sue to redraw them in their favor. An NPR analysis of federal court filings shows at least 17 states and cities are now in active legal battles over voting maps or election systems that were previously protected under the old rules. These fights aren’t about Congress. They’re about who gets to serve on school boards, city councils, and county commissions in places like Georgia, Florida, Alabama, and Texas. The stakes? Minority voters who’ve relied on federal protections for decades may now struggle to elect candidates of their choice in some areas. Take Fort Bend County, Texas, for example. A federal lawsuit there claims the county’s commissioners court map unfairly packs Black and Latino voters into one district, diluting their votes across the rest of the county. Under the old Section 2 standard, courts would’ve looked at whether the map made it harder for minority voters to elect candidates they preferred. Now, plaintiffs have to prove the map was drawn with racist intent—a nearly impossible hurdle in most cases. The result? Local governments that once settled these lawsuits by adding majority-minority districts are now digging in their heels. In Alabama, where the Supreme Court’s ruling came down just as the state was redrawing its congressional map, lawyers are already amending their legal arguments. The state’s previous map had been blocked by a federal court for violating the Voting Rights Act, but the new ruling gives Alabama a fresh chance to defend its lines. The same pattern is playing out in smaller jurisdictions. In [Jackson, Mississippi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jackson,_Mississippi), a recent lawsuit argues the city’s at-large elections for mayor and city council disadvantage Black voters, who make up over 80% of the population. Under the old rules, the lawsuit would’ve had a strong chance. Now, it’s an uphill battle. The Supreme Court’s decision didn’t just change the law—it changed the power dynamic in local politics. Cities and counties that used to negotiate settlements to avoid costly lawsuits are now betting that judges will side with them under the new, stricter standard. But the fights aren’t just defensive. Some plaintiffs are using the ruling to push for even more aggressive changes, arguing that the new standard means existing maps are even more vulnerable. In Florida, for instance, a coalition of civil rights groups is arguing that the state’s congressional map violates both the old and new interpretations of Section 2—meaning it should be struck down no matter what. The legal chaos is forcing local officials to make tough choices. Do they redraw maps now to avoid lawsuits, or do they wait and see if the courts uphold the new standard? In some places, the uncertainty is paralyzing. School boards in majority-minority districts are postponing redistricting plans, fearing that any change could trigger a lawsuit. City councils are delaying votes on new election systems, unsure if they’ll pass muster under the new rules. ## What the ruling actually changed The Supreme Court’s decision in Louisiana v. Callais centered on how courts should interpret Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act. The old standard, established in 1986, allowed lawsuits if voting practices had a discriminatory *effect*, even if there was no proof of racist intent. The new ruling says plaintiffs must show the discrimination was *intentional*—a much higher bar. For local governments, this means two things: First, maps and election systems that were previously vulnerable to challenges are now much harder to overturn. Second, it’s opened the door for governments to argue that even maps with obvious disparities aren’t illegal because there’s no proof of racist intent. The shift is already visible in the language of new lawsuits. Where plaintiffs once focused on the *impact* of a map—like whether it led to fewer minority candidates winning—they’re now digging through emails, meeting minutes, and legislative history to find evidence of racist motives. It’s a strategy that’s both more difficult and more time-consuming, giving governments more leverage to drag out legal battles. ## Where the fights are heating up The 17 jurisdictions facing active legal fights span the country, but they’re concentrated in the South and Southwest, where minority populations have grown fastest. In [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)), a lawsuit over the state’s school board elections in [Fulton County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fulton_County,_Georgia) argues the at-large system dilutes Black voting power. In [Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas), multiple cases target county commissioners courts, school boards, and city councils in places like [Harris County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harris_County,_Texas) and [Dallas County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dallas_County,_Texas). The outcome in these cases could reshape local politics for years. If courts side with plaintiffs under the new standard, it would signal that the Supreme Court’s ruling won’t just protect status quo maps—it could force governments to make meaningful changes. If courts side with governments, it would embolden more states to pass laws that make it harder for minorities to challenge election systems at all. Either way, the ruling has already changed the game. Local officials who once took the Voting Rights Act seriously are now treating it like a relic of a different era. And voters who’ve relied on it to protect their political voice are left wondering if their rights are still protected.
