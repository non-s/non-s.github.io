A bag of discounted lettuce costing just $1.15 turned out to be a bigger surprise than expected for two housemates in Western Australia. Laura Jones and Billie Le Pine bought the sealed bag of romaine from an unknown supermarket in Perth’s northern suburbs, assuming it was just a cheap meal option for their shared home. What they didn’t expect was a live frog the size of a man’s palm staring back at them when they tore open the bag the next day. The frog, identified later as a common green tree frog native to [Western Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Australia), was unharmed and later released safely outside by local wildlife authorities. The housemates told reporters they first thought it was a plastic toy before realizing it was very much alive and very confused by its new surroundings. ‘I screamed,’ Jones said in an interview with ABC [News Breakfast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ABC_News_Breakfast). ‘I thought, “This is the most random thing I’ve ever seen in a lettuce bag.”’ Le Pine added that the frog was ‘definitely bigger than a thumbnail, but not as big as a dinner plate—more like a man’s palm, honestly.’ The pair joked that if they’d bought the lettuce in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France), they might have gotten a two-for-one deal on the frog as well. Their unexpected guest made headlines across Australia, with the story trending on social media under hashtags like #FrogInMyLettuce and #WesternAustraliaNews. The incident also sparked a wave of similar reports from shoppers who’d found unexpected wildlife in their groceries—beetles in spinach, mice in cereal boxes, and even a snake in a cereal box once. But the frog in the lettuce remains the most talked-about case so far. The supermarket chain involved hasn’t publicly commented on how the frog ended up in the sealed bag, but food safety experts say it’s likely the result of poor handling practices during harvesting or packaging. ‘It’s not unheard of for small animals to sneak into produce during transport,’ said [Food Standards Australia New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_Standards_Australia_New_Zealand) spokesperson Mark Smith. ‘But a frog this size suggests it got in early in the supply chain, probably during harvesting.’ Smith added that while the incident was unusual, it didn’t pose a food safety risk, as the lettuce was sealed and the frog was on the outside of the produce when purchased. The housemates, now minor local celebrities, said they’ve received job offers from wildlife documentaries and comedy shows. Jones joked that she’s considering renaming her group chat to ‘Frog Squad’ and Le Pine said they might start a side hustle selling ‘surprise lettuce boxes’—though she quickly clarified that no frogs would be included. The story even caught the attention of [David Attenborough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Attenborough), who tweeted: ‘Nature’s surprises never end. #FrogInLettuce.’ For now, the lettuce bag that started it all sits in a plastic evidence bag in the WA Department of Primary Industries and Regional Development offices, waiting for its turn in the spotlight at a future agricultural expo. The frog, meanwhile, has been released into a local nature reserve, where officials hope it’ll live a long, lettuce-free life.
