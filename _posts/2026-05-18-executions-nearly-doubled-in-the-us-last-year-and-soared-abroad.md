The number of people executed worldwide spiked to its highest level in 44 years in 2025, with Iran alone accounting for over 80% of the global total. According to a new report from [Amnesty International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amnesty_International), at least 2,707 executions were carried out across 17 countries last year. That’s up 78% from 1,518 in 2024, marking the sharpest annual rise in decades. The surge was driven largely by Iran, where the government put 2,159 people to death—more than double the 1,029 executions recorded the year before. ## Iran's executions soar as dissent grows Amnesty calls Iran’s death penalty surge part of a broader crackdown on dissent since 2022, when protests erupted over women’s rights. The organization says the government has increasingly used executions as a tool to crush opposition, including for charges like ‘moharebeh’ (waging war against God) and drug-related offenses. In September, Amnesty warned that Iran had already surpassed its highest annual execution count in 15 years by mid-2025. Executions in the country have surged nearly every year since the protests began, with 2025 marking the deadliest yet. ## Saudi Arabia executes hundreds for drug crimes Saudi Arabia executed at least 356 people in 2025, according to Amnesty, making it the second-highest executioner in the world after Iran. Most of those killings were for drug-related offenses, continuing a trend of using the death penalty to enforce strict narcotics laws. The kingdom has been among the most aggressive in applying capital punishment for non-violent crimes, despite global criticism. ## U.S. executions nearly double, led by Florida The United States executed 47 people in 2025, nearly twice the 25 recorded in 2024. The jump was driven largely by Florida, which put 19 people to death—the most of any U.S. state. Texas followed with 10 executions, while Oklahoma executed five. The rise comes despite growing opposition to the death penalty in the U.S., where it’s only legal for murder or treason. Amnesty notes that the U.S. was the only country in the Americas to carry out executions in 2025. ## China’s numbers remain unknown China’s executions aren’t included in Amnesty’s count, but the organization estimates the country executes more people than the rest of the world combined. China keeps its death penalty statistics secret, making it impossible to verify exact numbers. Human rights groups have long criticized the country for using capital punishment broadly, including for corruption and drug offenses. ## Why the surge in executions? Experts point to several factors driving the global increase. In Iran, the government has framed executions as a way to maintain order amid ongoing protests and economic instability. In Saudi Arabia, drug-related executions reflect a zero-tolerance approach to narcotics, despite international condemnation. In the U.S., political shifts in some states have led to faster executions, with Florida ramping up its use of the death penalty under new leadership. The rise also comes as global monitoring groups like Amnesty face increasing restrictions in countries like Iran and Saudi Arabia, making it harder to track abuses. ## What happens next? The surge in executions raises serious questions about the future of capital punishment worldwide. In Iran, protests have continued despite the crackdown, suggesting the government may escalate repression further. In the U.S., the increase could fuel debates over the death penalty’s effectiveness and morality, especially as public support declines. Around the world, human rights groups are pushing for abolition, but governments like Saudi Arabia and Iran show no signs of slowing down. Without stronger international pressure or domestic opposition, the trend could continue.
