British Prime Minister Keir Starmer [Prime Minister of the United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom) now presides over a Parliament that has abandoned its once-collegial traditions, morphing into a battleground of personal rivalries and institutional dysfunction. Just two years into his premiership, Starmer’s Labour government is already mired in infighting and policy gridlock, a stark contrast to the orderly club Westminster once claimed to be. The collapse mirrors the chaos that consumed the Conservatives under Liz Truss [Liz Truss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liz_Truss), where internal divisions triggered a rapid fall from power after months of turmoil.

Starmer’s leadership is weakened by open hostility within his ranks, particularly from former Health Secretary Wes Streeting [Wes Streeting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wes_Streeting). Streeting’s abrupt resignation from a critical cabinet role signals deep fractures that threaten Labour’s stability. The move, driven by naked ambition, underscores a broader erosion of trust and cooperation across Westminster [Westminster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster). Chancellor Rachel Reeves [Rachel Reeves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rachel_Reeves) has warned that such instability risks severe economic consequences, casting doubt on Labour’s ability to govern effectively.

The House of Commons now resembles a shambles rather than a deliberative body. MPs from all parties trade barbs and personal attacks, abandoning the unwritten rules that once governed behaviour in the corridors of power. This breakdown reflects a wider societal shift away from deference to authority and towards immediate, often abrasive, confrontation. The result is a Parliament paralysed by performative conflict rather than constructive debate.

Labour’s rapid descent into chaos contrasts with the Tories’ years-long spiral, which culminated in Truss’s brief and disastrous premiership. Yet Starmer’s struggles demonstrate that no party is immune to Westminster’s new culture of instability. The absence of a clear successor within Labour’s ranks further complicates efforts to restore order, leaving the party grappling with internal power struggles while public confidence wanes.

Economists and political analysts warn that the erosion of Westminster’s institutional norms could have lasting effects on Britain’s governance. The loss of collegiality undermines policy continuity and erodes public trust in elected officials. As MPs prioritise personal advancement over collective responsibility, the risk grows that Parliament will remain mired in dysfunction, unable to address pressing national challenges.

What happens next remains uncertain. Starmer’s leadership hinges on his ability to rein in internal dissent and restore a semblance of order. But the collapse of Westminster’s club-like traditions suggests a deeper, systemic shift—one that may outlast any single prime minister.
