The UK’s next ambassador to Japan, Corin Robertson, is facing the possibility of being called to give evidence to MPs over her involvement in approving Peter Mandelson’s security clearance earlier this year. Robertson, who was the Foreign Office’s chief operating officer at the time, signed off on Mandelson’s clearance in late January 2025 despite vetting officials’ strong objections, according to evidence presented to the Commons foreign affairs committee this week. The committee is now reviewing whether Robertson should be formally questioned about the decision as part of its ongoing inquiry into the case. No date has been set for a vote, but sources say it could happen within the next two weeks. Mandelson, a former Labour cabinet minister and European commissioner, has not been accused of any wrongdoing but his case has raised serious questions about how security clearances are handled at the top levels of government. Robertson was nominated for the ambassadorial post in Tokyo in March 2025, a role that requires a high-level security clearance herself. The committee’s decision could delay or derail her confirmation if they demand her testimony, though such requests are rare for sitting diplomats. A Foreign Office spokesperson declined to comment on the specific case but said the department takes security vetting seriously and follows strict protocols. Mandelson, who stepped down from his role as a senior advisor to the European Commission in late 2024, has not spoken publicly about the clearance issue. His office did not respond to requests for comment. The inquiry comes at a time when the government is under pressure to tighten security vetting standards, especially for senior officials and diplomats. In 2023, the FCDO faced criticism after it emerged that dozens of officials had been granted clearances despite red flags in their background checks. This case is different because it involves a high-profile political figure rather than a career civil servant. Robertson’s potential testimony would likely focus on the internal discussions that led to the decision, including who approved it and why officials’ concerns were overruled. The committee has already heard from multiple FCDO officials who described the process as unusually rushed. One witness told MPs the clearance was granted in less than 48 hours, far faster than the standard three-week review period. Robertson’s nomination to Tokyo has added urgency to the inquiry, as diplomats typically need to start preparing for their posts months in advance. If the committee insists on her testimony, the Foreign Office may have to temporarily withdraw her nomination while the matter is resolved, delaying the UK’s ambassadorial transition in Japan. The foreign affairs committee is expected to hold a vote on the matter within the next fortnight. If they vote to summon her, Robertson would be the first senior diplomat in recent memory to testify before MPs over a security clearance decision. The outcome could set a new precedent for how such cases are handled in the future. For now, Robertson remains in her current role at the FCDO while the committee deliberates. The case has already led to informal reviews of the department’s vetting processes, though no formal changes have been announced. The government has insisted that the system works as intended, but the Mandelson case has exposed cracks in the oversight of high-level clearances.
