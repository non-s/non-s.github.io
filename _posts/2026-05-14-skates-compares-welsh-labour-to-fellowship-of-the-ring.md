Ken Skates [compared Welsh Labour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welsh_Labour) to the Fellowship of the Ring in a lighthearted speech to the Senedd. The Senedd member made the joke during a debate on the party’s unity and future direction. His remark came just months after he stepped down as a Welsh Labour minister, a role he held for nearly nine years under [Mark Drakeford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Drakeford)'s leadership of Wales. The comparison isn’t the first time politicians have used pop culture to describe political teams. But Skates’ choice of Tolkien’s fantasy saga stands out for its sharp contrast between myth and modern governance. The Fellowship of the Ring is a group of nine diverse companions who join forces to destroy a powerful ring. Welsh Labour, on the other hand, is Britain’s oldest political party and has governed Wales for over two decades. Their leadership of the Senedd—Wales’ devolved parliament—has been marked by steady, if sometimes slow, policy shifts rather than epic quests. Skates’ quip likely landed well with colleagues who’ve worked through long days and late-night votes, sometimes feeling like they’re on a journey no one fully signed up for. The joke also hints at the party’s internal tensions. Welsh Labour has faced criticism from both left and right wings, with some arguing it’s lost its way after years in power. Others see it as a pragmatic force holding Wales together through budget cuts and constitutional debates. The speech itself focused on the party’s next steps after Drakeford’s resignation in late 2023. That transition left the party without its longest-serving leader, raising questions about its future identity. Skates’ analogy might have been a playful way to remind colleagues that, like the Fellowship, they’re in this together—even if the destination isn’t always clear. The Senedd chamber, where the joke was made, is a place where serious policy battles play out daily. Yet even there, a well-timed reference to Middle-earth can break the tension. It’s a reminder that politics, for all its noise, still has room for creativity and humor. Whether Welsh Labour’s next chapter feels more like an epic saga or a quiet retirement remains to be seen. But for now, they’re stuck with the ring—and the responsibility that comes with it. The party’s next leader will have to decide: do they double down on the quest, or find a quieter path forward? One thing’s certain—Skates’ Fellowship analogy won’t be the last pop culture reference in the Senedd.
