The 2026 Academy of Country Music Awards will remember the night for one standout performance: Red Clay Strays, a rising country-rock band from Alabama, took home the Group of the Year award after their explosive rendition of 'Demons in Your Choir.' Backed by a 40-voice gospel choir from [Nashville’s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee) [Music City Mass Choir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_City_Mass_Choir), the band turned heads on the Las Vegas stage. Their win was the biggest upset of the night, beating established names like Brooks & Dunn and Zac Brown Band for the top prize.

The performance wasn’t just a win—it was a statement. Red Clay Strays stripped the song down to its raw, soulful core, trading polished country production for a grittier, gospel-infused sound. Frontman [Jake Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jake_Hill_(musician)) growled the lyrics over a pulsing rhythm section while the choir soared behind him, their harmonies lifting the track into something spiritual. The contrast between the band’s rawness and the choir’s polish created a sound that felt both familiar and fresh. By the end, the crowd gave them a standing ovation.

Red Clay Strays’ rise didn’t happen overnight. The band formed in 2019 in [Huntsville, Alabama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama), blending southern rock, country, and gospel influences. Their big break came in 2024 when 'Demons in Your Choir' went viral on TikTok. The song’s dark, introspective lyrics—about struggling with faith and doubt—struck a chord with listeners tired of polished country radio hits. By early 2025, the track topped [Billboard’s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)) Country Airplay chart for three weeks straight. Their debut album, *Blood on the Tracks*, dropped in October 2025 and went platinum by March 2026.

The ACM Awards judges clearly took notice. While traditional country acts dominated the nominations, Red Clay Strays’ win signaled a shift in what the industry rewards. Band members told [Rolling Stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rolling_Stone) after the show that they expected the win to open doors, but not this soon. 'We thought maybe in a few years we’d crack into this circle,' Hill said. 'But when the envelope got called, I just about dropped my guitar.'

Their performance also highlighted the growing influence of gospel music in country entertainment. The [Music City Mass Choir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_City_Mass_Choir) has been a staple in Nashville for decades, backing artists like [Dolly Parton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dolly_Parton) and [Kenny Chesney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenny_Chesney) at major events. But pairing them with a gritty country-rock band? That was new. The choir’s director, [Reverend Michael Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Thompson_(choir_director)), called it 'a beautiful collision of traditions.' The moment proved gospel’s power to elevate any genre when the moment’s right.

What’s next for Red Clay Strays? They’re already in the studio recording their second album, set for a late 2026 release. Sources close to the band say they’re leaning into their signature sound—more raw guitars, deeper harmonies, and lyrics that dig into personal struggles. They’re also planning a summer tour, with dates already announced for [Austin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austin,_Texas), [Atlanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlanta), and [Denver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denver). If their ACM performance is any indication, expect sold-out crowds and surprised critics.

The band’s win might also pressure [ACM Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_of_Country_Music_Awards) producers to rethink their voting process. For years, the awards leaned heavily toward radio-friendly acts with industry connections. Red Clay Strays’ victory—driven by grassroots buzz and streaming numbers—proves the audience wants something different. As Hill put it after the show: 'We made a record for the people who feel like outcasts. Turns out, that’s a lot of people.'
