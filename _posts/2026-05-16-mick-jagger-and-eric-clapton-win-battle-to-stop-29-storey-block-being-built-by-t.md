A planning inspector has sided with local councilors and celebrities including [Mick Jagger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mick_Jagger) to block a 29-storey tower planned for the south bank of the Thames near Battersea Bridge in southwest London. The 100-metre-high development proposed by [Rockwell Property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockwell_Property_Group) would have rivaled the iconic chimneys of the nearby [Battersea Power Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battersea_Power_Station).

The inspector ruled the tower was not “exemplary, extraordinary, remarkable or distinctive, just tall,” confirming the rejection issued by [Hammersmith and Fulham Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammersmith_and_Fulham). The council had refused the scheme in 2022, citing harm to the historic character and riverside views. Opponents argued the tower would dominate the skyline and alter the riverside setting protected by local conservation policies.

Celebrities including Jagger, guitarist [Eric Clapton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Clapton), actress [Felicity Kendal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felicity_Kendal) and comedian [Harry Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Hill) joined local campaign groups in opposing the project. The group argued the tower would harm the area’s heritage and residential character, despite Rockwell’s claims it would bring investment and housing.

## Campaigners cite heritage and skyline concerns

Campaigners said the tower’s height and location would overshadow the historic [Chelsea Embankment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chelsea_Embankment) and disrupt views protected under the [Thames Policy Area Design Guide](https://www.london.gov.uk/). The guide aims to preserve sightlines and the character of riverside areas. Opponents also pointed to the proximity to low-rise residential streets and the potential impact on property values and quality of life.

Rockwell Property argued the development would deliver 300 new homes, including affordable units, and claimed the design met local planning policies. The company said it was “disappointed” by the inspector’s decision and considering its next steps. Local councillors welcomed the ruling, calling it a victory for residents and heritage protection.

## What happens next

The inspector’s decision brings a two-year dispute to a close, but it may not end the broader debate over tall buildings in London’s conservation areas. Similar high-rise proposals have faced opposition in central London and along the Thames, where heritage and skyline policies are tightly enforced. The case highlights tensions between housing demand and heritage protection in one of the capital’s most sought-after neighborhoods.

Planning experts say the ruling reinforces the weight given to local character and conservation in high-profile appeals. It also signals that height alone is unlikely to justify approval in sensitive locations, even if the scheme includes affordable housing. For now, the Thames riverside remains unchanged, preserving views cherished by residents and visitors alike.
