Democratic Colorado Gov. Jared Polis reduced the prison sentence of Tina Peters, the former Mesa County clerk convicted of tampering with election equipment, and set her parole eligibility for June 1, 2026. The decision, announced Thursday, comes after a sustained lobbying effort by former President Donald Trump and his allies to secure Peters’ early release from state custody.

Peters, 61, was convicted in 2024 on multiple felony counts including tampering with voting machines and identity theft. Her sentencing drew national attention after Mesa County District Judge Matthew Barrett criticized her conduct during the hearing, saying Peters was an attention-seeking official who continued to promote false claims about a rigged election. Barrett sentenced her to nearly nine years in prison, the stiffest penalty imposed on any defendant in Colorado related to election interference after the 2020 presidential contest.

The Colorado Court of Appeals upheld Peters’ conviction in April but ordered resentencing, ruling that Barrett improperly considered Peters’ protected speech when imposing the sentence. The appeals court sent the case back to Mesa County District Court for resentencing, though Polis bypassed that process by commuting her sentence directly. The governor’s office did not provide a detailed explanation for the decision, though a spokesperson said it was based on Peters’ eligibility for parole under state guidelines.

Peters’ case stems from an incident in May 2021, when she and an accomplice allegedly facilitated the unauthorized copying of voting machine software in Mesa County. The breach occurred six months after the 2020 election and was part of a broader effort by Trump allies to substantiate baseless claims of widespread voter fraud. Peters, who served as Mesa County clerk and recorder from 2014 to 2022, has maintained her innocence and framed the prosecution as politically motivated retaliation for her support of Trump.

Legal experts note that Peters’ sentence has drawn scrutiny because it stands out among the relatively few convictions tied to attempts to overturn the 2020 election. While federal and state prosecutions have targeted Trump’s allies in Georgia, Arizona and elsewhere, most efforts to hold them accountable have stalled or resulted in lighter penalties. Peters’ early release, even on parole, contrasts with the outcomes faced by others convicted in similar cases.

Polis, a Democrat in his second term, has faced criticism from some legal analysts and election integrity advocates who argue that the commutation sends a mixed message about accountability for election-related crimes. Others, including Peters’ supporters, say the sentence was excessive and that the governor acted to correct an unjust punishment.

Peters is expected to be released from prison in June 2026 under the terms of her parole. The broader implications of the commutation remain unclear, though it may embolden other allies of Trump who remain under indictment or investigation for their roles in the 2020 election aftermath.
