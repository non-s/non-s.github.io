A stubborn Arctic blast that sent temperatures 10-15°C below normal across France, England, and Germany for more than a week is about to reverse course. A high-pressure system parked over the North Atlantic and a slow-moving low over southern Scandinavia pushed frigid air southward, turning parks into skating rinks and forcing people to dig out their winter coats. Now, that cold air is on its way out as warmer tropical air surges northward, pushing thermometers up by as much as 15°C in some places.

## Why the sudden change?
The shift is all down to a change in the jet stream [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_stream), the fast-moving air current that steers weather systems around the planet. Over the past week, the jet stream dipped sharply southward, dragging Arctic air into Europe. But now it’s flattening out, allowing warmer air to push in from the south. The UK’s Met Office [https://www.metoffice.gov.uk/](https://www.metoffice.gov.uk/) says London could hit 22°C by Thursday, nearly double this week’s chilly 11°C highs.

France is expecting an even bigger jump. After a week where Paris barely scraped 5°C, forecasters at Météo-France [https://www.meteofrance.com/](https://www.meteofrance.com/) predict temperatures could soar to 24°C by Wednesday. That’s the kind of warmth usually seen in late May, not mid-April. The rapid change has caught some people off guard, with garden centers reporting a surge in customers buying summer plants and barbecue equipment.

## The U.S. isn’t getting a break
While Europe bakes, parts of the U.S. are still dealing with winter’s last gasp. A separate weather system is dumping snow from the Midwest to the Northeast, with Chicago expecting up to 6 inches by Tuesday. The National Weather Service [https://www.weather.gov/](https://www.weather.gov/) warns that the snow could mix with rain, making roads slippery and disrupting travel just as people prepare for Easter weekend.

Meteorologists say these extreme swings are becoming more common as climate change [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change) alters global weather patterns. The rapid transitions between cold and warm spells put extra stress on infrastructure, from heating systems struggling to switch off to farmers wondering when to plant crops. The World Meteorological Organization [https://public.wmo.int/](https://public.wmo.int/) has noted that Europe has seen three times as many rapid temperature swings in the past decade compared to the 1980s.

## What’s next?
The warm spell in Europe won’t last long. By the weekend, temperatures are expected to drop again as a new low-pressure system moves in from the Atlantic. This time, though, the dip will be more modest—just 5-8°C below average—so it won’t feel like a return to full Arctic conditions. The U.S. will see a similar pattern, with the snow tapering off by Wednesday before a warmer weekend sets in.

For now, Europeans are making the most of the sudden burst of warmth. Parks in Paris are filling up with sunbathers, and outdoor café terraces are packed. But experts warn that these rapid changes aren’t just a quirk of the weather—they’re a sign of how climate systems are getting more erratic. The question now is whether this summer will bring more of the same extreme shifts, or if we’ll get a more stable season.
