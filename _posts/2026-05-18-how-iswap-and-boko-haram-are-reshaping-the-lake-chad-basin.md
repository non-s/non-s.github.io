Nigeria’s military announced the death of Abu-Bilal al-Minuki, the second-in-command of [ISIL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State_of_Iraq_and_the_Levant) in the Lake Chad Basin, after a joint raid with U.S. forces near [Lake Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Chad). The operation happened on a compound on the Nigerian side, close to the borders with [Chad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad) and [Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niger). Al-Minuki, a Nigerian from [Borno State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borno_State), was the most senior ISIL leader taken out in the region in years. But the killing didn’t stop the violence. Just days later, militants from [ISWAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_State%27s_West_African_Province) and [Boko Haram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boko_Haram) killed 43 civilians in fresh attacks across the basin. Most were farmers or fishermen, targeted for refusing to pay “taxes” or for working land militants claim as theirs.

The surge in killings follows a quiet comeback by Boko Haram, which had been overshadowed by its rival ISWAP for years. Regional forces focused hard on ISWAP because it used drones to stage deadly ambushes on military convoys and set up bomb factories inside ungoverned swamps. While soldiers chased drones and IEDs, Boko Haram’s leaders slipped back into villages along the lake’s edge, rebuilding cash flows from smuggling and extortion. Analysts say the group now runs a leaner operation, avoiding big pitched battles that draw airstrikes. Instead, it stages hit-and-run raids on soft targets like markets and fishing camps, then vanishes into the marshes.

Economic collapse around the lake made the comeback easier. Lake Chad has shrunk by 90% since the 1960s, turning once-fertile farmland into dust. With crops failing and fish stocks plummeting, families earn less than $2 a day. Militants exploit the desperation, paying farmers to plant on their land or ferry supplies across the lake. In some towns, fighters now control the only working wells, charging families for water. The Nigerian government’s aid programs are either late or missing entirely, leaving communities to fend for themselves.

ISWAP, meanwhile, hasn’t slowed down its drone campaign. The group’s technicians fly modified quadcopters over military bases, filming troop movements and guiding mortar strikes. Last month, ISWAP dropped explosives on a Nigerian army outpost near [Monguno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monguno), killing six soldiers. The attack showed how ISWAP’s tech edge lets it outmaneuver a military struggling with fuel shortages and poor maintenance. Soldiers say their radios and trucks often break down, while ISWAP fighters vanish into villages where locals fear retaliation for talking to the army.

International donors still fund most counterterror operations, but the money rarely reaches frontline units. A U.N. audit last year found that 40% of security funds meant for the lake region never left Abuja. Corruption and bureaucracy mean weapons and fuel take months to arrive, if they arrive at all. Meanwhile, the militants’ propaganda stays strong. ISWAP’s radio stations broadcast sermons in Kanuri and Hausa, while Boko Haram’s pamphlets promise “justice” for the poor. Young men with no jobs or schools to go to keep signing up.

The Lake Chad Basin now looks like a patchwork of militant fiefs. In some areas, ISWAP controls the roads and collects tolls. In others, Boko Haram runs extortion rackets on the lake’s ferry routes. Villagers say they’re trapped between fighters who demand loyalty and soldiers who treat everyone as suspects. Aid workers say malnutrition rates are climbing as families eat one meal a day. The Nigerian government talks of “clear, hold, and build” strategies, but on the ground, there’s little holding and almost no building.

What happens next? Militants are likely to keep exploiting the gaps. Unless governance improves and jobs return, the cycle of violence will keep spinning. The U.S. and Europe have pledged more surveillance drones and training, but locals say that alone won’t stop the killings. Without real economic revival and a government that shows up, the lake will stay a haven for militants—and a graveyard for civilians.
