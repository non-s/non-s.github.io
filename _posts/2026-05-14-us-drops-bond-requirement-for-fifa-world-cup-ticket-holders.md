The U.S. State Department announced Wednesday that fans from five African nations who qualified for the 2026 FIFA World Cup no longer face a hefty visa bond requirement. The exemption, effective immediately, applies to ticket holders from Algeria, Cape Verde, Ivory Coast, Senegal and Tunisia. These nations were among 50 countries subjected to a visa bond policy introduced by the Trump administration in 2025, requiring visitors to pay between $5,000 and $15,000 to obtain a U.S. tourist visa.

## Bond requirement waived for qualifying nations

Under the new policy, fans from the five African nations must still follow standard visa procedures but are exempt from the bond payment. To qualify, they must hold valid World Cup tickets and have registered through the FIFA PASS system, which expedites visa processing for tournament attendees. The State Department confirmed the waiver applies only to those with confirmed tickets and completed registrations.

The bond requirement, expanded in early 2026, has created significant barriers for fans from many countries seeking to attend the World Cup in the United States. While the exemption benefits fans from Algeria, Cape Verde, Ivory Coast, Senegal and Tunisia, supporters from other nations—including several in South America and Asia—still face the financial hurdle. Critics argue the bond policy disproportionately affects lower-income fans, particularly in regions where soccer is the dominant sport.

## Visa process streamlined for World Cup fans

FIFA PASS, a digital registration system launched by soccer’s governing body, allows fans to pre-register their travel documents and ticket details. The platform aims to reduce bureaucratic delays and ensure smoother entry for tournament attendees. U.S. officials have stated that the waiver is part of broader efforts to facilitate travel for World Cup spectators while maintaining security protocols.

The five African nations exempt from the bond requirement are among 16 total teams from the continent to qualify for the 2026 World Cup, co-hosted by the U.S., Canada and Mexico. The tournament, scheduled for June and July 2026, will feature 48 teams, the largest in FIFA history. Fans from the remaining 45 countries subject to the bond requirement must still pay the fee or seek alternative visa arrangements, which could impact attendance from key markets.

The State Department did not indicate whether additional exemptions would be granted as the tournament approaches. Travel restrictions and visa policies for sporting events often draw scrutiny, with human rights groups and soccer federations previously calling for more inclusive entry rules for fans. The waiver for African nations marks a rare concession in a policy criticized for limiting global access to major sporting events.
