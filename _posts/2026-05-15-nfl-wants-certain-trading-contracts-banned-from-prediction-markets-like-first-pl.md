The National Football League (NFL) has formally requested that regulators prohibit certain types of trading contracts in prediction markets, according to a letter reviewed by CNBC. The league specifically targets contracts tied to early-game events, such as the first play of a matchup, as well as those linked to player injuries. The proposal also seeks to raise the minimum age for participating in sports-related contract trading.

The NFL’s letter, sent to the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) [Commodity Futures Trading Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission), argues that these contracts encourage speculative trading on events with limited information. The league contends that such bets create unfair advantages and distort market integrity, particularly when outcomes hinge on unpredictable factors like player health or early-game momentum.

Prediction markets, which allow traders to bet on future events, have grown in popularity alongside sports wagering. Platforms like Kalshi and PredictIt have faced scrutiny over the types of contracts they offer, with critics arguing that some bets exploit uncertainty for profit. The NFL’s proposal reflects broader concerns about transparency and fairness in these markets.

Under the NFL’s plan, contracts predicting player injuries or first-play results would be banned. The league also wants to increase the minimum age for participation from 18 to 21, aligning with gambling laws in some states. The CFTC has not publicly commented on the proposal, but the letter signals the NFL’s intent to shape regulatory standards for sports-related prediction markets.

The letter comes as sports leagues increasingly engage with betting markets. The NFL has partnerships with major sportsbooks and has embraced in-game wagering as a revenue stream. However, the league now seeks to limit certain speculative contracts to protect market integrity. The proposal could set a precedent for how other sports leagues approach prediction markets.

If implemented, the changes would affect platforms facilitating these contracts. Kalshi, a prominent prediction market, has defended its approach, arguing that its contracts provide valuable insights into public sentiment. The NFL’s push suggests a divide between leagues and prediction market operators over acceptable betting types.

The CFTC will review the NFL’s proposal alongside public feedback. If approved, the rules could reshape how prediction markets operate, particularly for sports events. The outcome may influence other leagues considering similar measures.
