U.S. crude prices jumped 3% Monday after former President Donald Trump warned Iran the ‘clock is ticking’ on negotiations to end the war in Gaza. The warning came after weekend talks in Doha between U.S. and Iranian officials failed to reach a breakthrough, raising the specter of renewed hostilities between Washington and Tehran. While Trump didn’t specify what action he might take, his rhetoric—echoed by other U.S. officials—sent markets scrambling to price in the risk of another supply shock in the oil market.

The price of Brent crude, the global benchmark, rose above $85 a barrel Monday, up from about $82 Friday. U.S. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude also climbed, touching $80 a barrel before paring some gains. Traders cited the risk of a military escalation in the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint for global oil shipments. Even a minor disruption there—like a temporary halt to Iranian exports or a tanker attack—could send prices soaring further, analysts warned. The last time tensions spiked this high, in 2019, oil briefly jumped 20% after drone strikes on Saudi oil facilities.

## Trump’s warning puts markets on high alert
Trump’s comments came during a weekend speech in South Carolina, where he told supporters that Iran is ‘playing games’ and that the U.S. won’t tolerate further provocations. His warning followed reports that Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu [](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Netanyahu) had authorized a limited strike on Iranian nuclear sites in retaliation for Tehran’s support of proxy groups like Hamas and Hezbollah. While neither Israel nor Iran has confirmed such an attack, the threat alone has traders bracing for volatility. The White House declined to comment on Trump’s remarks, but National Security Council spokesperson Adrienne Watson said the administration is ‘monitoring the situation closely.’

Oil traders aren’t just watching the rhetoric—they’re also tracking Iran’s oil exports, which have held steady at around 1.5 million barrels a day despite U.S. sanctions. Most of that oil flows to China, India, and Syria, but any sudden halt could tighten global supplies. The International Energy Agency (IEA) estimates that a 2 million barrel-a-day disruption—less than 2% of global demand—could push prices past $100 a barrel. That’s a level last seen during the 2022 energy crisis after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine.

## Iran’s nuclear program looms over talks
Behind the scenes, the impasse over Iran’s nuclear program is the biggest sticking point. Talks to revive the 2015 nuclear deal—known as the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) [](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_Comprehensive_Plan_of_Action)—have stalled for months. Iran wants sanctions lifted in exchange for curbing its uranium enrichment, but the U.S. insists on stricter terms, including limits on Iran’s missile program. The deadlock has left Iran free to expand its nuclear capabilities, which Israel views as an existential threat. Netanyahu has repeatedly vowed to prevent Iran from obtaining a nuclear weapon, even if it means military action.

The economic fallout from a conflict could be severe. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) estimates that a prolonged disruption in Persian Gulf oil shipments would push gasoline prices in America up by 20-30 cents a gallon within weeks. Airlines and shipping companies, already grappling with high fuel costs, would face another hit. Meanwhile, European countries reliant on Middle Eastern oil—especially Germany—could see energy bills rise, complicating efforts to rebuild their economies post-Ukraine war.

## What happens next?
For now, markets are pricing in a 20-30% chance of a major supply disruption in the next 60 days, according to Goldman Sachs. But the real risk isn’t just a military strike—it’s the potential for Iran to retaliate asymmetrically, like targeting oil tankers or Saudi oil fields. Such moves would likely trigger a sharp but temporary spike in prices, followed by a slow decline as markets adjust. The bigger concern is whether this escalation drags the U.S. back into a prolonged conflict in the Middle East, something that could destabilize the region for years.

The next few weeks will be critical. Diplomats from the U.S., Iran, and Qatar are expected to meet again in mid-August, but expectations are low. Meanwhile, oil traders are bracing for the worst, loading up on futures contracts and hedging against further price spikes. One thing’s clear: until this standoff is resolved, the world’s energy markets—and the economies that depend on them—will stay on edge.
