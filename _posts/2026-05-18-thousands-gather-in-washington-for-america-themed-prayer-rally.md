Thousands of people gathered on the [National Mall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Mall) in Washington on Sunday for a daylong prayer rally designed to 'rededicate' the US as 'One Nation Under God.' The event featured worship music, stage decorations resembling federal buildings, and stained-glass windows depicting American founders alongside a white cross. Organizers called it a celebration of 250 years of US independence, but the rally drew sharp criticism for appearing to promote Christian nationalism in a country founded on secular governance.

Donald Trump and several top Cabinet officials took the stage to address the crowd, which organizers estimated at 2,000 people. The event was organized by Freedom 250, a public-private partnership with White House backing. Trump’s appearance included a video of him reading verses from 2 Chronicles in the Old Testament, footage later used during a marathon Bible-reading event in Washington.

## Critics call rally a violation of church-state separation

The rally’s overt Christian imagery and messaging sparked immediate backlash from civil liberties groups and constitutional scholars. Critics argue the event violated the First Amendment’s prohibition on government establishment of religion by blending political leadership with religious ceremony. The Freedom From Religion Foundation called the rally an 'egregious violation' of the separation between church and state, warning it could normalize Christian nationalism in American politics.

Freedom 250 described the event as a non-partisan celebration of faith and national identity. Organizers emphasized the rally’s focus on unity and spiritual renewal, with performances by Christian worship bands and speeches from evangelical Protestant leaders. The stage design included columns resembling federal buildings and stained-glass panels featuring historical figures like George Washington, all framed by a large white cross.

## Trump’s role raises questions about presidential authority

Trump’s participation in the rally raised concerns about the use of presidential authority to promote a specific religious worldview. The White House defended the event as part of broader celebrations marking 250 years of US independence, but legal experts noted the potential for government endorsement of religion. The rally’s timing, just months before the presidential election, also fueled speculation about its political motivations.

The event’s organizers streamed Trump’s video address to churches and other venues across the country, amplifying its reach. While Freedom 250 framed the rally as a spiritual gathering, critics saw it as a deliberate attempt to associate the US government with Christianity. The debate over the event’s constitutionality is likely to continue, with legal challenges possible in the coming weeks.

What happens next will depend on how the public and courts respond to the rally’s message. Civil liberties groups have vowed to monitor government actions following the event, while religious freedom advocates plan to file complaints with federal agencies. For now, the rally stands as a high-profile example of how faith and politics are increasingly intertwined in American public life.
