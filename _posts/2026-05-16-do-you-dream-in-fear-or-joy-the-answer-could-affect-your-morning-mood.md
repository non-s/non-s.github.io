Dreams of fear or joy may do more than just entertain you at night—they could influence how you feel when you wake up. A new study published in *Sleep* reveals that the emotions experienced in dreams can affect your mood the following morning. Researchers found that people who dreamed of fear were more likely to wake up in a lower mood, while those who dreamed of joy reported better psychological function after waking.

The study, conducted by a team of sleep researchers, highlights the role dreams play in emotional regulation. While sleep is widely recognized as essential for overall health, the specific impact of dream content on daily mood has been less understood. The findings suggest that dreams may act as a subconscious mechanism for processing emotions, helping the brain prepare for the day ahead.

## How Dreams Influence Morning Mood

The research team analyzed dream reports from participants and compared them to their morning mood assessments. Those who reported fear in their dreams—such as being chased or experiencing danger—were more likely to wake up feeling anxious or depressed. In contrast, participants who dreamed of positive experiences, like happiness or success, tended to wake up in a better mood.

Experts emphasize that these findings do not prove that bad dreams cause low moods, but they do indicate a strong association. The study’s lead author noted that dreams may reflect underlying emotional states, and processing these emotions during sleep could help regulate mood upon waking.

Sleep researcher [Dr. Matthew Walker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_Walker_(neuroscientist))—known for his work on sleep and the brain—has previously suggested that dreams help the brain consolidate memories and regulate emotions. While this study focuses on short-term mood effects, Walker’s broader research implies that dream content could have long-term psychological benefits or consequences.

## What This Means for Your Sleep and Mood

The study’s authors recommend paying attention to your dream content as a potential indicator of your emotional well-being. If you frequently wake up feeling drained or anxious, it may be worth examining the themes in your dreams. Keeping a dream journal could help identify patterns and provide insights into your emotional state.

While more research is needed to fully understand the connection between dreams and mood, the study offers a compelling reason to prioritize healthy sleep habits. Experts suggest maintaining a consistent sleep schedule, reducing stress before bed, and creating a relaxing sleep environment to improve both dream quality and morning mood.

For those who struggle with recurring nightmares, cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) or other sleep interventions may help. The study’s findings underscore the importance of addressing sleep disturbances, as they could have ripple effects on daily emotional function.

Researchers plan to expand their work by exploring how different types of dreams—such as lucid dreaming or recurring dreams—affect long-term mood and mental health. Future studies may also investigate whether interventions, like dream rehearsal therapy, can help shift dream content from fear-based to more positive experiences.
