Keep Britain Tidy just announced the winners of this year’s [Blue Flag awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Flag), and six UK beaches made the cut—earning the right to fly the iconic blue-and-white flag for cleanliness, safety, and eco-friendly practices. But Avon Beach in [Christchurch, Dorset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset) nearly squeaked in too. Nearly. It fell short by a razor-thin margin, thanks to a last-minute paperwork slip-up that broke the program’s strict rules.


The [Blue Flag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Flag) program, run by the [Foundation for Environmental Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundation_for_Environmental_Education) (FEE), sets tough standards. Beaches must pass water quality tests, have clean facilities, offer safety gear, and provide environmental education. This year, six UK beaches cleared every hurdle: [Bournemouth Pier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bournemouth_Pier), [Broadhaven South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broadhaven_South), [Croyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croyde), [Marlag Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marlag_Bay), [Perranporth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perranporth), and [Skegness North](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skegness).


Avon Beach, a popular spot with golden sand and gentle waves, had met most requirements—its water passed tests, lifeguards were on duty, and it had recycling bins. But when inspectors checked the fine print, they found a single missing form. The beach’s team had forgotten to file a final environmental management plan update before the deadline. It’s the kind of small but critical error that can cost a flag, even when everything else is perfect.


Keep Britain Tidy’s chief executive, [Allison Ogden-Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allison_Ogden-Newton), said the program isn’t flexible on paperwork. “Blue Flags aren’t just about what you can see,” she said. “They’re about what you can prove. If a beach can’t show it meets every standard on paper, it doesn’t get the flag, no matter how clean or safe it feels.” She confirmed Avon Beach can reapply next year once the paperwork is fixed.


This year’s winners span England’s south coast, Wales’ surf spots, and Lincolnshire’s family-friendly shores. Perranporth in Cornwall, known for its three-mile stretch of sand and strong waves, earned its flag for the 12th time. Broadhaven South in [Pembrokeshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pembrokeshire) kept its streak alive too, thanks to its dunes and clean water.


The flags matter beyond bragging rights. Blue Flag beaches attract more visitors, boost local economies, and push councils to keep standards high. Research shows tourists will travel farther for a guaranteed clean, safe beach—and they’ll pay more for it. For small towns like [Skegness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skegness), that can mean the difference between a quiet season and a profitable summer.


Keep Britain Tidy says the slip-up at Avon Beach is a reminder that Blue Flag status isn’t automatic. Beaches have to earn it every year, and one missed form can cost the flag. The charity urges beach managers to double-check paperwork early and avoid last-minute rushes.


For now, the six Blue Flag beaches are celebrating. Avon Beach’s team is already working to fix the issue and plans to reapply in 2025. If they succeed, Dorset could have another flag to add to its coastline’s growing list.
