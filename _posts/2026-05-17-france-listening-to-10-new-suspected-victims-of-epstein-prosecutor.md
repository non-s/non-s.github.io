French prosecutors are now listening to 10 new suspected victims of Jeffrey Epstein’s network, expanding an ongoing human trafficking investigation into how French perpetrators may have facilitated his crimes. Paris Public Prosecutor Laure Beccuau told RTL radio on Sunday that about 20 suspected victims have contacted authorities since she publicly urged people to come forward in February. Roughly half of them were already known, but the rest were entirely new to investigators. “There are around 10 of them,” Beccuau said. “The choice we’ve made for the time being is to listen to these victims.”

Some of these new complainants are based abroad, so French investigators have arranged meetings in Paris at times that work for the victims. Beccuau did not specify which countries the victims are from or whether any arrests have been made. The probe began after the US Justice Department released a trove of files related to Epstein’s disgraced financial empire and its ties to underage sex trafficking.

## Epstein’s shadow still looms over France
France’s magistrates are focusing on possible offences committed on French soil or involving French citizens who may have helped Epstein’s operations. This includes examining whether anyone in France profited from or facilitated his crimes, which could lead to charges of human trafficking, sexual exploitation, or complicity. Epstein died in US custody in 2019 while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges, but his network’s reach appears to extend into Europe.

The French investigation mirrors similar efforts in other countries after the release of the US files. These documents exposed details about Epstein’s associates, including his longtime associate Ghislaine Maxwell, who was convicted in the US in 2021 for helping procure underage girls for Epstein. Maxwell is serving a 20-year prison sentence. While the French probe does not name any suspects yet, it signals that authorities are taking seriously the possibility that Epstein’s network operated across borders.

## Why France reopened the case
French magistrates reopened their investigation in 2023 after US authorities handed over new evidence. This included files from the 2019 federal probe into Epstein’s finances and his connections to powerful figures. The US case had already linked Epstein to minors in multiple states, but the French angle suggests his operations may have had European tentacles. Prosecutors are now cross-referencing US evidence with French bank records, travel logs, and witness testimonies to build their case.

Beccuau emphasized that the focus remains on listening to victims first. “We’re not rushing to conclusions,” she told RTL. “We’re giving these people the space to speak, to describe what happened to them, and to provide the evidence they can.” So far, no charges have been filed in France, but the prosecutor’s office confirmed the investigation is active and expanding. Legal experts say the case could take months or even years to conclude, depending on how quickly victims come forward and how much evidence they provide.

The broader implications are significant. If French prosecutors can prove that Epstein’s network operated in Europe, it could force governments across the continent to re-examine their own records and potentially uncover more victims. It could also pressure law enforcement in other countries to reopen dormant cases or share intelligence. For now, though, the priority is clear: giving victims a chance to be heard while piecing together the full scope of Epstein’s reach.
