President Donald Trump escalated his rhetoric on Monday, warning that patience is wearing thin in negotiations with Iran. In a late-night post on Truth Social, he wrote: *“The clock is ticking fast on Iran. They don’t play nice, and they never have.”* His message came after Iranian state media reported that the US had not made any concrete concessions in response to Tehran’s latest proposals. The reports, which cite unnamed officials, suggest the talks are at a standstill with no clear path forward. The US State Department has not issued an official response, and a senior administration official declined to comment when reached by phone. The silence from Washington has left many wondering if the administration is rethinking its approach or simply waiting for Iran to blink first. Behind the scenes, diplomats from both sides are privately admitting the talks are in rough shape, with little progress on key issues like Iran’s nuclear program and its support for regional militias. The last round of indirect talks in Oman ended without an agreement, and since then, both sides have dug in their heels. Iran’s foreign minister, Hossein Amir-Abdollahian [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hossein_Amir-Abdollahian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hossein_Amir-Abdollahian), has publicly blamed Washington for the lack of movement, calling the US position “unrealistic.” Meanwhile, Trump’s team has signaled it won’t accept anything less than a deal that addresses Iran’s ballistic missile program and its influence in countries like Syria and Yemen. The standoff comes at a sensitive time. Iran’s economy is struggling under the weight of US sanctions, and its leaders are under pressure to show their people they’re not backing down. At the same time, Israel has warned it may take military action if Iran’s nuclear program advances too far. That threat has forced the US to tread carefully—too soft a stance could be seen as weakness, but too hard a line risks sparking a wider conflict. ## Trump’s tough talk isn’t just about Iran The president’s warning fits a pattern of escalating pressure on Iran since he took office. Last year, his administration imposed new sanctions targeting Iran’s oil exports and its Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. The moves were designed to squeeze Tehran’s economy and force it back to the negotiating table. But so far, Iran has refused to make major concessions. Instead, it’s accused the US of sabotaging the talks by demanding too much upfront. The White House insists it’s willing to negotiate, but only if Iran agrees to curb its nuclear ambitions and stop funding proxy groups across the Middle East. The problem is, Iran sees those demands as non-starters. Its leaders argue that their nuclear program is for peaceful purposes and that their support for groups like Hezbollah [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah) is a matter of national security. The deadlock has left diplomats scrambling for a face-saving way out. Some analysts suggest the talks could collapse entirely, leading to a new round of sanctions or even military action. Others believe both sides will find a way to keep talking, if only to avoid the worst-case scenario. One thing is clear: the longer the talks drag on, the harder it’ll be to reach a deal that satisfies everyone. ## What happens next? The next few days could decide the fate of the negotiations. Iran’s supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_Khamenei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_Khamenei), is expected to weigh in publicly, which could either soften Tehran’s stance or double down on its demands. Meanwhile, Trump’s team is reportedly weighing its options, including whether to offer limited sanctions relief to break the ice. But any concession from the US risks being seen as a sign of weakness, both at home and abroad. On the other side, Iran’s leaders are under pressure to deliver results. Protests have flared up in recent months over economic hardship, and the government can’t afford to be seen as surrendering to Washington’s demands. If the talks fail, the consequences could be severe. A resumption of nuclear enrichment at higher levels could trigger new sanctions or even a military strike from Israel. The US would then face a tough choice: either back Israel’s actions or try to rein it in, both of which could have major regional implications. For now, the clock is ticking—and neither side looks ready to blink.
