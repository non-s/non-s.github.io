The crowd of 10,000 at the University of Arizona [Wildcat Stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildcat_Stadium) turned rowdy when former Google CEO [Eric Schmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Schmidt) said AI would soon create more jobs than it eliminates. Schmidt, who led Google for a decade until 2011, told the class of 2024 that AI’s productivity gains would lead to new industries and roles. The response was immediate—loud boos cut through his speech, with some students shouting ‘Where are the jobs?’ and ‘You don’t get it!’ Schmidt paused, smiled, and said, ‘I hear you,’ before continuing his address. Videos of the moment spread quickly on social media, sparking debates about AI’s real-world impact on young workers.

## Other speakers faced similar pushback
Schmidt wasn’t alone. At [Boston University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_University)’s commencement, entrepreneur [Mark Cuban](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Cuban) got booed when he suggested AI would level the playing field for small businesses. A student in the crowd held up a sign that read ‘AI takes jobs.’ At [Purdue University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purdue_University), a tech CEO’s speech about AI’s ‘inevitability’ was met with scattered but audible groans. The pattern suggests a generational divide: older speakers often frame AI as progress, while graduates see it as a threat to their first jobs in fields like software, customer service, and even white-collar roles.

## Why the anger isn’t just about AI
Graduates aren’t just reacting to the technology itself—they’re reacting to the job market they’re entering. The class of 2024 is entering the workforce as tech layoffs continue and AI tools like chatbots replace entry-level roles. A recent [McKinsey report](https://www.mckinsey.com) found that 40% of companies are cutting entry-level positions due to automation. Schmidt argued that AI would create ‘new kinds of work we can’t even imagine yet,’ but many students don’t see evidence of that. One University of Arizona senior told NBC News, ‘I’m supposed to be excited about AI helping me find a job? My internship got canceled because they replaced it with an AI chatbot.’

## The speakers’ arguments weren’t wrong—just tone-deaf
Schmidt and others like him aren’t entirely wrong. Historically, technology has created more jobs than it destroyed long-term. The issue is timing. AI’s disruption is happening faster than past waves of automation, and it’s hitting young workers first. Economists at [MIT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology) note that while AI will eventually generate new roles, the transition could take a decade—leaving graduates in limbo. Cuban, who owns the Dallas Mavericks, argued that AI would let small businesses compete with giants like Amazon, but students pointed out that most startups fail within five years.

## The backlash reflects deeper frustration
The booing isn’t just about AI—it’s about a system that’s failing young workers. Wages for entry-level jobs have stagnated while costs for education and housing have skyrocketed. A [Federal Reserve study](https://www.federalreserve.gov) shows that 31% of recent grads are underemployed, working jobs that don’t require their degree. Schmidt’s optimism clashed with that reality. When he mentioned AI’s potential to ‘solve global problems,’ one student shouted, ‘We need jobs first!’ The moment captured a generation’s frustration: they’re told technology will save the economy, but they’re the ones staring at empty inboxes after submitting 200 job applications.

What happens next? The job market won’t wait for AI to create new roles. Companies are already using AI to cut costs, and policymakers are struggling to keep up. Some universities are adding AI ethics courses to address student concerns, but others are doubling down on tech-focused curricula. Schmidt’s speech might be remembered as a symbol of the disconnect between Silicon Valley’s vision and Main Street’s reality—but the graduates walking off that stage aren’t just leaving school. They’re entering a workforce where the rules are changing faster than anyone predicted.
