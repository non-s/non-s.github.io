Kiyoshi Kurosawa has spent decades crafting slow-burn psychological thrillers, but this year the Japanese director is stepping into new territory: the samurai genre. His film *The Samurai and the Prisoner* premiered at Cannes on Tuesday, marking his first time directing a period piece set in feudal Japan. The movie follows a samurai tasked with guarding a prisoner in a castle during a time of political unrest, blending Kurosawa’s signature tension with the action and intrigue of classic samurai films.


The timing couldn’t be more striking. Two of Kurosawa’s most famous former students are also in Cannes this year, each vying for the Palme d’Or. Ryusuke Hamaguchi, known for *Drive My Car* and *Wheel of Fortune and Fantasy*, and Koji Fukada, who directed *Harmonium* and *Love Life*, are both presenting their own films in the main competition. It’s a rare moment where a mentor and his top disciples are all on the festival’s biggest stage at once.


Kurosawa, now 63, has built a reputation over 40 years for films like *Cure* and *Tokyo Sonata*, which dive into Japan’s quiet crises and societal fractures. His move into samurai territory surprised some fans, but he’s argued that the genre’s focus on honor, betrayal, and power struggles aligns with his usual themes. The film was shot in Kyoto’s Toei Kyoto Studio Park, a location famous for housing sets from classic jidaigeki (period dramas) like *Mito Komon*. Sources on set said Kurosawa insisted on practical effects, including fight choreography by Junpei Morita, who worked on *The Last Samurai*.


The festival buzz so far has centered on Kurosawa’s signature style making the transition to swordplay. Early screenings described the film as more atmospheric than action-heavy, with long takes and sparse dialogue echoing his earlier work. One critic called it "a samurai movie that feels like a Kurosawa film first, and a samurai film second."


Hamaguchi and Fukada, both graduates of Tokyo University’s film program like Kurosawa, have taken very different paths. Hamaguchi’s films often explore human connection through quiet, dialogue-driven narratives, while Fukada leans into dark comedies and domestic dramas. Their presence in the competition highlights how Kurosawa’s influence extends beyond Japan’s borders, with both directors earning global acclaim. Hamaguchi’s *Drive My Car* won the Oscar for Best International Feature in 2022, and Fukada’s *Love Life* was nominated for the Golden Lion in 2022.


For Kurosawa, *The Samurai and the Prisoner* isn’t just a genre experiment—it’s a return to his roots. Before becoming a filmmaker, he studied literature and was deeply influenced by classic Japanese cinema, including the works of Masaki Kobayashi and Akira Kurosawa (no relation). The new film’s plot, set in the late Edo period, follows a ronin who must decide whether to obey orders or protect a prisoner who may hold the key to a rebellion. It’s a story that mirrors the moral dilemmas in his earlier work, but with swords instead of modern settings.


What happens next? Kurosawa’s film will screen again this week, with critics and audiences weighing in on whether he’s successfully merged his style with samurai tropes. Meanwhile, Hamaguchi and Fukada’s films will face their own verdicts from the jury led by Greta Gerwig. Whether one of them wins or not, this Cannes lineup proves Kurosawa’s legacy isn’t just in his own films—it’s in the artists he’s helped shape.
