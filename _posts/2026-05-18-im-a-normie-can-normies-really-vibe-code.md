Last weekend, I tried something I’d never done before: coding. Not because I needed to, but because I wanted to see if regular people like me could actually make something that works. The answer? Yeah, it’s shockingly doable. Here’s what happened when I sat down with [Claude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_(language_model)), an AI assistant, to build a tiny database tracking petty grievances — like why parking spots always disappear or why office printers jam at the worst times. Spoiler: we built it in under 48 hours using free tools anyone can access.


The project started as a joke. I’d seen people online talking about how "anyone can code now" because of AI helpers, but I didn’t believe it. So I set a challenge: build a working database with no prior coding experience, no budget, and no fancy software. Just me, a laptop, and an AI. The goal? Track the kind of small annoyances that make people roll their eyes or mutter under their breath. Things like "the coffee machine is always empty by 10 AM" or "why does the Wi-Fi cut out during Zoom calls?"


## Why anyone can try coding now

The tools we used are the real game-changer here. I started with [Google Sheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Sheets), which most people already know how to use. Then I used a free service called [Airtable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airtable) to turn those spreadsheets into a proper database. The AI wrote the code for me — mostly in a language called SQL that databases use — but I didn’t need to understand it to make it work. I just had to describe what I wanted, and the AI turned my words into working code. It felt like having a translator who spoke both English and computer language.


Claude handled the technical parts, but I made the decisions. I picked the categories for the grievances: "location," "time of day," "severity," and "repeat offender." Then I added sample complaints to test it. Within hours, we had a live database where I could sort grievances by location or severity. It wasn’t pretty, but it worked. Anyone could add a new complaint, and the database would update instantly. The whole thing cost nothing and took less time than binge-watching a Netflix season.


## What this means for "normies" who think coding is scary

I’m not a programmer. I’ve never taken a computer science class, and I can barely tell the difference between HTML and CSS. But here’s the thing: I didn’t need to know those things to build something useful. The AI did the heavy lifting, and I just had to tell it what I wanted in plain English. It’s like having a super-smart intern who never complains about coffee breaks.


This isn’t about replacing professional developers. It’s about giving regular people the power to automate small, annoying tasks. Maybe you want to track how often your kid leaves their shoes in the hallway. Maybe you run a tiny business and need a simple way to log customer complaints. You don’t need a degree in computer science — you just need a clear goal and the right tools. The AI handles the code; you handle the logic.


## The ugly parts (because nothing’s perfect)

Of course, it wasn’t all smooth sailing. The first version of our grievance tracker had a bug: it wouldn’t save new entries. I described the problem to the AI, and it fixed it in minutes. But that’s the thing about AI — it’s fast, but it’s not psychic. Sometimes you have to describe the problem in a way it understands, which means you need to think carefully about what you’re asking for. It’s not mind-reading; it’s more like very advanced autocomplete.


Another hiccup: the database looked like a spreadsheet that someone forgot to format. It worked, but it wasn’t pretty. If I wanted something more polished, I’d need to learn some basic design principles or hire a designer. But for a weekend project? Good enough is good enough. The point wasn’t to build a masterpiece; it was to prove that coding isn’t some mystical skill reserved for geniuses in hoodies.


## What’s next for the grievance tracker

Right now, the database is just a fun experiment. But I’m already thinking about how to expand it. Maybe add a voting system so people can upvote the most annoying grievances. Or link it to a map so you can see which neighborhoods have the worst parking. The possibilities are endless, and none of them require me to become a programmer overnight.


The bigger takeaway? Coding isn’t a black box anymore. With AI tools, you can dip your toes in without drowning. You won’t build the next big app in a weekend — but you can build something that solves a small problem, and that’s a win. The barrier to entry has dropped so low that "normies" like me can actually vibe code. And if I can do it, so can you.
