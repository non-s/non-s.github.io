Prime Minister Keir Starmer [survived](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer) the worst week of his leadership after nearly 90 Labour MPs signed a letter demanding he step down. The revolt followed the resignations of five junior ministers in just 48 hours, including junior minister Helen Hayes, who reportedly told colleagues Starmer had become a ‘lame duck’ leader. The scale of the backbench rebellion has not been seen in Labour since the party’s 2010 general election loss under Gordon Brown [Gordon Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Brown). MPs from all wings of the party signed the letter, signaling Starmer’s grip on the party is slipping fast.

The resignations started with junior minister Helen Hayes, who quit her role at the Department for Education on Wednesday. By Thursday, four more junior ministers had followed—one from the Treasury, one from the Home Office, and two from the Department for Business. Hayes reportedly told colleagues she no longer believed Starmer could win the next election. Her departure was the first by a minister since Starmer took office in July 2024, and it set off a wave of panic among Labour MPs already nervous about sinking poll numbers.

## Who wants Starmer out—and who might replace him?

Former Health Secretary Wes Streeting [Wes Streeting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wes_Streeting) has already said he would stand in any leadership contest, signaling he’s ready to challenge Starmer if enough MPs back him. Streeting, a centrist who served as shadow health secretary under Jeremy Corbyn, has been positioning himself as the party’s best shot at winning back disillusioned voters. He’s argued Labour needs to move faster on economic growth and public services, a message that resonates with many MPs frustrated by Starmer’s cautious approach.

Meanwhile, Andy Burnham [Andy Burnham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Burnham), the Mayor of Greater Manchester, is plotting a return to Parliament. Burnham confirmed he will stand in the Makerfield by-election next month, a seat Labour lost to the Conservatives in 2023. His decision to re-enter the fray is seen as a direct challenge to Starmer’s leadership, especially given Burnham’s history as a former cabinet minister under Gordon Brown and Tony Blair [Tony Blair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tony_Blair). Burnham’s allies say he’s positioning himself as the candidate who can unite the party’s left and right wings.

## Why this matters beyond Westminster

The crisis comes as Labour’s poll lead over the Conservatives has shrunk to single digits, a far cry from the 20-point advantage Starmer enjoyed last summer. The party’s internal polling, leaked to media outlets this week, shows voters are increasingly frustrated by Labour’s slow progress on key promises like NHS reform and economic recovery. The resignations and the backbench revolt suggest Starmer’s authority is eroding, not just within Westminster but across the country.

The next few weeks could determine whether Starmer survives or if Labour lurches into another leadership battle. Streeting and Burnham are the most visible challengers, but others could emerge if the pressure on Starmer intensifies. The party’s ruling National Executive Committee meets next week to discuss the crisis, and a formal confidence vote cannot be ruled out. For now, Starmer’s team insists he’s staying put, but the mood in Westminster is anything but settled.

What happens next depends on whether more ministers quit or if Starmer can rally enough MPs to his side. If the resignations keep coming, the party could face a leadership election before the next general election, scheduled for 2029. That would be a historic moment for Labour, marking the first time in over a decade it has changed leaders mid-term without losing an election.
