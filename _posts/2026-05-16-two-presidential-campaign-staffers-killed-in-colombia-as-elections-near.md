Two presidential campaign staffers were gunned down in Colombia on Friday night, just two weeks before the country votes in a presidential election to replace [Gustavo Petro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustavo_Petro). The victims were identified as Rogers Mauricio Devia Escoba, a former mayor of Cubarral, and his adviser Eder Fabian Cardona Lopez. Both men were reportedly shot by gunmen on motorbikes in the central department of Meta, a region known for ongoing armed conflict and drug trafficking. The attack happened less than a mile from a local government office, according to local reports. Right-wing presidential candidate [Abelardo de la Espriella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelardo_de_la_Espriella) announced the killings on X, calling the victims defenders of democracy. "Their only crime was believing in the Fatherland and not kneeling before the violent," he wrote, without providing details on the attackers' motives or identities. The ombudsman’s office later confirmed the attack and warned it could affect political rights ahead of the May 29 vote. They also noted a second attempt on a former mayoral candidate in the same area, though no further details were released. Police have launched an investigation but have not made arrests. The timing of the killings has raised concerns about election-related violence in a country where armed groups still operate despite a 2016 peace deal with the FARC rebels. Colombia’s elections are seen as a critical test for its fragile peace process. Petro, the country’s first leftist president, has faced criticism from right-wing factions over his handling of security and economic policies. His supporters argue his reforms are necessary to address inequality and violence. The killings come amid a surge in political campaigning, with candidates crisscrossing the country in final stretches of the race. Security forces have increased patrols in volatile regions, but critics say enforcement remains inconsistent. The ombudsman’s warning about political rights reflects growing anxiety that violence could suppress voter turnout or intimidate candidates. Local media reported that Devia Escoba had previously survived an assassination attempt in 2020, suggesting a pattern of targeted attacks against local leaders. The broader implications of these killings extend beyond the election. Colombia has seen a rise in armed group activity in recent years, with dissident FARC factions and other criminal organizations filling power vacuums left by the peace deal. The government has struggled to curb violence in rural areas, where armed groups often control local politics. This week’s attacks underscore the challenges facing Colombia’s democracy as it prepares for a pivotal vote. The elections will determine whether Petro’s agenda continues or if a conservative shift takes hold. For now, the focus is on the investigation and the safety of remaining campaign workers. The ombudsman’s office has called for urgent measures to protect political candidates and their teams ahead of the vote. If the killings go unaddressed, they could set a dangerous precedent for future elections.
