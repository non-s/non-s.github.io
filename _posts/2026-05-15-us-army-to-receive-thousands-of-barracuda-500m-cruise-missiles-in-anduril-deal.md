The U.S. Army will begin receiving at least 3,000 surface-launched Barracuda-500M cruise missiles from [Anduril](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anduril_Industries) in 2027 under a $1 billion framework agreement. The deal requires Anduril to deliver a minimum of 1,000 missiles each year for three years, according to a company release. The Barracuda-500Ms are designed for long-range precision strikes against land and maritime targets, with a range exceeding 500 nautical miles and a 100-pound warhead.

The agreement reflects a Pentagon push to rapidly expand affordable munitions production at scale. Existing long-range strike options are often criticized as too costly, difficult to produce in large quantities, or excessive for many mission profiles. Anduril’s Barracuda-500M is positioned as a more affordable alternative that maintains operational effectiveness without the premium price tag of larger systems.

The Barracuda-500M’s extended range and versatility make it suitable for both land and sea-based operations. The missile can engage fixed and moving targets, including fortified positions, air defense systems, and naval vessels. Its modular design allows for rapid adaptation to evolving mission requirements, including potential upgrades for different warhead types or guidance systems.

## US Army accelerates munitions procurement amid global tensions

The Army’s decision follows repeated warnings from military leaders about munitions shortages exposed during conflicts such as Russia’s war in Ukraine. The Pentagon has emphasized the need to replenish stockpiles while ensuring future production can meet sudden demand spikes. Anduril’s deal aligns with this strategy by prioritizing scalability and cost control without sacrificing capability.

Anduril, a defense technology company co-founded by [Palmer Luckey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palmer_Luckey), has rapidly expanded its presence in the defense sector, securing contracts for drones, AI-driven reconnaissance systems, and now long-range strike weapons. The Barracuda-500M represents one of the company’s first major forays into cruise missile production, signaling its ambition to compete with established defense contractors.

## Missile range and payload key to modern warfare needs

The Barracuda-500M’s 500-nautical-mile range places it among the longest-range land-attack cruise missiles in U.S. service. Its 100-pound warhead provides sufficient destructive power for most targets while remaining smaller and lighter than heavyweight munitions like the [Tomahawk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BGM-109_Tomahawk) missile. This balance allows for greater operational flexibility, including deployment from mobile launchers and ships.

Defense analysts note that the Army’s focus on stand-off weapons like the Barracuda-500M reflects a broader shift toward avoiding direct engagement with advanced air defenses. By striking from beyond detection ranges, U.S. forces can maintain the initiative while minimizing risk to personnel and aircraft. The missile’s design also supports integration with existing command-and-control networks, enabling rapid targeting updates in dynamic combat environments.

The first deliveries are scheduled for 2027, with the Army expected to begin operational testing shortly afterward. If successful, the Barracuda-500M could become a cornerstone of the Army’s long-range precision fires portfolio, complementing systems like the [PrSM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MGM-168_Army_Tactical_Missile_System) missile and future hypersonic weapons. The deal also positions Anduril as a key supplier in the Army’s push to modernize its missile inventory at an accelerated pace.
