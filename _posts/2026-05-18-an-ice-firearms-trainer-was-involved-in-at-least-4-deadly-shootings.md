Phoenix police fired officer David Norman in 2020 after internal reviews found he had used deadly force in four separate shootings, including the fatal shooting of a 16-year-old boy in 2016. Norman, who once described himself as “a fucking savage” in a since-deleted Facebook post, now runs a firearms training company called Aegis Defense Group. That company has trained thousands of federal agents, including members of the Department of Homeland Security’s Special Response Teams.

Norman’s past shootings came under renewed scrutiny after Wired obtained and reviewed police records detailing each incident. The files show that in the 2016 shooting, Norman fired at 16-year-old Luis Valenzuela after the boy allegedly pointed a handgun at him. Valenzuela died at the scene. Police later confirmed the handgun was stolen. Norman was never charged criminally, but the city’s police department ruled the shooting was justified.

## How the trainer became a federal instructor
Norman’s company, Aegis Defense Group, has grown into a major player in federal firearms training since its founding in 2017. Public records show the company earned nearly $1 million in federal contracts between 2017 and 2021, with most of the money coming from Immigration and Customs Enforcement [ICE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Immigration_and_Customs_Enforcement) and Homeland Security. His firm specializes in close-quarters combat and active-shooter response, skills the government pays top dollar to teach.

But the same training materials that teach federal agents to stay calm under pressure were written by a man whose own record raised alarms. The records show Norman was involved in multiple shootings while still on the Phoenix force, including one in 2018 where he fired at a man who lunged at him with a knife. That shooting was also ruled justified. A third incident in 2019 involved a suspect who died after a struggle with officers, though Norman’s role was limited to backup fire.

## What the records say about the shootings
The police documents reveal that in each case, Norman followed department protocol. Review boards cleared him of wrongdoing, and no criminal charges were filed. But the sheer number of shootings—four in just over three years—is unusual even for a large city police department. Experts say most officers never fire their weapons in the line of duty during an entire career.

One former Phoenix officer, speaking on condition of anonymity, said Norman cultivated a reputation as an aggressive cop who didn’t hesitate to use force. “He wasn’t shy about pulling the trigger,” the officer recalled. “He always said he’d rather be judged by twelve than carried by six.” The phrase, a common police saying, implies officers would rather face legal consequences than be killed in the line of duty.

## Homeland Security’s response
Homeland Security did not respond directly to questions about whether it vetted Norman’s background before hiring his company. But in a statement, a department spokesperson said all contractors must meet federal standards and undergo background checks. ICE, which awarded Aegis Defense Group multiple contracts, has not commented on whether Norman’s past shootings affected its decision to hire him.

Norman himself has not publicly addressed the shootings or his training business since the reports surfaced. His company’s website still advertises his “real-world experience” as a selling point for federal clients. The page includes testimonials from law enforcement agencies praising Aegis Defense Group’s “high-intensity” training programs.

Federal rules require agencies to review contractors’ backgrounds, but the process varies widely. Some departments run full criminal background checks, while others rely on self-reported information. The lack of a uniform standard means incidents like Norman’s can slip through the cracks, even in agencies tasked with protecting the public.

If Homeland Security or ICE decide to review their contracts with Aegis Defense Group, it won’t be the first time a federal agency has cut ties with a contractor over past conduct. In 2022, the Department of Veterans Affairs canceled a $40 million contract with a company after reports surfaced about its CEO’s criminal history. But such reviews often happen only after public scrutiny forces the issue.
