American drivers handed over an extra $40 billion at the pump last year because of Donald Trump’s [hardline Iran policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trump_administration_and_Iran). That’s not a typo—$40 billion more on gasoline and diesel than in 2023, according to energy analysts tracking the market. The bill for extra fuel alone now exceeds what the country spends annually to repair its 43,000 structurally deficient bridges or to upgrade its aging air traffic control systems. The jump reflects how Trump’s renewed sanctions and military posturing around Iran have disrupted oil shipping lanes, tightened global supply, and driven up prices at home.

The numbers come from a Financial Times analysis of U.S. Energy Information Administration data and real-time fuel price tracking. Gasoline prices averaged $3.60 a gallon in 2024—up from $3.45 in 2023—while diesel, critical for trucks and farm equipment, rose from $4.05 to $4.20. That might sound small, but across 260 million registered vehicles, it adds up fast. A typical driver filling a 15-gallon tank twice a month paid about $420 more over the year. For truckers running long hauls, the tab climbed into the thousands.

Trump’s second term has doubled down on the “maximum pressure” campaign he started in 2018. His administration reimposed sanctions lifted under Biden, targeted Iran’s oil exports, and threatened military action after recent attacks on Red Sea shipping. The moves have pushed Iran to lean harder on its regional allies—like [Houthis in Yemen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houthi_movement)—to disrupt trade. The result? Shipping insurance rates for tankers near Iran spiked by 300%, and some oil companies rerouted cargoes halfway around the world to avoid the risk.

Domestic oil production hasn’t filled the gap. U.S. shale drillers are pumping less than they did at the peak in 2019, and new drilling leases face legal challenges. Meanwhile, refiners—already running near capacity—have struggled with higher insurance and security costs for ships loading in the Persian Gulf. Those expenses trickle down to the pump. Even with domestic oil prices stable, retail fuel prices stayed elevated because of the global squeeze.

The White House argues the strategy is working. National Security Council spokesperson John Kirby told reporters last month that cutting off Iran’s oil revenue weakens its support for groups like [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah) and [Hamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas). “We’re protecting American interests by denying funds to terror groups,” Kirby said. But critics say the policy punishes everyday Americans more than it hurts Iran’s leadership. “You can’t sanction your way to peace,” said [Senator Bernie Sanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernie_Sanders) in a floor speech last week. “This is a tax on working families, plain and simple.”

## What happens next

Congress is now debating whether to soften some sanctions as part of a broader defense bill. A bipartisan group of senators wants to ease restrictions on limited oil sales to prevent further price spikes. But Trump has vowed to veto any bill that weakens his Iran policy. Energy analysts expect prices to stay high through at least mid-2025 unless sanctions are lifted or a major supply disruption occurs.

For drivers, that means filling up will likely cost more this year too. AAA projects national gas prices to average $3.75 a gallon in 2025—another $150 per driver on top of last year’s hike. Small businesses, especially farms and trucking fleets, are already cutting routes or raising prices to cover fuel costs. The ripple effect is spreading, and Washington isn’t close to a fix.
