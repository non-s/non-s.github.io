The Gunfighter Skies Air Show at [Mountain Home Air Force Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_Home_Air_Force_Base) ended in chaos Sunday when two Navy jets collided during a performance, sending both aircraft spiraling into the ground. Bystander videos posted to social media captured the moment the jets made contact midair, their paths twisting violently before they hit the earth in a fireball of dirt and flames. Four parachutes were visible above the wreckage as emergency crews rushed to the scene.

The Idaho Statesman reported that an announcer at the show told the crowd the four Navy pilots were "found safe." The base later confirmed in a statement that the aircrew involved are "in stable condition." The Air Force did not immediately release details on the pilots’ identities or the extent of their injuries.

## What happened during the crash
The collision occurred during a routine flight demonstration at the base’s annual air show, which features military aircraft from across the services. Witnesses described seeing the two jets—likely performing a close-formation maneuver—make contact before their pilots ejected. The wreckage landed in a remote area of the base, away from crowds, but sent a thick plume of smoke into the sky visible for miles.

Emergency responders, including firefighters and medical teams, arrived within minutes. The base went into lockdown as authorities secured the area and began investigating the cause. The Federal Aviation Administration and the National Transportation Safety Board have been notified, though the military will lead the probe.

## Air show resumes after brief shutdown
The Gunfighter Skies Air Show was shut down immediately after the crash, but organizers resumed activities later in the day after a safety review. Attendees reported hearing the announcement over the loudspeakers confirming the pilots’ safety. The base has not detailed whether the incident will affect future air shows or flight operations.

Military air shows often feature high-speed maneuvers and close formations, which carry inherent risks. The U.S. military has strict safety protocols for these events, but accidents can still happen. The last major air show crash occurred in 2011 at the [RIAT air show in the UK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RIAT), where a U.S. Air Force F-22 collided with a civilian aircraft, killing the pilot of the smaller plane.

What happens next will depend on the investigation. The military will review flight data, eyewitness accounts, and video footage to determine what went wrong. Until then, the pilots’ conditions remain the top priority. Their names have not been released, and no further details on their injuries have been provided.

For now, the base is focused on supporting the aircrew and their families while ensuring the investigation moves forward transparently. The incident serves as a stark reminder of the risks military pilots take every time they fly—even during a public demonstration.
