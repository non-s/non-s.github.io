Mortgage borrowers in the U.S. and Europe just got a rude surprise. Even though the [Federal Reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Reserve) and the [European Central Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Central_Bank) left their key interest rates unchanged at recent meetings, the rates people pay for new home loans climbed roughly half a percentage point in October alone. A typical 30-year fixed mortgage in the U.S. now costs close to 7.5%, up from 7% in September. In Germany, home loans that were around 4% last month now sit near 4.6%. Banks aren’t raising their prime rates—they’re just adding a risk premium because the market’s worried about oil and inflation.
