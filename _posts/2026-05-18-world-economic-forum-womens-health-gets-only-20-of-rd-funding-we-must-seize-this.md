The World Economic Forum just dropped a hard truth: for every dollar spent on global health research, only 20 cents goes toward women’s health. That’s not just unfair—it’s bad business. Women drive 70% of all healthcare spending decisions worldwide, yet the science, treatments, and tools we rely on were built mostly by and for men. The result? Doctors miss early signs of heart attacks in women half the time. Drug dosages are often based on male metabolisms. And diseases that uniquely affect women—like endometriosis or ovarian cancer—haven’t seen new treatments in decades.

We’re leaving a $1 trillion hole in the global economy by ignoring this gap. A 2023 study in the *Journal of Health Economics* found that every dollar invested in women’s health generates $2 to $3 in economic returns. Those returns come from fewer missed workdays, lower long-term care costs, and healthier families. The math is simple: investing in women’s health isn’t charity—it’s stimulus. Countries like Sweden and Canada have already started treating women’s health as a growth sector, not a cost center.

## Why women’s bodies get less attention

The problem starts in labs. Most clinical trials historically excluded women—especially pregnant or menstruating women—because researchers feared liability. That left huge blind spots. For example, women are 50% more likely to be misdiagnosed after a heart attack because symptoms like fatigue and jaw pain were overlooked in male-focused studies. Even today, 70% of pain research subjects are male, leading to treatments that work poorly for women.

Money flows where the research goes. Only 4% of NIH funding specifically targets diseases that affect women more than men. Endometriosis, a condition that affects 1 in 10 women, gets less research funding than male-pattern baldness. The World Health Organization [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) calls this a "silent crisis"—one that costs the global economy $28 billion annually just in lost productivity.

## The pandemic made things worse

COVID-19 exposed how fragile women’s health infrastructure is. Women made up 70% of the global healthcare workforce but got only 3% of COVID-19 research funding. Pregnant women were excluded from vaccine trials for months, leaving doctors guessing about risks. Domestic violence surged by 20% during lockdowns, yet shelters and hotlines saw funding cuts. The lesson? When crises hit, women pay the price twice—first as frontline workers, then as patients with no safety net.

The Bezos family’s $100 million donation to New York City’s maternal health programs shows the scale of the gap. Mayor Zohran Mamdani [Zohran Mamdani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zohran_Mamdani) called it "a down payment on fixing a broken system." But donations alone won’t close a $1 trillion hole. We need systemic change.

## How to turn the tide

The fix starts with data. Governments and investors must track funding by gender, not just by disease. The NIH already requires inclusion of women in clinical trials, but enforcement is weak. Private investors are waking up: firms like [Female Founders Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Female_Founders_Fund) are pouring capital into women-led health startups, but they’re still a drop in the bucket.

Companies are beginning to act. Pfizer [Pfizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pfizer) now tests new drugs on both sexes and adjusts dosing accordingly. Google’s [DeepMind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DeepMind) is using AI to predict ovarian cancer earlier by analyzing patterns in female patients’ scans. These are small steps, but they prove the model works.

## The cost of doing nothing

Ignoring women’s health isn’t just a moral failing—it’s economic suicide. The World Bank [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank) estimates that closing the gender gap in health could add $28 trillion to global GDP by 2040. That’s more than the annual output of the U.S. and China combined. And it’s not just about money. Women live longer but spend more years in poor health. That drags down entire economies.

The World Economic Forum’s report isn’t just a warning—it’s a roadmap. It calls for doubling R&D spending on women’s health within a decade. It urges governments to treat women’s health as infrastructure, not a luxury. And it demands investors stop treating women’s health as a niche.

The message is clear: fix women’s health, or the world pays the price. Every missed diagnosis, every preventable death, every wasted dollar is a cost we can’t afford.
