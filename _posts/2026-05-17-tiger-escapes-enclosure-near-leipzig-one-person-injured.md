A tiger that escaped from a private enclosure in Schkeuditz, a town just west of Leipzig, seriously injured a 73-year-old man before police shot the animal dead in a nearby residential garden on Saturday afternoon. The injured man, who cared for the tiger, was taken to a hospital with serious wounds, according to later reports. Initial statements from police had described his injuries as slight, but Bild newspaper and other outlets later reported he was hospitalized in stable condition after receiving treatment for serious wounds, including deep cuts and possible internal injuries. The man was airlifted to a larger hospital for further care, local emergency services confirmed. The incident unfolded just after 3 PM local time, with police responding to multiple emergency calls about a loose tiger in the area of Lützner Straße, a street lined with single-family homes and small gardens. Witnesses described the tiger walking calmly through the neighborhood before turning into a private garden where the injured man worked. Police arrived within minutes and found the animal standing near the man, who was bleeding heavily. Officers attempted to tranquilize the tiger but, when it charged, they fatally shot the animal to protect themselves and the public. The tiger had been kept in a private facility on an industrial estate owned by Carmen Zander, a well-known big-cat trainer often called the 'Tiger Queen' in German media. Zander has faced repeated legal troubles over the years due to concerns about the welfare and safety standards of her animal enclosures. In 2020, German authorities temporarily seized several of her big cats after an inspection found violations of animal welfare laws. Zander has defended her operations, arguing that her animals are well cared for and that she follows all legal requirements. Despite this, critics have long accused her of prioritizing spectacle over safety, pointing to past incidents involving escaped animals and public endangerment. The Schkeuditz facility is not a zoo but a private collection where Zander performs with her big cats, often at private events and in media appearances. Neighbors reported hearing loud roars shortly before the escape was reported, though the exact cause remains unclear. Investigators are now reviewing security footage from the facility and interviewing Zander and staff to determine how the tiger managed to get out. Preliminary reports suggest a possible breach in the enclosure’s fence or gate, though no official cause has been confirmed. Animal welfare groups have already called for stricter regulations on private big-cat ownership in Germany, citing this incident as further evidence that such animals should not be kept in private hands. The case is likely to reignite debates about Germany’s animal welfare laws, which vary by state and often leave loopholes for private collections. For now, emergency services and local authorities are focusing on supporting the injured man and reviewing safety protocols at similar facilities across the country. The incident also raises questions about the future of Zander’s operations, as authorities may now impose stricter oversight or even revoke her permits. In the coming days, investigators will also examine whether any criminal negligence played a role in the escape, including potential violations of animal welfare or public safety regulations.
