Israeli warplanes launched airstrikes in southern Lebanon on Friday, killing at least six people, including three paramedics working at a health center, hours after Israel and Lebanon agreed to extend a 48-hour ceasefire. The strikes targeted a densely populated area, according to Lebanese officials, who condemned the attack as a violation of the truce. Israel’s military did not immediately comment on the Lebanese strikes but confirmed a separate operation in Gaza that killed Izz al-Din al-Haddad, the military chief of Hamas. The [Israel Defense Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces) stated al-Haddad was responsible for orchestrating attacks against Israeli civilians and soldiers.

## Escalation follows fragile ceasefire extension
The airstrikes in Lebanon came as diplomats from both countries finalized a deal to extend a temporary halt to hostilities, which had been set to expire at midnight local time. Lebanese Prime Minister Najib Mikati described the Israeli strikes as a "blatant aggression" and summoned the U.S. and French ambassadors to demand international condemnation. The ceasefire, brokered by Qatar and Egypt, had halted cross-border fire for two days, offering a brief respite to civilians on both sides of the border.

Israeli officials did not link the Lebanese strikes to the Gaza operation but framed al-Haddad’s death as a significant blow to Hamas. Al-Haddad, a senior commander in Hamas’s military wing, had been a key figure in planning rocket attacks and infiltration attempts into Israel. The [Hamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamas) militant group has not yet confirmed his death but typically acknowledges losses within days of major strikes.

## Health center struck amid ongoing tensions
The health center in southern Lebanon, where three paramedics were killed, serves a community already strained by months of sporadic violence. Local health workers described the facility as overwhelmed, with limited supplies and personnel. The [United Nations Interim Force in Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Interim_Force_in_Lebanon) (UNIFIL) urged restraint and called for an investigation into the attack, emphasizing the need to protect civilian infrastructure.

In Gaza, the Israeli strike that killed al-Haddad targeted a residential building in [Khan Younis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khan_Younis), according to Palestinian officials. Residents reported a massive explosion followed by Israeli drone surveillance overhead. Hamas has vowed retaliation, but its ability to escalate has been constrained by Israel’s ongoing ground operations and air campaign, which have devastated much of the territory.

## Broader implications for regional stability
The dual operations in Lebanon and Gaza underscore the fragile state of ceasefires in the region, where sporadic violence often erupts despite diplomatic efforts. Analysts warn that the killing of a high-ranking Hamas official could provoke further attacks, potentially derailing fragile truces. The Lebanese government, already grappling with economic collapse and political instability, faces pressure to respond to the strikes without triggering a wider conflict.

The U.S. State Department reiterated its support for de-escalation but did not comment on the legitimacy of the Israeli operations. Meanwhile, Lebanese political factions, including [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), have called for unified resistance against Israel. The group, which has fought multiple wars with Israel, has so far limited its involvement to supporting Hamas and other factions without direct confrontation.

What comes next remains uncertain. The extended ceasefire in Lebanon could collapse if either side perceives the other as violating its terms. In Gaza, Hamas’s response to al-Haddad’s death may determine whether Israel intensifies its military campaign or seeks a temporary pause to negotiate hostage releases or aid deliveries.
