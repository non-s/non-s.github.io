> 📰 **Continuing coverage:** [Personal decision for Starmer on whether to fight leadership contest, minister says](/politics/2026/05/17/personal-decision-for-starmer-on-whether-to-fight-leadership-contest-minister-sa/)

David Lammy, one of Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s closest allies, shut down suggestions that Starmer is preparing to set a deadline for his own exit from Downing Street. Speaking to reporters outside the House of Commons, Lammy made it clear that no timetable is in the works, even as Labour MPs have been openly debating the party’s future direction. The deputy prime minister described the constant leadership chatter as a ‘spectacular own goal’—a self-inflicted blow that’s distracting the party from governing. ‘We’ve got to move on,’ Lammy said. ‘The prime minister is focused on delivering for working people, and that’s what we should all be doing too.’ His comments follow weeks of swirling rumors about Starmer’s grip on power after Labour’s weaker-than-expected performance in recent local elections and polls showing the Conservatives gaining ground. Some Labour MPs have floated the idea that Starmer might step aside if Andy Burnham [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Burnham] wins the upcoming Makerfield byelection on July 25 and no other challenger emerges. But Lammy dismissed those theories outright. ‘There’s no timetable. Full stop,’ he said. ‘Keir Starmer is our leader, and he’s getting on with the job.’ Burnham, the mayor of Greater Manchester, has been floated as a potential candidate if Starmer were to step down, though he hasn’t publicly commented on the speculation. Lammy called Burnham a ‘great addition to parliament’ but insisted his role isn’t part of any succession plan. ‘Andy Burnham is a first-rate politician who’s done a brilliant job for Greater Manchester,’ Lammy said. ‘But this isn’t about who’s next. It’s about what this government delivers now.’ The Labour leadership row comes as Starmer faces pressure to show quick results on economic growth, NHS waiting lists, and the cost of living. His government has struggled to shake off the ‘stagnant’ label critics attach to its first year in office, with inflation still above target and business confidence wavering. Meanwhile, the Conservatives, led by James Cleverly [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Cleverly], have been hammering Labour over its record, accusing Starmer of failing to deliver the ‘change’ voters were promised. ‘Labour’s lost its way,’ Cleverly told reporters this week. ‘They’re too busy fighting internal battles to focus on the real issues people care about.’ The Makerfield byelection, triggered by the death of Labour MP Lisa Nandy [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Nandy], is being watched closely as a bellwether for wider party sentiment. A poor showing by Labour could deepen the sense that Starmer’s leadership is under threat. But Lammy’s blunt refusal to entertain the idea of a timetable suggests Starmer isn’t ready to blink. ‘The prime minister isn’t going anywhere,’ Lammy said. ‘He’s here to stay and get the job done.’
