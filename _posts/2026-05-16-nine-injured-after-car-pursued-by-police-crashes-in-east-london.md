A high-speed police chase in east London ended in a violent crash just after 3:30 a.m. Monday, leaving nine people injured and forcing officers to close a stretch of Cranbrook Road in Ilford. The Metropolitan Police [Met Police](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Police) confirmed they pursued a vehicle they believed had been stolen before it collided with another car near the junction with North Circular Road. All nine injured were taken to nearby hospitals, though authorities say none of their injuries appear life-threatening. The driver of the stolen car, a man in his 20s, was arrested at the scene and is being questioned under suspicion of theft and dangerous driving. The Met’s roads and armed policing command is leading the investigation, with forensic teams at the scene gathering evidence. Local residents woke to the sound of sirens and police helicopters circling overhead, with one witness describing the crash as sounding like "an explosion." Police cordoned off the area for several hours as emergency services worked to clear debris and secure the scene. The road remains closed in both directions between Cranbrook Road and the North Circular, causing delays for morning commuters. Authorities haven’t yet released details about the stolen car’s make or model, or how it became involved in the pursuit. What’s clear is that this chase wasn’t the result of a routine traffic stop gone wrong—it started with officers recognizing the vehicle as stolen, triggering protocol for an immediate pursuit under Met guidelines. These pursuits are always high-risk operations, especially in densely populated areas like Ilford, where narrow streets and heavy traffic increase the danger to bystanders and officers alike. The Met hasn’t said whether the pursuing officers activated their sirens and lights before the crash, a standard procedure that could factor into the investigation. The nine injured include a mix of car occupants and pedestrians, though police haven’t specified their ages or conditions. Hospitals in the area—including Whipps Cross and Newham—have confirmed receiving patients, but no further details have been released. The Met’s traffic and public order unit is reviewing bodycam footage from the pursuit, which could reveal critical moments leading up to the crash. This incident comes just weeks after another high-profile chase in London ended with a suspect’s death after a collision in Croydon. While pursuits like these are statistically rare—accounting for less than 0.5% of all Met traffic stops—they often dominate headlines due to their dramatic endings. The Met has faced criticism in the past over its pursuit policies, with campaigners arguing that high-speed chases put innocent lives at greater risk than the potential benefits of catching thieves. In response, the force has tightened its guidelines in recent years, requiring supervisors to approve pursuits unless there’s an immediate threat to life. Still, incidents like this one show how quickly situations can spiral out of control. For now, Ilford’s roads remain a mess of broken glass and twisted metal, while investigators piece together what happened in those final, chaotic moments. The stolen car’s owner, still unaware their vehicle has been involved in a major incident, will likely face shock when they learn of the crash. The driver arrested at the scene is expected to appear in court later this week, though charges haven’t been formally announced. Until then, the Met is urging anyone who witnessed the chase or crash to come forward with information.
