> 📰 **Continuing coverage:** [Anderson Cooper ends 20-year run on 60 Minutes with final salute](/entertainment/2026/05/18/anderson-cooper-signs-off-60-minutes-after-20-years-hailing-the-programs-indepen/)

Anderson Cooper signed off from *60 Minutes* for the last time on Sunday, bringing a 20-year chapter to a close on one of TV’s most storied news programs. The veteran journalist, known for his sharp interviews and fearless reporting, marked the occasion with a reflective farewell that underscored the show’s unique role in journalism. "60 Minutes has always been a place, at least for me, that you get to step into somebody else’s shoes," Cooper said during his final segment, a line that summed up his time at the iconic program. His last broadcast featured a profile of a longtime CBS News correspondent, a nod to the show’s tradition of storytelling through personal narratives.

## What Cooper’s exit means for *60 Minutes*
Cooper’s departure leaves a noticeable gap at *60 Minutes*, where he built a reputation as one of the show’s most versatile reporters. From covering natural disasters to political scandals, his work spanned the globe and earned him three Emmy Awards. The show, which premiered in 1968, has seen high-profile exits in recent years, but Cooper’s exit is particularly significant given his long tenure and cultural influence. CBS hasn’t announced a direct replacement, but the network has emphasized that *60 Minutes* will continue with its usual rotation of correspondents, including [Lesley Stahl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesley_Stahl), [Scott Pelley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Pelley), and [Bill Whitaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Whitaker).

Cooper himself isn’t leaving CBS entirely. He remains a fixture at CNN, where he hosts *Anderson Cooper 360°* and contributes as a correspondent. His departure from *60 Minutes* reflects a broader shift in how major networks balance their star talent across platforms. It also raises questions about the future of the show, which has faced declining ratings in recent years as audiences migrate to digital platforms. While *60 Minutes* still draws millions of viewers weekly, its dominance isn’t what it once was during its heyday in the 1970s and 80s.

## Why *60 Minutes* still matters
Despite the changes, *60 Minutes* remains a benchmark for investigative journalism on television. The show’s signature format—long-form interviews, hidden-camera exposés, and in-depth investigative reports—has influenced countless programs. Cooper’s exit is a reminder of the show’s enduring legacy, even as it adapts to a shifting media landscape. His replacement won’t be announced soon, but CBS has a deep bench of talent ready to step in. For now, the focus is on keeping the show’s signature style alive while attracting younger viewers.

Cooper’s legacy at *60 Minutes* is undeniable. He joined the show in 2004 as a contributing correspondent and quickly became a standout reporter, known for his ability to connect with subjects and audiences alike. His segments ranged from covering the aftermath of Hurricane Katrina to investigating the financial crisis of 2008. He also became a familiar face to millions of Americans during CNN’s coverage of major events, including the 2016 presidential election and the COVID-19 pandemic. His departure leaves a void, but it also opens the door for new voices to rise within the program.

The show’s future will likely hinge on how well it can evolve without Cooper’s star power. CBS has already made strides in revamping the show’s digital presence, including shorter clips for social media and a stronger focus on streaming. But the core of *60 Minutes* remains its investigative reports, which still draw millions of viewers. Whether the show can maintain its relevance in an era of instant news and social media will depend on how well it balances tradition with innovation.

For Cooper, the goodbye to *60 Minutes* isn’t the end of his broadcasting career. He’ll continue to report for CNN and may even explore new projects outside the network. His exit from *60 Minutes* is a moment to reflect on his incredible run, but it’s also a chance to look ahead. The show will press on, but it won’t be the same without him.
