Thousands of Bulgarians lined the streets of Sofia on Friday, waving flags and holding signs as [Dara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dara) stepped off the plane, still clutching the iconic Eurovision trophy. The 22-year-old singer’s win in Vienna marked Bulgaria’s first-ever victory in the 60-year history of the contest, beating 24 other countries with her powerful ballad 'Fire.' Fans chanted her name and sang along to her hit as she made her way through the crowd, visibly moved by the outpouring of support. Many held homemade signs reading 'Bulgaria’s Queen' and 'Dara, we love you.' Security had to hold back the crowd as she boarded a bus to central Sofia, where a planned celebration concert was already underway at the city’s main square. The event organizers had set up giant screens broadcasting her victory performance on loop, drawing even more people into the streets. Local shops quickly sold out of shirts and hats bearing Dara’s face, a sign of how fast her newfound fame had spread across the country. Traffic in the capital came to a near standstill as fans blocked roads to catch a glimpse of her passing motorcade. Police estimated over 15,000 people gathered near the airport and along her route into the city, a turnout that dwarfed even the biggest political rallies in recent years. Social media lit up with clips of her emotional speech on stage in Vienna, where she dedicated the win to 'every Bulgarian who never gave up on their dreams.' The video of her singing 'Fire' from the winner’s podium has already racked up millions of views online, with fans in neighboring countries like Romania and Serbia also joining the celebration. ## Bulgarian media calls it a historic night Bulgaria’s national broadcaster BNT, which aired the contest live, interrupted regular programming Friday evening to broadcast Dara’s arrival. State news agency BTA called it 'a night that will be remembered for generations,' while private channels ran nonstop coverage of the festivities. Bulgaria’s president and prime minister both issued statements praising Dara’s achievement, with the prime minister calling her win 'proof that dreams can come true, no matter where you come from.' Even opposition politicians set aside their usual bickering to congratulate her, a rare moment of national unity. The win also sparked a wave of nostalgia, as Bulgarians recalled the country’s struggles to qualify for Eurovision finals over the years. Dara’s path to victory wasn’t easy: she first tried in 2016 and failed to make it past the semi-finals. Her persistence paid off this year, with the public voting her into the top three and the jury placing her in the top spot. ## What happens next for Dara and Bulgaria? Dara’s record label confirmed she’ll release a new single within weeks, likely a reworked version of 'Fire' with a full orchestra. Bulgarian tourism officials are already talking about leveraging her win to boost summer travel to the country, which has seen a steady rise in visitors since 2022. The prime minister even joked in a TV interview that he’d offer her a job as a cultural ambassador, though Dara laughed it off, saying she just wanted to 'sing and make people happy.' The Eurovision win comes with a cash prize of €30,000, which Dara said she plans to split between her family and a charity supporting young musicians in Bulgaria. Her band members, who’ve been with her since her first audition, were front and center at the celebrations, with one telling reporters, 'We knew she had it in her. This is just the beginning.' ## Global reaction sparks memes and tributes Fans in Poland, where Dara lived briefly as a child, posted old photos of her growing up in Warsaw. In Ukraine, where she performed last year, social media users shared clips of her emotional thank-you speech in Kyiv. Even in countries where Bulgarian isn’t widely understood, her victory became a trending topic, with international media calling it 'the feel-good story of the year.' Late-night talk shows in the U.S. and Europe have already booked her for upcoming segments, eager to hear her story firsthand. For Bulgaria, the win couldn’t have come at a better time. The country’s economy has been shaky since the pandemic, and tourism figures were down 15% last year. But the viral moment of Dara’s win—her tearful speech, the crowd’s roar, the flag-waving—has already lifted spirits nationwide. One local shop owner told a reporter, 'We needed this. It’s like the whole country exhaled at once.'
