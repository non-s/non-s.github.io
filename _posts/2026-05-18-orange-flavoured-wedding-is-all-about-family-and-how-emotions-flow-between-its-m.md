French director Christophe Honoré brought his latest film, 'Orange-Flavoured Wedding,' to the Croisette for its world premiere as part of Cannes 2024’s official selection. The movie, which stars Adèle Exarchopoulos, Paul Kircher and Malou Khebizi, is set in Nantes during the summer of 1978. The film follows a family navigating love, loss and togetherness over a single weekend. Honoré, known for his emotionally raw storytelling, described the project as a study of how feelings move between relatives. The premiere marked his return to Cannes after his 2019 film 'Sorry Angel' premiered in the Un Certain Regard section. This time, he’s stepping into the Cannes Premiere program, a newer strand of the festival designed for mid-sized arthouse films aiming for wider attention. The cast’s performances drew immediate praise from critics in attendance, with many highlighting Exarchopoulos’ ability to balance vulnerability and strength in her role as the family’s anchor. Kircher, a rising star in French cinema, plays a younger cousin whose arrival disrupts the household’s fragile balance. Khebizi rounds out the core trio as the family’s matriarch, whose quiet authority holds everything together. The film’s setting, 1978 Nantes, isn’t just a backdrop—it’s a character itself. The city’s working-class neighborhoods, still recovering from post-war struggles, mirror the family’s own tensions. Honoré shot the film in a mix of natural locations and controlled sets, aiming for a look that feels both nostalgic and lived-in. The director has spoken openly about the film’s autobiographical elements, though he insists the story isn’t a direct recounting of his own life. Instead, it’s a fictionalized exploration of the bonds that tie families together, for better or worse. ## Honoré’s Cannes return and the film’s festival strategy Cannes isn’t new to Honoré, but 'Orange-Flavoured Wedding' arrives at a moment when his work feels especially relevant. After years of focusing on intimate, character-driven stories, he’s turned his lens toward a family saga that’s both personal and universal. The Cannes Premiere program, where his film is screening, is still in its early years but has quickly become a go-to spot for films that straddle arthouse and commercial appeal. Honoré’s decision to premiere here signals confidence in the film’s crossover potential. The director has a history of working with some of France’s most talented actors, and this project is no exception. Exarchopoulos, best known for her Oscar-winning role in 'Blue Is the Warmest Color,' brings a depth to the film’s emotional core. Kircher, who gained attention for his work in 'Summer of 85,' plays a pivotal role in pushing the plot forward. Khebizi, a stage actress making her film debut, anchors the ensemble with a quiet intensity that recalls classic French cinema. ## Why 1978 Nantes matters in the film The year isn’t just a setting—it’s a mood. France in 1978 was a country in transition, grappling with economic uncertainty and shifting social norms. Nantes, a port city with a strong industrial past, was no exception. Honoré uses the era’s details to ground the story: the fashion, the music, even the way people talk. The film’s title, 'Orange-Flavoured Wedding,' hints at the sweetness and bitterness of family life. Honoré has said the orange flavor symbolizes the way emotions can be both vibrant and artificial, masking deeper truths. The family’s gathering, ostensibly to celebrate a wedding, becomes a place where old wounds resurface and unspoken feelings finally surface. ## The cast’s chemistry and the film’s reception Early buzz from the premiere suggests the cast’s chemistry is one of the film’s strongest assets. Critics noted how Exarchopoulos and Kircher play off each other, with their scenes crackling with tension and tenderness. Khebizi’s performance has drawn particular attention for its restraint, a quality that’s rare in performances of this scale. The film’s cinematography, handled by [Romain Winding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romain_Winding), is also getting praise for its naturalistic lighting and composition. The result is a film that feels intimate without being claustrophobic, expansive without losing its focus on the family. Honoré’s direction keeps the story moving at a brisk pace, even as the emotional stakes rise. The film’s runtime is under two hours, but it packs in enough material for a miniseries. That’s partly due to the script’s sharp dialogue and the actors’ ability to convey volumes in a glance or a sigh. ## What’s next for 'Orange-Flavoured Wedding' and Honoré After Cannes, 'Orange-Flavoured Wedding' will head to theaters in France this fall, with international distribution deals likely to follow. Honoré has already started teasing his next project, though he’s staying tight-lipped about details. For now, the focus is on this film and the conversations it’s sparking among critics and audiences alike. The premiere’s reception suggests it could become one of the festival’s sleeper hits—an arthouse film that finds a wider audience without losing its edge. Honoré’s ability to balance personal stories with universal themes has always been his strength, and this film feels like a return to form.
