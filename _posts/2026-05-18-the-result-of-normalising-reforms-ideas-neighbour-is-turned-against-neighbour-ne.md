A Reform UK councillor in [Colchester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colchester), Essex, is facing backlash after allegedly posting on social media that he couldn’t ‘believe the amount of Nigerians in town … should melt them all down and fill in the pot holes.’ The comment, uncovered by local media, surfaced just weeks after Reform UK won 100 seats in England’s May local elections, marking the first time the party has a meaningful presence in local government. The unnamed councillor, who has not responded to requests for comment, is not the only Reform-affiliated politician facing scrutiny for inflammatory rhetoric. In [Boston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston,_Lincolnshire), Lincolnshire, another Reform councillor shared a post calling for the deportation of ‘non-British’ residents, including those who’ve lived in the UK for decades. The posts reflect a growing pattern: where Reform’s anti-immigration messaging once stayed in Westminster, it’s now shaping how local councils treat ethnic minorities and immigrants in everyday life.\n\nThe party’s rise in local politics has put a spotlight on its leadership. [Richard Tice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Tice), Reform UK’s deputy leader, dismissed reports of racist remarks as ‘smearing and sneering’ from opponents. But the comments aren’t isolated. In [Birmingham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birmingham), a Reform councillor for [Sutton Coldfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sutton_Coldfield) has been recorded repeatedly using the term ‘Pakis’ in public meetings—a slur that’s sparked formal complaints. These incidents raise a troubling question: as Reform’s influence grows, how will its rhetoric change the way ethnic minorities are treated in the communities they call home?\n\n\n## From ‘concern’ to policing existence
Local campaigners say the shift is already happening. In towns where Reform now holds sway, residents report increased suspicion toward ethnic minorities, from routine stops by police to landlords refusing to rent to non-white tenants. In [Rochdale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rochdale), Greater Manchester—a town where Reform won two seats—community leaders say they’ve seen a spike in hate incidents since the election. ‘It’s not just about immigration anymore,’ said [Zara Mohammed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zara_Mohammed), secretary general of the [Muslim Council of Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_Council_of_Britain). ‘It’s about policing how we exist. People are being told they don’t belong, not just in the country, but in their own streets.’\n\nThe rhetoric isn’t just coming from elected officials. In [Mansfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mansfield), Nottinghamshire, a Reform councillor’s Facebook group has become a hub for posts claiming that ‘ethnic enclaves’ are ‘taking over’ the town. Members share videos of Black and Asian families in public spaces, often with captions like ‘This isn’t England anymore.’ These posts aren’t just online noise—they’re shaping real-world behavior. Landlords in Mansfield have reportedly told prospective tenants of Middle Eastern or African background that their properties ‘aren’t available,’ while local businesses owned by ethnic minorities say they’re facing increased scrutiny from council inspectors.\n\n## The domino effect on local policies
Reform’s presence in local government isn’t just symbolic—it’s already influencing policy. In [Barrow-in-Furness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrow-in-Furness), Cumbria, a Reform councillor has pushed for a review of ‘non-English speaking’ residents receiving council services, arguing that taxpayer money shouldn’t fund ‘translation bloat.’ The proposal was voted down, but not before sparking outrage among local immigrant communities. In [Southend-on-Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southend-on-Sea), Essex, a Reform councillor has called for a ‘British values’ test for all council employees, a move critics say echoes far-right talking points.\n\nThese aren’t fringe demands. They reflect a broader strategy by Reform UK to use local government as a testing ground for policies that mainstream parties would never touch. ‘They’re normalizing ideas that were once taboo,’ said [Jenny Rowena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenny_Rowena), a researcher at the [Institute of Race Relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Race_Relations). ‘It starts with language, then it moves to policy, and before you know it, whole communities are treated like they don’t belong.’\n\n## What happens next?
For now, the backlash is growing. In Colchester, local activists have organized vigils outside the town hall, demanding the Reform councillor resign. In Boston, the Labour Party has called for an independent inquiry into Reform’s conduct in local government. But the party shows no signs of backing down. Richard Tice has doubled down on his party’s anti-immigration stance, arguing that ‘voters are tired of political correctness’ and that Reform is ‘giving voice to concerns that others ignore.’\n\nThe real test will come in the next general election. If Reform’s local gains translate into Westminster seats, the party could push for national policies that further marginalize ethnic minorities. For now, though, the damage is already being done. In towns across England, neighbors are turning on neighbors—not because of what they’ve done, but because of who they are. And that’s a shift that won’t be easy to reverse.
