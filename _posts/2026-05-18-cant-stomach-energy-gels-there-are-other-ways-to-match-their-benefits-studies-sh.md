A new study in the *British Journal of Sports Medicine* found runners and cyclists don’t have to choke down sticky energy gels to keep their energy up during long races. Researchers compared three fueling methods: traditional energy gels, real food like bananas or dates, and sports drinks. The result? No difference in performance for those who skipped the gels, but far fewer stomach problems. ‘The gels work, but they’re not the only way,’ said [Dr. Louise Burke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louise_Burke_(nutritionist)), lead author and a sports nutrition expert at [Australian Catholic University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_Catholic_University). ‘Some athletes struggle with digestion or taste. This study shows they’ve got real options.’

The study tracked 24 trained runners over a 10-week period. Each runner did three long runs—one with gels, one with real food, and one with sports drinks. The food group ate bananas, dates, or dried fruit during runs. The drink group sipped sports drinks with carbs. The gels provided 60 grams of carbs per hour. The food and drink groups matched that intake. After each run, runners rated their effort, fatigue, and any stomach issues. Performance times were nearly identical across all three groups. But here’s the catch: 60% of runners reported stomach discomfort with gels, compared to 20% with real food and 30% with sports drinks.

The problem with gels isn’t just taste. They’re packed with fast-digesting sugars and can spike blood sugar, leading to crashes or nausea. ‘Gels hit the system too hard for some people,’ said [Dr. Asker Jeukendrup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asker_Jeukendrup), a sports nutrition researcher who wasn’t involved in the study. ‘Sports drinks and real food spread carbs out more evenly. That’s easier on the stomach.’ Jeukendrup has worked with elite athletes for decades and often recommends adjusting fueling based on individual tolerance. ‘It’s not about forcing one method. It’s about finding what works for your body.’

## Why real food might be the better bet
Real food isn’t just easier on the stomach—it’s often cheaper and more accessible. Bananas, dates, and even white bread with jam are packed with carbs and natural sugars. They also provide fiber, vitamins, and minerals that gels lack. ‘Athletes often overlook real food because it’s messy or inconvenient,’ said Burke. ‘But during a long race, a few bites of banana can do the same job as a gel—without the stomachache.’ The study didn’t test every food option, but Burke noted that oatmeal, rice cakes, or even a peanut butter sandwich could work for slower-paced athletes.

Sports drinks are another solid alternative, especially for those who don’t want to chew mid-run. Most contain a mix of carbs and electrolytes, which help with hydration and energy. The key is choosing a drink with at least 6% carbs. Anything lower won’t fuel you properly. ‘Many runners grab water during races and wonder why they hit the wall,’ said Jeukendrup. ‘You need carbs in your system. Sports drinks or gels just make it easier to get them in.’

## What athletes should try next time
If you’re tired of gels but still need fuel, start small. Test real food or sports drinks on shorter training runs first. See how your stomach reacts. Then, try it in a longer run or race. ‘The biggest mistake is changing fueling strategies on race day,’ said Burke. ‘Your gut needs time to adapt.’ She also recommends practicing with whatever you plan to use. ‘Don’t eat a banana for the first time at mile 15 of a marathon.’ For those who love the convenience of gels but hate the side effects, Burke suggests cutting them with water or trying lower-sugar versions. ‘Some gels now have 30 grams of carbs instead of 60. That might be easier to handle.’

The study’s findings don’t mean gels are useless. For some athletes, they’re the easiest way to get carbs in fast. But for others, they’re a recipe for disaster. The takeaway? Don’t force something that doesn’t work. Your body’s feedback is the best guide. Whether it’s a sports drink, a handful of dates, or a gel you’ve diluted, the goal is the same: keep energy up without wrecking your stomach. And if you’re planning a big race, start experimenting now—not the day before.
