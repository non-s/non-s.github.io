US intelligence agencies now say Cuba has amassed more than 300 military drones, a buildup that’s rattling Washington. The warning follows a rare trip to Havana by CIA director [William Burns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Burns), who pressed Cuban officials to avoid any actions that could escalate tensions. Burns didn’t hold back, telling Cuba’s government it needed to end its ‘totalitarian rule’—a blunt message from a top US spy chief. The drones aren’t just sitting in warehouses. Analysts fear they could be used to surveil or even strike US targets, including the naval base at Guantanamo Bay and nearby Key West, Florida.

## Why the US is worried about Cuba’s drones

The concern isn’t just about the drones themselves. It’s about who’s helping Cuba get them. US officials say Iran has been quietly supplying military hardware to Cuba, including drones and other tech that could be turned into weapons. Iran has long backed groups like Hezbollah and Houthi rebels, and its ties to Cuba add a new layer to its regional influence. The US sees this as a direct threat—especially since Cuba sits just 90 miles off Florida’s coast. A single drone could fly from Havana to Key West in under an hour.

Cuba isn’t backing down. The government insists its drone program is purely defensive, calling it a response to US aggression. Cuban officials point to the decades-long US embargo and military presence at Guantanamo as justification for beefing up their defenses. ‘We have every right to defend ourselves,’ said a spokesperson for Cuba’s foreign ministry. ‘This is not about preparing for war—it’s about preventing one.’ Still, the US isn’t buying it. Satellite images show Cuba’s drone fleet growing fast, with models that can carry explosives or conduct surveillance.

## Russia’s role in Cuba’s military expansion

Iran isn’t alone in helping Cuba modernize its military. Russia has also deepened ties, supplying everything from advanced radar systems to fighter jet upgrades. In May 2024, Russian warships made a rare stop in Havana, a move that didn’t go unnoticed in Washington. The US has accused Russia of using Cuba as a staging ground for intelligence gathering, a claim both Moscow and Havana deny. But the timing is suspicious. Just weeks after the Russian ships docked, Cuba announced a new batch of drones ready for deployment.

The US response hasn’t been subtle. The Pentagon has stepped up surveillance flights near Cuba and is reviewing its own drone defenses in Florida. Military planners are also running war games to simulate how to counter a drone strike from Cuba. ‘We can’t ignore the threat,’ said a senior US official who spoke on condition of anonymity. ‘Cuba’s drones, paired with Iran’s supply chain, change the calculus.’ The worry isn’t just about a single attack—it’s about Cuba becoming a hub for drone technology that could spread to other US adversaries in the region.

## What happens next

For now, the US is trying to keep the pressure on diplomatically. Burns’s trip to Havana was part of a broader effort to isolate Cuba further if it doesn’t back off its military buildup. But Cuba’s government has shown little sign of yielding. ‘The drones stay,’ said a Cuban military source. ‘So does our right to defend ourselves.’ The standoff leaves Washington with few good options. Sanctions aren’t likely to change Cuba’s stance, and direct military action would escalate tensions into a crisis neither side wants.

The bigger question is what this means for the region. If Cuba becomes a drone exporter, it could destabilize neighbors like Jamaica and the Bahamas, which have no defenses against such attacks. The US might try to intercept shipments heading to Cuba, but that risks open conflict. For now, the drones sit on Cuban tarmacs, silent but loaded with potential. One misstep—and the tension could turn into something far worse.
