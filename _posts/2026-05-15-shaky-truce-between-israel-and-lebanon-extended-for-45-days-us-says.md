Israel and Lebanon have agreed to a 45-day extension of their ceasefire after two days of talks in Washington, the US State Department announced Monday. The extension follows renewed diplomatic efforts aimed at stabilizing the fragile truce between the two nations, which has faced repeated disruptions in recent months.

State Department spokesperson Tommy Pigott called the negotiations "productive" and said further discussions would be held from June 2 to June 3. The announcement came as tensions flared on Monday with Israel launching airstrikes on the southern Lebanese city of Tyre. The strikes targeted [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah) positions, according to Lebanese security sources, and followed days of sporadic cross-border clashes.

The extension marks the latest attempt to prevent a full-scale conflict between Israel and Lebanon, where a fragile truce has held since October following months of intense fighting. The truce was initially brokered by the US and France, with support from regional partners, but sporadic violence has persisted along the border.

## Ceasefire faces persistent challenges

Analysts warn that the truce remains fragile, with both sides accusing each other of violations. The Israeli strikes on Tyre on Monday underscored the volatility of the situation, even as talks continued in Washington. Lebanese officials condemned the airstrikes, calling them a violation of the truce, while Israel stated it was responding to threats from [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah).

The upcoming negotiations in early June are expected to focus on further de-escalation measures, including border monitoring and humanitarian aid access. The US has reiterated its commitment to brokering a lasting solution, but the path forward remains uncertain. Previous ceasefires have collapsed within weeks, leaving both sides on high alert.

The situation is closely watched by regional powers, including [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran), which supports [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), and [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia), which backs Lebanon’s government. International observers have warned that further escalation could draw in other actors, raising fears of a wider regional conflict.

## What happens next?

The 45-day extension provides a temporary reprieve, but the underlying issues between Israel and Lebanon remain unresolved. Analysts say the next round of talks will be critical in determining whether the truce can hold or if the region will slide back into violence. The US has called for restraint on both sides, but the airstrikes on Tyre suggest that military action continues to overshadow diplomacy.
