More than 550 countertop workers in California have developed silicosis, a chronic and deadly lung disease, after years of cutting natural or engineered stone without proper protection. The cases are concentrated in Southern California’s stone fabrication shops, but epidemiologists warn this isn’t just a West Coast problem. Similar clusters are appearing in Texas, Florida, and Ohio, where workers handle the same materials with little to no safety gear. The disease is irreversible, and many victims are in their 30s and 40s—too young to be this sick, too old to start over. [California Department of Public Health](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/) has identified at least 550 confirmed cases since 2019, with another 100 likely on the way once testing catches up. But experts say the real number could be much higher because many workers don’t report symptoms until it’s too late. The dust from cutting granite, quartz, and marble contains silica, a mineral that scars lung tissue permanently. Even short-term exposure over months can trigger the disease, which slowly suffocates victims as their lungs harden and stop working. Doctors are seeing younger patients—some in their 20s—who’ve spent just a few years in the trade before their lungs give out. One worker in his early 40s, who asked not to be named, told NPR he started noticing shortness of breath three years ago. By the time he saw a doctor, his lungs were already 60% damaged. He’s now on oxygen and can’t work. The industry has known about the risks for decades, but enforcement of safety rules is inconsistent. OSHA’s silica standard requires employers to limit exposure to 50 micrograms per cubic meter of air over an 8-hour shift, but many shops ignore it. A 2022 investigation by [NPR](https://www.npr.org/) found that some California fabricators were still using dry cutting methods, which send clouds of dust into the air, instead of wet saws that trap the particles. Worse, many workers are undocumented immigrants who fear speaking up about unsafe conditions. They’re paid piece rates—$100 for a slab of granite cut by hand—which pushes them to work faster and skip safety gear. Advocates say the problem is getting worse because engineered quartz, a cheaper alternative to natural stone, contains even more silica. The material is in 90% of new countertops, and its popularity has exploded over the last decade. But the dust it creates is finer and more toxic, making it harder for lungs to clear. ## The workers left behind by safety rules. The first sign of a problem usually comes when workers start coughing up blood or struggle to climb stairs. By then, the damage is done. Doctors at [University of California, San Francisco](https://www.ucsf.edu/) are treating some of the sickest cases, including men in their 30s who can no longer walk across a room without stopping. One patient, a 34-year-old father of two, was diagnosed last year. His lung function is now at 20% of normal. He’s waiting on a transplant list, but even that’s a long shot. His story is one of hundreds. The workers who survive long enough to sue often face years of legal battles. Most employers either go bankrupt or settle for pennies on the dollar, leaving families with medical bills and no income. In California, a 2023 law now requires fabricators to report silica-related illnesses to the state, but compliance is spotty. And while OSHA has fined some shops, penalties are often too small to force real change. ## Why this isn’t just a California problem. States like Texas and Florida are seeing the same trend. In Houston, a cluster of 30 cases was linked to a single fabrication shop that ignored safety rules for years. In Ohio, a worker in his 40s died last month after his silicosis went untreated for too long. The CDC’s [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/) has called silica dust a "serious occupational hazard," but without stronger federal action, the problem keeps growing. The industry says it’s trying to do better. Trade groups like the [Marble Institute of America](https://www.marble-institute.com/) now offer safety training, and some fabricators have switched to wet cutting and vacuums. But experts say voluntary measures aren’t enough. They’re pushing for stricter enforcement, mandatory health screenings for workers, and bans on the most dangerous cutting methods. Until then, more men—and increasingly, women—will keep getting sick, often too late to do anything about it. The next steps aren’t complicated. OSHA needs to audit more shops, states must enforce reporting laws, and fabricators have to invest in safer tools. But money and politics are slowing down the response. Workers like the 40-year-old in California are running out of time.
