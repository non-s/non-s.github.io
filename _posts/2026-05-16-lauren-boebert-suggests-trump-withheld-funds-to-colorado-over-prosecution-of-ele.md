U.S. Rep. [Lauren Boebert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lauren_Boebert), a Republican from Colorado, accused former President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump) on Monday of withholding federal funds for a clean drinking water project in retaliation for the state’s prosecution of election denier [Tina Peters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tina_Peters_(election_administrator)). Boebert made the claim on social media, suggesting the move by Trump freed up resources for other projects despite the state’s ongoing water infrastructure needs.

Peters, a former [Mesa County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesa_County,_Colorado) clerk and recorder, was sentenced to nearly nine years in prison for allowing unauthorized access to voting records amid efforts to overturn the 2020 election results, which Trump lost to Democrat [Joe Biden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Biden). The case centered on Peters’ role in a data breach that exposed sensitive voting system information to conspiracy theorists and far-right activists.

## Colorado governor commutes Peters’ sentence

On Friday, Colorado’s Democratic governor, [Jared Polis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jared_Polis), commuted Peters’ sentence and ordered her release on June 1, reducing her prison term to time served. The decision comes amid ongoing political tensions in Colorado over election integrity and partisan disputes over voting laws. Boebert, a vocal Trump ally, framed the commutation as a step toward resolving the funding dispute.

Boebert’s allegation follows a pattern of disputes between conservative lawmakers and the Biden administration over federal funding allocations. The clean drinking water project, aimed at addressing water contamination issues in rural Colorado, had been a priority for state officials. Boebert’s office did not immediately respond to requests for additional details or evidence supporting her claim.

Trump’s administration has previously clashed with Colorado officials over election policies, including the state’s decision to send mail ballots to all active voters. Peters’ case became a focal point for Trump’s allies, who have repeatedly questioned the legitimacy of the 2020 election results despite no evidence of widespread fraud.

## Broader implications for federal-state relations

The dispute highlights tensions between state and federal governments over funding and election-related policies. Peters’ commutation may ease some of the immediate political pressure, but the underlying issues—including Boebert’s allegations—could continue to shape debates over federal funding and election integrity in Colorado. State officials have not confirmed whether any federal funds were withheld or delayed as a result of Peters’ prosecution.

For now, Peters’ release signals the end of her legal battle, but her case remains a flashpoint in national conversations about election security and political accountability. The broader implications for federal funding and state-federal relations will likely unfold in the coming months as Colorado navigates its water infrastructure challenges and political divisions.
