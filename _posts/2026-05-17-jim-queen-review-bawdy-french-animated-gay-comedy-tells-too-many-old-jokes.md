French animation has a knack for pushing boundaries, but ‘Jim Queen’ pushes a little too hard—and not in a good way. The spoof comedy, helmed by [Alexis Beaumont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Beaumont), a director known for edgy adult animations, riffs on gay clichés with the subtlety of a sledgehammer. It’s structured like a ‘South Park’ episode: short, sharp cuts, crude humor, and a premise so simple it borders on ridiculous. That’s fine if it works, but here, the jokes feel like they were written in the ‘90s and forgotten in a drawer until now.

The plot follows Jim Queen, a flamboyant gay god who lives in a heavenly disco where the walls pulse with neon light and the clouds are made of glitter. One day, he catches a mysterious illness that starts turning him straight. The twist isn’t subtle—within minutes, he’s trading rainbow flags for a wife, a minivan, and a sudden craving for golf. The film leans so hard into the punchline that it forgets to build any real stakes or surprises. By the halfway mark, the joke wears thin, and the story collapses under the weight of its own predictability.

If you’ve seen any gay-themed comedy from the last 30 years, you’ve seen this movie’s DNA. The humor relies on over-the-top stereotypes: the screaming queen with a lisp, the butch lesbian who hates heels, the gay best friend who’s one joke away from being a caricature. There’s no nuance, no fresh angles—just recycled material repackaged for a 2024 audience that deserves better. The animation itself is slick, with a ‘South Park’-inspired style that’s colorful and fast-paced, but it can’t save the script from its own laziness.

Even the film’s attempt at social commentary falls flat. It throws in a few jabs at conversion therapy and anti-gay policies, but the context feels tacked on, like a public service announcement crammed into a raunchy comedy. There’s no real bite to it—just a wink and a nudge that says, *‘Look how edgy we are!’* without earning the right to be. The result is a movie that mistakes shock value for wit, and the audience for fools.

‘Jim Queen’ isn’t all bad. The animation is snappy, the voice acting is energetic, and the disco-heaven setting is visually fun. But those strengths can’t outweigh the film’s biggest flaw: it doesn’t have anything new to say. It’s a comedy built on borrowed energy, recycling jokes that were tired when ‘[Queer as Folk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Queer_as_Folk_(UK))’ was on TV. For fans of lowbrow humor, it might scratch an itch, but for anyone hoping for something smarter or more original, it’s a disappointment.

What happens next? The film is hitting European festivals first, likely aiming for a streaming release later this year. Whether it finds an audience will depend on how much people are willing to tolerate recycled gags for the sake of a few laughs. One thing’s for sure: if this is the state of gay comedy in 2024, the bar isn’t just low—it’s buried under a mountain of stereotypes.
