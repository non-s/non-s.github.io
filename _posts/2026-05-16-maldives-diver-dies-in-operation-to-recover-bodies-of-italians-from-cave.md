A Maldivian military diver died after suffering decompression sickness during a dangerous operation to recover the bodies of four Italian scuba divers who drowned in an underwater cave. Mohamed Mahudhee, 34, was part of a team searching for the divers in Vaavu Atoll, a group of islands in the central Maldives. Italian officials confirmed the four men had died while attempting to explore caves at a depth of 50 meters (164 feet) on January 31. The Maldivian government reported Mahudhee’s death on February 3, two days after the recovery mission began. The divers were part of a group from Italy exploring the cave system when they failed to resurface. Local authorities launched an immediate search and recovery operation, deploying military divers despite hazardous underwater conditions. According to Italy’s foreign ministry, the men were experienced scuba divers who had entered the cave without proper authorization. ## Recovery operation halted after diver’s death The recovery operation was suspended following Mahudhee’s death to assess the risks and coordinate further steps. Military officials confirmed the diver was transported to a hospital in Malé, the capital, where he died from complications related to decompression sickness. The condition, also known as the bends, occurs when divers ascend too quickly, causing nitrogen bubbles to form in the bloodstream. The Maldives National Defense Force (MNDF) issued a statement expressing condolences to Mahudhee’s family and noted the dangers of the underwater environment in Vaavu Atoll. Local marine experts described the cave as part of a complex network of underwater tunnels and caverns, known for strong currents and limited visibility. ## Italians had no authorization for cave exploration Italian authorities stated the four divers had not obtained permission to explore the cave, violating local regulations requiring permits for such activities. The Maldives Marine Research Institute (MMRI) confirmed that unauthorized cave diving poses significant risks, including entrapment and drowning. Rescue teams recovered the bodies of three divers on the first day of the operation, while the fourth was located the following day. The incident has raised concerns about safety protocols for scuba diving in the Maldives, a popular destination for tourists seeking coral reefs and marine life. The Maldives Ministry of Tourism emphasized that all cave diving activities must comply with national safety guidelines and obtain prior approval from authorities. ## Broader implications for diving safety in the Maldives The death of Mahudhee underscores the dangers faced by rescue teams in high-risk recovery missions. The Maldives, known for its pristine waters and vibrant marine ecosystems, attracts thousands of divers annually. However, incidents like this highlight the need for stricter enforcement of diving regulations and improved training for emergency responders. Local dive operators called for mandatory safety courses for all divers exploring underwater caves, even those with advanced certifications. The Maldives government has not yet announced an official investigation into the incident, but marine safety advocates urged a review of protocols to prevent future tragedies. For now, the recovery operation remains paused as authorities evaluate the next steps, including potential legal actions against those responsible for the unauthorized cave exploration.
