London’s Metropolitan Police confirmed they detained 43 people during Saturday’s marches, as far-right activists led by [Tommy Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tommy_Robinson_(activist)), co-founder of the now-dissolved [English Defence League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Defence_League), clashed with counter-protesters. The far-right march, described by Robinson as a call to prepare for the next 'battle of Britain,' drew hundreds of supporters to central London. Meanwhile, a counter-demonstration—overlapping with pro-Palestinian groups—amplified tensions, with police struggling to keep the two sides apart.

## Police deploy extra resources to manage unrest

Officers in riot gear sealed off key areas near Parliament Square and Whitehall as the two processions inched closer. Videos shared on social media showed scuffles breaking out between groups, with officers making arrests for public order offenses, assault, and possession of offensive weapons. A police spokesperson said officers used mounted units, baton rounds, and dispersal tactics to prevent violence from escalating further.

Robinson, who has a history of organizing inflammatory rallies, addressed the crowd before the march, warning that 'the next battle of Britain is coming.' His remarks echoed his past rhetoric, which has often targeted Muslim communities and immigration policies. The far-right figure has been banned from entering the U.S. and has faced multiple convictions for contempt of court and public order offenses.

## Pro-Palestinian protesters join counter-demo

The counter-protest began as a show of solidarity with Palestinians amid the Israel-Hamas war, but it quickly merged with anti-fascist and left-wing groups opposing Robinson’s rally. Organizers of the pro-Palestinian demonstration had secured a separate permit, but the overlap created confusion and heightened risks. Police confirmed they arrested several individuals for breaching protest conditions and obstructing officers.

Witnesses reported seeing smoke flares and debris tossed between groups, though no serious injuries were immediately reported. The Metropolitan Police described the day as 'challenging' due to the unpredictable nature of the crowds and the rapid merging of demonstrations. Officers from multiple boroughs were called in to reinforce local teams.

## Far-right groups target immigration and Gaza war

Robinson’s rally focused on two main grievances: opposition to immigration and criticism of Western support for Israel in the Gaza conflict. His supporters carried signs reading 'Stop the invasion' and 'Gaza is a distraction,' reflecting a growing trend among far-right movements to link domestic and foreign policy issues. Analysts say this strategy helps them attract broader support by tapping into economic anxieties and cultural fears.

The Metropolitan Police have faced criticism in the past for their handling of far-right events, particularly after the 2017 [Charlottesville rally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unite_the_Right_rally) in the U.S., where clashes left one counter-protester dead. Since then, London authorities have adopted a more proactive approach, including pre-emptive arrests and stricter protest conditions. Saturday’s operation followed that model, with police monitoring social media for threats and deploying undercover officers to gather intelligence.

## What happens next?

Robinson has vowed to continue organizing marches, calling Saturday’s event a 'success' despite the arrests. The Metropolitan Police have not ruled out further detentions as they review footage from body-worn cameras and CCTV. Meanwhile, pro-Palestinian groups are planning a larger demonstration for next weekend, raising concerns about renewed clashes. Authorities are urging organizers on all sides to submit detailed plans in advance to avoid repeat incidents.
