The MV Hondius pulled into Rotterdam’s [Waalhaven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waalhaven) Monday morning under Dutch biosecurity protocols after confirming hantavirus cases among crew and passengers. Health inspectors boarded immediately, wearing full protective gear, to begin a 48-hour deep disinfection of all interior spaces—cabins, dining halls, lounges, and ventilation systems. The ship, operated by [Hurtigruten Expeditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurtigruten), had just completed a 21-day Antarctic expedition with 150 passengers and 90 crew when symptoms first appeared in late February. Three people later died, and at least 24 others tested positive, including medical staff who treated patients onboard, according to the [Netherlands National Institute for Public Health and the Environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands_National_Institute_for_Public_Health_and_the_Environment) (RIVM).

## Where the outbreak started
The first cases emerged among crew members preparing for the voyage in Ushuaia, Argentina, in late January. By mid-February, passengers reported fevers, muscle pain, and respiratory distress after the ship left [Port Stanley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_Stanley) in the [Falkland Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falkland_Islands). Hantavirus isn’t spread person-to-person; it comes from inhaling dust contaminated by infected rodent urine or droppings. Experts suspect exposure happened during cargo handling or shore excursions where rodents were present. The ship’s medical team initially treated symptoms as severe flu, missing the diagnosis until cases worsened. By the time the outbreak was confirmed, the vessel was already en route to Antarctica, with over 200 people onboard.

Dutch authorities activated emergency protocols within hours of receiving the ship’s arrival notice. The Rotterdam port authority closed the terminal to all other vessels, rerouting supplies and redirecting emergency vehicles. The 150 passengers who remained on board overnight were confined to their cabins under medical supervision, while 60 crew members and medical staff were quarantined in a nearby hotel. RIVM confirmed all will undergo 14-day isolation, with daily temperature checks and PCR tests. The ship itself is now sealed off, with air conditioning systems shut down to prevent further spread during cleaning.

## How hantavirus spreads and what it does
Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) is rare but deadly, with a 38% fatality rate in the U.S., according to the [U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention). Most cases occur in rural areas where people encounter rodent nests. The strain aboard the MV Hondius matches the Andes virus, which can also spread through direct contact with infected rodents or their saliva. Symptoms—fever, chills, and severe respiratory failure—develop within 1 to 8 weeks of exposure. There’s no cure or vaccine; treatment focuses on oxygen support and managing organ failure. The virus isn’t contagious between humans, but the outbreak still forced global tracking because of the ship’s international passenger list.

This isn’t the first time a cruise ship has triggered a public health scare. In 2019, a [Grandeur of the Seas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grandeur_of_the_Seas) voyage ended early after norovirus sickened 200 passengers. But hantavirus is far rarer and deadlier. The MV Hondius incident is the largest documented hantavirus outbreak linked to a single vessel. Norway’s [Folkehelseinstituttet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Public_Health_(Norway)) and Argentina’s [ANLIS-Malbrán](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANLIS-Malbrán) are assisting with contact tracing after passengers dispersed to multiple countries. Some travelers have already reported mild symptoms post-disembarkation, raising concerns about delayed diagnoses.

## What happens next
The disinfection crew will use vaporized hydrogen peroxide to sterilize every surface, a process that takes about 48 hours. After clearance from RIVM, the ship will undergo another round of tests before it’s allowed to sail again. Passengers who tested negative will be released from quarantine once they clear a 14-day monitoring period. The three fatalities were all crew members under 50; two were from Chile, one from the Philippines. Their families have been notified, and repatriation arrangements are underway. The incident has already prompted Hurtigruten to review its rodent control protocols for future Antarctic voyages, including mandatory pre-voyage fumigation of cargo holds.

Global health agencies are watching closely. The [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) has flagged the case as a reminder of how easily pathogens can travel via tourism. While hantavirus isn’t new, the cruise ship setting amplifies risks. Experts warn that climate change is expanding rodent habitats in the Antarctic Peninsula, increasing the chance of future outbreaks. For now, Rotterdam’s port is a controlled zone, and the city’s health teams are on high alert. The MV Hondius won’t leave until authorities are certain it’s safe—both for the ship and the people who might board it next.
