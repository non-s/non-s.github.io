Toronto rapper [Drake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drake_(musician)) has taken aim at producer and radio personality [DJ Khaled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DJ_Khaled) in a newly released diss track from his latest album *Ice Man*. The song, titled “Your People Are Still Waitin’ for a Free Palestine,” includes lines directly challenging Khaled’s silence on the Israel-Hamas war and the humanitarian crisis in Gaza.

Drake’s criticism comes amid widespread public calls for celebrities to voice support for Palestinian civilians. While stars like [Mark Ruffalo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Ruffalo), [Seth Rogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seth_Rogen), and [Doja Cat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doja_Cat) have spoken out, Khaled has not publicly addressed the conflict. The Florida-based producer, known for hits like “All I Do Is Win” and “I’m the One,” has historically maintained a largely apolitical public image.

## Drake’s Message Echoes Broader Cultural Shift

The diss track reflects a growing trend among musicians to use their platforms for political commentary. In recent weeks, artists including [Kendrick Lamar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kendrick_Lamar) and [Vic Mensa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vic_Mensa) have released songs or statements addressing social justice issues, including the war in Gaza. Drake’s reference to “Your people” suggests an appeal to Khaled’s Arab-American community, many of whom have been vocal about Palestinian rights.

Khaled, who is of Palestinian descent, has faced criticism before for not taking a public stance on the conflict. In 2021, during the last major escalation between Israel and Hamas, he remained silent despite calls from fans and activists. His current silence continues that pattern, prompting Drake’s direct challenge.

## Album Context and Industry Reactions

*Ice Man*, released on March 14, 2025, marks Drake’s first project of the year and includes collaborations with [J. Cole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Cole) and [SZA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SZA). The album has drawn attention not only for its music but also for its timely political undertones. Tracks like “Your People Are Still Waitin’” are being analyzed for their lyrical depth and cultural messaging.

Industry observers note that Drake’s move could pressure other high-profile figures to clarify their positions. The diss is seen as part of a broader conversation about celebrity influence and social responsibility in times of global crisis. Whether Khaled responds publicly remains uncertain.

Drake has a history of using his music to comment on social issues, from racial injustice to economic inequality. This latest release signals continued engagement with geopolitical matters, especially those affecting marginalized communities.
