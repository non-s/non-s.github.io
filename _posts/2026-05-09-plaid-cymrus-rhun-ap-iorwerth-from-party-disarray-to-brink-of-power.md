Rhun ap Iorwerth, Plaid Cymru’s [leader since June 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhun_ap_Iorwerth), is on track to become Wales’s first nationalist first minister after steering the party from the brink of collapse to the verge of power. The 50-year-old former BBC Wales journalist has transformed Plaid Cymru’s electoral prospects since taking over from Adam Price, reversing years of decline with a surge in support ahead of the next Senedd election.

## From presenter to politician
Ap Iorwerth’s political rise began when he left broadcasting in 2010 to stand as Plaid Cymru’s candidate for Ynys Môn. Elected to the Senedd in 2011, he spent 13 years as an opposition member before replacing Price as leader. His transition from television presenter to party leader has been marked by a focus on pragmatic governance, contrasting with Plaid Cymru’s traditional independence-first rhetoric.

The party’s fortunes have shifted dramatically under his watch. Polls now place Plaid Cymru within striking distance of winning the most seats in the next Senedd election, expected in 2026, potentially making ap Iorwerth the first nationalist first minister of Wales. His leadership has emphasized cost-of-living pressures, NHS funding, and devolution rather than a sole focus on independence.

## Party faces critical tests
Ap Iorwerth’s tenure has not been without challenges. Internal divisions over strategy and policy resurfaced in 2023, with critics arguing Plaid Cymru’s message lacked clarity. His response—shifting to a more centrist, issues-based approach—has alienated some traditional supporters but won over undecided voters. The party’s polling bounce followed years of stagnation, with ap Iorwerth credited for steadying the ship.

The next 18 months will test his leadership as Plaid Cymru prepares to face Welsh Labour in a high-stakes election. Ap Iorwerth has framed the contest as a choice between continued Labour rule and a new era of nationalist governance, but faces scrutiny over Plaid Cymru’s ability to deliver on its promises.

## Independence debate takes backseat
Unlike his predecessors, ap Iorwerth has deprioritized immediate independence efforts in favor of building Plaid Cymru’s credibility as a governing party. His approach has drawn praise from moderates but criticism from hardline nationalists who argue it dilutes the party’s core mission. The shift mirrors broader trends in Scottish politics, where the SNP has also softened its independence rhetoric.

Wales’s constitutional future remains a defining issue, but ap Iorwerth has signaled he will not force a referendum without clear public support. Instead, he has focused on securing cross-party backing for incremental reforms, including greater fiscal powers for the Senedd.

## What’s next for Wales
If Plaid Cymru wins the next election, ap Iorwerth would become the first nationalist first minister in Welsh history, reshaping the country’s political landscape. His leadership could accelerate demands for further devolution or even independence, depending on public sentiment. However, the party’s ability to govern effectively will determine whether its surge in support is sustained.

The coming months will reveal whether ap Iorwerth’s gamble on a more pragmatic path pays off. For Wales, the stakes could not be higher: a nationalist government would mark the most significant shift in Welsh politics in decades.
