SpaceX’s next-generation Starship rocket, identified as V3, has officially entered the spotlight with a dramatic redesign that sets it apart from its predecessors. The rocket stands 70 meters tall—an increase from earlier versions—and is engineered to carry more than 100 metric tons to the moon, according to company statements and technical documents reviewed by Space.com.

The V3 iteration represents a complete overhaul, not just an incremental update. Engineers have reconfigured the rocket’s structure, increased propellant capacity, and upgraded Raptor engines to improve thrust and efficiency. These changes are designed to support NASA’s [Artemis program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_program), which aims to return humans to the lunar surface by 2026.

## Starship V3 vs. V1 and V2: What changed

Compared to the earlier V1 and V2 models, the V3 features a stretched upper stage, a reinforced booster, and a new interstage section that enhances engine performance during ascent. The vehicle’s diameter remains consistent at 9 meters, but the structural reinforcement allows for higher internal fuel loads and greater payload flexibility.

SpaceX has also refined the heat shield tiles and thermal protection system, addressing recurring issues seen in previous test flights. The company confirmed these upgrades during a May 11 launch rehearsal at [Starbase in Boca Chica, Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Starbase), where engineers conducted a full wet dress rehearsal with the V3 prototype.

## Reusability remains a core focus

Like its predecessors, the V3 is designed for full reusability. The Super Heavy booster and Starship upper stage are both intended to return to Earth for recovery and reuse, reducing the cost of each mission. SpaceX has emphasized that rapid turnaround and low operational expenses are central to the V3’s design philosophy.

The company has not yet announced a target launch date for the V3’s first orbital test flight, but industry analysts expect it to occur within the next 12–18 months. The rocket’s increased capacity and reliability are expected to play a key role in supporting commercial and government lunar missions.

## What’s next for SpaceX’s moon rocket

SpaceX plans to conduct additional ground tests and suborbital hops before proceeding to full orbital flights. Engineers are also preparing the orbital launch pad at Starbase for V3 operations, including upgrades to propellant storage and vehicle integration systems. If successful, the V3 could become the backbone of NASA’s lunar return efforts and enable new commercial ventures in deep space.
