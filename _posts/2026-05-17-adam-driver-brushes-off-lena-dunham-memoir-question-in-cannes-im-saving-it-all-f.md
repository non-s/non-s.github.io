Adam Driver made it clear at Cannes 2024 he isn’t here to rehash old drama from Lena Dunham’s memoir. When reporters brought up *Famesick*—Dunham’s new book that alleges behind-the-scenes conflicts during their time on HBO’s *Girls*—Driver shut it down with a simple shrug. “I’m saving it all for my book,” he told Variety on the red carpet. The comment came moments after he arrived for the premiere of *Ferrari*, where he plays Enzo Ferrari opposite [Shailene Woodley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shailene_Woodley) and [Pierfrancesco Favino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierfrancesco_Favino). The memoir’s claims, including Dunham’s portrayal of Driver as difficult on set, have reignited long-standing tensions tied to the show’s legacy. But at Cannes, Driver wasn’t playing along for the cameras or the press pack.

## Driver’s silence speaks volumes
Driver’s response wasn’t just a deflection—it was a statement. By redirecting attention to his own writing, he’s signaling he won’t engage with Dunham’s version of events, at least not publicly. That’s no small move for an actor who’s spent years carefully curating his image, from his breakout role on *Girls* to leading blockbusters like *Star Wars* and *BlacKkKlansman*. The memoir’s release has already sparked debate online, with some fans siding with Dunham and others arguing the book’s portrayal feels one-sided. But Driver, who hasn’t publicly addressed the allegations before, isn’t giving anyone new material to dissect.

Famesick dropped earlier this month, and while the book isn’t solely about Driver, his cameo in its pages has kept the story alive years after *Girls* ended. Dunham’s memoir paints a picture of a chaotic set, where her own health struggles and creative differences with the cast collided. Driver’s presence in the book—even as a supporting figure—has kept the old wounds fresh for fans and critics alike. But at Cannes, he made it clear he’s done talking about it in someone else’s terms.

## The memoir’s impact on the *Girls* legacy
The book’s release has forced a reckoning with *Girls*, the show that made Driver a star. Created by and starring Dunham, the series ran from 2012 to 2017 and became a cultural touchstone for millennials. But its legacy has always been complicated. Critics praised its raw portrayal of young adulthood, while others called it out for its lack of diversity and self-indulgent storytelling. Driver’s arc—from a moody, ambitious writer to a more world-weary version of himself—was one of the show’s strongest elements. Yet the memoir suggests the behind-the-scenes reality was far messier than what aired on screen.

Dunham’s book also reignites debates about accountability in creative partnerships. The memoir describes moments where Driver allegedly clashed with cast members and crew, framing him as someone who didn’t always play well with others. Driver, who’s known for his intense preparation and professionalism, hasn’t addressed the specifics, but his refusal to engage at Cannes speaks for itself. For a man who’s built a career on controlling his narrative, this is classic move: ignore the noise, stay focused on the work.

## What’s next for Driver and the book he’s writing?
Driver’s book isn’t just a side project—it’s been in the works for years. He’s hinted at it before, calling it a chance to tell his own story on his terms. Given his rise from a Brooklyn acting class to the biggest franchises in Hollywood, it’s no surprise he’d want to set the record straight. The book could cover everything from his early days in New York theater to his experiences on *Girls* and the pressures of playing Kylo Ren in *Star Wars*. Fans have been speculating for months about what he’ll include, especially after he revealed in a 2023 interview with *The New York Times* that he keeps a detailed journal of his life.

For now, Driver’s priorities are clear. With *Ferrari* hitting theaters later this year and potential projects like a *Star Wars* sequel still in the air, he’s not slowing down. But his book could be the place where he finally unpacks the parts of his career—and his past—that he’s kept close to the vest. Whether it’ll directly address Dunham’s memoir remains to be seen. What’s certain is that Driver isn’t about to let someone else write his story, especially not in the way they choose.

Some fans are already wondering if the book will offer a counter-narrative to Dunham’s memoir. Others think it might skip the drama entirely and focus on his craft. Either way, Driver’s refusal to engage at Cannes suggests he’s in no rush to spill the tea. He’s writing his own instead—and when it drops, it’ll be the final word on at least some of these old rumors.

For now, the Cannes crowd got a glimpse of Driver’s poker face, but the real reveal is still to come. And when it does, it might just rewrite how we remember his years on *Girls* and beyond.
