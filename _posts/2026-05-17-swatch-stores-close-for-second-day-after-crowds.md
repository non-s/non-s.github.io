Swatch Group [Swatch Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swatch_Group) shut stores in London, New York, and other major cities for a second day after a £335 limited-edition pocket watch sparked chaos. Shoppers camped overnight outside flagship stores, clogging sidewalks and forcing police to step in. The model, called the “Swatch x Omega Scilla,” sold out in minutes at most locations, leaving many empty-handed after hours of waiting in the cold. Social media lit up with videos of crowds pushing past barricades and arguing with staff trying to enforce one-person-per-line rules. The watch is a collaboration with Omega [Omega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omega_SA), Swatch’s luxury stablemate, and features a retro design inspired by Omega’s vintage timepieces. This isn’t the first time Swatch’s low-cost, high-impact drops have turned into a circus. The brand’s previous collaborations—like the Swatch x Tiffany [Tiffany & Co.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiffany_%26_Co.) collab—sold out in seconds and left fans furious. But the pocket watch launch went further, drawing criticism for how Swatch handled the crowds. Videos showed security struggling to control the surge, with some shoppers physically jostling to get inside. A Swatch spokesperson said the company “regrets any inconvenience” and blamed the chaos on “unprecedented demand.” They added that online sales would open later this week to ease pressure on stores. The watch’s price tag—£335—is steep for Swatch, but its Omega connection makes it a collector’s item. Omega’s vintage Speedmaster and Seamaster models sell for thousands, so this limited run at a fraction of the cost is a rare chance for fans to own a piece of the brand. The collaboration was announced last month with little warning, catching even Swatch’s own staff off guard. “We weren’t prepared for this,” said one London store manager. “We had to call in extra security and shut the doors twice just to get people to line up properly.” The fallout highlights how Swatch’s strategy of blending Swiss watchmaking with streetwear hype has backfired. The brand built its reputation on affordable, fun watches, but its high-end collabs now risk overshadowing its core products. Critics say Swatch is prioritizing viral moments over customer experience, and this week’s scenes prove it. Swatch isn’t commenting on whether it will change its launch strategy, but fans aren’t holding their breath. “They knew this would happen,” said a watch collector in New York. “They just didn’t care until it blew up in their faces.” The online sale is expected to launch Thursday, but with just 5,000 units available worldwide, most agree the real chase isn’t over yet. If Swatch repeats the mess in-store, the brand’s reputation for hype over substance may take a bigger hit than its sales numbers.
