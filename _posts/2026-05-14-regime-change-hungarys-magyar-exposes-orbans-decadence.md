Hungary’s new Prime Minister Peter Magyar has sparked nationwide debate after releasing videos from Viktor Orban’s former official residences and two ministries, exposing lavish spending that starkly contrasts with the ousted leader’s public image. The footage, which includes rooms adorned with gold fixtures, chandeliers, and ornate furnishings, has drawn eight million views in a single day—a significant number in a country of just under 10 million people. Magyar, a pro-European conservative who took office in May 2026, said touring Orban’s Buda Castle residence reminded him of Nicolae Ceausescu’s extravagant palaces in Romania. Orban, who frequently touted his rural upbringing and modest origins, had decorated his official spaces with nearly 100 paintings borrowed from the [Hungarian National Gallery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_National_Gallery), some valued at over $1 million each. Experts estimate the art collection alone could be worth more than $10 million.

## Public reaction and political fallout
The videos have gone viral across Hungarian social media, with many users expressing outrage over the contrast between Orban’s austerity rhetoric and the opulent spending. Opposition figures have seized on the footage to demand further scrutiny of Orban’s administration. “This is not about politics; it’s about transparency,” said one Budapest resident. “If our leaders are living like kings while telling us to tighten our belts, something is deeply wrong.” The revelations have also drawn international attention, with European Union officials calling for Hungary to address concerns about public spending and governance. Orban, who ruled Hungary since 2010, has not publicly responded to the videos but has previously defended his use of official residences as necessary for representing the state.

## Historical parallels and public memory
The exposure of Orban’s lavish lifestyle echoes past scandals involving deposed autocrats in Eastern Europe. Footage of [Nicolae Ceausescu’s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu) gold-plated bathrooms and [Viktor Yanukovych’s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viktor_Yanukovych) private estate in Ukraine became symbols of their regimes’ excesses. Analysts suggest Magyar’s move may be an attempt to distance his government from Orban’s legacy of centralized power and perceived corruption. “These images tap into deep public resentment,” said a political scientist at [Eötvös Loránd University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s_Lor%C3%A1nd_University). “For many Hungarians, Orban’s rhetoric about simplicity and sacrifice rang hollow against the reality of his administration’s spending.”

## Legal and institutional responses
Hungary’s new government has announced an audit of all official residences and state-owned properties to review spending and asset use. The move follows promises to restore public trust in government institutions. “We must ensure that public funds are used responsibly,” Magyar said in a statement. The audit could lead to legal action against former officials or staff involved in the procurement and display of the artworks. Meanwhile, critics argue that the damage to Orban’s reputation is already irreversible, with the videos serving as a cautionary tale about unchecked power. The scandal also raises questions about the future of Hungary’s cultural heritage, as some of the paintings in the videos were part of state collections that have faced funding cuts in recent years.

## What’s next for Hungary
The fallout from the videos is expected to dominate Hungary’s political discourse in the coming months, with opposition parties pushing for broader reforms. Magyar’s government has pledged to hold Orban’s allies accountable and implement stricter oversight of state spending. However, some analysts warn that without deeper structural changes, public skepticism may persist. For now, the videos have become a symbol of Hungary’s transition—a moment where the curtain was pulled back on a decade of rule marked by centralized control and perceived decadence. The government’s next steps could determine whether this moment leads to lasting reform or merely a temporary shift in public perception.
