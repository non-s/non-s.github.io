The head of France’s largest film producer, Canal+ [Canal+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canal%2B), confirmed on Wednesday that the group will no longer collaborate with over 600 actors, directors, and other cinema professionals who signed a public letter protesting the political influence of billionaire Vincent Bolloré [Vincent Bolloré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincent_Bollor%C3%A9). The decision was disclosed as the Cannes Film Festival [Cannes Film Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival) kicked off its 77th edition this week, making it a high-profile moment for both the industry and the protest movement.

The open letter, published Monday, accused Bolloré—a media mogul with deep ties to France’s rightwing political circles—of using his media empire to sway public opinion and government policy. Among the signatories are major names like Juliette Binoche [Juliette Binoche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juliette_Binoche), Raymond Depardon [Raymond Depardon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Depardon), Sepideh Farsi [Sepideh Farsi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sepideh_Farsi), and Arthur Harari [Arthur Harari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Harari), whose film *Anatomy of a Fall* won an Oscar in 2024 and is screening in Cannes’ main competition with his new film, *The Unknown*.

## Why Canal+ is taking this stand

Maxime Saada, CEO of Canal+, framed the blacklist as a response to what he called a "dangerous interference" in France’s cultural and political landscape. In an interview with French media, Saada argued that Bolloré’s media holdings—including Europe 1, CNews, and Prisma Media—have become tools for pushing a right-leaning agenda that conflicts with the values of artistic independence. "We won’t fund or distribute work from those who actively undermine the integrity of our industry," Saada said. Canal+ is France’s biggest film investor and a major player in European cinema, funding around 40% of French films each year.

The blacklist doesn’t just affect future projects. Industry insiders say past collaborations with signatories could face review or termination. Some producers are already scrambling to replace directors attached to films in development, while others are quietly reassessing their ties with Canal+ itself. The move has sparked a rare public rift in France’s tightly knit film community, where political disagreements rarely spill into professional boycotts.

## The petition’s demands and Bolloré’s response

The petition, titled "Against the Bolloré Dictatorship," demands a clearer separation between Bolloré’s media assets and his political activities. It accuses him of using channels like CNews—a news network often criticized for its rightwing slant—to amplify far-right voices while suppressing dissenting opinions. Bolloré has dismissed the claims as exaggerated, calling the petition a "political stunt" led by left-leaning elites. His lawyers declined to comment on the blacklist but pointed to past court rulings that cleared his companies of bias in news coverage.

The protest comes as Bolloré’s influence in French media has surged. His group, Bolloré Media, owns stakes in major outlets, including a 100% share of Europe 1, a top radio station, and a majority stake in Prisma Media, which publishes *Femme Actuelle* and *Capital*. Critics argue his media holdings give him outsized control over France’s cultural narrative, especially ahead of next year’s presidential election.

## What happens next for the film industry

The blacklist could reshape funding and distribution deals for years. Canal+ finances about 300 films annually, and its withdrawal from projects will leave gaps in budgets and marketing. Some producers are already turning to international investors or streaming platforms like Netflix and Amazon to fill the void. Others worry the move sets a precedent where media moguls could punish artists for political speech, even if it’s protected under French law.

For the signatories, the fallout is immediate. Juliette Binoche, who has starred in films like *The English Patient* and *Three Colors: Blue*, now faces potential loss of funding for her next project. Harari, whose *Anatomy of a Fall* won the Palme d’Or in 2023, told local press he’s "disappointed but not surprised" by Canal+’s stance. "Art should challenge power, not serve it," he said. Meanwhile, Cannes’ artistic director, Iris Knobloch, declined to comment on the controversy but emphasized the festival’s role as a space for free expression.

The broader question is whether other French media groups will follow Canal+’s lead. Groupe M6 and TF1, two other major broadcasters, have so far remained silent. But if the blacklist gains traction, it could force the industry to pick sides—artistic freedom or financial survival.

The next few months will show how deep the cracks go. For now, the film world is watching closely as Cannes unfolds, knowing that this protest might just be the opening salvo in a much larger battle.
