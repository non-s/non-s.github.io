German artist Georg Baselitz [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Baselitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Baselitz), whose upside-down paintings became his signature and whose rebellious streak defined his six-decade career, died Sunday in Berlin. He was 88. The cause of death was not immediately disclosed, but his lawyer confirmed the news to German media. Baselitz was one of Germany’s most internationally recognized artists, celebrated for breaking rules and reshaping modern art after World War II. His work hangs in major museums from New York’s MoMA to London’s Tate Modern, where critics often called him the last great provocateur of 20th-century European painting. He once said, “Art is not there to be pleasant. Art is there to be contested.” That motto defined everything he did, from the classroom to the canvas. His family released a statement calling him “a man who painted the world his way—upside down, inside out, and always on his own terms.” They asked for privacy during this time of mourning. The funeral will be private, but a public memorial is being planned for later this year in Berlin, where he lived and worked since the 1960s. Friends told reporters Baselitz had been in declining health for months, though he continued sketching until the end. In his last months, he completed a series of small ink drawings that gallery owners described as surprisingly bold and fresh, proving his restless mind never stopped working. They will be exhibited posthumously at a Berlin gallery in June. Baselitz’s death leaves a major gap in the art world. No living German artist has carried his level of influence since his rise in the 1960s, when he became the face of a generation that refused to forget the war’s shadows or obey its rules. ## Known first as Hans-Georg Bruno Kern, he was born in 1938 in Deutschbaselitz, a small village in Saxony, then part of Nazi Germany. At 18, he moved to East Berlin to study at the Academy of Fine Arts, but his time there lasted only weeks. The East German regime required art students to work stints at factories or state projects as part of their training. Baselitz refused. He chose instead to paint in the style of Pablo Picasso [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Picasso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Picasso), whose work he admired but whose politics he didn’t share. When officials tried to force compliance, he packed his bags and walked across the still-open border to West Berlin in 1957. That defiance set the tone for his life. In 1961, he adopted the name Georg Baselitz—borrowing from his hometown—as a way to break from his past and start fresh. He later said it felt like “burning the bridge behind me.” The move also saved him from conscription into East Germany’s military, a fate he dreaded. Once in the West, he dove into the city’s fractured art scene, where abstract expressionism and informalism dominated. But Baselitz didn’t follow the crowd. He turned instead to figurative painting with wild, expressive brushwork and distorted figures. His breakthrough came in 1963, when his first solo exhibition in West Berlin caused a scandal. Critics called his paintings grotesque and crude. One reviewer wrote they looked like “a child’s nightmare.” Baselitz didn’t care. He knew he’d found his voice. ## By 1969, Baselitz had perfected his most famous trick: painting subjects upside-down. A man on a horse, a still life, a self-portrait—it didn’t matter. He flipped them all. The idea wasn’t just to shock. It was to force viewers to look again, to see the familiar in a new way. He once explained, “When something is upside-down, it becomes a new thing. You notice details you’d ignore right side up.” Critics struggled to place him. Was he a neo-expressionist? A satirist? A painter of memory and trauma? Baselitz resisted labels. He called himself simply an artist who painted what he saw—and what he remembered. His childhood in war-torn Germany haunted his work. The upside-down figures weren’t just stylistic choices; they reflected a world that had lost its footing, where nothing felt normal or stable. His paintings became windows into a fractured psyche, both his own and Europe’s. In the 1970s and 1980s, Baselitz expanded into sculpture, carving raw wooden figures that twisted and leaned like his paintings. He also began working in series, revisiting themes of heroes, oracles, and mythological figures—always with a twist. His 1980s *Heroes* paintings showed broken, isolated figures in tattered uniforms, echoes of soldiers and survivors. They resonated deeply in a divided Germany still healing from war. Baselitz never softened his edge. In the 1990s, he courted controversy again by painting a series of dark, erotic nudes that some critics called misogynistic. He shrugged off the backlash, saying art shouldn’t be polite. By the 2000s, he was a global star, commanding millions at auction. His 2017 painting *Dubuffet Tree* sold for $4.2 million at Christie’s New York. But he never stopped working, even after a stroke in 2015 slowed him down. In interviews late in life, he said he painted to stay alive. “If I stop,” he told a German newspaper in 2020, “I die.” His death leaves behind a legacy that’s hard to overstate. He didn’t just make art; he challenged what art could be. He proved that painting could still shock, question, and haunt decades after abstract art seemed to have won. Museums will keep his work alive. Scholars will dissect his choices. But the man who flipped the world upside-down is gone. What remains is his art—and the reminder that sometimes, the most powerful thing you can do is look at the world differently.
