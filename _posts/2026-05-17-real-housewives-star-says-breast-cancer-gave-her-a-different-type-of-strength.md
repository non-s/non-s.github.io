Guerdy Abraira [Real Housewives of Miami star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_Housewives_of_Miami) never expected a breast cancer diagnosis to rewrite her life. In March 2023, she was preparing for a cosmetic procedure when her doctor spotted something unusual. "My breast was very dense," she told Healthline. "So dense that the doctor said, ‘We’re not touching anything until you get a mammogram.’" That mammogram changed everything. It revealed stage 1B estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer in her left breast. This specific type of cancer grows when exposed to estrogen, which meant her treatment would need to target both the tumor and her hormone levels. "You feel such helplessness," Abraira said. "There’s a disbelief you can’t shake for the first few months. Then you just have to face the reality of it."\n\nShe’s been open about the emotional toll—shock, fear, and frustration—but also about the unexpected strength that came after. Abraira isn’t just talking about physical recovery. She’s talking about a shift in how she sees herself. Before the diagnosis, she was focused on body image and aesthetic changes. After it, she realized her worth wasn’t tied to her appearance. "I had to relearn how to see myself," she admitted. "It’s not about looking a certain way anymore. It’s about feeling strong and capable."\n\nBreast cancer at stage 1B is treatable, but it’s still serious. Doctors caught hers early because of that mammogram, which happened right before her planned surgery. "If I’d gone under the knife first, they might not have found it until later," she said. Her treatment included a lumpectomy to remove the tumor, followed by hormone therapy to block estrogen. She’s now in remission, but the experience left her with more than just a clean bill of health.\n\n## How breast density affects cancer detection
Dense breast tissue is common—about 40% of women have it—but it makes mammograms harder to read. Cancer can hide in dense areas, which is why doctors often recommend extra screenings like ultrasounds or MRIs. Abraira’s case shows why those extra steps matter. Her dense tissue masked the tumor until the mammogram finally caught it. "Density doesn’t mean you’ll get cancer," she clarified. "But if you do, it’s a reason to be extra vigilant."\n\nDoctors recommend women with dense breasts discuss their risk with their healthcare providers. Some states even require doctors to inform patients if their mammogram shows dense tissue. Abraira now advocates for better awareness. "I want women to know their bodies and push for the tests they need," she said. "No one should have to fight twice because a mammogram wasn’t enough."\n\n## The mental side of cancer recovery
Abraira’s story isn’t just about medical treatments—it’s about the psychological battle. She described months of grief, not just for her health but for the life she thought she had. "You mourn the version of yourself that didn’t have to do this," she said. "But then you realize you’re building a new version—one that’s stronger than before."\n\nHer advice for others going through similar struggles is simple: "Let yourself feel everything. The anger, the sadness, the fear. But don’t let it define you. You’re still here. You’re still fighting." She also leans on her support system—family, friends, and even her Real Housewives castmates, who’ve been vocal about her journey. "They remind me I’m not alone," she said. "And that makes all the difference."\n\nThe cancer didn’t just change her body. It changed her priorities. She’s more focused on health, less on perfection. She’s also more vocal about her experience, hoping to reduce stigma around breast cancer—especially for women who look like her. "Breast cancer doesn’t discriminate," she said. "And neither should the conversation around it."\n\nFor Abraira, the road ahead is about more than just staying cancer-free. It’s about using her platform to help others. She’s working with breast cancer awareness groups and sharing her story publicly. "I want to be the voice I needed when I was diagnosed," she said. "Someone who says, ‘You’re going to get through this.’"
