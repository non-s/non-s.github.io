Israel and Lebanon reconvened in Washington on Wednesday for the second day of their third round of direct talks, with [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah) clashes in southern Lebanon showing no signs of easing. The negotiations, hosted by the U.S., aim to address long-standing border disputes and reduce escalating violence between the two sides. Diplomats from both countries are under pressure to reach a framework agreement to prevent further military confrontation.

The talks follow weeks of intensified exchanges along the Israel-Lebanon border, where exchanges of fire have displaced thousands and raised fears of a broader conflict. [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), a militant group backed by Iran, has exchanged rocket fire with Israel in response to what it calls Israeli aggression. Israel has conducted airstrikes in Lebanon, citing security threats. Both sides have reported casualties, though exact numbers remain unverified.

## U.S. mediation efforts intensify
The Biden administration has stepped up diplomatic efforts to broker a ceasefire, with U.S. officials shuttling between Israeli and Lebanese delegations. Secretary of State [Antony Blinken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antony_Blinken) has emphasized the urgency of the talks, warning that continued violence risks destabilizing the entire region. The U.S. has proposed a phased de-escalation plan, including a halt to cross-border attacks and a return to the 2006 ceasefire lines.

Lebanon’s delegation, led by caretaker Foreign Minister [Abdallah Bou Habib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdallah_Bou_Habib), is pushing for guarantees on Lebanese sovereignty and the withdrawal of [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah) fighters from the border area. Israel, represented by National Security Advisor [Tzachi Hanegbi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzachi_Hanegbi), insists on security assurances before any concessions. The gaps between the two positions remain significant, but negotiators have expressed cautious optimism about incremental progress.

## International pressure mounts
The United Nations and European Union have also urged restraint, with UN Secretary-General [António Guterres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Guterres) calling for an immediate end to hostilities. The escalation has drawn condemnation from regional powers, with [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran) reportedly increasing its support for [Hezbollah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezbollah), while [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia) has called for dialogue. Analysts warn that a prolonged conflict could draw in other actors, further complicating the crisis.

The humanitarian toll is already severe, with hospitals in southern Lebanon overwhelmed and thousands displaced. Aid agencies report shortages of medical supplies and food, exacerbating an economic crisis in Lebanon that is among the worst in its history. The talks in Washington are seen as a last-ditch effort to avoid a full-scale war, which could have catastrophic consequences for the region.

What happens next remains uncertain. If the talks fail, both sides may resort to further military action, risking a wider confrontation. However, a breakthrough could pave the way for broader negotiations, including the resolution of Lebanon’s ongoing political paralysis and economic collapse. The international community is watching closely, with the hope that diplomacy can prevail over brute force.
