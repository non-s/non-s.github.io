CBS News insiders say correspondents and staffers are bracing for sweeping changes at 60 Minutes after Bari Weiss [takes full control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bari_Weiss) of the program. Rumors of layoffs and editorial shifts have intensified as the show’s 58th season nears its May 17 finale, with employees and veterans questioning why the industry-leading program would be overhauled. The program remains the most-watched news show on television, drawing 10.1 million viewers for its April 12 episode featuring Pope Leo and a segment on great white sharks.

Reports of internal tension at CBS News have mounted in recent weeks, with staffers describing a growing divide over editorial direction and management style. Weiss, a former [New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times) opinion editor and founder of the free speech advocacy site The Free Press, assumed a leadership role at CBS News in late 2023. Insiders say her arrival has coincided with a push for what some describe as a more conservative editorial stance, though Weiss has not publicly outlined specific changes to the program.

## Weiss faces pressure to modernize 60 Minutes amid industry decline
The push for change comes as traditional television news programs struggle to retain viewers amid rising competition from digital platforms. Despite its dominance, 60 Minutes has faced criticism from some quarters for its perceived lack of diversity in storytelling and a reluctance to adapt to newer formats. Weiss has previously expressed support for rethinking legacy media’s approach to journalism, arguing that traditional outlets must evolve to survive. Her critics, however, warn that rapid changes could alienate the show’s core audience.

Sources close to the program say Weiss has privately met with senior producers to discuss potential restructuring, including possible layoffs and a shift in editorial priorities. Some correspondents have privately expressed concern that their roles could be at risk, though no official announcements have been made. The show’s future format remains unclear, but insiders say Weiss is likely to prioritize stories that align with her stated commitment to free speech and open debate.

## 60 Minutes remains a ratings juggernaut despite challenges
Despite the uncertainty, 60 Minutes continues to outperform competitors by a wide margin. The April 12 episode’s 10.1 million viewers marked the show’s highest total for the current broadcast season, according to Nielsen data. Its dominance has made it a target for industry observers who question why a winning formula would need to be disrupted. Some employees argue that the program’s success proves its current approach is working, while others suggest that complacency could leave it vulnerable in an evolving media landscape.

The show’s long history, dating back to 1968, has made it a cultural institution, but its future may hinge on how Weiss balances tradition with innovation. The May 17 season finale will provide the first clear signal of what’s to come, with staffers and viewers alike waiting to see whether the program will double down on its established format or embrace a more aggressive overhaul. The outcome could set a precedent for how other legacy news organizations approach digital transformation and audience retention in the years ahead.
