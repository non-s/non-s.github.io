French prosecutors confirmed Friday that around ten people who hadn’t previously reached out now say they were victimized by Jeffrey Epstein’s trafficking network, expanding the probe into possible crimes committed in France or involving French accomplices. The late financier died in 2019 while awaiting trial in the U.S. on sex trafficking charges, closing his criminal case but leaving open civil and international investigations worldwide. These new accounts signal the case isn’t closed in Europe, where law enforcement continues to dig into Epstein’s operations and the networks that helped him move money and people across borders. The prosecutors did not say whether the new victims were minors or adults, or specify when the alleged abuse occurred, but stressed the fresh leads could reshape the scope of the French investigation moving forward. Their identities remain confidential under French law, protecting victims while shielding investigations from public interference. French courts have already convicted several associates of Epstein, including Ghislaine Maxwell [Ghislaine Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghislaine_Maxwell), who was sentenced to 20 years in prison in 2022 for trafficking underage girls for Epstein. Maxwell’s conviction hinged partly on testimony from victims who came forward years after their abuse, showing how long it can take for survivors to feel safe speaking out. These new voices suggest the French case could still grow, especially if prosecutors uncover links to other countries where Epstein operated. The probe now includes examining how Epstein’s network moved money through French banks and whether French officials or elites turned a blind eye to his activities. Investigators are also looking into Epstein’s frequent trips to Paris and the Riviera, where he owned properties and hosted high-profile gatherings that sometimes involved underage girls. The new victims’ accounts could help prosecutors connect dots between Epstein’s U.S. operations and his European networks, filling gaps left by his death. So far, French authorities have not charged anyone in connection with the new leads, but the expansion shows the case remains active and could lead to further indictments. Survivors’ advocates say these disclosures underscore how Epstein’s crimes spanned continents, leaving a trail of unanswered questions that law enforcement is still untangling. The timing of these disclosures isn’t random—it comes as France faces renewed scrutiny over its handling of sex crimes, with recent laws making it easier for victims to seek justice decades after abuse occurred. This could embolden others with similar stories to come forward, knowing their accounts might finally be taken seriously. For now, prosecutors are reviewing the new evidence, interviewing witnesses, and coordinating with international partners to see if these leads point to crimes committed on French soil or involving French citizens. The case remains open, and the public may hear more in the coming months as investigations progress.
