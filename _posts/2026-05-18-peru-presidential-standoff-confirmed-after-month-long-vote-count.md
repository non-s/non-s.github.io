Peru’s presidential race is heading to a high-stakes runoff after the country’s national election board officially confirmed leftist economist Roberto Sanchez as the opponent to Keiko Fujimori. The decision came on May 17, ending weeks of uncertainty after a painstaking vote count that lasted over a month. Sanchez secured more than 2 million votes in the first round, placing him just behind Fujimori, who led with 2.6 million votes. The runoff, likely to be held in late June or early July, will decide whether Peru swings further right or shifts toward a leftist government for the first time in decades.


Fujimori, 47, is the daughter of [Alberto Fujimori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alberto_Fujimori), Peru’s controversial former president who served from 1990 to 2000 and was later imprisoned for human rights abuses and corruption. Now she’s running on her third attempt to reclaim the presidency, positioning herself as a defender of law and order. Her campaign has focused on economic stability and cracking down on crime, themes that resonate with voters tired of instability. But her family’s legacy still looms large, with critics warning she could roll back democratic gains if elected.


Sanchez, 51, is a little-known figure outside political circles but has gained traction by promising to address Peru’s deep inequality and corruption. His rise from obscurity to the runoff reflects widespread frustration with traditional parties. However, his path to the second round wasn’t smooth. The public prosecutor’s office has accused him of financial crimes, though he denies wrongdoing. The allegations have fueled tensions, with Sanchez calling them politically motivated. The controversy adds to the high stakes of the upcoming vote, where Peruvians will choose between continuity and change.


## What the disputed results mean for Peru

The confirmation of Sanchez’s place in the runoff has already sparked backlash. Ultraconservative candidate [Rafael Lopez Aliaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Lopez_Aliaga), who finished third with over 1.5 million votes, has refused to recognize the outcome. He’s accused the election board of bias and vowed to challenge the results in court. His refusal to accept the vote count raises fears of further unrest, especially after Peru’s turbulent 2022 protests that toppled a president. Lopez Aliaga’s supporters, known for their hardline stances, could mobilize against the runoff if they feel cheated.


The election board insists the count was fair and transparent, but the disputes highlight deeper issues in Peru’s democracy. The country has seen five presidents in five years, each removed by scandal or protest. The runoff between Fujimori and Sanchez is seen as a referendum on whether Peruvians want to double down on the status quo or take a risk on a leftist outsider. Polls show a tight race, with neither candidate holding a clear lead ahead of the final vote.


## What happens next

The runoff must happen within 30 days of the official results, so Peruvians can expect the vote to take place by mid-July at the latest. Campaigning will ramp up immediately, with both candidates scrambling to win over Lopez Aliaga’s voters. Fujimori’s team is likely to focus on painting Sanchez as a radical who could destabilize the economy, while Sanchez’s camp will frame Fujimori’s ties to her father’s authoritarian past as a liability. The winner will inherit a country grappling with soaring inflation, violent protests, and a collapsed trust in institutions.


Regional observers, including the [Organization of American States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_American_States), have already warned all sides to respect the process. But given Peru’s history of political violence and unrest, even a smooth runoff won’t guarantee stability. The next president will face an uphill battle to unite a fractured nation and restore faith in democracy after years of chaos.
