Kore-eda Hirokazu’s *Sheep in the Box* starts with a premise that sounds like a fairy tale: a small humanoid named Kakeru lives inside a box, learning about the world through a screen. The film opens with Kakeru waking up to a new day, his voice warm and curious as he talks to the woman who feeds him. It’s a charming setup, but the story quickly stumbles over its own simplicity. The plot never builds real tension or stakes, leaving Kakeru’s journey feeling more like a meandering stroll than a meaningful adventure. By the time the credits roll, he’s learned a few tricks and made a couple of friends, but he still doesn’t have a clue who he really is. That’s a problem when your main character is supposed to be the heart of the movie.

## Kore-eda’s signature touch can’t save the weak script
Kore-eda is known for his quiet, humanistic stories, like *Shoplifters* and *Still Walking*, where small moments carry big emotional weight. But here, his gentle touch feels out of place. The script doesn’t give him much to work with. Kakeru’s growth is uneven—he starts off almost toddler-like, stumbling over words and concepts, then suddenly acts like a kid who’s been studying for years. The transitions feel jarring, as if the film can’t decide how smart Kakeru should be at any given moment. The supporting cast, including a woman who acts as his caretaker and a grumpy neighbor, never get enough screen time to feel real. Their relationships with Kakeru lack depth, making the story feel even emptier.

The visuals are undeniably pretty. Kore-eda’s camera lingers on soft light filtering through windows, on Kakeru’s big, round eyes reflecting the world around him. But beauty alone can’t carry a movie. The film’s most frustrating flaw is its refusal to commit. Is this a story about what it means to be human? A tale of loneliness and connection? A fable about growing up? It tries to be all three, but ends up being none. The ending, where Kakeru finally steps out of his box, feels more like a checklist item than a satisfying conclusion. He doesn’t learn anything profound. He just… moves on.

## Why the film’s flaws stand out even more
Part of the problem is the film’s tone. It’s too sweet for its own good, never letting the audience sit with uncomfortable questions. Kakeru’s existence raises big issues—what does it mean to be alive? Who gets to decide a machine’s purpose?—but the movie skirts around them. Instead, it focuses on cuddly moments: Kakeru hugging his caretaker, playing with a stray cat, or watching fireworks from inside his box. Those scenes are fine, but they don’t add up to a story. The film’s lack of structure means the emotional beats land flat. When a character cries or laughs, it doesn’t feel earned.

Even the performances struggle to save the material. The actor voicing Kakeru does his best with what he’s given, but the script doesn’t give him much to work with. The woman who cares for him (played by [Nakasato Yûko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakasato_Y%C3%BBko)) brings warmth, but her character’s arc is underwritten. The neighbor, played by [Kora Kiyoshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kora_Kiyoshi), tries to add some grit, but his presence feels like an afterthought. The chemistry between them never feels natural, making Kakeru’s world feel even more artificial.

## What’s next for Kore-eda and AI stories
Kore-eda isn’t the first director to tackle AI as a subject. Films like *Her* and *Ex Machina* asked sharper questions about humanity and technology, but they also committed to their themes. *Sheep in the Box* doesn’t. It’s possible Kore-eda was aiming for something lighter, a fable for kids or a gentle bedtime story. But even at its most innocent, a story needs a spine. Right now, this one feels like it’s missing a few crucial vertebrae. The film arrives at a time when AI is on everyone’s mind, from Hollywood writers’ strikes to real-world debates about regulation. But *Sheep in the Box* doesn’t add much to the conversation. It’s a soft, forgettable entry in a genre that deserves sharper thinking.
