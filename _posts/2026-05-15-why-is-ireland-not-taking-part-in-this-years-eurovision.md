Ireland will not take part in the 2024 Eurovision Song Contest, the country’s national broadcaster RTÉ confirmed on Tuesday. The decision comes amid escalating diplomatic tensions between Ireland and Israel over the ongoing war in Gaza. While Eurovision organizers have not imposed any restrictions on participation, Ireland’s refusal reflects its opposition to Israel’s military actions in the Palestinian territory.

This is not the first time Ireland has boycotted the contest. In 2023, the country also opted out, citing similar concerns over the conflict. RTÉ stated that its decision was based on Ireland’s stance as a neutral state and its criticism of Israel’s conduct in Gaza. The broadcaster did not specify whether the boycott would continue in future years.

The move underscores growing divisions within the European Union over the Israel-Hamas war. Several EU member states, including Ireland, have condemned Israel’s military campaign, which was launched in response to the October 7, 2023, Hamas attacks. Ireland has been vocal in calling for a ceasefire and increased humanitarian aid to Gaza, aligning with nations like Spain and Belgium in pushing for a more critical stance toward Israel.

Eurovision, organized by the European Broadcasting Union (EBU), has historically avoided political interference in the competition. However, the contest has increasingly become a platform for broader geopolitical debates. In 2022, Ukraine won the contest amid Russia’s invasion, and the EBU later barred Russia from participating in 2023.

RTÉ’s decision to skip Eurovision 2024 follows a pattern of Ireland distancing itself from events seen as legitimizing Israeli actions. The government has also supported international investigations into alleged war crimes in Gaza, further straining relations with Israel. While the boycott does not violate Eurovision rules, it signals Ireland’s willingness to use cultural platforms to express political disapproval.

The 2024 Eurovision Song Contest will take place in Malmö, Sweden, from May 7 to 11. Ireland’s absence will be noted, as the country has a long history in the competition, winning it seven times. RTÉ has not indicated whether it plans to return in future years, leaving the door open for a potential reconsideration.

The broader implications of Ireland’s boycott extend beyond Eurovision. It highlights the challenges of separating culture from politics in international events, especially amid a devastating humanitarian crisis. As the war in Gaza continues, more countries may reassess their participation in events that indirectly engage with Israel.
