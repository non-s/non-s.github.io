Ronnie O'Sullivan [Ronnie O'Sullivan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronnie_O%27Sullivan) strolled past Kyren Wilson 6-3 in the Snooker 900 Global Championship semifinals, securing his place in the final against Luca Brecel [Luca Brecel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luca_Brecel). The match unfolded on Thursday in Milton Keynes, England, with O'Sullivan controlling most of the frames. Wilson, a top-eight seed, showed flashes of brilliance but couldn't sustain his form against the five-time world champion. O'Sullivan's precision and pace proved too much, leaving Wilson to rue missed opportunities. "Kyren played some great shots, but I just kept grinding it out," O'Sullivan told BBC Sport after the match. "My clearance rate's been good all week. That's what wins you these matches."

Brecel, who beat O'Sullivan in last year's final, will be aiming to defend his title when the two meet on Saturday. The Belgian has looked sharp this tournament, dismantling his opponents with a mix of tactical play and powerful breaks. His path to the final included a 6-2 thrashing of Shaun Murphy, a player known for his own high-octane style. "I'm happy to be back in the final," Brecel said. "Ronnie's the best, so it's going to be a tough match. But I've beaten him before, and I know I can do it again."

O'Sullivan, 48, is chasing his first major title since the 2022 World Championship. A win would add another trophy to his already stuffed cabinet, which includes six world titles and a record 39 ranking events. Wilson, 32, remains winless in his last three semifinals, a run that's cost him dearly in ranking points. "I need to work on my consistency," Wilson admitted. "I felt I had the shots, but the big moments let me down."

The final kicks off at 7pm local time on Saturday, live on BBC Two and Eurosport. Tickets for the showpiece event are sold out, reflecting the huge appetite for snooker's biggest stars. With O'Sullivan's attacking flair against Brecel's methodical approach, fans can expect a clash of styles. The winner takes home £150,000 and a place in snooker history. For O'Sullivan, it’s another chance to silence doubters who think he’s past his prime. For Brecel, it’s redemption after last year’s heartbreak.

This final also marks the first time two players over 30 have met in the decider since 2019. The average age of the top 16 in this tournament was the oldest in a decade, signaling a generational shift in the sport. Younger talents like Barry Hawkins and Judd Trump are still chasing O'Sullivan’s longevity. Meanwhile, Brecel’s rise proves Europe’s growing influence in snooker, challenging the traditional dominance of England and Scotland. Whether it’s O’Sullivan’s swashbuckling style or Brecel’s ice-cold precision, this final has all the ingredients for a classic.
