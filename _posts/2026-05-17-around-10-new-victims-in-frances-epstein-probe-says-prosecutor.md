Paris prosecutors confirmed Sunday that around 10 new people have come forward with allegations in the ongoing investigation into the late US sex offender Jeffrey Epstein. The prosecutor’s office said the new claims surfaced as French authorities dig deeper into Epstein’s network of associates and activities that took place in France and involved French victims. The case remains active, with investigators examining potential links to other high-profile figures who may have been connected to Epstein’s operations. The probe is part of a broader effort to uncover the full extent of Epstein’s crimes, which have already led to multiple convictions and lawsuits in the U.S. and abroad.

The announcement by the Paris prosecutor’s office comes nearly five years after Epstein died in a New York jail cell while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges. Epstein was found hanged in his cell in August 2019, though authorities have never officially ruled out homicide. His death halted the U.S. case, but investigations into his associates and potential accomplices continued in multiple countries, including France, where prosecutors have been methodically reviewing his connections to European elites and business figures.

## Epstein’s French connections under scrutiny
Investigators in France have been focusing on Epstein’s frequent trips to the country, where he owned a luxury property in Paris and socialized with political, financial, and cultural figures. French media reports have previously linked Epstein to several prominent individuals, including late former French president François Mitterrand’s grandson, Frédéric Mitterrand, who has denied any wrongdoing. Prosecutors are now reviewing hundreds of documents, flight logs, and financial records to determine whether Epstein used his European connections to facilitate his crimes or evade scrutiny. The probe has also expanded to include potential victims who may not have been previously identified in earlier U.S. investigations.

French authorities have been working closely with U.S. law enforcement and foreign counterparts to piece together Epstein’s activities across borders. The Paris prosecutor’s office has not provided details on the identities of the new alleged victims, their ages, or the timeframe of the alleged abuse. However, legal experts say the expansion of the case could lead to further charges against Epstein’s associates, who may have played roles in enabling his crimes. The investigation is one of several ongoing efforts to hold accountable those who may have benefited from or ignored Epstein’s behavior.

The Epstein case has already resulted in multiple criminal convictions in the U.S., including that of his former girlfriend and accomplice Ghislaine Maxwell, who was sentenced to 20 years in prison in 2022 for trafficking underage girls for Epstein. Maxwell, who was Epstein’s closest associate, is appealing her conviction. Meanwhile, Epstein’s estate remains a target of lawsuits from alleged victims seeking compensation, including a $50 million claim filed in New York last year by one of the women who accused Epstein of abuse.

French prosecutors have not indicated when they expect to conclude their investigation or whether they plan to file charges against any individuals connected to Epstein. The case remains one of the most high-profile international probes into the late sex offender’s network, with potential implications for political and financial figures who may have interacted with him. Investigators are also reviewing whether Epstein’s operations in France involved other victims who have yet to come forward. The probe underscores how Epstein’s crimes spanned multiple countries and how his network may have operated for decades without detection.

The ongoing investigation is a reminder of the global reach of Epstein’s crimes and the ongoing efforts to uncover the truth decades after his earliest known abuses. While Epstein is no longer alive to face justice, the cases against his associates and the new victims coming forward show that his legacy of exploitation is still being addressed. Prosecutors in France and elsewhere are determined to ensure that anyone who enabled or ignored his crimes is held accountable, even as the legal process continues to unfold.
