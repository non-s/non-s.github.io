Britain’s former cabinet ministers are sounding the alarm over deepening social divides and want ordinary people to help fix it. The Independent Commission on Community and Cohesion, co-chaired by [Sajid Javid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sajid_Javid)—a former Conservative chancellor—and Labour’s [Jon Cruddas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jon_Cruddas), has launched the National Conversation project. It’s asking Britons to submit their own definitions of what it means to belong to a community and to be British. The goal is to gather enough responses to shape a shared vision for the country’s future, one they argue is at risk of fracturing along cultural and political lines.


Javid did not mince words when he spoke about the stakes. “We’re at a moment where this country risks being torn apart by our differences,” he told reporters. He pointed to rising tensions over identity, immigration, and economic inequality as key pressure points. Cruddas, who served as Labour’s head of policy under Gordon Brown, echoed the urgency. “People feel ignored and left behind,” he said. “This project is about giving them a voice before the gaps get wider.” The commission, which includes faith leaders, business figures, and local council chiefs, plans to publish its findings next spring.


## Why now? The backdrop of division

The timing isn’t accidental. Polls show Britons are more polarized than they’ve been in decades. YouGov data from last month found that 62% of people think the country is “more divided than it was five years ago.” The Brexit fallout still lingers, while issues like the English Channel migrant crossings and debates over statues have sharpened cultural clashes. Meanwhile, trust in politicians is near historic lows. Only 17% of Britons say they trust the government to do what’s right, according to Edelman’s 2025 Trust Barometer.


The commission isn’t the first to call for national soul-searching. In 2021, the government’s own Commission on Race and Ethnic Disparities concluded that Britain isn’t institutionally racist, a finding that sparked fierce backlash. Critics argued it downplayed systemic inequality. This new effort distances itself from that controversy by focusing on shared values rather than grievances. “We’re not here to rehash old arguments,” Javid said. “We’re here to ask what brings us together.”


## How the project works

The National Conversation is straightforward: anyone over 16 can submit a short written or video response answering two questions. First, “What does community mean to you?” Second, “What kind of country do you want to live in?” The commission will collect responses through an online portal and offline events in towns and cities across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. They’re targeting at least 10,000 submissions to ensure a broad range of voices.


Organizers say they’re not looking for polished manifestos. They want raw, personal stories—like a retired nurse in Hull describing how her local food bank keeps her neighborhood alive, or a young Muslim woman in Birmingham explaining how she balances her faith with British identity. The commission will use AI tools to analyze themes in the responses, but they stress human judgment will decide what makes the final cut. “We’re not building an algorithm,” Cruddas said. “We’re building a conversation.”


## Who’s on the commission—and who’s missing

The 20-person commission includes a mix of politicians, activists, and academics. Notable names include [Baroness Sayeeda Warsi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sayeeda_Warsi,_Baroness_Warsi), the former Conservative co-chair of the party, and [Dame Louise Casey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louise_Casey), who led the government’s review into integration. But some critics point out gaps. “Where are the voices from the left-behind areas like Blackpool or Sunderland?” asked [Jenny Turner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenny_Turner), a community organiser in Manchester. “If this is truly national, it needs to reach people who don’t usually get heard.”


Others question whether the project can avoid political hijacking. With a general election looming, some fear the findings will be weaponised by parties trying to score points. Javid dismissed the idea. “This isn’t about scoring political points,” he said. “It’s about whether we can still call ourselves a society.” Still, the commission has no formal ties to any party, and its funding comes from private donations and charitable trusts.


## What happens next?

The commission will spend the next six months gathering responses, then host public debates in early 2026 to discuss the themes that emerge. The final report, due in summer 2026, won’t have legal power—but organizers hope it will influence policy. They’re already talking to the Cabinet Office about presenting the findings to ministers. Cruddas hinted at one potential outcome: a national “civic contract” that sets shared values for schools, workplaces, and local councils. “It’s not about forcing unity,” he said. “It’s about giving people a reason to believe in it again.”


For now, the project is a gamble. Will 10,000 voices be enough to shift the needle? Can a country this fractured even agree on what it’s arguing about? The commission isn’t promising answers—just a chance for people to be heard before the gaps get any wider.
