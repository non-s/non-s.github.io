Germany’s labour market flipped faster than expected. In early 2023, companies still begged for workers. Now, firms like [Siemens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siemens) and [Volkswagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volkswagen_Group) have quietly paused hiring. The Federal Employment Agency reported 3.01 million unemployed on Wednesday, up from 2.8 million just two months ago. That’s the highest since the 2008 financial crisis ended in 2009.

## A sudden shift after years of shortages
The change is brutal. For a decade, Germany struggled with too few workers. Birth rates were low, and skilled trades like electricians or nurses stayed in demand. But now, orders are slipping. Industrial production fell 1.5% in June as global demand cooled. Companies that once poached staff now freeze payrolls. The Ifo Institute’s latest survey shows 22% of firms plan to cut jobs in the next three months, double the rate from last winter.

## Who’s getting hit first
Blue-collar workers feel the pain fastest. Temporary staffing agencies report a 30% drop in orders since spring. Factories in [Bavaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bavaria) and [North Rhine-Westphalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Rhine-Westphalia) have already let go hundreds. Even white-collar roles aren’t safe. Tech firms in [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin) and [Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich) cut 5% of their workforce this summer after overhiring during the pandemic boom.

## Government scrambles to react
The Social Democrats and Greens, who built their policies around labour shortages, now face a mess. Labour Minister [Hubertus Heil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubertus_Heil) admitted the government “didn’t see this coming” and promised quick retraining programs. But critics say the programs take months to kick in, while layoffs happen now.

Economists point to three big reasons behind the turnaround. First, China’s weak demand for German cars and machines. Second, high energy costs still squeezing factories. Third, Germans are spending less on home improvements and cars, two sectors that kept the economy afloat last year. The Bundesbank now expects the economy to shrink slightly in 2024, the first annual drop since 2020.

## What happens next
The jobless spike won’t last forever. Analysts expect a rebound by mid-2025 if global trade recovers. But for now, the pain is real. Workers in their 50s, who once had job security, now get warnings. Apprentices who trained for years find no openings. The government’s €2 billion “job bridge” fund, meant to help older workers, is already oversubscribed. Small firms, which drive 50% of Germany’s economy, are the most vulnerable. Many can’t afford severance packages and just shut down instead.

The bigger worry? This isn’t just Germany. France and Italy report rising unemployment too. The Eurozone’s jobless rate hit 6.5% in June, up from 6.4% in May. If Germany’s downturn spreads, Europe’s fragile recovery could stall. For now, the question isn’t if more people will lose jobs, but how fast the government can soften the blow.
