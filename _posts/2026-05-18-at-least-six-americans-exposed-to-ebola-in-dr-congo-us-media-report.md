At least six Americans working in the Democratic Republic of Congo were potentially exposed to Ebola, with one now showing symptoms, U.S. media reported Tuesday. The exposure happened during an ongoing outbreak in Goma, a major eastern city with over a million residents. Goma sits right on the border with Rwanda, making it a regional hotspot for the virus. The American with symptoms was among a group of workers responding to the outbreak, though officials haven’t confirmed their exact roles or employers. The U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention) and the State Department are tracking the situation, but they haven’t released details about the affected individuals or their locations. The CDC told reporters it’s monitoring the case and following standard protocols for exposure management. Ebola spreads through direct contact with bodily fluids from an infected person or contaminated surfaces. Symptoms usually appear within 2 to 21 days and include fever, severe headache, muscle pain, and bleeding. The virus has killed more than 2,200 people in DR Congo since the outbreak began in 2018, making it the second-deadliest in history. Goma has been a key battleground in the fight against the disease. Last year, health workers struggled to contain cases after a person infected with Ebola traveled to the city from a rural area. That case led to a brief suspension of cross-border travel with Rwanda. The current outbreak is concentrated in North Kivu and Ituri provinces, where violence and distrust of outsiders have complicated response efforts. Health teams often face attacks from armed groups, which delay vaccinations and contact tracing. The World Health Organization [WHO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) has warned that the outbreak remains a public health emergency of international concern. International aid groups like Médecins Sans Frontières [MSF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res) have been on the front lines, setting up isolation centers and running vaccination campaigns. The U.S. Agency for International Development [USAID](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USAID) has also funded treatment and prevention programs. Health experts say the risk to Americans in Goma is low, but the exposure raises questions about the safety of international responders. The CDC has travel advisories in place for the region, urging Americans to avoid non-essential travel. Officials are also reviewing how the exposure happened to prevent future incidents. They’re likely looking at protective gear, training, and protocols for workers on the ground. For now, the focus is on isolating the symptomatic American and tracing anyone else who might have been exposed. The CDC says it’s too early to know if this case will spread or if it’s contained. The broader risk to the public in the U.S. remains minimal, but the situation is fluid. Health workers in Goma say they’re stretched thin, with cases popping up in new areas. The outbreak shows no signs of slowing down, and the arrival of the American case only adds to the pressure. Experts warn that without stronger security and community trust, the virus will keep finding ways to spread.
