Andy Burnham, Angela Rayner and Wes Streeting are the three Labour figures most often mentioned as possible successors to Keir Starmer if he resigns. Their names surfaced quickly after seven Labour MPs urged him to step down on Sunday, citing concerns over his leadership. None has officially confirmed they’re running, but each has supporters and detractors inside the party already sizing them up.


Burnham, the mayor of Greater Manchester, is the most experienced of the three. He’s been an MP for 20 years, served as health secretary under Gordon Brown and nearly beat Starmer in the 2020 Labour leadership race. His regional stronghold in the north gives him serious clout, and he’s built a reputation as a steady hand during crises like the Covid pandemic and the Manchester Arena bombing investigation. But some see him as too cautious, and his past support for austerity when he was in government still haunts him.


## Why Rayner and Streeting stand out

Angela Rayner has been Labour’s deputy leader since 2020 and is seen as the grassroots favourite. Her story—raised by a single mum in Stockport, leaving school at 16, working in care before getting into politics—resonates with the party’s working-class base. She’s also close to the unions, which gives her financial and organisational support. But her public clashes with the party’s top brass, including Starmer, have made her enemies in Westminster. Her recent suspension over a tweet about Israel hasn’t helped her cause.


Wes Streeting, the shadow health secretary, is the youngest and most media-savvy of the three. At 42, he’s a rising star who’s managed to keep his London seat safe despite the party’s struggles in the capital. Streeting’s sharp debating style and willingness to take on tough interviews make him a favourite with political junkies. He’s also a vocal critic of the Conservatives, which plays well with Labour’s base. But his past comments about improving schools by sacking “bad teachers” have drawn flak from the unions, and his lack of a regional power base could hurt him in a leadership race.


The timing matters because Labour is polling well nationally but losing ground in key seats. If Starmer goes, the party needs someone who can hold the coalition together—young voters, union members, and disaffected Tories. Burnham’s northern roots could help in the Red Wall seats, Rayner’s working-class appeal might win back traditional Labour areas, and Streeting’s modernising energy could attract younger, urban voters. But none of them have shown they can unite a party that’s split between its left and right wings.


All three have ruled out running against Starmer while he’s still leader, but politics moves fast. The seven MPs who called for his resignation—including shadow minister Sam Tarry—haven’t given up. They argue Labour’s polling lead isn’t enough if the party can’t convert it into seats. The next few weeks will show whether this is a serious threat to Starmer or just a noisy minority. Either way, the conversation has shifted from whether Labour can win in 2024 to what happens if they don’t.
