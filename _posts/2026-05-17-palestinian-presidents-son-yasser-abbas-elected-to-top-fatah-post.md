The Palestinian Authority’s ruling party just got a little more family-run. Yasser Abbas, the 64-year-old son of President Mahmoud Abbas, won a seat Sunday on Fatah’s central committee, initial results show. Fatah has controlled the Palestinian Authority for decades, and this win extends the family’s direct influence over the party’s top decision-making body. The election comes as the PA faces growing pressure to finally hold long-delayed presidential and parliamentary elections, something critics say is long overdue. The central committee is Fatah’s most powerful body, setting policy and picking the party’s slate for future elections. It’s a role that matters because Fatah still dominates Palestinian politics, even as younger generations push for change. Abbas’s win isn’t a surprise—he’s been quietly rising through Fatah’s ranks for years—but it does lock in another generation of his family in a party that’s been accused of clinging to power too long. The Palestinian Authority is struggling with low public trust, rising costs, and deadlock with Hamas in Gaza, so the timing of this election matters. Fatah’s leadership is walking a tightrope: they need to show they’re still relevant, but they also face protests over not holding elections for 17 years. The central committee’s next moves could decide whether the party tries to reform or doubles down on keeping things the way they are. Mahmoud Abbas, 88, has been president since 2005 and shows no sign of stepping aside, even as health rumors swirl. His son’s new role suggests the family isn’t ready to give up control anytime soon. The committee’s makeup now leans even more toward Abbas loyalists, making it harder for critics inside the party to push for reforms. Fatah’s next big test will be whether it can unite enough to survive the next election cycle—or if infighting and public frustration will finally force a shakeup. For now, the family’s grip looks stronger than ever, but the pressure for real change isn’t going away.
