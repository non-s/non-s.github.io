Indian foreign ministry officials pushed back hard on Monday after Dutch Prime Minister Rob Jetten raised questions about press freedom and minority rights during talks with Narendra Modi in The Hague. The officials called Jetten’s concerns a 'lack of understanding' of India’s complex society and long political history. India and The Netherlands used the visit to formally upgrade their relationship to a 'strategic partnership,' a move that puts more focus on defense, technology, and green energy cooperation between the two countries. Prime Minister Modi’s trip to the Netherlands marked his first official visit to the country, a milestone in a broader European tour that included stops in Norway and Belgium.

The Dutch prime minister’s remarks about press freedom and minority rights weren’t made in public, but were reported by Indian and Dutch media after the closed-door meetings. Jetten reportedly asked Modi’s government about restrictions on journalists and protections for religious and ethnic minorities in India. New Delhi responded with a sharp statement, saying the questions reflected 'a lack of understanding of India’s diversity, its constitutional framework, and its democratic traditions.' Foreign ministry spokesperson Arindam Bagchi told reporters that India’s democracy is 'robust' and its institutions are 'strong,' adding that the country has 'no lessons to learn' on these issues.

The exchange comes at a time when press freedom groups have documented a rise in legal cases against journalists in India, especially those covering sensitive topics like religious tensions or government policies. Reporters Without Borders ranks India 161st out of 180 countries in its 2025 Press Freedom Index, a drop from previous years. Minority rights groups also point to growing violence and discrimination against Muslims, Christians, and other groups, often fueled by inflammatory rhetoric from some political leaders. The Dutch government has taken a more vocal stance on human rights in recent years, especially under Jetten’s leadership, which has led to occasional friction with countries like India that see such comments as interference.

Hours after the strategic partnership announcement, India’s foreign ministry issued another strong statement, this time condemning what it called a 'dangerous escalation' after a drone strike on a nuclear power plant in the United Arab Emirates. India didn’t name any group responsible for the attack, but media reports linked it to Iran-backed Houthi rebels in Yemen. The UAE operates two nuclear reactors near Abu Dhabi, and the strike raised alarms about the vulnerability of civilian nuclear facilities in conflict zones. New Delhi called the attack 'reckless' and warned it could set a dangerous precedent for regional stability. The UAE hasn’t commented publicly on the incident, but the International Atomic Energy Agency has expressed concern and called for an investigation.

Back in India, the southern state of Andhra Pradesh is trying a different approach to boost birth rates. State officials announced cash incentives for families with a third child or more, part of a wider push to reverse the country’s falling fertility rate. The Total Fertility Rate in Andhra Pradesh has dropped to 1.9 births per woman, below the replacement level of 2.1 needed to maintain a stable population. Authorities are offering 50,000 rupees ($600) for a third child and 100,000 rupees ($1,200) for a fourth, a move critics say could backfire by increasing pressure on poor families. The state’s fertility rate has been falling for years, mirroring a national trend driven by urbanization, higher education levels, and rising costs of raising children. Demographers warn that rapid aging could strain India’s economy in the coming decades if the trend isn’t reversed.

Prime Minister Modi’s European tour also included a stop in Norway, where he met with business leaders and government officials to discuss trade and clean energy. The trip was part of India’s broader effort to strengthen ties with Europe amid global uncertainty, including rising tensions between the U.S., China, and Russia. India has been positioning itself as a neutral but assertive player, balancing its relationships with major powers while pushing for investments in technology and manufacturing. The strategic partnership with The Netherlands is expected to bring more Dutch companies to India, especially in water management, agriculture, and renewable energy sectors.

What happens next could depend on how both sides manage the fallout from the sharp exchange over rights issues. The Dutch government may choose to raise the concerns privately in future talks, or it could make them public to put more pressure on India. Meanwhile, press freedom groups are watching closely to see if the Indian government responds to the criticism with policy changes or tighter controls. In Andhra Pradesh, the cash incentives for larger families will be tested in the coming months, with early signs suggesting they might not be enough to reverse the fertility decline. The strike on the UAE nuclear plant has already drawn international attention, and New Delhi’s condemnation shows it’s taking the threat seriously. The bigger picture is India’s attempt to navigate a world where its democratic values are increasingly under scrutiny, even as it seeks to play a bigger role on the global stage.
