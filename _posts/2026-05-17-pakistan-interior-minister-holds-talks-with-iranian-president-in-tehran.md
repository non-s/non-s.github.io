Pakistan’s interior minister [Mohsin Naqvi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohsin_Naqvi) arrived in Tehran on Tuesday for high-level talks with Iran’s president [Masoud Pezeshkian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masoud_Pezeshkian), the first meeting between the two countries’ top officials in months. The visit comes as tensions between Iran and the US are rising, with no clear path to de-escalation in sight. Both sides see Pakistan as a potential go-between to reduce the heat in the region, especially after Pakistan’s earlier attempts to broker indirect negotiations between Tehran and Washington failed to gain traction.

During the closed-door meeting, Naqvi and Pezeshkian covered a lot of ground. Security topped the agenda, with both sides agreeing that any new flare-ups could spill over into neighboring countries. Iran’s president stressed the need for regional stability, while Naqvi pushed for stronger border controls to stop militant groups from using either country as a hideout. The two also discussed how to protect trade routes that run through their shared 900-kilometer border, a major economic lifeline for both nations.

## A push to fix trade and border issues

Trade between Pakistan and Iran has been shaky for years. Sanctions on Iran have choked its ability to sell oil and gas, while Pakistan’s own economic troubles make it hard to buy anything beyond basic goods. Still, both sides are desperate to keep their border trade alive. Pezeshkian brought up the need to speed up a long-delayed gas pipeline project that would send Iranian gas to Pakistan, though funding and US pressure have stalled it for a decade. Naqvi didn’t give any new promises but said Pakistan is still committed to the deal.

Border security was another sore point. Iran has accused Pakistan of not doing enough to stop militant groups like Jaish al-Adl, which has launched attacks into Iran from Pakistani soil. Naqvi didn’t dismiss the claims but pointed to Pakistan’s recent crackdown on militants in Balochistan, the province that borders Iran. He also said both countries are setting up a joint border force to patrol the area more effectively.

## Iran-US tensions loom over the talks

The shadow of US-Iran relations hung over the entire meeting. Iran’s leadership has made it clear they’re fed up with what they call American bullying, while the US has warned Iran not to escalate after a series of direct strikes and proxy attacks in recent months. Pakistan, which has close ties with both sides, has been trying to play mediator, but so far, it hasn’t moved the needle much. Naqvi’s trip was seen as a chance to reset those efforts, even if no breakthroughs were expected.

Pezeshkian used the meeting to stress that Iran isn’t looking for war but won’t back down either. He repeated Iran’s demand for the US to lift sanctions and stop interfering in the region. Naqvi, meanwhile, stuck to a more diplomatic line, saying Pakistan wants dialogue but also stability—especially since any direct conflict could drag the whole region into chaos.

## What’s next for Pakistan and Iran

Both sides called the talks productive, but no concrete deals were announced. The gas pipeline remains stalled, and militants on the border are still a problem. Still, the fact that the two leaders met at all sends a message that neither country wants things to get worse. The next step is likely more technical talks between officials, possibly within weeks, to work out the details on security and trade. A second visit by a Pakistani delegation isn’t out of the question, but for now, both sides are keeping expectations low.

For Pakistan, the stakes are high. A war between Iran and the US would force Islamabad to pick a side, something it’s desperate to avoid. For Iran, the meeting was a chance to show it’s not isolated, even as it faces growing pressure from Washington. The real test will be whether these talks lead to real action—or if the old problems resurface within months.
