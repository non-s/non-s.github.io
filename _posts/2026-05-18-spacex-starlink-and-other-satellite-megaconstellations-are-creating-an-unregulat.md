In the past seven years, SpaceX has sent more than 7,000 Starlink satellites into low Earth orbit, creating a global internet network. Each one eventually dies by burning up in the atmosphere, leaving behind a trail of aluminum oxide particles. That’s the big problem here. These particles linger and interact with other chemicals in the atmosphere, but no one’s tracking how they change Earth’s protective layers. The Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) and other agencies focus on launch safety, not the long-term effects of satellite debris. That leaves scientists uneasy. They compare this to running an experiment on the sky without a control group or oversight. The first signs of trouble might not even show up for decades, if ever. Right now, the scientific community is flying blind. ## How aluminum oxide changes the sky When a Starlink satellite re-enters, it sheds roughly 100 pounds of aluminum oxide dust. That’s not a small amount. Over time, these particles can seed clouds and alter how heat escapes Earth. They might even thin the ozone layer, though no one knows for sure. The issue isn’t just SpaceX. Companies like OneWeb and Amazon’s Project Kuiper plan to launch thousands more satellites. Together, they could add tens of thousands of new objects to the atmosphere. No single country or agency is responsible for studying the cumulative impact. The U.S. is the only country that has even tried to assess the problem, and its report last year admitted the data is incomplete. ## Why scientists are sounding the alarm The scientific community has been raising alarms for years, but the warnings are getting louder. In 2023, a team at the University of Southern California calculated that Starlink alone could release up to 360 tons of aluminum oxide annually as satellites burn up. That’s a tiny fraction of the total aluminum in the atmosphere, but it’s concentrated in specific layers where satellites orbit. The particles don’t spread evenly. They cluster in the mesosphere and stratosphere, where they can linger for years. ## No one’s in charge of this mess The lack of regulation is the core issue. The Outer Space Treaty of 1967 set basic rules for space activity, but it doesn’t cover atmospheric pollution. The FAA licenses launches, the FCC approves satellite communications, and NASA tracks debris. None of them have authority to study or limit aluminum oxide releases. International bodies like the United Nations Office for Outer Space Affairs (UNOOSA) [https://www.unoosa.org/](https://www.unoosa.org/) have discussed the problem, but progress is slow. Meanwhile, companies keep launching. SpaceX alone plans to add 30,000 more Starlink satellites in the coming years. If this keeps up, the sky might not look the same in 50 years. ## What happens next? For now, scientists are pushing for more research. They want governments to fund studies on aluminum oxide’s effects and push for international agreements. Some researchers suggest satellites could be redesigned to burn up cleaner or avoid releasing particles. But that would slow down launches and add cost, which companies may resist. The FAA’s 2023 environmental review suggested voluntary guidelines, but voluntary isn’t enough when the stakes are this high. Until someone takes charge, the experiment continues. And no one knows how it ends.
