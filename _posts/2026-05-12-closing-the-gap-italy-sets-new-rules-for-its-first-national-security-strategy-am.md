Italy is poised to publish its first-ever national security strategy, breaking a decades-long impasse that made it the only G7 nation without such a framework. The document, expected in the coming months, marks a significant shift in how [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy) approaches defense and security amid rising global tensions.

For generations, Italy avoided formalizing a national security strategy due to a mix of political instability, institutional rivalries, and reliance on U.S. security guarantees in Europe and the Mediterranean. Successive governments cited a relatively stable post-Cold War environment as justification for the delay. However, recent changes—including increased domestic political cohesion and mounting external pressures—have created new incentives to act.

The push for a strategy gained momentum as Italy faced evolving threats, including cyberattacks, migration crises, and energy insecurity. Analysts say the strategy could help align military, diplomatic, and economic policies under a unified framework. Italy’s [Ministry of Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(Italy)) has been coordinating with other agencies, including the [Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_International_Cooperation_(Italy)) and intelligence services, to finalize the plan.

The absence of a formal strategy has long drawn criticism from NATO allies, who argue it complicates coordination on shared security challenges. Italy’s participation in missions like the [Russo-Ukrainian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russo-Ukrainian_War) support efforts has further exposed gaps in its defense planning. Now, with the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union) and [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO) emphasizing collective security, Italy’s move aligns with broader regional trends.

Domestic politics played a key role in breaking the deadlock. Prime Minister [Giorgia Meloni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giorgia_Meloni) has prioritized defense modernization since taking office in 2022, including increased military spending. Her government’s push for a strategy signals a break from past indecision, though challenges remain in balancing competing priorities like economic growth and social spending.

Critics question whether the strategy will deliver tangible results or remain a symbolic gesture. Past attempts at defense reforms have stalled due to bureaucratic resistance and budget constraints. Still, supporters argue that even a basic framework could improve Italy’s ability to respond to crises, from hybrid warfare to climate-related risks.

Looking ahead, Italy’s strategy could serve as a model for other nations reconsidering their own security postures. If implemented effectively, it may strengthen Italy’s role in [NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NATO) and [EU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union) defense debates. The document’s release will be closely watched by policymakers and analysts alike.
