Health professionals are sounding alarms over a viral social media trend called ballmaxxing, where men inject fluids directly into the testicles to increase their size. The practice involves using substances such as saline or surgical lubricants like Surgilube, with some users claiming their testicles grew to the size of grapefruits. Medical experts, however, warn that such procedures pose serious health risks, including severe infections, scarring, and possible permanent damage to male genitalia.

The trend is the latest in a string of social media-driven body modification fads, following past crazes like looksmaxxing, fibermaxxing, and sleepmaxxing. Ballmaxxing has gained traction on platforms such as TikTok and Instagram, where users share before-and-after images and instructional videos. The rapid spread of the trend has prompted doctors and public health advocates to issue urgent warnings about its dangers.

## What is ballmaxxing and how does it work?

Ballmaxxing involves the injection of fluids into the scrotum or directly into the testicles to enhance their appearance and perceived size. Proponents of the trend often cite aesthetic preferences or attempts to boost confidence as their motivation. Social media posts frequently showcase dramatic results, with some users claiming their testicles have swollen to several times their normal size. However, these claims are not supported by medical evidence and lack professional oversight.

Urologists and sexual health specialists have expressed alarm at the lack of sterilization, proper technique, and anatomical knowledge involved in these procedures. Injecting non-sterile fluids or performing the injections improperly can lead to infections, abscesses, and even systemic blood infections. The scrotum's delicate tissue is not designed to withstand such interventions, increasing the risk of complications.

## Medical risks and long-term consequences

Doctors emphasize that ballmaxxing is not a medically approved or safe practice. The primary risks include localized infections, which can spread and lead to sepsis if bacteria enter the bloodstream. Other potential complications include tissue necrosis, chronic pain, and irreversible damage to the testicles, which may affect fertility and hormone production. Some men have required surgical intervention to remove infected or necrotic tissue, with some cases resulting in the loss of one or both testicles.

In addition to physical harm, the psychological impact can be severe. Users who experience complications may face long-term health issues, including chronic pain, disfigurement, and emotional distress. The World Health Organization has previously warned about the dangers of unsafe body modification practices promoted on social media, highlighting the need for public awareness and education.

Healthcare providers urge anyone considering ballmaxxing to seek professional medical advice instead. Urologists recommend discussing body image concerns with a licensed therapist or healthcare provider who can offer safe, evidence-based alternatives. The American Urological Association has also issued statements discouraging such procedures due to the high risk of harm.

What happens next in the broader context of social media-driven body trends remains uncertain. As platforms continue to grapple with misinformation and unsafe practices, calls for stricter content moderation and public health warnings grow louder. Meanwhile, doctors are treating an increasing number of patients presenting with complications from self-injected genital enhancements. Public health campaigns are expected to ramp up to counter the spread of dangerous trends like ballmaxxing.
