Iran’s top diplomat accused Washington on Friday of deep-seated mistrust as the primary barrier to reviving stalled nuclear talks. Foreign Minister [Abbas Araghchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbas_Araghchi) made the remarks during a [BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS) foreign ministers’ gathering in the Indian city of [Nagpur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nagpur), where regional security and diplomatic standoffs dominated the agenda.

Araghchi said Iran remains open to negotiations but will not accept an agreement that it views as one-sided. “What we need is a deal that is fair and balanced,” he told reporters after closed-door discussions. His comments underscore the widening gap between Tehran and Washington despite years of intermittent diplomacy aimed at restoring the 2015 nuclear accord, officially known as the [Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_Comprehensive_Plan_of_Action).

## Nuclear talks remain deadlocked after years of diplomatic swings
The JCPOA, which lifted sanctions on Iran in exchange for curbs on its nuclear program, has been on life support since 2018 when then-President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump) withdrew the US from the agreement and reimposed sanctions. Since then, Iran has incrementally breached key restrictions, including uranium enrichment levels and stockpile limits, while demanding sanctions relief and guarantees against future US withdrawal.

Efforts to revive the deal have repeatedly stalled, most recently in late 2023, when indirect talks mediated by [Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oman) and other regional actors collapsed without resolution. Araghchi’s latest statement signals Iran’s continued hardline stance, rejecting any framework that does not address its core concerns over sovereignty and economic sovereignty.

## BRICS expands focus as Iran seeks new alliances
The BRICS bloc, which now includes [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt), [Ethiopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopia), [Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran), [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia), and the [UAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates), has emerged as a platform for countries frustrated with Western-led global institutions. Araghchi’s presence at the meeting highlights Iran’s pivot toward non-Western alliances amid stalled talks with the US and Europe.

Iranian officials have repeatedly cited Washington’s inconsistent policies and perceived bad faith as reasons for their reluctance to re-engage in formal negotiations. The Biden administration has expressed willingness to return to the JCPOA but has ruled out lifting all sanctions or providing guarantees against future withdrawal.

## Regional tensions rise as nuclear diplomacy falters
The impasse comes as regional tensions escalate, including attacks on shipping lanes in the [Gulf of Oman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman) and exchanges of fire between Israel and Iran-linked groups in [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria) and [Lebanon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebanon). Analysts warn that prolonged diplomatic failure could further destabilize the region, increasing the risk of miscalculation or escalation.

Araghchi did not indicate whether indirect talks would resume or propose a timeline for future discussions. He emphasized that trust must be rebuilt before any meaningful progress can occur, adding that Iran would continue to assert its rights under international law while seeking equitable treatment in global forums.

The next round of regional talks is expected to focus on regional security rather than the nuclear file, with [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India), which currently holds the BRICS chair, playing a mediating role. Meanwhile, the US has reiterated its commitment to diplomacy but has not signaled flexibility on core sanctions or enforcement demands.

What happens next remains unclear. Without a breakthrough in trust or a willingness to compromise on either side, the stalemate over Iran’s nuclear program is likely to persist, with broader implications for global non-proliferation efforts and regional stability in the Middle East.

O ministro das Relações Exteriores iraniano, Hossein Amir-Abdollahian, declarou que a falta de confiança na postura americana inviabiliza avanços concretos, enquanto o país exige um acordo "justo e equilibrado" — uma condição que soa cada vez mais distante diante das tensões geopolíticas crescentes.

O tema ganha relevância para o Brasil e os falantes de português porque reflete não apenas a crise diplomática no Oriente Médio, mas também os desafios de mediação em um cenário global cada vez mais fragmentado. Para o governo brasileiro, que busca posicionar-se como ponte entre Ocidente e Oriente, a estagnação das conversas pode limitar sua capacidade de influenciar negociações futuras, especialmente se a escalada do conflito afetar o fornecimento de energia ou a estabilidade regional. Além disso, a postura iraniana de exigir garantias "justas" ecoa demandas históricas de países em desenvolvimento, como o Brasil, que também enfrentam pressões por tratados internacionais assimétricos.

A próxima rodada de diálogos, ainda sem data definida, pode definir se o BRICS terá algum papel efetivo na descompressão da crise ou se o impasse persistirá, colocando em xeque não só o acordo nuclear, mas também a credibilidade do próprio bloco emergente.

## 🇪🇸 Resumen en Español

El estancamiento de las negociaciones nucleares entre Irán y Estados Unidos en el marco de la cumbre del BRICS refleja la creciente desconfianza entre ambas partes, según denunció el ministro de Exteriores iraní. La falta de avances amenaza con prolongar la crisis diplomática en un momento clave para la estabilidad regional.

El contexto actual se enmarca en la tensión acumulada desde la salida de EE.UU. del acuerdo nuclear en 2018, que Irán ha respondido con un aumento de su enriquecimiento de uranio. Para los hispanohablantes, especialmente en América Latina, este conflicto tiene implicaciones geopolíticas, pues afecta a los flujos energéticos y al equilibrio de poder en Oriente Medio, región clave para el suministro global de petróleo. La posibilidad de un nuevo acuerdo —o de una escalada militar— podría redefinir las alianzas internacionales y el precio de los combustibles en el mercado global.
