Australia’s [Nature Repair Market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_Repair_Market) was supposed to be a game-changer. Instead, it’s barely off the ground. The Albanese government [swept to power in 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Australian_federal_election) promising to fix a decade of environmental neglect. Voters bought it—polls showed overwhelming support for stronger protections. But four years later, the government’s budget and new environmental laws tell a different story. The nature repair market, meant to fund conservation, isn’t working. Threatened species like the [koala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koala) and [Leadbeater’s possum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leadbeater%27s_possum) are still at risk, and the reforms haven’t stopped the decline. It’s not just a policy failure—it’s an existential one for Australia’s unique ecosystems. 

## The market that wasn’t

The [Nature Repair Market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_Repair_Market) was touted as a way to trade credits for restoring ecosystems. Landowners could earn money by protecting or regenerating habitats, and companies could buy credits to offset their environmental impact. The idea? Make conservation profitable. But the market’s launch has been slow. Rules are unclear, funding is scarce, and so far, just a handful of projects have been approved. Worse, the government’s own data shows most credits aren’t being used to protect the places they were meant to. Instead, they’re sitting idle. The result? Australia’s biodiversity continues to collapse. [Recent reports](https://www.environment.gov.au/) show 19 mammals have gone extinct since 1788, and over 1,900 species are now listed as threatened. 

The government insists the market is still in its early days. But critics say that’s no excuse. The [Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environment_Protection_and_Biodiversity_Conservation_Act_1999) reforms, passed in 2024, were supposed to fix the broken environmental laws that let species vanish. Instead, they’ve added more bureaucracy without real enforcement. The new laws give the government power to step in when states fail to protect wildlife—but so far, it’s rarely used. Meanwhile, Australia’s environment is paying the price. 

## Broken promises and voter frustration

Labor won in 2022 partly because voters were sick of seeing forests bulldozed, rivers polluted, and animals pushed to the brink. The party promised to stop the rot. Yet the budget for 2024-25 cut funding for environmental programs by $140 million. At the same time, the government approved new coal and gas projects that will worsen climate change—the biggest threat to wildlife. It’s a pattern: talk big on environment, but deliver on industry. 

Voters aren’t happy. Polls show trust in the government’s environmental handling has dropped sharply. Even Labor’s own supporters feel let down. The nature repair market was supposed to be the fix, but it’s not working. Most Australians agree species conservation should come before profit. Yet the market’s design favors big landowners and corporations over small farmers and Indigenous groups trying to protect country. 

## What’s next?

The government says it’s still tweaking the market rules. But tweaks won’t fix a system that’s fundamentally flawed. Australia needs real action: enforce strict protections for threatened species, stop approving projects that destroy habitats, and properly fund conservation. Otherwise, the nature repair market will remain a paper tiger—and more species will vanish. The question isn’t just whether Labor can deliver on its promises. It’s whether Australia can afford to keep waiting.
