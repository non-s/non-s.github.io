The Female Film Club [announced](https://www.femalefilmclub.com) the second edition of its Global Short Film Fund at Cannes 2025, nearly doubling the prize money to €33,000 ($38,600). The fund supports emerging female filmmakers by covering production costs, post-production, and now virtual production thanks to three new partners: AI filmmaking platform [IMGN.AI](https://www.imgn.ai), post-production house [Disauthority](https://www.disauthority.com), and virtual production specialist [Mo-Sys](https://www.mo-sys.com).

## Cannes’ growing support for female filmmakers

The fund was first launched last year to address the lack of funding for short films directed by women. It’s now become a fixture on the Cannes circuit, running parallel to the festival’s official selections. This year’s announcement came during a packed press conference on the Croisette, where organizers emphasized the fund’s role in leveling the playing field.

IMGN.AI is contributing its AI-assisted editing tools, while Mo-Sys is offering virtual production services—like LED volume setups and motion control—that were once out of reach for most short film budgets. Disauthority is handling color grading and sound design for the winning project. The prize also includes a cash award, mentorship from industry veterans, and a screening slot at a Cannes-affiliated venue.

## Who’s behind the fund and why it matters

The Female Film Club was founded in 2020 by a group of female producers, directors, and executives frustrated by the industry’s gender gap. Their goal is simple: get more female-directed short films made and seen. The Global Short Film Fund is their flagship program, but they also run workshops and networking events year-round.

This year’s winners will be chosen by a jury that includes [Jane Campion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Campion), the Oscar-winning director of *The Piano*, and [Ava DuVernay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ava_DuVernay), whose *Selma* and *13th* have redefined modern filmmaking. Campion called the fund “a real step toward fixing the imbalance” in Cannes’ short film selections.

## How the new partners will change the game

IMGN.AI’s involvement means short filmmakers can now edit their footage faster and with less manual labor. The platform’s AI tools can sync audio, color-grade clips, and even suggest cuts—tasks that used to take days. Mo-Sys, known for its work on *The Mandalorian*, is bringing its virtual production expertise to the fund, offering winners access to LED walls and camera tracking for a fraction of the usual cost.

Disauthority’s post-production package is no small perk either. Winners get professional color grading and sound mixing, which can cost tens of thousands on the open market. Combined with the €33,000 cash award, the fund now covers nearly every step of the filmmaking process.

## What’s next for the fund and Cannes’ short films

Applications for the fund open in June, with a deadline of August 1. Winners will be announced in September, and their films will screen during the 2026 Cannes festival. The Female Film Club says it’s already planning a third edition, with talks underway to add more partners like streaming platforms and film schools.

For now, the fund’s expansion sends a clear message: Cannes is serious about supporting female filmmakers beyond its main competition. And with tools like AI and virtual production now within reach, the next generation of short filmmakers might just have the resources they need to compete on the world’s biggest stage.
