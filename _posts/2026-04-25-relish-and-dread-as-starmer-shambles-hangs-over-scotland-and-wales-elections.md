Sir Keir Starmer’s [Labour Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)) is under the microscope as Scotland and Wales prepare for local and devolved elections on May 7. The prime minister’s leadership has drawn sharp reactions, with some voters and candidates describing his tenure as a ‘shambles’ while others defend his approach. BBC political editor [Laura Kuenssberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laura_Kuenssberg) traveled to both nations to gauge local sentiment ahead of the vote.

In Wales, Labour’s grip on power is being tested as the party faces pressure from Plaid Cymru and the Conservatives. First Minister [Mark Drakeford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Drakeford) has stepped down, leaving a leadership contest that could reshape the party’s future. Voters in Cardiff and Swansea told Kuenssberg they are torn between loyalty to Labour and frustration over Starmer’s policies, particularly on public services. One Swansea resident said, ‘We need change, but not this kind of uncertainty.’

## Starmer’s leadership tested in Scotland

Scotland’s elections present a different challenge for Starmer, with the [Scottish National Party (SNP)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNP) still dominant in polls despite recent setbacks over independence and leadership turmoil. Labour aims to regain ground, but candidates admit Starmer’s unpopularity in some areas risks dragging down their campaigns. In Glasgow, a Labour candidate conceded that ‘the Starmer factor is real,’ adding that voters are more focused on local issues than Westminster drama.

Labour’s internal polling leaked to the media suggests Starmer’s approval ratings remain low among undecided voters, particularly in the north of England and parts of Wales. The party’s strategy hinges on distancing itself from recent controversies while emphasizing economic stability—a message that has yet to resonate strongly. A Labour MP from Merseyside, speaking on condition of anonymity, admitted the party is ‘playing catch-up’ in areas where voters feel ignored.

## Voters react to Starmer’s tenure

Across both nations, voters describe a sense of ‘relish and dread’—relief that the Conservatives are out of power but anxiety over Labour’s direction. In Edinburgh, a young voter told Kuenssberg she supported Labour but feared Starmer’s policies would fail to address Scotland’s housing crisis. Meanwhile, in north Wales, a retired teacher praised Starmer’s handling of the economy but criticized his approach to education funding. ‘He’s competent, but not inspiring,’ she said.

Labour’s campaign has tried to pivot to local issues, with candidates focusing on NHS funding and education rather than national leadership. Yet analysts note that Starmer’s personal brand remains a liability in key swing areas. A pollster from Cardiff University, who requested anonymity, said Labour’s message risks getting ‘lost in the noise’ of broader dissatisfaction with Westminster politics.

The May 7 elections will test whether Starmer’s Labour can overcome its leadership woes or if voters will look elsewhere for alternatives. With Plaid Cymru and the Conservatives both positioning themselves as viable options, the results could force Labour to rethink its strategy before the next general election. For now, the party remains in a defensive crouch, hoping to minimize losses while avoiding a full-blown crisis.
