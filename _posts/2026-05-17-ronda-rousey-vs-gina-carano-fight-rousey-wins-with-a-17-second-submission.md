Ronda Rousey [retired Gina Carano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gina_Carano) in under 20 seconds Saturday night in Los Angeles, ending the long-awaited MMA rematch with a dominant armbar submission. The fight lasted just one round at featherweight, marking Carano’s first competitive bout since 2009. What was billed as a nostalgic showdown between two of women’s MMA’s earliest stars turned into a one-sided spectacle before the crowd could even settle in. Rousey, 39, entered the cage knowing this would likely be her final fight. The former UFC women’s bantamweight champion and 2008 Olympic judo bronze medalist has been upfront about her plans to retire after this comeback, and she made sure to leave on her own terms. Carano, 44, parlayed her fighting fame into Hollywood roles like *Fast & Furious* and *Deadpool*, but Saturday proved she’s a step behind the elite grapplers of the modern era. Rousey showed why she was once the most feared striker in women’s MMA, using her superior reach and clinch work to control Carano immediately. A single right hand sent Carano stumbling toward the fence, and Rousey pounced like a predator, locking in the armbar that forced the tap at 0:17 of the first round. The crowd erupted, not because of the length of the fight, but because of what it represented. This wasn’t just a victory—it was a statement. Rousey’s dominance reminded everyone why she was once the face of women’s MMA before stepping away in 2016. Her career included a UFC title reign, a WWE run, and a brief stint in WWE as an in-ring performer, but Saturday was about proving she could still end careers the way she once started them. Carano’s Hollywood detour kept her name in the spotlight, but her grappling and cardio couldn’t match Rousey’s. The loss marks the end of Carano’s fighting dreams, at least for now. She’s walked away from the cage before—first in 2011, then again after a failed return in 2013—and Saturday’s quick defeat might keep her there permanently. For Rousey, the fight was her final statement. She’s already hinted this was it, and barring a sudden change of heart, she won’t lace up the gloves again. The women’s MMA scene has changed drastically since Rousey’s prime, with newer stars like [Amanda Nunes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amanda_Nunes) and [Zhang Weili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhang_Weili) dominating in ways she never did. Saturday’s performance wasn’t just about beating Carano—it was about reminding the world she was still Ronda Rousey, the fighter who once made armbars famous. The event drew big names from Hollywood and combat sports, but the only thing anyone talked about after was how fast it was over. Rousey’s legacy as one of the most influential fighters in women’s MMA is secure. Carano’s legacy as a pioneer is too, even if her competitive career fizzled out faster than expected. The fight itself might not go down as one of the greatest, but its place in the history of women’s MMA is already written.
