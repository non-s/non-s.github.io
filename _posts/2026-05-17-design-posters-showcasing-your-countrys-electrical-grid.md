A small team of energy data enthusiasts just released grid2poster, a free tool that transforms complex electrical grid information into eye-catching posters. The project, hosted on GitHub, pulls live data from open energy databases like the [European Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Network_of_Transmission_System_Operators_for_Electricity) (ENTSO-E) and the [U.S. Energy Information Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Energy_Information_Administration) (EIA) to generate accurate visuals of national power systems. No coding skills are needed — users upload data or let the tool fetch it automatically, then tweak colors, fonts, and layouts before downloading a high-resolution PDF ready for printing or sharing online.

## How it works and who it’s for

The tool started as a side project by [Open Energy Transition](https://github.com/open-energy-transition), a collective focused on making energy data more accessible. Their goal isn’t just pretty posters — it’s to help people grasp how their electricity actually gets to their homes. Teachers use it to explain grid basics to students. Activists print posters for town halls to push for renewable energy. Even journalists have grabbed grid visuals to illustrate stories about blackouts or green energy growth. The posters highlight key stats like total generation capacity, renewable vs. fossil fuel shares, and major transmission lines. One user in Germany told Hacker News they printed a poster for their local energy cooperative to show members how the regional grid handles solar and wind power.

## The data behind the design

Grid2poster pulls from reliable sources, but users can also upload their own datasets. The tool supports formats like CSV and JSON, so organizations with custom energy data can adapt it for their needs. For example, a city’s sustainability office might plug in local solar panel installation records to show growth over time. The posters update automatically if the underlying data changes, so a poster made today could reflect last week’s grid shifts. The team behind the project checks data sources regularly to catch errors or outdated links.

## What people are saying

Early users on Hacker News called it a "fantastic way to make energy data approachable" and praised the clean, modern designs. One commenter said they used a poster in a presentation to explain the Netherlands’ grid to policymakers — and it got more questions than their usual slide deck. Another noted that the tool’s simplicity removes a big barrier for non-experts trying to engage with energy topics. The project’s GitHub page has over 300 stars and a handful of contributors adding new features, like templates for different countries or languages. The team encourages users to suggest improvements or submit fixes, keeping the tool evolving with community input.

## What’s next for grid2poster

The developers plan to add more customization options, like letting users highlight specific power plants or regions. They’re also working on a web version so people can generate posters directly in their browser without installing anything. For now, the tool remains free and open-source, with no plans to monetize it. The team’s message is clear: they want more people to see and understand their energy systems. If you’ve ever stared at a dense spreadsheet of grid data and wished for a simpler way to explain it, this is the tool for you.
