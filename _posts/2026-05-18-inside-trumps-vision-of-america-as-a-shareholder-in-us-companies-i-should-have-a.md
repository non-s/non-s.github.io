Donald Trump has a simple idea to boost the U.S. economy: the government should take ownership stakes in major American companies. At a meeting last year with Intel’s CEO [Lip-Bu Tan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lip-Bu_Tan), the president floated the idea of giving the country a 10% stake in the chip giant—just for free. When Tan agreed, Trump said he immediately thought, *“Shit, I should have asked for more.”* The exchange, revealed in an interview with Fortune, shows how Trump views corporate power as a tool for national strength rather than private gain. He insists he’s not trying to take over companies but to ensure they thrive. *“There’s nothing in it for me other than I want companies to do well,”* he told Fortune. *“I want to help American companies.”* His argument is that if the government has skin in the game, it can push for policies that benefit the broader economy—like lower prices, more jobs, and higher wages. Critics, however, see it as a step toward government overreach. *“Some people actually think it’s un-American,”* Trump said. *“They say, ‘You’re taking their company away.’”* He dismisses that view, framing it instead as a patriotic investment in the country’s future. The idea isn’t new. During his presidency, Trump floated similar ideas for energy companies and other sectors, but nothing ever materialized. Now, with the U.S. facing rising competition from China and Europe, he’s reviving the concept as a way to keep American firms competitive. **How Trump’s plan would work in practice** Trump’s proposal isn’t about forcing companies to sell shares. He’s talking about voluntary deals, where the government gets a stake in exchange for something—like tax breaks, subsidies, or regulatory relief. For example, he’s suggested that companies could get help with their energy costs in exchange for giving the U.S. a small ownership slice. *“I’m not saying it’s easy,”* he admitted. *“But if we do it right, it could be a game—wait, no, it could be a win for everyone.”* His comments about Intel show how casually he approaches these ideas. He doesn’t see it as a hostile takeover but as a negotiation where both sides benefit. The Intel CEO reportedly didn’t flinch when Trump made the offer. That might be because Intel has benefited from billions in federal subsidies and tax breaks over the years. Still, the optics of the U.S. government owning part of a private company would be highly unusual. **The national debt distraction** Trump also used the interview to float another unconventional idea: using America’s natural resources to pay down the national debt. He didn’t spell out how, but he suggested that assets like oil reserves, rare earth minerals, or even land could be leveraged. *“We’ve got so much stuff,”* he said. *“The value is enormous. Why aren’t we using it?”* Economists would likely scoff at the idea of monetizing natural assets to reduce debt. The national debt is over $34 trillion, and selling off mineral rights or land wouldn’t put a serious dent in it. But Trump’s point isn’t about the math—it’s about forcing a rethink of how the U.S. manages its wealth. He’s argued in the past that the U.S. could save money by drilling more oil domestically or exploiting other resources, rather than relying on imports. His broader message is that America’s financial problems aren’t just about spending—they’re about underutilized assets. **What’s next?** Trump’s ideas about government stakes in companies and natural resources are far from becoming policy. For now, they’re more about shaping the conversation around economic nationalism and corporate accountability. If he wins a second term, he could push harder for these ideas, especially if Republicans control Congress. But even then, the legal and practical hurdles would be massive. Companies would fight tooth and nail to avoid government interference. Courts would likely scrutinize any deals. And the public might not buy the argument that the U.S. needs to own part of Intel to stay competitive. Still, Trump’s willingness to float these ideas shows how he blends business instincts with political strategy. He sees deals—whether in real estate or government policy—as transactions where the strongest player gets the best outcome. Whether that’s good for the country is a debate for another day.
