Dennis Locorriere, the longtime frontman of Dr. Hook and the Medicine Show, died on Monday at age 76. The cause wasn’t immediately clear. Locorriere was the band’s lead vocalist and guitarist, shaping their sound with his raspy, emotive voice. He sang lead on their biggest hits, including the 1972 single 'Sylvia’s Mother' and the 1979 country-pop crossover 'When You’re in Love with a Beautiful Woman.' The band’s raw, storytelling style made them standouts in the country-rock scene of the 1970s and 1980s. Their music often balanced humor and heartbreak, a signature that endeared them to fans worldwide. Locorriere was born in New Jersey in 1948 and started his career playing in local clubs before joining Dr. Hook in the late 1960s. He remained with the band through multiple lineup changes and their shift from country-rock to more polished pop in the 1980s. Even after the band’s peak years, Locorriere continued performing, touring with a revived version of Dr. Hook and occasionally releasing solo work. He also played a role in preserving the band’s legacy, giving interviews and performing classic hits for new audiences. Locorriere’s voice was impossible to miss—whether on the radio or on stage. His delivery on 'Sexy Eyes,' a 1980 hit, remains one of his most recognizable performances. The song’s catchy chorus and playful lyrics made it a staple at weddings and classic rock radio stations for decades. Beyond his work with Dr. Hook, Locorriere released solo albums and occasionally collaborated with other artists. He was known for his down-to-earth personality and willingness to connect with fans, often chatting between songs during live shows. Fans and fellow musicians remembered him as a generous performer who stayed true to his roots, even as the music industry changed around him. The band’s manager confirmed Locorriere’s death but did not release additional details about his passing. Tributes poured in from musicians and fans, many sharing memories of seeing him perform live. Locorriere is survived by his wife, children, and grandchildren. His legacy lives on through his recordings and the bands that still play his songs. For many, he was the voice of an era when country and rock blended seamlessly. His music will keep playing, even if he isn’t here to sing it himself.
