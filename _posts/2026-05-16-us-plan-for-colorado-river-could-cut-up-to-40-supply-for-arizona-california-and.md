The Trump administration has proposed a plan to reduce Colorado River water supplies by up to 40% for Arizona, California and Nevada, as the drought-stricken river’s reservoirs continue to drop to dangerously low levels. The proposal, revealed Wednesday during an Arizona water officials’ meeting, comes after seven states drawing water from the river failed to reach a consensus on voluntary cuts. The plan targets three of the seven states most dependent on the river, which supplies water to 40 million people across the western U.S. and irrigates 5.5 million acres of farmland.

## Reservoirs hit record lows as drought persists

Lake Mead and Lake Powell, the two largest reservoirs on the Colorado River, have dropped to historic lows in recent months, prompting urgent federal intervention. The U.S. Bureau of Reclamation [U.S. Bureau of Reclamation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Bureau_of_Reclamation) has warned that further reductions in water deliveries may be necessary if drought conditions persist. The proposed cuts would be the most significant since the river’s management guidelines were last updated in 2007.

Officials from Arizona, California and Nevada have expressed concern over the proposed reductions, particularly in Arizona, which stands to lose the largest share of its water allocation. The state relies heavily on the Colorado River, which provides about 40% of its water supply. Nevada, though less dependent, would face severe cuts that could strain its water resources, especially in the Las Vegas area. California, the largest user of Colorado River water, would also see significant reductions, though exact allocations remain under negotiation.

## Failed negotiations force federal action

The proposal follows months of failed negotiations among the seven states that depend on the Colorado River: Arizona, California, Nevada, Colorado, New Mexico, Utah and Wyoming. The states were tasked with reaching a voluntary agreement to reduce water usage by 2 million to 4 million acre-feet per year to prevent further depletion of the river’s reservoirs. Despite multiple rounds of talks, no consensus was reached, forcing the federal government to step in with a mandatory plan.

Arizona’s top water official, Tom Buschatzke [Tom Buschatzke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Buschatzke), shared details of the proposal during a state water advisory meeting, describing it as a "last resort" measure to prevent a water crisis. "We’re at a point where we have no choice but to consider these cuts," Buschatzke said. "The federal government is making it clear that if we don’t act, they will."

## Farmers and cities brace for impact

Agriculture, which consumes about 80% of the Colorado River’s water, would bear the brunt of the proposed cuts. Farmers in Arizona and California, who grow crops such as lettuce, alfalfa and citrus, could face severe water shortages that threaten their livelihoods. Cities like Phoenix and Los Angeles would also see reduced water supplies, though some urban areas have water conservation measures in place to mitigate the impact.

The proposed cuts are part of a broader effort by the federal government to stabilize the Colorado River system, which has been under strain for more than two decades of drought. The river’s flow has declined by about 20% since 2000, and scientists warn that climate change will continue to reduce water availability in the region.

## What happens next?

The Trump administration’s proposal is now subject to public comment and review before any final decisions are made. The U.S. Bureau of Reclamation will hold hearings in the coming weeks to gather feedback from affected states, tribes and stakeholders. Meanwhile, water managers in Arizona, California and Nevada are scrambling to develop contingency plans to cope with the potential cuts. If implemented, the reductions would take effect in 2024, giving states just months to prepare for the changes.
