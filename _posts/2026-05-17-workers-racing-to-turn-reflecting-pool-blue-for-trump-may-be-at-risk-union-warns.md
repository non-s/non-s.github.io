A trade union monitoring the renovation of Washington DC’s [National Mall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Mall) reflecting pool says crews are moving too fast and may be cutting corners on safety. The union spokesman, [Gregory Tew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor_and_Congress_of_Industrial_Organizations), told reporters Friday that workers appear to be skipping standard protocols to finish repainting the pool in time for President Donald Trump’s July 4 celebration of the US’s 250th anniversary. The pool, one of the capital’s most visible landmarks, was drained and fenced off in May, shutting down a popular tourist photo spot near the [Lincoln Memorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial). The fencing blocks public access, so tourists can no longer walk its edge or toss coins into its waters. It’s normally a 10-minute walk from the [Martin Luther King Jr. Memorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Luther_King_Jr._Memorial). That’s where King delivered his 1963 “I Have a Dream” speech, a defining moment in American history tied to the reflecting pool’s image. The drained basin sits directly between the Washington Monument and the Lincoln Memorial, making it a prime backdrop for the anniversary fireworks show Trump wants to broadcast nationwide. Contractors hired by the [National Park Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Park_Service) have been working around the clock to repaint the pool’s interior white, a project Trump announced last year during a rally in South Carolina. Park Service officials have not responded to repeated requests for comment on the union’s safety concerns or the timeline. The union’s warning follows a pattern of rushed projects tied to Trump’s public events, including preparations for his rallies and the recent State of the Union address, where crews worked overnight to set up staging. At the reflecting pool, workers are using heavy equipment to scrape decades of algae and grime off the concrete bottom before applying fresh paint. The union says the haste is unnecessary since the pool normally gets a full renovation every 10 years, and the last one was completed in 2019. Crews are also installing new LED lighting along the pool’s edge, which is part of the anniversary spectacle Trump has promised. The lighting is meant to create a “glowing blue” effect for TV cameras, a color Trump has repeatedly demanded despite objections from designers who say the natural blue of the water won’t match. Designers proposed a soft white light to complement the monument, but Trump insisted on blue, which the union says adds another layer of pressure to finish on schedule. ## What safety risks are workers facing? The union’s main concern is that crews are skipping the required 48-hour drying period between coats of paint to meet the July 4 deadline. That could lead to peeling or uneven surfaces, which would require redoing the work later. Workers are also using respirators less than required while sanding old paint, raising concerns about silica dust exposure. The union says the Park Service has not provided full safety briefings or updated equipment for the accelerated schedule. At least one worker told union reps he was told to skip the usual breaks to keep up with the pace, which violates federal labor rules. The union has filed a complaint with the [Occupational Safety and Health Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration) but hasn’t received a response. ## How does this connect to Trump’s July 4 plans? Trump first mentioned repainting the reflecting pool during a 2023 rally, calling it a “beautification project” to honor American history. His team sees the July 4 event as a chance to showcase a “perfect” America, and the reflecting pool’s blue glow is part of that vision. The event will feature a speech from Trump, a military flyover, and a fireworks display over the Mall, with networks like Fox News and Newsmax set to broadcast it live. Critics say the rush reflects a pattern of Trump’s events, where aesthetics and optics often take priority over process. Last year, crews installed temporary bleachers for a Veterans Day event at the [Arlington National Cemetery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery) just days before the ceremony, leading to complaints from families of buried veterans. The reflecting pool project is costing taxpayers about $2 million, a figure the union says could balloon if mistakes require rework. Park Service officials have defended the timeline, saying the pool needs to be ready for the thousands expected to gather on the Mall for the anniversary. But the union argues the project could easily wait until fall, after the celebrations, without disrupting the public. The pool has been closed to the public since May 1, and there’s no clear sign of when it will reopen. For now, tourists can only view it from a distance, snapping photos of the fenced-off basin with the monuments behind it. ## What happens next? The union plans to escalate its complaint to Congress next week, asking lawmakers to investigate the safety shortcuts. Meanwhile, the Park Service has scheduled a press tour of the pool for June 20, where officials will likely defend the project’s progress. The reflecting pool is expected to reopen to the public in August, weather permitting, but the union warns that rushed work could leave it in worse shape than before. If the paint doesn’t cure properly, the pool may need to be repainted within a year, costing another $1 million. For the workers, the pressure is only increasing as the deadline looms. One painter, who asked not to be named, told union reps he’s been working 12-hour shifts with no overtime pay. “They keep saying July 4 is non-negotiable,” he said. “But at what cost?”
