The World Cup 2026 will open June 11 in Mexico, with 48 national teams set to converge on the United States, Mexico, and Canada for training, acclimatization, and the group stage. Each team has selected a base camp—its primary hub for lodging, practice, recovery, and match-day preparation—before advancing in the tournament. The camps typically include full training facilities, gyms, medical centers, recovery zones, and media areas, ensuring teams have everything needed during the competition’s first phase.

Argentina, Brazil, England, Portugal, Spain, and Iran are among the nations to have confirmed their base camps. Argentina will train in [Houston, Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houston), while Brazil is based in [Los Angeles, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles). England’s players will stay and practice in [Atlanta, Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlanta), and Portugal’s squad will be in [New York City, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City). Spain has chosen [Kansas City, Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri), and Iran will be based in [Toronto, Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toronto).

## How base camps are selected and what they include

Host nations offered bids to FIFA, which allocated base camps based on accessibility, training quality, fan access, and logistical support. Each facility must meet FIFA’s standards for fields, medical care, recovery, and security. Teams often add bespoke elements—like altitude chambers or hydrotherapy pools—tailored to their needs during the six-week tournament window.

With rosters now largely finalized, focus shifts to logistics. Teams will arrive weeks before their first matches to acclimate to time zones and climates, especially for those playing in high-altitude venues like Mexico City or mountainous areas in Canada. The host cities have upgraded infrastructure, hotels, and training grounds to accommodate the increased demand.

## Confirmed base camps for top teams

France, Germany, Italy, and the Netherlands are expected to finalize their base camps in the coming weeks, with strong contenders likely selecting locations in [Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles), [Dallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dallas), or [Vancouver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancouver). These hubs will serve as home bases through at least the group stage, with some teams potentially relocating after advancing.

Teams from Asia and Africa—including Japan, South Korea, and Morocco—are also finalizing their choices, with many favoring U.S. cities with international airports and mild June weather. The proximity to match venues often influences the decision, especially for teams drawn in Groups A, B, or C, which open in Los Angeles, Mexico City, and Guadalajara, respectively.

## Fan access and economic impact

Base camps double as fan engagement zones during training sessions, which are open to the public at scheduled times. Host cities expect a tourism boost from visiting supporters, with local businesses preparing for increased foot traffic around team hotels and training grounds. Cities like Atlanta, Houston, and Toronto have marketed their facilities aggressively to attract teams, citing modern amenities and strong transport links.

Security and privacy are also priorities. FIFA and local authorities coordinate to protect teams from disruptions while allowing controlled access for accredited media and fans. Training schedules are released only days in advance to prevent ambushes or overcrowding.

The final list of base camps will be published by FIFA before May 1, giving fans time to plan visits or watch public training sessions. Until then, teams continue finalizing squads and staff, with medical and recovery teams already touring proposed facilities.

As the June 11 kickoff approaches, the focus on base camps reflects their dual role: a sanctuary for preparation and a showcase for host cities’ readiness to host global football’s biggest event.
