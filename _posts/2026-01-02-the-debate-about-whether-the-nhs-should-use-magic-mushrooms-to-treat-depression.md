The National Health Service is weighing whether to incorporate psychedelic mushrooms into depression treatment protocols after a surge in clinical trials since 2022. Researchers in the UK have joined international efforts to test psilocybin, the psychoactive compound in magic mushrooms, as a potential alternative to standard antidepressants. Early data suggests the substance may rapidly reduce symptoms in patients with treatment-resistant depression, often within hours of a single dose. [Imperial College London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_College_London) and other institutions have led these studies, which follow a global movement toward psychedelic-assisted therapies.

## NHS weighs psychedelic therapy amid growing mental health crisis
More than 1.2 million people in the UK were waiting for mental health treatment as of 2023, according to NHS England. Traditional antidepressants, while effective for many, fail to help roughly 30% of patients, leaving a significant gap in care. Psychedelic-assisted therapy offers a potential solution by targeting the brain’s serotonin receptors in ways conventional drugs do not. The NHS’s mental health directorate has taken note, with officials monitoring trial outcomes closely. A spokesperson for [NHS England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NHS_England) confirmed internal discussions are underway about future policy shifts.

## How psychedelic therapy works against depression
Psilocybin therapy involves a controlled setting where patients receive a single dose under medical supervision. The experience typically lasts 6-8 hours, often accompanied by talk therapy before and after. Unlike daily antidepressants, which build up in the system over weeks, psilocybin’s effects can be immediate and long-lasting for some patients. Studies published in [Nature](https://www.nature.com/) and [The New England Journal of Medicine](https://www.nejm.org/) report symptom reductions persisting for months in some cases. Researchers caution that the approach is not a cure-all and requires rigorous screening to avoid adverse reactions.

## Regulatory and ethical hurdles remain
The UK’s [Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency](https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/medicines-and-healthcare-products-regulatory-agency) has not approved psilocybin for depression treatment outside of trials. Legal barriers in many countries, including the UK, classify psilocybin as a Class A drug, complicating research. However, the [Health and Social Care Committee](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm201921/cmselect/cmhealth/822/82202.htm) has called for accelerated trials and potential rescheduling to facilitate broader access. Ethical concerns include the risk of bad trips, which could worsen mental health in vulnerable patients. Advocates argue the potential benefits outweigh risks if strict protocols are followed.
