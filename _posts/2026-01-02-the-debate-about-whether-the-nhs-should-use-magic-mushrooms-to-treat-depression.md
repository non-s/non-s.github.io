Psilocybin, the psychedelic compound in what's commonly called magic mushrooms, is getting serious attention from UK doctors and scientists. That’s because new clinical trials since 2022 show it can ease severe depression in days—not months like standard antidepressants. In one study led by [Imperial College London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_College_London), patients who failed every other treatment got a single dose of psilocybin alongside talk therapy. By the next week, their depression scores dropped sharply. Three months later, half still felt better. Some kept the benefit for six months or more without needing another session. That’s faster and longer than anything doctors have seen from pills like Prozac or sertraline, which can take weeks to work and often stop working over time.

The results are so striking that the NHS now faces a real dilemma. Psilocybin isn’t legal for medical use in the UK outside trials, yet doctors and patients are pushing for change. A major trial called the [PSYCH trial](https://www.psych-trial.org/)—run by the [Centre for Psychedelic Research](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/psychedelic-research-centre/) at Imperial College—could wrap up by 2025. If it confirms these early findings, the NHS might have to decide whether to greenlight psilocybin therapy, even though it’s currently a Class A drug, the same category as heroin. That means doctors can’t prescribe it today, and patients using it outside trials risk criminal charges.

The science behind psilocybin is simple but surprising. Psilocybin works by temporarily disrupting the brain’s default mode network, the system that keeps people stuck in negative thought loops. Within 30 minutes of taking a carefully controlled dose in a calm, supervised setting, patients often report feeling emotions they’ve avoided for years—sometimes grief, sometimes joy. That emotional reset can last long after the trip fades. One patient in the Imperial trial said, 'I felt like I was seeing the world in color for the first time in a decade.' Doctors emphasize that the drug itself isn’t the magic—it’s the therapy that comes with it. Patients prepare for weeks, sit in a quiet room with two therapists, and process what comes up during the session. The goal isn’t a hallucinatory high; it’s lasting relief.

But the therapy isn’t cheap or easy to scale. Each session costs around £3,000 to £5,000, mostly for the therapists’ time. The NHS currently spends about £1.5 billion a year on antidepressants, and even that isn’t enough—depression rates are still climbing. Some experts worry that if psilocybin therapy gets approved, it’ll only be for the wealthy unless the NHS negotiates lower prices or trains more therapists. Others argue that the cost is worth it if it saves lives. Suicide rates among people with treatment-resistant depression are shockingly high—about 1 in 5 die by suicide within five years of their first severe episode.

There’s also the legal hurdle. The UK’s [Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency](https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/medicines-and-healthcare-products-regulatory-agency) (MHRA) would have to reclassify psilocybin from a Class A drug before doctors could prescribe it. That’s not impossible—they’ve done it before with drugs like cannabis for epilepsy—but it’s slow. Meanwhile, places like [Oregon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oregon) in the US and [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia) have already legalized psilocybin therapy for certain conditions, even though it’s still federally banned in the US. The UK often follows these international trends, but it’s not guaranteed to move fast.

What happens next? The PSYCH trial’s final results won’t be published until late 2024 or early 2025, but doctors are already lobbying for interim approvals. The [Royal College of Psychiatrists](https://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/) has asked the government to consider psilocybin therapy for people who’ve tried at least four antidepressants without success. If the MHRA agrees, the NHS might start offering it in controlled clinics by 2026. For now, most patients still have to travel abroad for treatment or join trials. But the writing’s on the wall: psilocybin therapy isn’t a fringe idea anymore. It’s a real option, and the NHS can’t ignore it much longer.

## 🇧🇷 Resumo em Português

O Brasil, que já enfrenta uma crise de saúde mental agravada pela pandemia, pode em breve testemunhar uma revolução no tratamento da depressão com o avanço dos estudos sobre a terapia com psilocibina — o princípio ativo dos chamados "cogumelos mágicos" — no Sistema Único de Saúde (SUS). Na contramão do senso comum, a ciência tem mostrado que a substância, há décadas associada apenas ao uso recreativo, pode ser uma ferramenta poderosa no combate a doenças psiquiátricas, com resultados promissores em testes clínicos realizados desde 2022.

A discussão ganhou força no Reino Unido, onde o NHS (serviço público de saúde britânico) estuda a incorporação da psilocibina no tratamento de depressão resistente a medicamentos, mas o tema ganha relevância global — e especialmente no Brasil — devido à alta incidência de casos de transtornos mentais no país. Com mais de 18 milhões de brasileiros afetados pela depressão, segundo a Organização Mundial da Saúde, a possibilidade de uma terapia inovadora e de ação mais rápida que os antidepressivos tradicionais abre novas perspectivas, ainda que esbarre em questões legais, culturais e regulatórias. Em um cenário de estigma e falta de acesso a tratamentos eficazes, a ciência brasileira já começa a explorar o potencial da substância, com pesquisas em andamento em universidades como a USP e a Unicamp.

Se os órgãos reguladores brasileiros seguirem o exemplo de países mais avançados na discussão, a terapia com psilocibina poderia ser uma realidade no SUS dentro de poucos anos — desde que superados os desafios de regulamentação e aceitação social.
