The NHS isn’t handing out shrooms just yet, but it’s running trials to see if psilocybin—the compound in magic mushrooms—could help people with severe depression. Since 2022, clinical studies across the UK have focused on whether a single controlled dose, paired with therapy, can break cycles of suffering that drugs like SSRIs can’t touch. The results so far are hard to ignore. In one small trial at [Imperial College London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_College_London), 12 out of 20 patients saw their depression scores drop by at least half after three weeks. That’s the kind of response usually seen only in ketamine clinics—not from antidepressants that take weeks to work and sometimes don’t work at all.

## How psilocybin therapy works

Psilocybin isn’t like your usual antidepressant. It doesn’t tweak serotonin levels gradually. Instead, it floods the brain with activity in areas linked to self-reflection and emotional processing—like hitting reset on a stuck record. Patients take the drug in a controlled setting, often wearing eye shades and listening to calming music, while therapists guide them through the experience. The goal isn’t just to feel better for a day. It’s to help people process trauma or negative thought patterns that fuel their depression. The effects can last months, not days. That’s what makes the early data so intriguing. But the therapy is still experimental. No psilocybin drug has full approval from [MHRA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicines_and_Healthcare_products_Regulatory_Agency), the UK’s drug regulator, though they’re reviewing applications fast.

## The UK’s cautious approach

Britain’s rollout is slower than in the US or Canada. The FDA has already designated psilocybin a “breakthrough therapy” for depression, and Oregon became the first US state to legalize regulated psilocybin therapy in 2023. Canada, meanwhile, allows doctors to prescribe psilocybin for end-of-life distress. The UK’s [NHS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Health_Service) is taking a wait-and-see approach. Trials are limited to specialist centers like the [Maudsley Hospital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlem_Royal_Hospital) in London and [Johns Hopkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_Hospital) in Baltimore—wait, that’s in the US—sorry, [Manchester Royal Infirmary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_Royal_Infirmary). The NHS has partnered with charities and universities to run these studies, but they’re not offering psilocybin therapy in clinics yet. Even if the trials succeed, regulators will need to see years of safety data before approving it for general use.

## Who’s getting this treatment—and who isn’t

Right now, psilocybin therapy is only available to a tiny slice of patients: adults with treatment-resistant depression who’ve tried at least two antidepressants without success. That’s a narrow group, but it’s also the hardest to help. These are the people stuck in a cycle of despair, often unable to work or socialize. The trials aren’t open to everyone. Patients go through rigorous screening to rule out risks like psychosis or heart conditions that could make psilocybin dangerous. Even then, the experience can be intense. Some describe it as emotionally overwhelming, though most say the discomfort is worth it for the relief that follows.

## The big hurdles ahead

Psilocybin’s path to mainstream medicine faces three big roadblocks. First is the stigma. Magic mushrooms still carry a cultural baggage of raves and arrests, even though the compound in them is now being studied as a serious medical tool. Second is the cost. Therapy sessions require trained guides for hours, which isn’t cheap. The NHS can’t afford to roll this out widely without proof it saves money long-term by reducing hospitalizations or sick days. Third is the law. Psilocybin is a Class A drug in the UK, meaning unauthorized possession can land you in prison. Even if the MHRA approves a medicine based on it, the government will have to reclassify psilocybin or create a legal framework for medical use—no small political lift.

## What happens next

The next two years will be critical. The NHS is expected to publish more trial results by 2025, and drug companies are racing to develop psilocybin-based treatments that regulators might approve first. [Compass Pathways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_Pharmaceuticals), a London-based biotech firm, is leading one of the biggest trials and plans to seek FDA approval in the US next year. If they succeed, it could force the UK to speed up its own approval process. But even if psilocybin gets the green light, don’t expect it to replace Prozac overnight. It’ll likely be reserved for the toughest cases, at least at first. Still, for people who’ve run out of options, it offers a sliver of hope—one that’s not just another pill to swallow.

## 🇧🇷 Resumo em Português

O Reino Unido deu um passo histórico ao testar, pela primeira vez pelo sistema público de saúde (NHS), a terapia com psilocibina — o princípio ativo dos chamados “cogumelos mágicos” — no tratamento de casos graves de depressão, abrindo uma nova fronteira na saúde mental. Enquanto países como o Brasil ainda debatem a regulamentação de substâncias psicodélicas para uso terapêutico, a iniciativa britânica acende um alerta e uma oportunidade: será que a ciência brasileira, com sua biodiversidade única, está perdendo tempo ao ignorar o potencial dessas substâncias?

A decisão do NHS surge após estudos preliminares mostrarem que uma ou duas sessões de terapia assistida com psilocibina, combinada a acompanhamento psicológico, podem produzir efeitos duradouros em pacientes resistentes a tratamentos convencionais, como antidepressivos e eletroconvulsoterapia. No Brasil, onde a depressão afeta mais de 13 milhões de pessoas segundo a Organização Mundial da Saúde e o SUS enfrenta filas intermináveis para atendimento em saúde mental, a discussão ganha urgência. Especialistas brasileiros já apontam a Amazônia como um laboratório natural para pesquisas com plantas e fungos psicodélicos nativos, mas a falta de marcos legais e preconceitos culturais travam avanços. Enquanto nações como o Canadá e os EUA já avançam em protocolos regulatórios, o Brasil corre o risco de ficar para trás em uma área que pode revolucionar a psiquiatria.

O próximo passo será cobrar do governo federal e das agências reguladoras, como a Anvisa, a revisão das políticas sobre substâncias controladas, incentivando pesquisas clínicas e abrindo espaço para que a ciência brasileira não fique apenas assistindo ao boom de inovações no exterior.
