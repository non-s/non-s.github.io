Health Secretary Wes Streeting [left Downing Street](https://www.gov.uk/government/people/wes-streeting) less than 20 minutes after arriving for a previously unannounced meeting with Prime Minister Keir Starmer on Tuesday, raising immediate questions about the agenda and outcome. Streeting’s brief visit came amid escalating tensions over NHS funding and ongoing junior doctor strikes, which have disrupted services across England for weeks. Sources familiar with the meeting described it as ‘short and tense,’ though no further details were disclosed. The prime minister’s office confirmed the encounter but declined to provide specifics, stating only that ‘constructive discussions’ were held. Downing Street also refused to elaborate on the reasons for Streeting’s swift departure, which was captured on camera as he exited the building at 15:47 GMT. The health secretary, a Labour MP for Ilford North, has faced mounting pressure from the British Medical Association over pay disputes and funding shortfalls. The BMA has threatened further industrial action if demands for a 26% pay rise and improved staffing levels are not met, a scenario that could force the government into emergency negotiations. Streeting’s office did not respond to requests for comment before publication. The abrupt meeting follows weeks of critical coverage over the NHS’s financial strain, with waiting lists exceeding 7.6 million and record-high ambulance delays reported last month. Analysts suggest the government may be seeking to assert control over NHS strategy ahead of a potential autumn spending review. Streeting, a former Treasury minister, has previously warned that the NHS requires an additional £30 billion annually to meet demand, a figure the government has not yet committed to. The prime minister’s team has emphasized ‘long-term planning’ rather than immediate cash injections, though the junior doctor dispute has forced a rethink. Tuesday’s encounter also comes days after Chancellor Rachel Reeves [delivered her first fiscal statement](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/chancellor-rachel-reeves-delivers-first-fiscal-statement), which included a £3.5 billion package to ‘reform and modernize’ public services, including the NHS. However, the funding allocated fell short of NHS England’s requests, leaving the service’s financial outlook uncertain. Streeting’s early exit from No 10 may signal unresolved differences between him and the prime minister on healthcare priorities. While Starmer has pledged to ‘fix the NHS,’ his government’s room for maneuver is constrained by fiscal constraints and competing spending demands. The junior doctor strikes, now in their fifth week, have already cost the NHS an estimated £2 billion in lost productivity and emergency care delays. With no resolution in sight, Streeting’s brief meeting suggests the government is struggling to balance public service demands with economic realities. Further updates are expected in the coming days as both sides assess their next moves.
