Andalucía’s regional election on Sunday will determine whether the conservative [People’s Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)) (PP) solidifies its dominance in Spain’s most populous region and sets the stage for next year’s general election. Polls suggest the PP, led by [Juanma Moreno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Moreno_Bonilla), will secure an outright majority, extending its control over Andalucía’s 109 seats. This would mark a historic third term for the PP in a region long considered a Socialist stronghold.

The Socialist Party (PSOE), led by Prime Minister [Pedro Sánchez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_S%C3%A1nchez), faces another bruising defeat after recent losses in local and European elections. PSOE has governed Andalucía for nearly four decades but now trails the PP by double digits in most surveys. The party’s struggles reflect broader voter fatigue with Sánchez’s coalition government, which relies on support from the left-wing [Sumar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumar_(Spain)) alliance and regional partners.

## Far-right Vox seeks to expand influence in Andalucía

The [Vox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vox_(Spain)) party, which has surged in national polls, will test its strength in Andalucía, a region where it has previously underperformed. Founded in 2013, Vox advocates for nationalist policies, opposes regional autonomy, and targets immigration and gender laws. Its performance Sunday could signal whether its growth has plateaued or continues to reshape Spain’s political map ahead of the 2025 general election.

Analysts say the PP’s expected victory strengthens Moreno’s profile as a potential future national leader. His handling of Andalucía’s economy, including job growth and infrastructure projects, has drawn praise. Meanwhile, Sánchez’s government faces criticism over inflation, housing costs, and disputes with regional leaders over fiscal policy.

## Election seen as bellwether for Spain’s 2025 vote

The Andalucía ballot is the last major regional election before Spain’s general vote, expected in late 2025. Political observers view the outcome as a referendum on nationwide sentiment, particularly among middle-class and rural voters who shifted toward the right in recent years. A strong PP performance could embolden the party to push for early national elections, while a weaker-than-expected result may force Sánchez to reassess his leadership strategy.

Turnout typically exceeds 60% in Andalucía, reflecting the region’s deep political engagement. The campaign has focused on economic stability, migration control, and cultural identity, themes that dominate Spain’s national debate. Polling stations open at 9 a.m. Sunday and close at 8 p.m., with results expected late evening.

What happens next could determine whether Spain moves toward a more conservative government or remains on its current left-leaning path. The election’s outcome will also test the durability of Vox’s appeal and the PSOE’s ability to recover from its recent setbacks.
