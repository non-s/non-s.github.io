British Prime Minister [Keir Starmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keir_Starmer) on Tuesday reaffirmed his commitment to remain in office despite the resignation of four junior ministers following last week’s UK election losses. Starmer met with his Cabinet as internal pressure against his leadership mounted, with lawmakers demanding clarity over his future plans. A major speech on Monday, where Starmer declared he was “not walking away,” failed to quell dissent within his party. The resignations underscore growing dissatisfaction among Labour MPs over the party’s performance in the May 2 elections, which saw significant losses in local and regional contests, including former Labour strongholds in Wales and Scotland. The first to resign was junior minister [Miatta Fahnbulleh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miatta_Fahnbulleh), who urged Starmer to “set a timetable for an orderly transition.” She was followed by [Jess Phillips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jess_Phillips), the safeguarding minister, who cited concerns over leadership direction despite calling Starmer “a good man fundamentally.” Welsh MP [Alex Davies-Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alex_Davies-Jones) and Glasgow South West MP [Zubir Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zubir_Ahmed) also tendered resignations, citing Labour’s poor showing in the Welsh parliament and Scottish constituencies. The resignations mark the most significant challenge to Starmer’s authority since he became prime minister in July 2024 following the Labour Party’s landslide victory in the general election. ## Starmer rejects calls to step down amid internal turmoil Starmer’s office issued a statement on Tuesday reiterating his stance, calling the resignations “regrettable” but stressing his determination to lead the party forward. The prime minister has faced criticism for his handling of key policy issues, including economic recovery and public services, which contributed to Labour’s underperformance in the recent elections. Analysts note that the resignations reflect deeper divisions within the Labour Party over Starmer’s cautious approach and perceived lack of bold policy initiatives. The losses in traditionally Labour-held areas, particularly in Wales and Scotland, have raised questions about the party’s ability to retain its electoral base ahead of the next general election, expected in 2030. ## Welsh and Scottish losses highlight regional discontent The resignations from Welsh and Scottish MPs highlight growing discontent in regions that were once Labour strongholds. [Alex Davies-Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alex_Davies-Jones), representing a Welsh constituency, cited the party’s poor performance in the Senedd elections, where Labour lost several seats to Plaid Cymru and the Conservatives. Similarly, [Zubir Ahmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zubir_Ahmed), a Scottish MP, pointed to Labour’s struggles in Glasgow, where the party failed to secure key council seats. The internal party turmoil comes as Starmer prepares for a potential reshuffle to reassert control and address concerns over his leadership. While some Labour MPs have called for a leadership contest, Starmer’s allies insist he retains the support of the parliamentary Labour Party. The prime minister is expected to deliver a further statement in the coming days to outline his plans for rebuilding Labour’s electoral prospects. The next major test for Starmer will be the party’s annual conference in September, where he may face renewed calls for change if the internal unrest persists.
