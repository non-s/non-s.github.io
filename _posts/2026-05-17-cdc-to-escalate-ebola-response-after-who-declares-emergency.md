The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention) is scaling up its fight against Ebola in the Democratic Republic of Congo [DRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo) and Uganda after the World Health Organization [WHO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization) declared the outbreak a Public Health Emergency of International Concern. The CDC’s incident manager Satish Pillai told reporters Sunday the agency will deploy extra staff to the two countries and activate its emergency response center. They’ll also provide lab testing, contact tracing, and surveillance support through existing country offices. Pillai stressed that the risk to the US stays low, even with the emergency declaration. The outbreak is driven by the Bundibugyo ebolavirus strain. Unlike the more common Zaire strain, there’s no approved vaccine or treatment for this one. Ebola spreads when infected people show symptoms like fever, vomiting, or diarrhea, but it’s not airborne. That’s why health workers focus on isolating patients and tracking anyone they’ve come in close contact with.

## Why this outbreak matters now
The WHO’s emergency call isn’t just a formality. It’s a signal that the outbreak is spreading in ways that could overwhelm local health systems. So far, over 200 cases and 150 deaths have been reported, mostly in remote areas of eastern DRC. The WHO’s declaration means more money and resources will flow to the region. The CDC’s quick response is part of that effort. They’re not waiting for cases to reach US shores. Instead, they’re helping local teams get ahead of the virus before it jumps borders. The Bundibugyo strain is less deadly than Zaire ebolavirus—about 30% case fatality rate compared to 50%—but it’s still deadly enough to cause serious harm in areas with weak health infrastructure. Travel and trade restrictions in the region are already tightening, which could slow the response if not managed carefully.

## What the CDC’s doing differently this time
This isn’t the first Ebola outbreak in the region, but the CDC is approaching it with lessons from past failures. They’re using a mix of old-school boots-on-the-ground work and new tech tools. For example, they’re using portable lab equipment to speed up testing in villages where labs are hours away. They’re also improving how they track contacts—using digital tools to follow up faster and avoid missing anyone. Pillai said the CDC’s emergency response center is running 24/7, coordinating with WHO and local health ministries. The agency has also sent teams to train health workers on how to safely handle patients and dispose of medical waste. These aren’t just small tweaks. They’re big changes from how the world responded to the 2014 West Africa Ebola outbreak, which killed over 11,000 people before it was contained.

## The bigger picture: why this isn’t just a local problem
Ebola doesn’t respect borders. Even if the risk to the US is low, the outbreak could spread to neighboring countries like Rwanda or Burundi, creating a regional crisis. That’s why the WHO’s emergency call matters. It’s meant to trigger a global response, not just a local one. But global help comes with challenges. Money and supplies need to get to the right places quickly, and governments in the region must cooperate. Right now, tensions between DRC and Uganda over border security are complicating things. Some health workers worry that mistrust could slow down the response. Meanwhile, misinformation about Ebola—like claims that it’s a hoax or that vaccines don’t work—is spreading in some communities. The CDC’s teams are working with local leaders to counter that.

## What happens next
The CDC’s emergency response center will stay active for at least the next few months. They’ll keep sending staff and supplies as long as the outbreak lasts. The WHO’s emergency committee will meet again in three months to reassess the situation. If the outbreak worsens, more international help could arrive. But if it’s contained, the focus will shift to rebuilding health systems in the region so they’re ready for the next outbreak. For now, the biggest concern is whether the response can keep up with the virus. Ebola moves fast, and in places with poor roads and weak health systems, it’s easy for cases to slip through the cracks. The CDC and WHO are racing against time, but they’re also racing against human behavior—like people refusing to isolate or health workers getting sick from lack of protective gear.
