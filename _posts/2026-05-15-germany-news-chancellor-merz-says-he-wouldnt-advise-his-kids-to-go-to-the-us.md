German Chancellor [Friedrich Merz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Merz) said Friday he would not recommend that young Germans, including his own children, consider moving to the United States for education or employment at this time. Speaking at a Catholic convention in Germany, Merz cited what he described as a rapidly deteriorating social climate in the U.S. as the primary reason for his stance. ‘My great admiration for America is not increasing at the moment,’ Merz said, adding that the current environment raises concerns about personal safety and societal stability.

The chancellor’s remarks follow a period of heightened diplomatic tension between [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany) and the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States), particularly during the contentious tenure of former U.S. President [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump). While Merz acknowledged that he had a ‘good phone call’ with Trump earlier this year, he emphasized that the broader social and political atmosphere in America has become less welcoming for foreigners, especially young professionals and students.

## Germany’s economic slowdown deepens in second quarter

Germany’s federal government has revised downward its economic outlook for the second quarter of 2026, citing a sharp slowdown driven by rising energy costs and declining industrial output. Energy-intensive industries across the country, including chemicals and steel, have reported job cuts and reduced production schedules as a result of higher input prices. Economists warn that if the trend continues, it could further strain Germany’s already fragile growth prospects and increase unemployment in key manufacturing regions.

The slowdown comes as Europe’s largest economy grapples with structural challenges, including an aging workforce, high regulatory burdens, and growing competition from Asian manufacturers. Industry leaders have called for urgent policy measures to ease energy costs and accelerate digital transformation to restore competitiveness.

## Whale death sparks questions over stranded humpback’s fate

Separately, Denmark is conducting tissue sampling of a dead humpback whale found off the coast of the island of [Anholt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anholt) in the Baltic Sea. The carcass discovery has reignited concerns about the welfare of a humpback whale that was previously stranded off Germany’s Baltic coast for weeks. Environmental groups are calling for a full investigation into the whale’s death and the possible role of shipping traffic or pollution in the region.

Marine biologists are analyzing the samples to determine the cause of death and whether the animal had been weakened by its earlier entanglement or exposure. The incident has highlighted ongoing concerns about marine biodiversity and the impact of human activity on protected species in European waters.

As Germany navigates rising geopolitical tensions, economic headwinds, and environmental challenges, Chancellor Merz’s comments on the U.S. underscore a broader shift in public sentiment about international mobility and opportunity. While the chancellor did not rule out future engagement with America, his remarks reflect growing caution among German policymakers toward cross-Atlantic migration and educational exchange during a period of global uncertainty. The government is expected to release further economic data next month, which may clarify the depth of the slowdown and inform policy responses.
